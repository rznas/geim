# Design Lenses Reference: Foundation Lenses

This document contains the core foundational lenses introduced in the "establishing-design-foundations" skill. These are the lenses every game designer should internalize first.

For the complete collection of 113 lenses, refer to "The Art of Game Design" book or the Deck of Lenses card deck/app.

---

## How to Use Design Lenses

### What Are Lenses?

- **NOT blueprints or recipes**: They don't tell you what to do
- **Examination tools**: Ways to view your design from different perspectives
- **Question sets**: Each lens is a set of questions to ask yourself
- **Perspective shifters**: Help you see aspects you might miss

### When to Use Lenses

- **During initial concepting**: Generating and refining ideas
- **During design**: Making decisions about mechanics, story, aesthetics, technology
- **During playtesting**: Analyzing what's working and what's not
- **When stuck**: Shift perspective to find new solutions
- **When something feels wrong**: Diagnose problems from multiple angles

### How to Apply a Lens

1. **Focus on the lens**: Read its questions
2. **Think about your game**: Apply questions to your specific design
3. **Be honest**: Don't defend your design; truly examine it
4. **Take notes**: Write down insights and contradictions
5. **Take action**: Use insights to improve your design
6. **Move to next lens**: Repeat with different perspective

### Lens Combinations

- Use multiple lenses in sequence for deeper analysis
- Some lenses work particularly well together (noted below)
- Don't try to use all lenses at once—focus on 3-5 per session

## Foundation Lenses

These are the first lenses introduced in "The Art of Game Design" and form the foundation for all other lenses.

### Lens #4: The Lens of Surprise

**Category**: Experience Design, Fun

**When to use**: When designing core gameplay loops, feedback systems, rewards, or story beats

**Questions to ask**:
- What will surprise players when they play my game?
- Does the story in my game have surprises? Do the game rules? Does the artwork? The technology?
- Do your rules give players ways to surprise each other?
- Do your rules give players ways to surprise themselves?

**Why it matters**:
- Fun = pleasure + surprises
- Surprise triggers pleasure centers in the brain
- Random patterns are more pleasurable than predictable ones
- Even unpleasant surprises activate pleasure centers
- Without surprise, experiences become stale and predictable

**Examples in practice**:
- **Mario**: Hidden blocks, secret areas, unexpected power-ups
- **Poker**: The reveal, unexpected hands, bluffing surprises
- **Portal**: Surprise uses for portal mechanics, unexpected solutions
- **Among Us**: Surprise betrayals, unexpected alibis

**Combines well with**:
- Lens #5 (Fun)
- Lens #6 (Curiosity)
- Lens #8 (Problem Solving)

**Anti-patterns**:
- Surprises that feel unfair or punishing
- Too many surprises (becomes overwhelming)
- Telegraphing surprises (ruins the effect)
- Random surprises with no player agency

### Lens #5: The Lens of Fun

**Category**: Experience Design, Player Enjoyment

**When to use**: Throughout the entire design process, especially when gameplay feels flat or boring

**Questions to ask**:
- What parts of my game are fun? Why?
- What parts need to be more fun?

**Why it matters**:
- Fun is desirable in nearly every game
- Not all games are "fun" in the same way (some are thrilling, some relaxing, some challenging)
- Fun defies simple analysis—sometimes you just know it
- Finding and amplifying fun is core to game design

**How to analyze fun**:
1. **Identify the moment**: Pinpoint exactly when fun happens
2. **Understand the components**: What elements create that fun?
3. **Analyze the why**: Why is this particular combination fun?
4. **Find the pattern**: Is this replicable? Expandable?
5. **Consider the opposite**: What would make it not fun?

**Types of fun** (from Nicole Lazzaro's research):
- **Hard fun**: Challenge, mastery, problem-solving
- **Easy fun**: Exploration, discovery, role-play
- **Altered states**: Excitement, relief, visceral sensations
- **The people factor**: Social interaction, competition, cooperation

**Examples in practice**:
- **Tetris**: Satisfying line clears, rising tension (hard fun + altered states)
- **Minecraft**: Creative freedom, discovery (easy fun)
- **Dark Souls**: Mastery through challenge (hard fun)
- **Party games**: Social laughter, shared experiences (people factor)

**Combines well with**:
- Lens #4 (Surprise)
- Lens #7 (Endogenous Value)
- Lens #8 (Problem Solving)

**Anti-patterns**:
- Assuming "fun" means the same thing to everyone
- Over-designing for one type of fun, ignoring others
- Forgetting that timing matters (fun in moderation, exhausting in excess)

### Lens #6: The Lens of Curiosity

**Category**: Player Motivation, Experience Design

**When to use**: When designing game goals, progression systems, story reveals, or any system meant to drive engagement

**Questions to ask**:
- What questions does my game put into the player's mind?
- What am I doing to make them care about these questions?
- What can I do to make them invent even more questions?

**Why it matters**:
- Play = manipulation that indulges curiosity
- Players engage when they're curious about outcomes
- Curiosity drives exploration, experimentation, and persistence
- Without curiosity, goals feel arbitrary and meaningless

**How to create curiosity**:
1. **Ask interesting questions**: "Can I beat this?" "What's in there?" "What happens if...?"
2. **Make them care**: Connect to player motivations, emotions, goals
3. **Partial information**: Show enough to intrigue, hide enough to wonder
4. **Multiple mystery layers**: When one question is answered, reveal another
5. **Player-generated questions**: Allow systems that create emergent questions

**Types of curiosity-driving questions**:
- **Achievement**: "Can I beat this challenge?"
- **Discovery**: "What's behind that door?"
- **Story**: "What happens next?"
- **Mastery**: "Can I do this better/faster?"
- **Social**: "How do I compare to others?"
- **Experimentation**: "What happens if I try this?"

**Examples in practice**:
- **Zelda**: What's in that treasure chest? How do I reach that area?
- **Mystery games**: Who is the killer? What really happened?
- **Roguelikes**: What items will I find this run?
- **Competitive games**: Can I beat this opponent?

**Combines well with**:
- Lens #7 (Endogenous Value)
- Lens #8 (Problem Solving)
- Lens #4 (Surprise)

**Anti-patterns**:
- Questions without answers (feels cheap)
- Answers without questions (players don't care)
- Too obvious (no curiosity)
- Too obscure (frustration, not curiosity)

### Lens #7: The Lens of Endogenous Value

**Category**: Game Economy, Player Motivation

**When to use**: When designing scoring systems, collectibles, currencies, progression systems, or any in-game rewards

**Questions to ask**:
- What is valuable to the players in my game?
- How can I make it more valuable to them?
- What is the relationship between value in the game and the players' motivations?

**Why it matters**:
- Endogenous value = value generated inside the game
- The more compelling your game, the greater the internal value created
- Players only care about things that help them achieve their goals
- Value is a direct reflection of how much players care about succeeding

**How endogenous value works**:
- **Monopoly money**: Only valuable during Monopoly
- **Game score**: Only matters if it unlocks something or provides bragging rights
- **Collectibles**: Only valuable if they serve a purpose or trigger completionist drive
- **Status**: Only matters in social contexts where it's recognized

**Creating strong endogenous value**:
1. **Connect to goals**: Does this help me win/complete/achieve?
2. **Make it scarce**: Abundant things have little value
3. **Require skill**: Easy-to-get items have less value
4. **Enable new possibilities**: Opens doors to new strategies/areas
5. **Create meaning**: Through story, context, or significance

**Examples in practice**:
- **Sonic 2 rings**: Protect from damage + grant extra lives = HIGH value
- **Bubsy yarn balls**: Just points with no function = NO value
- **Dark Souls souls**: Currency + leveling + loss on death = HIGH value
- **Gold in many RPGs**: Buys equipment, upgrades, items = varies based on availability

**Indicators of strong endogenous value**:
- Players willing to take risks to obtain it
- Players express frustration when they lose it
- Players actively seek it out (not just collect passively)
- Players make trade-offs and decisions around it
- Players care when competitors have it

**Indicators of weak endogenous value**:
- Players ignore collectibles after initial curiosity
- No one reads the score/points
- Players skip optional rewards
- No emotional reaction to gaining or losing items

**Combines well with**:
- Lens #6 (Curiosity)
- Lens #8 (Problem Solving)
- Lens #23 (Motivation)

**Anti-patterns**:
- Collectibles that serve no purpose
- Scoring that doesn't affect gameplay or status
- Abundant resources that eliminate scarcity
- Rewards that don't match the effort required

### Lens #8: The Lens of Problem Solving

**Category**: Game Mechanics, Core Design

**When to use**: When defining core gameplay, designing levels, creating challenges, or analyzing why something isn't engaging

**Questions to ask**:
- What problems does my game ask the player to solve?
- Are there hidden problems to solve that arise as part of gameplay?
- How can my game generate new problems so that players keep coming back?

**Why it matters**:
- Games = problem-solving activities approached with a playful attitude
- All games present problems (even if simple: "How do I win?")
- People enjoy solving problems—it's an evolved survival mechanism
- When problem-solving is removed, it ceases to be a game

**Types of problems in games**:
1. **Spatial**: How do I navigate this space?
2. **Strategic**: What's the best move?
3. **Tactical**: How do I execute this maneuver?
4. **Social**: How do I cooperate/compete with others?
5. **Physical**: Can I perform this action?
6. **Logical**: What's the pattern or solution?
7. **Resource management**: How do I optimize limited resources?
8. **Information**: What do I need to know, and how do I learn it?

**Problem qualities**:
- **Clear goals**: Player knows what success looks like
- **Appropriate difficulty**: Not too easy, not too hard
- **Multiple paths**: More than one way to solve (usually)
- **Interesting choices**: Tradeoffs and decisions matter
- **Feedback**: Player knows when they're making progress
- **Solvability**: Actually possible to solve (even if hard)

**How problems create engagement**:
- **Curiosity**: "Can I solve this?"
- **Challenge**: "This is hard but doable"
- **Mastery**: "I'm getting better at this"
- **Accomplishment**: "I did it!"
- **New problems**: "What's next?"

**Examples in practice**:
- **Chess**: How do I checkmate the opponent?
  - Hidden problems: Board control, tempo, material advantage
  - Generated problems: Each opponent move creates new problem
- **Portal**: How do I reach the exit?
  - Hidden problems: Physics puzzles, timing challenges
  - Generated problems: Each level introduces new mechanics
- **Civilization**: How do I win (multiple victory conditions)?
  - Hidden problems: Resource management, diplomacy, warfare
  - Generated problems: Opponent actions, random events, tech tree choices

**Combines well with**:
- Lens #6 (Curiosity)
- Lens #7 (Endogenous Value)
- Lens #9 (Elemental Tetrad)
- Lens #38 (Challenge)

**Anti-patterns**:
- Problems with no clear solution
- Problems that are trivial or tedious
- Problems that solve themselves (no player agency)
- Same problem repeated without variation
- Problems that punish experimentation

### Lens #9: The Lens of the Elemental Tetrad

**Category**: Holistic Design, Element Balance

**When to use**: Throughout the entire design process, especially during initial concepting and when something feels "off"

**Questions to ask**:
- Is my game design using elements of all four types?
- Could my design be improved by enhancing elements in one or more of the categories?
- Are the four elements in harmony, reinforcing each other and working together toward a common theme?

**The four elements**:
1. **Mechanics**: Procedures, rules, goals, player actions
2. **Story**: Sequence of events, narrative, context
3. **Aesthetics**: Look, sound, feel, sensory experience
4. **Technology**: Materials, medium, what makes it possible

**Why it matters**:
- All four elements are equally important
- Each powerfully influences the others
- Neglecting one element weakens the entire design
- Harmony between elements creates coherent experience

**How to apply this lens**:

**Step 1: Inventory each element**
- **Mechanics**: List all rules, systems, player actions
- **Story**: Outline the narrative or thematic elements
- **Aesthetics**: Describe the look, sound, feel
- **Technology**: Identify the platform, tools, materials

**Step 2: Evaluate each element**
- Is it present and developed?
- Is it serving the experience?
- Could it be stronger?

**Step 3: Check for harmony**
- Do mechanics support the story?
- Do aesthetics reinforce the mechanics?
- Does technology enable the vision?
- Do all four work toward the same theme?

**Examples of strong tetrad harmony**:

**Space Invaders**:
- **Technology**: Custom motherboard enables advancing army
- **Mechanics**: Shooting, defense, increasing speed
- **Story**: Aliens invading Earth (changed from soldiers for theme fit)
- **Aesthetics**: Simple but effective sprites, color strips, tense audio
- **Result**: All four elements tell story of battling alien army

**Portal**:
- **Technology**: Portal mechanic possible with 3D engine
- **Mechanics**: Physics-based puzzles using portal placement
- **Story**: Mysterious test facility, GLaDOS, escape narrative
- **Aesthetics**: Clean, sterile, threatening environment; memorable audio
- **Result**: All elements create sense of confined experimentation

**Common imbalances**:
- **Strong mechanics, weak story**: Fun but forgettable
- **Strong story, weak mechanics**: Beautiful but boring
- **Strong aesthetics, weak mechanics**: Pretty but hollow
- **Possible technology, weak vision**: Technically impressive but meaningless

**Combines well with**:
- Lens #10 (Holographic Design)
- Lens #11 (Unification)
- All other lenses (provides framework for everything)

**Anti-patterns**:
- Believing one element is more important than others
- Developing elements in isolation
- Adding elements that don't serve the theme
- Technology limitations forcing compromise of vision (sometimes necessary, but be aware)

### Lens #10: The Lens of Holographic Design

**Category**: Design Practice, Holistic Thinking

**When to use**: Constantly—this lens is about how you think about design itself

**Questions to ask**:
- What elements of the game make the experience enjoyable?
- What elements of the game detract from the experience?
- How can I change game elements to improve the experience?

**Why it matters**:
- Must see skin (experience) and skeleton (elements) simultaneously
- Only focusing on experience: Can't understand why or how to improve
- Only focusing on elements: Create beautiful theory, terrible practice
- Holographic design = seeing both at once

**The challenge**:
- **Skin-only view**: "This feels wrong" but can't identify cause
- **Skeleton-only view**: "This should work" but player experience is poor
- **Holographic view**: "This feels wrong BECAUSE of these specific elements"

**How to develop holographic vision**:

**Step 1: Practice alternating focus**
- Play your game (feel the experience)
- Pause and analyze (examine the elements)
- Play again (feel the impact of elements)
- Repeat until you can do both at once

**Step 2: Connect experience to elements**
- "I felt excited WHEN the music intensified" (aesthetics → experience)
- "I felt confused BECAUSE the rules weren't explained" (mechanics → experience)
- "I felt immersed WHEN the story and mechanics aligned" (story + mechanics → experience)

**Step 3: Predict element changes**
- "If I change this mechanic, the experience will become more strategic"
- "If I improve these aesthetics, the experience will feel more tense"
- "If I adjust this story, the experience will be more meaningful"

**Holographic design in practice**:

**During gameplay**:
- Notice: "I'm bored right now"
- Analyze: "This section has no challenge" (mechanic)
- Predict: "Adding enemies would increase tension"
- Feel: "Yes, that would make this moment more exciting"

**During playtesting**:
- Observe: Player looks confused
- Identify: Tutorial explanation is unclear (mechanics + aesthetics)
- Consider: Better visual indicators + simpler rules
- Verify: "Would this make the experience clearer?"

**During design**:
- Experience goal: Want players to feel wonder
- Element choices:
  - Mechanics: Discovery and exploration systems
  - Story: Mysterious world with revelations
  - Aesthetics: Beautiful, otherworldly visuals
  - Technology: Seamless streaming for no loading breaks

**Skill progression**:
1. **Beginner**: Can only focus on one (experience OR elements)
2. **Intermediate**: Can alternate between both
3. **Advanced**: Can hold both in mind simultaneously
4. **Master**: Natural integration of experience and element thinking

**Combines well with**:
- Lens #9 (Elemental Tetrad)
- All other lenses (provides framework for thinking)

**Anti-patterns**:
- Getting lost in theory without feeling experience
- Trusting feelings without understanding causes
- Assuming correlation equals causation
- Defending design choices instead of analyzing them

## Using These Lenses Together

### Suggested Lens Sequences

**For initial concept development**:
1. Lens #8 (Problem Solving): What problem will players solve?
2. Lens #6 (Curiosity): What questions will drive them?
3. Lens #9 (Elemental Tetrad): How do all four elements serve this?
4. Lens #10 (Holographic Design): Does this create the intended experience?

**For fun/engagement problems**:
1. Lens #5 (Fun): What's fun? What's not?
2. Lens #4 (Surprise): Where are the surprises?
3. Lens #7 (Endogenous Value): What do players actually care about?
4. Lens #10 (Holographic Design): How do elements create (or destroy) fun?

**For player motivation issues**:
1. Lens #6 (Curiosity): What questions drive engagement?
2. Lens #7 (Endogenous Value): What has value in the game?
3. Lens #8 (Problem Solving): Are the problems interesting?
4. Lens #5 (Fun): Is the problem-solving fun?

### The Lens Session Ritual

When facing a design challenge:

1. **Set the stage** (5 minutes)
   - State the problem clearly
   - Gather materials (cards/app, notebook)
   - Clear your mind

2. **Apply lenses** (20-30 minutes)
   - Choose 3-5 relevant lenses
   - For each lens:
     - Read the questions
     - Think deeply about your game
     - Write down insights
     - Don't defend—just observe

3. **Synthesize** (10 minutes)
   - Review all insights
   - Look for patterns
   - Identify contradictions
   - Prioritize changes

4. **Take action** (ongoing)
   - Implement one change at a time
   - Test the impact
   - Apply lenses again to verify

## Key Principles

1. **No lens is perfect**: Each has blind spots—use many lenses
2. **Lenses reveal, not solve**: They show problems, you find solutions
3. **Be honest**: Don't defend your design to yourself
4. **Write it down**: Insights fade quickly—capture them
5. **Test your hunches**: Lenses generate hypotheses—playtesting verifies
6. **Return often**: Same lens at different times reveals new insights
7. **Make it ritual**: Regular lens sessions keep design sharp
