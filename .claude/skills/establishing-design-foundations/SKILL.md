# Establishing Design Foundations

## Overview

This skill teaches the fundamental mindset and frameworks for game design based on Jesse Schell's "The Art of Game Design." It covers the essential mental models that every game designer must internalize before tackling specific design challenges.

## Core Framework: The Lens-Based Methodology

Game design lacks a "unified theory"—there's no single formula for creating good games. Instead, designers use **lenses**: perspective-based tools that let you examine your design from multiple angles.

### What Are Design Lenses?

- **Lenses are NOT blueprints or recipes**—they are examination tools
- Each lens is a set of questions to ask yourself about your design
- 113+ lenses exist, each offering a unique perspective
- No single lens is perfect; use many lenses for a complete view
- Available as cards or mobile app for easy reference during design sessions

**Key Principle**: Good game design happens when you view your game from as many perspectives as possible.

## The Most Important Skill: Listening

Before learning any design techniques, master the foundation of all design work: **deep, thoughtful listening**.

### What Is Deep Listening?

Listening is not just hearing words—it's:
- Observing body language, tone, facial expressions, gestures
- Constantly questioning: "Is that right?" "Why is it that way?" "What does this mean?"
- Putting yourself at risk of being contradicted or upset
- Accepting new information with an open mind
- As Herman Hesse wrote: "Listen with a silent heart, with a waiting, open soul. Without passion, without desire, without judgment, without rebuke."

### The Five Kinds of Listening

Every game designer must actively listen to five sources:

**1. Listen to Your Team**
- You build games together and make crucial decisions collectively
- Together, your team shares all necessary skills
- Deep team listening creates unity and shared understanding
- See: [five-kinds-of-listening-guide.md](five-kinds-of-listening-guide.md) for team listening techniques

**2. Listen to Your Audience**
- Players are the ultimate judges of your game
- If they aren't happy, you've failed
- Must know them better than they know themselves
- Understand their desires, fears, and motivations
- See: [five-kinds-of-listening-guide.md](five-kinds-of-listening-guide.md) for audience listening techniques

**3. Listen to Your Game**
- Get to know your game inside and out
- Like a mechanic diagnosing an engine, learn what's wrong by listening to gameplay
- Observe patterns, player behavior, emergent properties
- See: [five-kinds-of-listening-guide.md](five-kinds-of-listening-guide.md) for game listening techniques

**4. Listen to Your Client**
- The client pays for the game—must understand their true needs
- Listen deeply to discover what they really want, beyond stated requirements
- See: [five-kinds-of-listening-guide.md](five-kinds-of-listening-guide.md) for client listening techniques

**5. Listen to Yourself**
- Often the most difficult but most powerful tool
- Tune into your own creative instincts and intuition
- Source of tremendous creativity
- See: [five-kinds-of-listening-guide.md](five-kinds-of-listening-guide.md) for self-listening techniques

## Building Designer Confidence

Confidence is essential for decision-making. Use these techniques:

### The Magic Words

When confidence wavers, repeat:
```
I am a game designer.
I am a game designer.
I am a GAME designer.
I am a game DESIGNER.
```

Emphasize different words to reinforce different aspects of your identity.

### Embracing Failure

- **You will fail many more times than you succeed**
- Failures are your only path to success
- Each failure brings you closer to a phenomenal game
- **"If you aren't failing, you aren't trying hard enough"**
- Professional juggler saying: "If you aren't dropping, you aren't learning"

### The Secret of the Gifted

**Two kinds of gifts:**

1. **Minor gift**: Innate skill that comes naturally (but you may not enjoy it)
2. **Major gift**: Love of the work itself

**The major gift is more important.** If you love game design:
- You'll design games using whatever limited skills you have
- You'll keep doing it, improving through practice
- Your love will shine through in your work
- Your skills will grow to match or exceed those with only the minor gift

**How to know if you have it**: Start down the path and see if it makes your heart sing.

## Understanding What a Game Is

Before designing games, understand what makes something a game.

### Essential Definitions

**Fun**: Pleasure with surprises
- Not just any pleasure (eating, relaxing)
- Has special excitement and sparkle
- Involves unexpected elements

**Play**: Manipulation that indulges curiosity
- Freely answering questions of your own choosing
- Not obligated—done because you want to
- Involves willful action and change
- "What happens when...?" "Can I...?" "How many times...?"

**Toy**: An object you play with
- Good toy = object that is fun to play with
- Simpler than games

**Game**: A problem-solving activity, approached with a playful attitude

### Ten Qualities of Games

1. **Entered willfully**: Not forced, chosen freely
2. **Have goals**: Clear objectives to achieve
3. **Have conflict**: Obstacles preventing easy goal achievement
4. **Have rules**: Defined boundaries and allowed methods
5. **Can be won and lost**: Outcomes matter
6. **Are interactive**: Player and game affect each other
7. **Have challenge**: Not too easy, not too hard
8. **Create internal value**: Endogenous value matters inside the game
9. **Engage players**: Create mental immersion
10. **Are closed, formal systems**: Clear boundaries ("magic circle")

### The Magic Circle and Problem-Solving

When we solve problems, our minds create miniature internal realities:
- Simplified version of the real world
- Contains only necessary elements for the problem
- We become engaged and "forget" the outside world
- Elements gain internal importance (endogenous value)
- This internal problem space IS the game

**Key Insight**: Games are our internal problem-solving system made external.

## The Elemental Tetrad

Every game consists of four basic elements. **All four are equally important.**

### The Four Elements

**1. Mechanics**
- Procedures and rules of your game
- Goal and how players try to achieve it
- What makes a game a game (vs. linear media)
- What players can and cannot do

**2. Story**
- Sequence of events that unfolds
- May be linear/pre-scripted or branching/emergent
- Can be minimal or complex
- Provides context and meaning

**3. Aesthetics**
- How the game looks, sounds, smells, tastes, feels
- Most direct relationship to player experience
- Includes visual design, audio, tactile feedback
- Even non-digital games have aesthetics (board design, component quality)

**4. Technology**
- Materials and interactions that make the game possible
- NOT just high-tech: includes paper, pencil, dice, cards
- The medium for aesthetics, mechanics, and story
- Enables certain things, prohibits others

### Element Relationships

The elements powerfully influence each other:
- **Mechanics chosen** → affect story possibilities, aesthetic needs, technology requirements
- **Story desired** → influences mechanics design, aesthetic tone, technology choice
- **Aesthetics planned** → shape technology needs, story pacing, mechanical feel
- **Technology available** → constrains mechanics, aesthetic possibilities, story delivery

**Visibility Gradient**:
- Most visible: Aesthetics
- Middle: Mechanics and Story
- Least visible: Technology

**None is more important than the others.** Each deserves equal attention.

## Holographic Design

Master the ability to see your game on multiple levels simultaneously.

### The Dual Vision

**Skin**: The player's experience (what they feel, think, enjoy, hate)
**Skeleton**: The elements that create the experience (mechanics, story, aesthetics, technology)

### The Challenge

**Trap to avoid**: Focusing only on one level
- **Only skin**: Understand what feels good but not why or how to improve it
- **Only skeleton**: Create beautiful theory that's horrible in practice

**Goal**: See skin and skeleton at once
- Feel the power of the experience
- Understand which elements cause that experience
- Know why it works that way
- See how to improve it

This takes practice. Start by alternating focus, then work toward simultaneous awareness.

## Core Design Lenses

Use these foundational lenses to examine any game design.

### Lens #4: The Lens of Surprise

Ask yourself:
- What will surprise players when they play my game?
- Does the story have surprises? The rules? The artwork? The technology?
- Do rules give players ways to surprise each other?
- Do rules give players ways to surprise themselves?

**Why it matters**: Surprise triggers pleasure centers even during unpleasant surprises. Random patterns are more pleasurable than predictable ones.

### Lens #5: The Lens of Fun

Ask yourself:
- What parts of my game are fun? Why?
- What parts need to be more fun?

### Lens #6: The Lens of Curiosity

Ask yourself:
- What questions does my game put into the player's mind?
- What am I doing to make them care about these questions?
- What can I do to make them invent even more questions?

**Example questions games create**:
- "Can I beat this level?"
- "What happens next?"
- "Can I defeat this opponent?"
- "What's in that treasure chest?"

### Lens #7: The Lens of Endogenous Value

Ask yourself:
- What is valuable to the players in my game?
- How can I make it more valuable to them?
- What is the relationship between value in the game and players' motivations?

**Key concept**: Things that have value inside the game exist only inside the game. Monopoly money only matters during Monopoly. The more compelling your game, the greater the endogenous value created.

**Example**: Sonic the Hedgehog 2 rings vs. Bubsy yarn balls
- Sonic rings protect you and grant extra lives → high endogenous value
- Bubsy yarn balls give meaningless points → zero endogenous value
- Result: Sonic 2 more compelling than Bubsy

### Lens #8: The Lens of Problem Solving

Ask yourself:
- What problems does my game ask the player to solve?
- Are there hidden problems that arise during gameplay?
- How can my game generate new problems so players keep coming back?

**Remember**: Every game with a goal presents a problem to solve.

### Lens #9: The Lens of the Elemental Tetrad

Ask yourself:
- Is my game design using elements of all four types?
- Could my design be improved by enhancing elements in one or more categories?
- Are the four elements in harmony, reinforcing each other toward a common theme?

**Analysis technique**: Examine each element separately, then together as a whole.

### Lens #10: The Lens of Holographic Design

Ask yourself:
- What elements of the game make the experience enjoyable?
- What elements detract from the experience?
- How can I change game elements to improve the experience?

**Practice**: Shift focus between skin and skeleton until you can see both simultaneously.

## Focus on Fundamentals

### Why Study Simple Games?

- Videogames are natural growth of traditional games into new mediums
- Same principles govern all games
- Classic games withstood the test of time
- Success isn't due to technology novelty—deeper qualities
- Must understand simple games before complex ones
- "We might as well omit to study Nature because she is old" —Thoreau

### Genre-Agnostic Principles

Focus on fundamentals, not genre-specific tips because:
- Genres come and go
- Basic principles are human psychology constants
- Will work for ages to come
- Master fundamentals → master any genre → invent new genres
- Can dig deep in fertile places vs. covering shallow ground

### Draw Inspiration from Everything

**Talk to strangers**: Don't limit yourself to game industry knowledge
- Use principles from music, architecture, film, science, painting, literature
- Other fields have hundreds/thousands of years of hard work to learn from
- **Design is the same everywhere**
- Everything you know and experience is fair game

**"Do not forget to entertain strangers, for by so doing some have unwittingly entertained angels."** —Hebrews 13:2

## The Path to Mastery

### Books Won't Make You a Designer

- Game design is an activity, not a set of principles
- Only one path: design games that people actually like
- Build the game, play it, let others play it
- When it doesn't satisfy (it won't), change it
- Repeat dozens of times

**Designer saying**: "Your first ten games will suck—so get them out of the way fast."

### Continuous Learning Cycle

1. **Study**: Learn principles and frameworks
2. **Practice**: Apply them in actual game design
3. **Assimilation**: Internalize what works
4. **Synthesis**: Combine ideas into new insights
5. **Repeat**: Continuously for the rest of your life

## Practical Application Workflow

When starting a new game design project:

**Step 1: Set Your Foundation**
1. Say the magic words to build confidence
2. Commit to embracing failure as learning
3. Identify which of the five listening types matter most for this project

**Step 2: Define the Core**
1. What problem will players solve?
2. What playful attitude do you want to create?
3. What questions will ignite player curiosity?

**Step 3: Balance the Tetrad**
1. Sketch initial ideas for all four elements
2. Ensure all four are present and working together
3. Check that each element supports the others

**Step 4: Apply Multiple Lenses**
1. View your design through foundational lenses (#4-10)
2. Ask all questions for each relevant lens
3. Note insights and contradictions
4. Iterate based on what you discover

**Step 5: Practice Holographic Design**
1. Play your game (experience the skin)
2. Analyze the elements (examine the skeleton)
3. Connect experience to elements
4. Identify improvements

## Common Pitfalls

**Pitfall 1: Lacking Confidence**
- **Symptom**: "I don't know what I'm doing," "Maybe I just got lucky"
- **Solution**: Use the magic words, embrace failure, trust the process

**Pitfall 2: Focusing Only on One Element**
- **Symptom**: "Story is most important" or "Mechanics are everything"
- **Solution**: Remember all four elements are equally important

**Pitfall 3: Skeleton Without Skin**
- **Symptom**: Perfect theory, terrible gameplay
- **Solution**: Always feel the player experience while analyzing elements

**Pitfall 4: Skin Without Skeleton**
- **Symptom**: Know it feels wrong but can't figure out why or how to fix it
- **Solution**: Analyze which elements cause the poor experience

**Pitfall 5: Ignoring Fundamentals**
- **Symptom**: Chasing trends, copying mechanics without understanding
- **Solution**: Study classic games, focus on timeless principles
