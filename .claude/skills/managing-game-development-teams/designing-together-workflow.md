# Designing Together Workflow

## The Magic Words Variation

We forgot one important variation on our magic words:

> **I am *a* game designer.**

Not THE game designer. A game designer.

## The Problem with Solo Design

### The Terrified Designer's Approach

**Fear:** Other team members want to contribute design ideas. This threatens status as designer and forces arguments about "right" design.

**Response:** Withdraw from team, ignore opinions, produce design completely independent.

**Effect (Predictable):**
- All beautiful ideas team members had are crushed
- Love they had for the game dries up and blows away
- Designer frustrated that team "unwilling and unable to realize the designer's glorious vision"
- Game pleases no one

### Why This Happens

**The core fear:** Involving others means:
- Loss of control
- Design by committee
- Dilution of vision
- Endless arguments
- Hijacked game design

**The reality:** Most people on team with design ideas don't want to hijack the game design—they just want their ideas to be heard, because they too want the game to be great!

---

## The Collaborative Design Approach

### The Foundation: Set Your Ego Aside

If you can do this, you'll quickly realize the benefits of including the team in design process.

### Four Benefits of Collaborative Design

**1. Have more ideas to choose from**
- Diversity of perspectives
- More creative solutions
- Avoid blind spots

**2. Weed out flawed ideas quickly**
- Team spots problems immediately
- No time wasted pursuing dead ends
- Collective wisdom catches edge cases

**3. Be forced to view the game from many perspectives**
- Programmers see technical implications
- Artists see visual possibilities
- Different players types represented
- Holistic understanding emerges

**4. Make everyone on the team feel like they own the design**
- Personal investment in success
- Pride in contribution
- Confidence during implementation
- Alignment on decisions

---

## The Power of Shared Understanding

### Why Implementation Ownership Matters

**The reality:** Not all design decisions get made ahead of time.

**What happens:** Hundreds of tiny decisions get made all the time—not by the designer but by:
- Programmers implementing systems
- Artists creating assets
- Sound designers crafting audio
- Executives making business calls

### When Team Has Solid, Shared Understanding

**Result:** All these little decisions reinforce the design of the game.

**Outcome:** Project has unified robustness and solidity it can't get any other way.

### The Pride of Ownership

**Common and healthy:** Many different people on project feel their contribution was most important part of the game.

**What this means:** Many team members feel personal ownership and responsibility for the game.

**How to amplify:** Avoid "overfleshing" your designs.

---

## The "Underfleshed" Design Technique

### What It Means

Leave some ambiguity in detailed design of your game, particularly for parts you aren't sure about.

### Why It Works

**Forces developers to:**
- Think about what that section should be like
- Come up with ideas for detailed implementation
- Exercise design thinking

**Why it's effective:**
- They're often closest to that part of the game
- Their instincts about detailed design often quite good
- If their ideas are good and go into the game, they feel real pride of ownership

### When to Use

- Parts of design you're uncertain about
- Features that need specialized expertise
- Systems that require technical knowledge
- Areas where implementer will have better perspective

## The Four-Phase Collaborative Design Cycle

### Overview

Not everyone has stamina for three-hour debates about inventory interface layout. Establish a core design team, but keep whole team involved at key moments.

---

### Phase 1: Initial Brainstorming

**Participants:** As much of team as possible

**Purpose:**
- Generate wide range of ideas
- Get everyone excited
- Establish shared starting point
- Surface different perspectives

**Activities:**
- Blue-sky ideation
- Problem identification
- Opportunity exploration
- No criticism, just ideas

**Mindset:** Quantity over quality, divergent thinking

**Output:**
- Long list of ideas
- Identified areas needing deeper exploration
- Shared understanding of problem space

**Duration:** 1-2 hours, can be multiple sessions

**Facilitator role:**
- Keep everyone contributing
- Prevent early criticism
- Record all ideas visibly
- Encourage wild ideas

---

### Phase 2: Independent Design

**Participants:** Core design team members

**Purpose:**
- Think deeply about ideas independently
- Avoid groupthink
- Develop personal perspectives
- Prepare for productive discussion

**Activities:**
- Individual thinking time
- Personal prototyping/sketching
- Research and analysis
- Writing up proposals

**Mindset:** Deep, focused, individual work

**Output:**
- Each core team member brings ideas to discussion
- Personal proposals or prototypes
- Thought-through perspectives

**Duration:** Varies by complexity (hours to days)

**Important:** Everyone does this independently, no collaboration yet

---

### Phase 3: Design Discussion

**Participants:** Core design team members

**Purpose:**
- Bring independent ideas together
- Discuss and evaluate
- Try to come to consensus

**Activities:**
- Present individual proposals
- Compare and contrast approaches
- Debate trade-offs
- Prototype integration
- Reach agreements

**Mindset:**
- Objective (see [team-communication-guide.md](team-communication-guide.md))
- Focus on problem-solving
- Build on each other's ideas

**Key principles:**
- Objectivity (discuss "the spaceship idea," not "my idea")
- Phrase alternatives as questions ("What if we did B instead of A?")
- Focus on how ideas solve problems
- Be willing to compromise

**Output:**
- Consensus on design direction
- Clear decisions on implementation approach
- Documented reasoning
- Identified areas needing whole team input

**Duration:** Can be multiple sessions over days/weeks

**When done:**
- Core team has reached consensus
- Can articulate the design clearly
- Ready to present to whole team

---

### Phase 4: Design Presentation

**Participants:** Core design team presents to whole team

**Purpose:**
- Present progress to whole team
- Allow time for comments and criticism
- Identify new problems
- Get buy-in from everyone

**Activities:**
- Core team presents design decisions
- Explain reasoning behind choices
- Demonstrate prototypes if available
- Field questions and concerns
- Discuss implications

**Common outcome:** Often turns into brainstorming, kicking off next round of iterative cycle

**Critical importance:**
- Present ideas to rest AS SOON AS POSSIBLE after core team consensus
- Don't wait too long or people feel left out
- Momentum maintained

**Mindset:**
- Presenters: Open to feedback, not defensive
- Audience: Constructive, focused on making game better

**The moment of truth:**
The moment you sense someone doesn't buy into an idea (even if they verbally claim they agree), you must:
1. Stop everything
2. Find out why
3. Try to find a way to get them on board

**Why this matters:**
If you don't, they may secretly disagree with direction and lose their love for the game. When that happens, valuable contributions they would have made are lost.

**Rule:** No decision should be final until team agrees it is final.

---

## The Continuous Cycle

After Phase 4, the cycle repeats:

```
Phase 1 (Brainstorming)
    ↓
Phase 2 (Independent Design)
    ↓
Phase 3 (Design Discussion)
    ↓
Phase 4 (Design Presentation)
    ↓
[New problems identified, feedback collected]
    ↓
Phase 1 (Brainstorming on next iteration)
...
```

## Core Design Team Selection

### Who Should Be on Core Design Team?

**Criteria:**
- **Interested** in detailed design discussions
- **Productive** at these kinds of sessions
- Have **stamina** for long debates
- Can **communicate** effectively
- **Respect** other perspectives

**Not everyone needs to be on core team:**
Some people prefer to:
- Contribute in brainstorming only
- Review and comment rather than debate details
- Focus on implementation rather than design

**This is healthy!** Not everyone needs to be in every detailed session.

### Typical Core Team Composition

**Usually includes:**
- Lead designer
- Producer or project lead
- Lead programmer
- Lead artist
- Sometimes: Key specialists (narrative designer, systems designer, etc.)

**Size:** Generally 3-7 people
- Small enough for productive discussion
- Large enough for diverse perspectives

## Best Practices for Each Phase

### Brainstorming Best Practices

**Do:**
- Encourage wild ideas
- Build on others' suggestions
- Stay focused on problem
- Use "yes, and…" instead of "yes, but…"
- Capture everything visibly

**Don't:**
- Criticize ideas during generation
- Get stuck on one idea too long
- Let one person dominate
- Rush to solutions

**Tools:**
- Whiteboard or large paper
- Sticky notes
- Digital collaboration tools (Miro, Mural)

---

### Independent Design Best Practices

**Do:**
- Actually work independently (don't chat about it)
- Document your thinking
- Create prototypes when possible
- Research similar approaches
- Come prepared to explain your reasoning

**Don't:**
- Just think about it without documentation
- Copy what you think others will suggest
- Skip this phase to "save time"
- Come unprepared to discussion

**Outputs:**
- Written proposals
- Sketches/diagrams
- Prototype code or paper prototypes
- Research notes

---

### Design Discussion Best Practices

**Do:**
- Present ideas objectively
- Listen to understand, not to respond
- Ask clarifying questions
- Focus on problem-solving
- Document decisions and reasoning
- Be willing to compromise
- Use "disagree and commit" when necessary

**Don't:**
- Defend ideas emotionally
- Dismiss others' ideas quickly
- Talk over people
- Rehash settled arguments
- Leave without consensus

**Facilitation tips:**
- Have someone facilitate who isn't proposing
- Use timer for presentations (equal time)
- Take breaks in long sessions
- Document live (visible to all)

---

### Design Presentation Best Practices

**Do:**
- Present clearly with visuals
- Explain reasoning, not just decisions
- Invite questions and concerns
- Really listen to feedback
- Stop when someone doesn't buy in
- Work toward genuine agreement
- Document concerns even if resolved

**Don't:**
- Rush through presentation
- Be defensive about criticism
- Ignore non-verbal cues of disagreement
- Move forward without consensus
- Dismiss concerns as "not understanding"

**Red flags to watch for:**
- Someone quiet who's usually vocal
- Verbal agreement without eye contact
- "I guess that could work" (lukewarm support)
- Questions that suggest fundamental disagreement
- Body language showing discomfort

## Handling Disagreement

### The Two-Person Principle

**Remember:** It takes two people to have a disagreement.

**What this means:** If one member of team is stubborn on particular point, you must:
- Treat them with respect they deserve
- Work with them until meaningful compromise found

### The Key Question

When someone is stuck disagreeing:

**Ask:** "What would it take to bring you in?"

**Why it works:**
- Directly addresses what's needed for buy-in
- Often reveals underlying concern
- Opens negotiation rather than standoff
- Shows respect for their perspective

### Making Their Case Understood

**Ask them to explain:** "Why is this point so important to you?"

**Often:** Hearing their reasoning helps rest of team understand importance, and resistance melts away.

**Sometimes:** The explanation reveals a concern everyone shares, and design naturally shifts.

## Intel's "Disagree and Commit"

### What It Means

"We can't always agree on the best path, but we can come to agreement on what we are going to do right now."

### How to Use It

**When:** Core team can't reach unanimous agreement after good-faith discussion

**The agreement:**
- Team member acknowledges they don't fully agree
- But commits to making the chosen path work
- With intention to genuinely support it
- And revisit if clear problems emerge

**What it's NOT:**
- Being overruled
- Fake agreement
- Resentful compliance

**What it IS:**
- Professional choice to support team decision
- Trust in the process
- Willingness to be wrong
- Unity over ego

### Why It's Necessary

**Without it:** One team member not buying in slows efforts of everyone.

**Like a car engine:** Single cylinder not firing:
- Cuts performance in half
- Ultimately ruins the engine

**One team member who doesn't buy into design:**
- Slows everyone
- Can tear team apart in the end

## Avoiding Common Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Design by Committee

**Symptom:** Every decision requires approval from everyone, nothing gets decided

**Cause:** No clear decision-making authority, everyone has veto power

**Solution:**
- Core design team makes detailed decisions
- Whole team informed and given chance to comment
- Established decision-making process
- Clear ownership for different areas

---

### Pitfall 2: Steamrolling

**Symptom:** Dominant personality pushes through ideas without real discussion

**Cause:** Lack of process, fear of conflict, personality imbalance

**Solution:**
- Structured discussion process
- Everyone gets equal presentation time
- Objective facilitation
- Address power dynamics explicitly

---

### Pitfall 3: Silent Disagreement

**Symptom:** People say they agree but don't really buy in

**Cause:** Fear of conflict, exhaustion, not feeling heard

**Solution:**
- Watch for non-verbal cues
- Stop and address when detected
- Private follow-ups
- Create safe space for honest disagreement

---

### Pitfall 4: Analysis Paralysis

**Symptom:** Endless discussion, no decisions

**Cause:** Perfectionism, fear of being wrong, unclear criteria

**Solution:**
- Set time limits on discussion phases
- Use "disagree and commit"
- Define decision criteria upfront
- Accept that some decisions are reversible

---

### Pitfall 5: The Idea Graveyard

**Symptom:** Ideas from non-core team members never considered seriously

**Cause:** Core team not actually open to input, presentation phase is theater

**Solution:**
- Take all feedback seriously
- Explain why ideas can't be used (if that's the case)
- Incorporate good ideas from presentation phase
- Follow up on how feedback influenced decisions

## Measuring Collaborative Design Success

### Signs It's Working

- Team members from all disciplines contribute design ideas
- Discussions focus on problems and solutions, not egos
- People excited to see their ideas in the game
- Implementation details reflect team's collective wisdom
- Everyone can articulate the design vision
- "I designed that part" comes from multiple team members

### Warning Signs

- Only lead designer talks about design
- People check out of design discussions
- Implementations surprise the "designer"
- Team members say "I just do what I'm told"
- Design decisions get relitigated during implementation
- People feel their ideas aren't heard

## The Long-Term Benefits

### Stronger Game

When whole team participates:
- Game benefits from many perspectives
- Flaws caught early
- Implementation aligns with design
- Unified, robust final product

### Confident Implementation

When everyone involved:
- Solid, shared understanding of design
- Hundreds of tiny decisions reinforce design
- Implementation with intention, not confusion
- Problems solved creatively within design vision

### Team Satisfaction

When team owns design together:
- Everyone feels pride in final product
- Multiple people feel their contribution was crucial
- Shared victory
- Love for the game maintained

## Quick Reference: The Collaborative Design Cycle

### Phase 1: Initial Brainstorming
- **Who:** Whole team
- **What:** Generate wide range of ideas
- **Output:** Long list, identified areas for exploration
- **Duration:** 1-2 hours

### Phase 2: Independent Design
- **Who:** Core design team
- **What:** Think deeply, independently
- **Output:** Individual proposals
- **Duration:** Hours to days

### Phase 3: Design Discussion
- **Who:** Core design team
- **What:** Discuss, evaluate, reach consensus
- **Output:** Agreed design direction
- **Duration:** Multiple sessions over days/weeks

### Phase 4: Design Presentation
- **Who:** Core team presents to whole team
- **What:** Present, collect feedback, identify problems
- **Output:** Team buy-in, next iteration topics
- **Duration:** 1-2 hours

### Then: Repeat the cycle

## Final Thoughts

### From Ed Catmull (Pixar)

> "People are more important than ideas, because if you give a good idea to a mediocre group, they'll screw it up. If you give a mediocre idea to a good group, they'll fix it."

### The Truth About Design

Everyone who touches a game exerts some influence on its design.

**You need everyone on the team to pull together** if the glorious vision you share is ever to come to light.

### The Designer's Real Role

Not to have all the answers.

Not to dictate every decision.

**The designer's role:** Facilitate the process where the team discovers the answers together.
