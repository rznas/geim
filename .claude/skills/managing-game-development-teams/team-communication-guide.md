# Team Communication Guide

## The Ten Keys to Team Communication

These ten keys are listed in order, forming a dependency chain. Each builds on the previous ones, culminating in love for the game.

---

## 1. Objectivity

**Why it matters:** In the passionate throes of design, it's easy to become attached to ideas. Without objectivity, design discussions become wars of opinions and gut feelings.

### The Tool: Problem Statement (Lens #14)

All team discussion must focus on **how well ideas solve the problems at hand**. Personal preferences don't matter—only whether ideas solve the problem.

### Techniques for Maintaining Objectivity

**Depersonalize ideas:**
- Don't say "my idea" or "Sue's idea"
- Say "the spaceship idea" objectively
- This separates ideas from individuals and gives them to the team

**Phrase alternatives as questions:**
- **Bad:** "A is no good. I like B better."
- **Good:** "What if we did B instead of A?"
- This lets the group collectively discuss relative merits without defensiveness

**Ask neutral questions:**
- "How does this solve the problem?"
- "What problems might this create?"
- "What are the trade-offs?"

### Benefits of Objective Culture

When you maintain objectivity:
- Everyone brings you design questions without hesitation (no danger of awkward situations)
- People want you in every design session (your presence defuses tense struggles)
- Even shy team members feel they can speak freely
- Ideas that might have hidden in shadows come confidently to light

## 2. Clarity

**Why it matters:** If communication is not clear, there is going to be confusion. Confusion prevents meaningful collaboration.

### Two Aspects of Clarity

**1. Understanding Each Other**

Techniques:
- **Check understanding:** "Does that make sense?" "Can you explain what you heard?"
- **Illustrate ideas when possible:** Sketches, diagrams, examples
- **Never pretend to understand:** No matter how embarrassed, keep asking questions until you truly understand
- If someone else isn't clear, help them clarify: "Can you give me an example?" "What do you mean by...?"

**2. Being Concrete and Specific**

Compare these statements:

| Vague | Concrete & Specific |
|-------|---------------------|
| "I'll design the combat system by Thursday" | "I'll e-mail you a 3–5-page description of the interface for the turn-based combat system by this Thursday at 5 p.m." |

The second version:
- Specifies the deliverable format (description)
- Defines page length (3-5 pages)
- Clarifies scope (interface for turn-based combat)
- Sets exact deadline (Thursday 5 p.m.)
- Specifies delivery method (e-mail)

### Clarity Checklist

Before ending any design discussion:
- [ ] Does everyone understand the decision?
- [ ] Are next steps concrete (who does what by when)?
- [ ] Have we illustrated complex ideas visually?
- [ ] Has anyone said "I think so" instead of "yes" to understanding?

## 3. Persistence

**Why it matters:** Humans have terrible memories. Verbal communication is momentary—easily misunderstood and forgotten.

### WRITE THINGS DOWN!

**Benefits of persistent communication:**
- Can be checked later by everyone
- Saves you from solving the same problems repeatedly
- Moving ideas from working memory to documents improves thinking
- Helps flesh out creative ideas

### Media for Persistence

Use every medium that might be useful:
- **Notebooks** - Personal capture of ideas and decisions
- **E-mail** - Broadcast decisions to team
- **Forums/Mailing lists** - Ongoing discussions
- **Wikis** - Living documentation
- **Fileshares** - Shared assets and documents
- **Printed documents** - Important references
- **Design meeting notes** - Shared summaries (usually producer's responsibility)

### Best Practices

**In meetings:**
- Someone takes notes that can be shared with team
- Notes distributed within 24 hours of meeting
- Action items clearly identified with owners and deadlines

**In email:**
- Include everyone on the team (avoid people feeling left out)
- Use descriptive subject lines
- Summarize decisions at top, details below

**In documents:**
- Record decisions AND reasons why
- Date all updates
- Make accessible to entire team

### Warning: Don't Let Persistence Replace Communication

Writing things down enables communication but doesn't replace it. Documents must be accompanied by dialog.

## 4. Comfort

**Why it matters:** When people are comfortable, they are less distracted and communicate more freely.

### Physical Comfort Requirements

**Meeting space needs:**
- Quiet environment (minimal distractions)
- Right temperature (not too hot or cold)
- Enough chairs for everyone
- Large writing surface (whiteboard, table, screen)
- Ergonomic seating for longer sessions

**Personal needs:**
- Not hungry (provide snacks/meals for long sessions)
- Not thirsty (water/beverages available)
- Not overtired (schedule meetings at productive times)

**Result:** People who are physically uncomfortable will be terrible communicators.

### Emotional Comfort

Emotional comfort is even more important than physical comfort. People need to feel:
- Safe to express opinions
- Not judged harshly for ideas
- Welcome to participate
- Valued as team members

**Creating emotional comfort:**
- Welcoming tone at start of meetings
- Acknowledge contributions positively
- Address conflicts respectfully
- Give everyone a chance to speak
- Notice when someone seems uncomfortable and check in privately

## 5. Respect

**Why it matters:** People who do not feel respected tend to speak little, and when they do, they're often not honest about their feelings. People who feel respected speak freely, openly, and honestly.

### The Foundation of Good Listening

The secret to good listening is to **respect the person you are listening to**.

### How to Show Respect (It's Easy If You Remember)

**Simply treat them, at every moment, how you would like to be treated.**

**Specific behaviors:**
- **Don't cut them off** - Let them finish their thoughts
- **Don't roll your eyes** - Even if you think they're wrong
- **Be polite and patient** - At all times
- **Find nice things to say** - Even if you have to stretch a little
- **Look for commonalities** - It's easiest to respect people like ourselves

**When you make a mistake:**
- Don't rush to defend what you said
- Rush instead to apologize sincerely
- Acknowledge impact over intent

### The Respect Loop

If you manage to respect your teammates at all times, they cannot help but respect you. When everyone feels respected, they communicate at their best.

### The "What If I'm Wrong?" Mantra

When all else fails, repeat to yourself: **"What if I'm wrong?"**

This opens your mind to others' perspectives and naturally cultivates respect.

## 6. Trust

**Why it matters:** Respect is impossible without trust, and vice versa. If I can't trust what you say and do, how can I know whether you respect me?

### How Trust Builds

Trust is **not** something that works on faith alone. Trust gradually builds up over time through experience.

**The formula:** Quantity of communication > Quality of communication

**Why quantity matters:**
- People who see each other day in and day out, constantly talking and solving problems together, gradually learn how much they can trust each other
- A group who barely knows each other and only meets once a month has no idea who can be trusted with what
- There's something in the nuance of face-to-face communication that allows subconscious decisions about trust

### Trust Indicators: The Lunch Test

**The easiest way to figure out who trusts who: observe who eats lunch together.**

Most animals (including humans) are very selective about who they eat with.

**Warning signs:**
- Artists eat separately from programmers → pipeline problems likely
- Xbox team eats separately from PlayStation team → porting problems likely
- Management eats separately from developers → alignment problems likely

### Building Trust Practices

**Maximize face-to-face communication:**
- Open office layouts (not individual offices)
- Teams seated together
- Regular informal interactions
- Shared meals and breaks

**Create opportunities for communication about anything:**
- Not just project-related topics
- Social events
- Casual conversations
- Shared experiences

**High-bandwidth communication** about anything builds trust for everything.

### Why Game Studios Use Open Offices

To ensure constant face-to-face communication where team members "can't help but have constant face-to-face communication with one another all day long."

## 7. Honesty

**Why it matters:** Just as comfort depends on respect and respect depends on trust, trust depends on honesty.

### The Honesty-Trust Loop

If you develop a reputation for dishonesty in any area, others will:
- Be afraid to be honest with you
- Withhold important information
- Not share their true feelings
- Inhibit team communication

### The Challenge of Honesty in Game Development

**Reality:** Game development can get very political. You may need to stretch the truth about some things from time to time.

**Non-negotiable:** Your team must always feel certain they are getting the truth from you.

**The balance:** Be strategic with external communications, but always truthful with your team.

### Building Honest Communication

**Lead by example:**
- Admit when you're wrong
- Acknowledge when you don't know something
- Share bad news quickly, not just good news
- Give credit to others for their ideas

**Create safe spaces for honesty:**
- "I need your honest opinion about..."
- "What am I missing here?"
- "Tell me what you really think, not what you think I want to hear"

**Reward honesty:**
- Thank people for difficult truths
- Act on honest feedback
- Don't punish messengers of bad news

---

## 8. Privacy

**Why it matters:** Being honest isn't always easy, because sometimes the truth can be painful. People will tell you their true feelings in one-on-one conversations much more easily than in public.

### Why Privacy Enables Honesty

**In public forums:**
- Personal pride and ego are on display
- Fear of judgment from peers
- Reluctance to create conflict
- Pressure to conform to group opinion

**In private conversations:**
- Safer to express unpopular opinions
- Easier to admit confusion or concerns
- More comfortable discussing interpersonal issues
- Freedom to question decisions without seeming contrary

### When to Use Private Conversations

**Ideal situations for one-on-one:**
- Someone seems uncomfortable but won't speak up publicly
- Sensitive feedback about someone's work
- Personal concerns about project direction
- Interpersonal conflicts
- Career or role discussions
- Ideas someone isn't ready to share publicly

### Benefits Beyond Honesty

Private conversations also:
- Build trust (creates virtuous circle: more trust → more honest communication → more trust)
- Show individual team members they're valued
- Allow you to gauge team sentiment more accurately
- Give you early warning of problems before they explode publicly

### Practice

**Make time for regular one-on-ones:**
- Schedule recurring meetings with each team member
- Take impromptu walks for casual conversations
- Grab coffee/lunch individually
- Check in privately after tense public meetings

**What you'll discover:**
People will often present ideas and discuss problems they simply did not feel comfortable discussing publicly.

## 9. Unity

**Why it matters:** During design, there will be many conflicting opinions and arguments. This is healthy and natural. But ultimately, the team must arrive at a decision everyone agrees upon.

### The Unity Principle

**It takes two people to have a disagreement.**

If one team member is stubborn on a particular point, you must treat them with respect and work with them until meaningful compromise is found.

### Intel's Phrase: "Disagree and Commit"

We can't always agree on the best path, but we can come to agreement on what we are going to do right now.

**What this means:**
- Team members sometimes need to go down a path they don't fully agree with
- For the sake of team unity and forward progress
- With commitment to make it work even if they'd prefer something else

### The Danger of Disunity

**The car engine metaphor:**
Just as a single cylinder not firing in a car engine:
- Cuts performance in half
- Ultimately ruins the engine

One team member who does not buy into the design:
- Slows the efforts of everyone
- Can tear the team apart in the end

### Reaching Unity: Practical Techniques

**When someone disagrees stubbornly:**
1. **Ask them to explain why** - Understanding their reasoning helps the team see the importance
2. **Ask "What would it take to bring you in?"** - Directly addresses what's needed for buy-in
3. **Look for underlying concerns** - Often the stated disagreement masks a deeper concern
4. **Find creative compromises** - Sometimes there's a third option that satisfies everyone
5. **Time-box the disagreement** - "Let's try approach A for two weeks, then evaluate"

### What Unity Is NOT

Unity does **not** mean:
- Everyone always agrees
- No debates or arguments
- Silent acceptance of decisions
- Ignoring concerns

Unity means: Healthy debate leads to committed action.

### The Goal of Communication

**The goal of all communication is unity.** Without it, the love for the game will be jeopardized.

---

## 10. Love

**Where the chain leads:** Without objectivity, clarity, persistence, comfort, respect, trust, honesty, privacy, and unity, the love a team has for the game will be jeopardized.

**With all these things:** The team's love for the game will shine through, and you will have no choice but to make an incredible game.

### Love as the Foundation and Goal

**Love is both:**
- **The foundation** - The secret to successful teamwork
- **The goal** - What all communication practices protect and enable

### The Complete Chain

```
Objectivity → Clarity → Persistence → Comfort → Respect → Trust → Honesty → Privacy → Unity → LOVE
```

Each link depends on the previous one. Break any link, and love is at risk.

### Maintaining the Chain

**Regular checks:**
- Is the team communicating objectively? (Using problem statements, not opinions)
- Is communication clear? (Everyone understands, specifics defined)
- Are we writing things down? (Decisions persisted and accessible)
- Are people comfortable? (Physically and emotionally)
- Is everyone respected? (Treated as they want to be treated)
- Is trust building? (Quantity and quality of communication)
- Are we honest with each other? (Even when difficult)
- Do we use privacy appropriately? (One-on-ones for sensitive topics)
- Can we reach unity? (Disagree and commit when necessary)
- Does the team love the game? (The ultimate measure)

## Quick Reference: Communication Red Flags

**Watch for these warning signs:**

| Red Flag | Indicates Problem With | Action Needed |
|----------|------------------------|---------------|
| "That's a bad idea" | Objectivity | Reframe as "How does this solve our problem?" |
| Confused faces | Clarity | Stop and check understanding |
| "I thought we decided..." | Persistence | Write decisions down immediately |
| People avoiding meetings | Comfort (emotional) | Check in privately, address underlying issues |
| Eye-rolling, cutting off | Respect | Call it out gently, model better behavior |
| Eating separately by discipline | Trust | Create more opportunities for cross-discipline interaction |
| Rumors and gossip | Honesty | Address issues directly and transparently |
| Silence in meetings, explosion later | Privacy | Schedule more one-on-ones |
| Endless debates, no decisions | Unity | Use "disagree and commit" framework |
| Detachment, going through motions | Love | Address immediately using Lens #100 |

## Practical Exercise: Team Communication Audit

Use this to assess your team's current communication health:

**For each key, rate 1-5 (1=poor, 5=excellent):**

- [ ] **Objectivity:** Design discussions focus on problems, not opinions
- [ ] **Clarity:** Everyone understands decisions and next steps
- [ ] **Persistence:** Important decisions are written and accessible
- [ ] **Comfort:** Team has comfortable space and feels emotionally safe
- [ ] **Respect:** Everyone treats each other as they want to be treated
- [ ] **Trust:** Team communicates frequently and openly
- [ ] **Honesty:** People share difficult truths when necessary
- [ ] **Privacy:** One-on-ones reveal honest feelings and concerns
- [ ] **Unity:** Team can disagree and commit to decisions
- [ ] **Love:** Team talks about the game with genuine excitement

**Total Score: _____ / 50**

- **40-50:** Excellent communication culture
- **30-39:** Good foundation, some areas need attention
- **20-29:** Significant communication issues to address
- **Below 20:** Communication crisis, immediate intervention needed

**Follow-up:** For any key rated 3 or below, create a specific action plan to improve that area.
