# Playtesting Framework

## The Reality of Playtesting

Playtesting is essential but uncomfortable. It's necessary to serve as a wakeup call and force you to solve ugly problems you've been putting off.

### Four Types of Testing (Not Playtesting)

Before we dive into playtesting, understand what playtesting is NOT:

1. **Focus groups** - Interview potential players about likes/dislikes to determine if they like a game idea
   - Useful for: Feature prioritization
   - Limitations: Often poorly run, can be manipulated to kill ideas

2. **QA testing** - Quality assurance looking for bugs
   - Useful for: Finding technical problems
   - Limitations: Nothing to do with how enjoyable the game is

3. **Usability testing** - Determining whether interface and systems are intuitive and easy to use
   - Useful for: Interface refinement
   - Limitations: Necessary but not sufficient for fun

4. **Playtesting** - Getting people to play your game to see if it creates the intended experience
   - **This is what designers care about most**
   - This is what this document focuses on

---

## The Uncomfortable Truth

### Why Designers Hate Playtesting (But Must Do It Anyway)

**The terror:** You're afraid people won't like your game.

**Why it hurts:** When you work hard on a game, it becomes a little piece of yourself. Having people engage with it and reject it hurts. A lot.

**The reality:** Having people hate your work is probably one of the most painful parts of being a game designer.

### The Invitation

Playtesting feels like an engraved invitation that reads:

> *You are cordially invited*
> *to tell me why I suck*
> *Bring a friend — Refreshments Served*

### Why It Must Be Uncomfortable

**The point:** Playtesting makes clear that decisions you were completely comfortable with are completely wrong.

**The opportunity:** You need to find these things out as soon as possible, while there's still time to do something about them.

### The Mindset Shift

**If players like your game:** Great!

**If players don't like your game:** Also great! You have a chance to ask them why and fix it.

**The key:** Let go of your fears and embrace playtesting for what it is—a wonderful opportunity to make your game better.

## The Six Playtesting Questions

Every playtest is defined by six key questions. Master these, and you'll conduct effective playtests.

---

## Question 1: WHY Are We Playtesting?

### The Prototype Parallel

Remember: Every prototype is designed to answer a question. A playtest is a kind of prototype—not of the game, but of the game experience.

**If you don't enter your playtest with specific goals in mind, you stand a good chance of wasting your time.**

### The Wrong Question

**"Is my game fun?"** - Not specific enough!

### The Right Questions

Generally, you want your questions to be as specific as possible:

**General questions:**
- Do men and women play my game differently?
- Do kids like my game better than adults?
- Do players understand how to play?
- Do players want to play a second time? A third? A twentieth? Why?
- Do players feel the game is fair?
- Are players ever bored? Confused? Frustrated?
- Are there any dominant strategies or loopholes?
- Does the game have hidden bugs?
- What strategies do players find on their own?
- Which parts are most fun? Least fun?

**Specific questions:**
- Should the "A" button or "B" button be used for jumping?
- Is level three too long?
- Is the asparagus puzzle too hard?
- Do players notice the background music change in boss fights?
- Does the character's walk animation feel too slow?

### Creating Your Question List

**Sources for good playtesting questions:**
- Use the design lenses throughout the book
- Observe your own concerns and uncertainties
- Ask team members what they're unsure about
- Review previous playtest surprises

**Before every playtest:**
1. Write down your "why" questions
2. Prioritize them (most important first)
3. Design the test to answer these questions
4. Share the list with observers

---

## Question 2: WHO Should We Test With?

### Matching Testers to Questions

Who you pick is entirely determined by what you want to learn. Most likely, you want people in your target demographic. But even then, there are choices.

### Tester Type 1: Developers

**Who:** People who work on the game

**Pros:**
- Right there! Can play a lot, for a long time
- Give meaningful, thoughtful feedback
- No need for NDAs (already know everything)
- Can provide immediate technical insights

**Cons:**
- Too close to the game—closer than any real player will be
- Distorted opinions from deep familiarity
- Can't replicate fresh player experience

**When to use:**
- Early prototypes
- Technical feasibility tests
- Quick sanity checks
- When other testers aren't available

**Warning from design experts:** Some say it's dangerous to playtest with people who work on the game.

**Practical approach:** Playtest with developers but take what they say with a grain of salt. Don't skip the valuable insights, but validate with other tester types.

---

### Tester Type 2: Friends and Family

**Who:** Personal connections of developers

**Pros:**
- Highly available
- Comfortable talking to you
- Will share ideas even after playtest ends
- Good for early feedback

**Cons:**
- Don't want to hurt your feelings (have to deal with you regularly)
- Predisposed to like the game (already like you)
- Will be trying to like it (not representative of real world)

**When to use:**
- Very early prototypes
- Initial concept validation
- When you need quick, gentle feedback
- Between formal playtests

**How to get more honesty:**
- Explicitly ask for criticism: "I need your help finding problems"
- Test with friends of friends (less personal connection)
- Use written surveys (easier to be honest)

---

### Tester Type 3: Expert Gamers

**Who:** Hardcore players who've played every variety of your game type

**Pros:**
- Detailed, technical feedback with specific examples
- Can compare to similar games knowledgeably
- Excited to contribute (boosts their "expert" credentials)
- Catch subtle balance and depth issues

**Cons:**
- Represent small percentage of gaming public (like gourmands vs regular eaters)
- Often more jaded than average gamers
- Demand more complex and difficult gameplay
- Can lead to overtuning for elite niche audience

**Warning example:**
*The Sims Online* - Expected huge success, ended up crashing failure. One reason: overtuned for most hardcore players who were more excited about creating content than experiencing it.

**When to use:**
- Core mechanics depth testing
- High-level strategy validation
- Competitive balance
- Expert mode design
- Technical gameplay polish

**Critical caveat:** Balance their feedback with your actual target demographic.

---

### Tester Type 4: Tissue Testers (Fresh Meat)

**Who:** People who have never seen your game before

**Why "tissue testers":** Like a Kleenex tissue, they can only be used once (reference to freshness)

**Pros:**
- See game with fresh eyes
- Notice things you've become blind to
- Critical for usability questions
- Test communication (do they understand without explanation?)
- Measure initial appeal

**Cons:**
- Games are generally played multiple times over many sessions
- Only testing first-time appeal may lead to game that gets boring after multiple plays
- Can't test long-term engagement

**When to use:**
- Tutorial testing
- First impression testing
- Onboarding flow validation
- Communication clarity
- Initial appeal assessment

**Important balance:** Don't ONLY test with tissue testers, or you'll optimize for first impression at cost of long-term fun.

---

### The Complete Testing Strategy

**Nearly every game will test with some combination of all four types** sometime during design process.

**The key:** Having the right testers at the right times to answer the most questions as thoroughly as possible.

**Typical progression:**
1. **Early concept:** Developers + friends
2. **Paper prototype:** Developers + friends + expert gamers (for genre validation)
3. **Working prototype:** Mix of all types, emphasis on target demographic
4. **Polish phase:** Tissue testers (fresh perspective) + expert gamers (depth)
5. **Pre-launch:** Large-scale with target demographic

---

## Question 3: WHEN Should We Test?

### The Excuse

"The game isn't ready yet."

### The Truth

The sooner you playtest, the sooner you'll know whether your game is on the right track.

**You can playtest your game at any stage.**

### Playtesting Timeline

**Pre-Idea**
- **Method:** Focus group of target audience
- **Questions:** "What kind of games do you like? What kind would you like to see?"

**Initial Concept**
- **Method:** Describe concept, gauge reactions
- **Questions:** "We're thinking we might make a game that works like this… what do you think?"

**Paper Prototype**
- **Method:** Invite playtesters (not just developers) to play paper version
- **Questions:** Core mechanics, basic gameplay loop, rule clarity

**Whitebox Prototype**
- **Method:** Test coded game without art
- **Important:** Give caveats, then get them playing
- **Insight:** People give more honest feedback about uglier games anyway

**Working Prototype**
- **Method:** Keep playtesting as late into process as possible
- **Questions:** All the important experience questions

**Finished, Shipped Game**
- **Method:** Post-launch playtesting and analytics
- **Why:** Games as a Service—players expect fixes and new features
- **Insight:** Shipping is like the biggest playtest of all!

---

### WUBALEW: The Testing Schedule Rule

**Acronym:** WUBALEW
**Meaning:** **W**hen **U**seful, **B**ut **A**t **L**east **E**very **W**eek

**Why weekly:**
- Ensures you never go too far without real feedback
- Makes testing routine, not special/scary
- Builds team confidence gradually
- Catches problems early and often

**Making it work:**
- Put recurring weekly playtest on calendar
- Rotate who runs it
- Keep it lightweight (2-3 testers is enough)
- Document insights immediately

## Question 4: WHERE Should We Hold It?

### Location Option 1: In Your Studio

**Where:** Where you actually make games

**Pros:**
- Developers are all there
- You are there
- Game is there
- Super convenient
- Everyone on team can observe real people playing

**Cons:**
- Playtesters might not feel completely comfortable
- Strange surroundings
- Afraid to have fun while others are working
- May inhibit honest reactions

**How to make it work:**
- Go out of your way to make it comfortable
- Provide private room if possible
- Offer snacks and drinks
- Ask testers to bring friends (increases comfort)
- Let team members observing stay invisible (one-way mirror or separate room)

---

### Location Option 2: Playtesting Lab

**Where:** Special labs designed for playtesting (some large companies have internal labs, third-party companies offer service)

**Pros:**
- Designed for playtesting!
- One-way mirrors
- Cameras on playtesters
- Playtesting experts ask right questions
- Detailed notes taken professionally
- Carefully selected group of right testers

**Cons:**
- Usually very expensive
- May feel artificial
- Not always necessary

**When worth it:**
- High-stakes projects with budget
- Need for rigorous, documented results
- Targeting very specific demographics
- Publisher or stakeholder requirements

---

### Location Option 3: Public Venue

**Where:** Shopping mall, college campus, games fest (PAX, Indiecade), street corner, museums

**Pros:**
- Usually doesn't cost much
- Many testers if you find right venue
- Real-world environment
- Diverse perspectives

**Cons:**
- Hard time finding "right" testers (matching demographic)
- Distractions from other venue activities
- Testers may not give full attention
- Less controlled environment

**Success story:**
Schell Games testing *Daniel Tiger's Neighborhood* games worked out arrangement with Pittsburgh Children's Museum to test periodically right in museum—endless supply of 3-5-year-old children and parents (precisely what was needed).

**When to use:**
- Need large quantity of feedback
- Testing accessibility/broad appeal
- Want diverse perspectives
- Target demographic congregates at venue

---

### Location Option 4: Playtester's Home

**Where:** After people buy your game, they'll play it at home—why not test there now?

**Pros:**
- See game played in natural habitat
- Real conditions
- Testers have friends over naturally
- See real social interaction through game
- More relaxed, authentic behavior

**Cons:**
- Limited playtest (only 1-2 designers can observe)
- Small number of people per session
- May need to lug special hardware
- Time spent configuring machines
- Logistical challenges

**When to use:**
- Home/family games
- Want to understand natural play patterns
- Testing social dynamics
- Later in development (when portable/stable)

---

### Location Option 5: Online

**Where:** The internet! Digital distribution.

**Pros:**
- Lots of people can test
- Many different machine configurations
- Essential for stress testing
- Critical for massively multiplayer play
- Scale testing
- Can gather automatic analytics

**Cons:**
- Quantity at price of quality
- Less insight per tester (not in same room)
- Hard to keep game secret
- First impressions matter—unfinished version may hurt reputation

**Solutions:**
- Use controlled beta (Steam Early Access, closed beta invitations)
- Create safe spaces with controlled player expectations
- Clearly label as "beta" or "early access"
- Select participants carefully

**When to use:**
- Multiplayer testing at scale
- Server load testing
- Multiple platform configurations
- Post-launch ongoing tuning
- Large-scale data collection

---

### Choosing Your Location

**Base decision on your "why?" questions.**

Often, the simplest ideas are best.

**Simple example from Curt Bererton (Facebook game alpha):**
> "One thing that we did that I found super effective in one of our alphas was to put a text box about 2–3 lines of text in height below the game. All the text box said was 'tell us how to make our game better,' and you could send one message per session after which the box would go away. Emails would go to a mailing list to which anyone on the team could sign up..."
>
> "This simple text field worked remarkably well during alpha and beta, and we even kept it in production for an adjustable small percentage of players (and all paying users) such that we'd get around 30 messages per day... You'd think you would get a lot more people swearing or something, but pretty much everything was very high quality feedback. We found a ton of bugs, user experience issues, as well as just great ideas."

## Question 5: WHAT Will We Look For?

Two types of things to look for in your playtest.

### The First What: Things You Know You're Looking For

**Source:** These come from your "why?" question list.

**Planning:**
- Design playtest to get answers to these questions
- Ensure you have a way to answer every question on your list
- Skip parts of game not relevant to these questions (save time)
- Consider making several mini-tests if one test can't answer all questions

**Example:**
If question is "Is level 3 too long?", make sure:
- Testers play level 3
- You measure how long it takes
- You watch for boredom/engagement signs
- You ask post-play about pacing

---

### The Second What: Things You Don't Know You're Looking For

**The skill:** Only truly observant designers who've learned to listen deeply to players can find these.

**The key:** Keep your eyes open for **surprises**.

**Prerequisites for surprise:**
You must already have ideas about what will happen:
- "Players will attack level two a certain way"
- "They will get excited at the start of level three"
- "The villain will feel scary"

**When to jump on surprises:**

Whenever anything out of the ordinary happens (good or bad):
- Girls like your game more than boys (when you expected opposite)
- Villain makes people laugh (when you thought he'd be scary)
- Players intrigued by something you thought unimportant
- Players debating strategies you never considered

**What to do:**
- Find out why!
- Even if you weren't testing for these things, take advantage
- Understand these surprises deeply

**The reward:**
> "The insight that comes from understanding these surprises is the sweetest fruit that grows on the playtesting tree."

## Question 6: HOW Will We Conduct It?

Now that you've figured out why, who, where, and what—the rubber meets the road. How will you actually do it?

---

### Should You Even Be There?

**One school of thought:** Dangerous to have developers present when game is tested.

**The danger:**
- Emotional investment causes encouraging players to overlook flaws
- "Infect" players with insider's viewpoint
- Cannot stay objective

**If this is you:** You definitely should not be there.

**The shame:** There's so much more you'll learn being present in person than from just reading survey data or watching recorded videos.

**The recommendation:** Find ways to restrain these corrupting impulses so you can be there in person.

**Benefits of being present:**
- Real-time observation of emotions
- Can ask follow-up questions immediately
- See context for confusing behaviors
- Build empathy with players
- Notice subtle body language

---

### What Do You Tell Them Up Front?

**Option 1: Tell them nothing**
- Let game speak for itself
- Especially if testing whether they can figure it out themselves
- Good for: Onboarding, tutorial, self-explanatory design

**Option 2: Give them context**
- Most playtests need some introduction
- Get them started without spoiling discoveries

**Critical warning:** A few misplaced words right before play begins can spoil entire test.

**Bad example:**
"Your goal is to defeat the evil Chronos."
- Result: Players might start looking for him immediately
- Consequence: Miss important details they would have found naturally

**Best practices:**
- Take careful note of what you say to testers
- Write it down ahead of time
- Use same introduction for all testers (consistency)
- May need to change intro based on early test results

**The hidden benefit:**
Over multiple playtest sessions, you'll gradually tune your introduction speech:
- Trimming a word here
- Adding a phrase there
- Until speech is very clear and efficient

**Write this down!** This speech becomes the foundation of your in-game tutorial.

**Why tutorials created this way are excellent:**
They've been iteratively tested with real players, refined based on what actually works.

---

### Where Do You Look?

**Where most people look:** Where the player looks (at the screen).

**Why this makes sense:** See what the player sees.

**Where to really look:** Player faces.

**Why:**
- Don't just want to see what players are doing
- Want to see **how they feel** when doing it
- Facial expressions give wealth of data that never comes out in interviews or surveys

**Practical approach:**
- Spend most time watching faces
- Steal quick glances at screen for context
- Read emotions: excitement, confusion, boredom, frustration, delight

**Origin of this skill:**
Street performing—need to read crowd emotions quickly to tune performance in real-time. Keep entertaining parts, move quickly through boring parts.

**Application to games:**
Same skill applies—read player emotions as they play, determine how game should change to improve emotional experience.

**The universal skill:**
We're all equipped to do this—it just has to be practiced.

---

### Best Solution: Record Everything

**The ideal:** Eyes everywhere at once:
- On the game
- On players' faces
- On their hands (using controls as expected?)

**The solution:** Modern video technology!

**Setup:**
Multiple cameras feeding to single split-screen image:
- Camera 1: Game screen
- Camera 2: Player face
- Camera 3: Player hands

**Benefits:**
- Go back later and see how all three interrelate
- Review multiple times
- Share with team members who couldn't attend
- Create highlight reels
- Document for stakeholders

---

### What Other Data Should You Collect During Play?

Watching and recording gives lots of information, but you can gather more with planning.

**Important events to log:**
- How long players spent in character creator?
- How many hits to defeat villain?
- What was average player score?
- Which weapons used most?
- Deaths per level
- Time to complete sections
- Button presses per minute
- Number of times checked map

**For digital games:**
- Log automatically (goldmine of data)
- Essential for MMOs and free-to-play
- Enables continuous tuning

**For physical games:**
- Make careful notes when events occur
- Use simple tally system
- Have dedicated note-taker

**The new art: Quantitative design**
- Rigorous analytics systems
- Content management systems
- Continuous tuning and testing in perpetuity

**Dangers:**
- Can lead to dull designs
- Fear of trusting own instincts as designer

**Benefits (if dangers overcome):**
- Subtle art
- New opportunities to understand player behavior
- Data-driven iteration

---

### Will I Disturb the Players Midgame?

**The trade-off:**

**Risk of disturbing:**
- Interfere with natural play patterns
- Break immersion
- Change behavior

**Risk of NOT disturbing:**
- Miss insight you can't get any other way
- Player's state of mind changes after play
- May have no recollection later

**The recommendation:** Most designers favor only interrupting when player does something truly surprising that designer doesn't understand.

---

### Think-Aloud Protocol

**What it is:** Encourage person to verbalize all internal thoughts into stream-of-consciousness.

**Example:**
"Let's see… I'm supposed to find bananas, but I don't see any… I wonder what's behind that log… Yow! Bad guys! Ouch! Take that! Okay… Hey, is that a banana up on that hill?"

**Benefits:**
- Direct access to decision-making process
- Understand player reasoning
- Recommended by human-computer interaction experts

**Challenges with games:**

1. **Changes behavior:**
   - Act of speaking changes how people behave
   - Often become more thoughtful and careful
   - Not representative of natural play

2. **Paralysis:**
   - Some people can't play and talk simultaneously
   - Stop talking during stressful moments
   - Frustrating because stressful moments are when you need insight most

3. **Not natural for everyone:**
   - Some players can do it naturally (very useful)
   - Others can be trained through repeat playtests
   - Identify good think-aloud players

**Warning:**
Well-meaning interaction experts can completely ruin playtests by constantly peppering players with questions to elicit think-aloud.

**Decision:** When and whether to use this technique is something you'll need to decide for yourself.

## Post-Play Data Collection

You'll gain tremendous information from observation. But you can gain even more with meaningful follow-up.

**Two methods:**
1. Surveys
2. Interviews

---

### Surveys

**Purpose:** Have players answer straightforward questions about your game that are easily quantified.

#### Eight Survey Tips

**1. Use pictures whenever possible**
- When asking about game elements or scenes
- Ensures player knows what you mean
- Visual references reduce confusion

**2. Online surveys save time**
- Systems: SurveyMonkey, Google Forms
- Easy to set up
- Free or inexpensive
- Automatic data aggregation

**3. Don't use 1-10 scales**
- More consistent results with 5-point scales
- Each point clearly labeled:
  - Terrible
  - Pretty bad
  - So-so
  - Good
  - Excellent

**4. Don't put too many questions**
- People tune out near the end
- Results won't be worth much
- Quality over quantity

**5. Give survey right after play**
- While things are fresh in their mind
- Before they leave
- Immediate feedback

**6. Have someone on hand for questions**
- Answer clarifying questions
- Ensure testers understand what's being asked
- Address confusion immediately

**7. Note age and gender of each tester**
- See if these have connection to opinions
- Identify demographic patterns
- Understand your audience better

**8. Don't take survey data as gospel**
- Unlikely your survey is truly scientific
- Playtesters tend to make things up when unsure
- Use as one data point, not only data point

---

### Interviews

**Purpose:** Ask players questions too complex for simple survey. Get sense of how they really felt with emotion in faces and voices.

#### Interview Best Practices

**Have a script of questions**
- Ready when you interview people
- Leave space to write responses
- Leave space for general notes when conversation takes unexpected turns
- Be ready for surprises!

**Interview people privately**
- When possible
- People speak more honestly one-on-one than when others (especially people they know) are listening
- If tester has friends who are testing:
  - Do private interview first
  - Consider group interview after (see if new information emerges when close friends talk together)

**Playtesters will avoid hurting your feelings**
- Especially if they know (or think) you helped make the game
- Staying objective sometimes not enough

**Permission technique:**
Make big show of saying: "I need your help. This game has some real problems, but we're not sure what they are. Please, if there is anything at all you don't like about this game, it will be a great help to me if you let me know."
- Gives tester permission to speak honestly
- Frames criticism as helping (which it is)

**Avoid memory tests**
- Don't ask: "On level 3, when you got to yellow butterflies, you flew left instead of right. Why?"
- Generally get blank stares
- Players busy playing; don't form memories about things not immediately relevant to game goal
- If you need answers to specific moment questions, ask during play

**Don't expect playtesters to be game designers**
- Questions like "Would the game have been better if level three were harder?" may not get results you want
- Players always think they want game easier
- Most playtesters not skilled at thinking about/discussing game mechanics

**Better alternatives:**
- Instead of: "Should level three be harder?"
- Ask: "Were any parts of level three boring?"
- Gets honest answer and information you're looking for

**Ask for more than you need**
- Instead of: "What was your least favorite part?"
- Ask: "What were your three least favorite parts?"
- Get more data
- Sorted by priority
- What stands out most comes first

**Consider a video closet (Barbara Chamberlin technique)**
- Space set aside with video camera or iPad
- Whiteboard with questions
- Players go in one at a time
- Read question, think, reflect
- Turn camera on and talk through answer

**Benefits:**
- Counters groupthink
- Helps quieter players offer their voice
- Works best when you've asked related questions outside closet first
- Players can provide more depth or different perspective
- Easy to share with team and clients (all recorded)

**Why it works:**
Somehow people will say things to a camera they'd never say to your face.

**Set your ego aside**
- Very hard to listen to someone tell you how bad your game is
- Sorely tempted to step in and defend game
- Tell them how it's supposed to be

**Resist this urge:**
- No one cares how game was supposed to be during interview
- Right now, all that matters is how this playtester feels about the game, and why
- When temptation rises, ask objective questions:
  - "What don't you like about it?"
  - "Can you tell me more about that?"
  - "Why do you think you feel that way?"

---

### FFWWDD: Six Essential Questions

**Acronym by Shawn Patton**

Six easy-to-remember questions to ask players after they play your game. This set is the go-to at Schell Games.

#### The Six Questions (In This Order)

**1. What was the most FRUSTRATING moment or aspect of what you just played?**
- Gives players chance to vent about what irritated them
- Gets negativity out first

**2. What was your FAVORITE moment or aspect of what you just played?**
- Tells you about high point of experience
- What worked well

**3. Was there anything you WANTED to do that you couldn't?**
- Players tell you about small desires
- Immediate limitations they bumped into

**4. If you had a magic WAND to wave, and you could change, add, or remove anything from the experience, what would it be?**
- Players really think outside box on this one
- Different from question 3 (seems similar but isn't)
- Gets big-picture wishes

**5. What were you DOING in the experience?**
- Tells you about player's goal
- What they thought the point was
- Reveals if your communication was clear

**6. How would you DESCRIBE this game to your friends and family?**
- Great information about perceptions of overall experience
- How they'd market it themselves
- What stands out most in their mind

#### Why This Order Matters

1. **Frustrating** first - Vent, get it out
2. **Favorite** second - Balance with positives
3. **Wanted** third - Small, immediate desires
4. **Wand** fourth - Big, creative wishes (different from wanted)
5. **Doing** fifth - Their understanding of goal
6. **Describe** sixth - Overall perception and message

Use this lens for every playtest to ensure it's as good as it can be:

- **WHY** are we doing a playtest?
- **WHO** should be there?

- **WHERE** should we hold it?
- **WHAT** will we look for?

## Common Playtesting Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Waiting Until Game Is "Ready"

**Problem:** Postponing tests because "game isn't ready yet"

**Reality:** You can playtest at any stage

**Solution:** Test early and often (WUBALEW)

---

### Mistake 2: Only Testing with One Type of Tester

**Problem:** Only testing with developers, or only with friends, or only with experts

**Solution:** Use combination of all four tester types at appropriate stages

---

### Mistake 3: Vague Goals

**Problem:** Testing to see "if it's fun" without specific questions

**Solution:** Create specific question list before every test

---

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Surprises

**Problem:** Only looking for answers to predetermined questions

**Solution:** Stay alert for unexpected behaviors and reactions—these are goldmines

---

### Mistake 5: Defending Your Game

**Problem:** Explaining how it's "supposed to work" during or after test

**Solution:** Set ego aside, ask questions, listen objectively

---

### Mistake 6: Poor Data Collection

**Problem:** No recording, no notes, relying on memory

**Solution:** Video recording + note-taker + surveys/interviews

---

### Mistake 7: Testing Too Infrequently

**Problem:** Only testing when major problems arise

**Solution:** WUBALEW - When Useful, But At Least Every Week

- [ ] WHERE: Location chosen based on goals

- [ ] HOW: Introduction speech prepared and written down
- [ ] Recording equipment set up (camera, audio, screen capture)

- [ ] Team members briefed on observation protocol

- [ ] Team members observing silently

## Measuring Playtesting Success

### Signs of Good Playtesting Practice

- Weekly playtests feel routine, not terrifying
- Specific questions guide every test
- Mix of tester types used appropriately
- Surprises lead to valuable insights
- Tutorial/onboarding refined from introduction speeches
- Team confident in design direction
- Changes based on playtest feedback improve game measurably

### Warning Signs

- Playtests postponed frequently
- No specific questions prepared
- Only testing with one type of tester
- Defending game during or after tests
- No recordings or notes
- Ignoring feedback that challenges assumptions
- Team uncertainty about whether game is working
