# Managing Game Development Teams

Coordinate teams, documentation, and playtesting to create great games through collaboration and iteration.

## When to Use This Skill

- Starting a new game project and assembling a team
- Facilitating design discussions and decision-making
- Creating project documentation for memory and communication
- Planning and running playtesting sessions
- Addressing team communication or collaboration issues
- Coordinating between disciplines (design, engineering, art, production)
- Establishing workflows for iterative development

## Core Principles

### The Secret of Successful Teamwork: Love

**The most important factor determining team success is love—love for the game and love for the audience.**

Without this, no amount of process, tools, or methodology will save your project. With it, all differences and disagreements will be set aside in service of making the game wonderful.

#### Three Love Problems to Identify and Fix:

1. **Team members incapable of loving any game**
   - **Solution:** Get them off the team (no other cure exists)

2. **Team members in love with a different game**
   - **Solution:** Work with them to find something in the current game they can love, or help them contribute ideas that bridge the gap

3. **Team members in love with different visions of the same game**
   - **Solution:** Get everyone on the same page through thorough communication and respect. Stop everything when someone doesn't buy into an idea—find out why and get them on board.

#### If You Can't Love the Game, Love the Audience

When you can't love the game itself:
1. Find one thing in the game you can be proud of and excited about
2. Think of the game as a gift for your audience—put your love for them into creating their special moment
3. As a last resort: pretend to love it. Sometimes pretending transforms into real love.

> **Key Insight:** "The love you put into that moment [the game] shines through and into the hearts of your audience."

## Quick Reference: Design Lenses

### Lens #100: The Lens of Love
**When team motivation is flagging:**
- Do I love my project? If not, how can I change that?
- Does everyone on the team love the project? If not, how can that be changed?

### Lens #101: The Lens of the Team
**When evaluating team health:**
- Is this the right team for this project? Why?
- Is the team communicating objectively?
- Is the team communicating clearly?
- Is the team comfortable with each other?
- Is there an air of trust and respect among the team?
- Is the team ultimately able to unify around decisions?

### Lens #102: The Lens of Documentation
**When deciding what to document:**
- What do we need to remember while making this game?
- What needs to be communicated while making this game?

### Lens #103: The Lens of Playtesting
**When planning any playtest:**
- **Why** are we doing a playtest?
- **Who** should be there?
- **When** should we test?
- **Where** should we hold it?
- **What** will we look for?
- **How** will we get the information we need?

## Workflow 1: Establishing Team Collaboration

### Step 1: Build Team Love for the Game

**Assess current state:**
- Does each team member love the game?
- Can they articulate what excites them about it?
- Do they think about it constantly like "children anticipating Christmas"?

**If love is missing:**
- Identify which love problem applies (see Three Love Problems above)
- Take immediate action—this is not something that fixes itself

### Step 2: Set Up Collaborative Design Process

Use the four-phase cycle (see [designing-together-workflow.md](designing-together-workflow.md) for complete details):

1. **Initial brainstorming** → Involve as much of the team as possible
2. **Independent design** → Core design team thinks independently
3. **Design discussion** → Core team discusses and reaches consensus
4. **Design presentation** → Present to whole team, collect feedback

**Key principle:** Everyone on the team should feel they own the design. This means hundreds of tiny implementation decisions will reinforce the design rather than undermine it.

### Step 3: Establish Communication Standards

Implement the **Ten Keys to Team Communication** (see [team-communication-guide.md](team-communication-guide.md)):

1. **Objectivity** - Focus on problem statements, not opinions
2. **Clarity** - Ensure understanding, be concrete and specific
3. **Persistence** - Write things down in permanent media
4. **Comfort** - Create physically and emotionally comfortable environments
5. **Respect** - Treat others as you want to be treated
6. **Trust** - Build through high-bandwidth, frequent communication
7. **Honesty** - Be truthful so trust can form
8. **Privacy** - Use one-on-one conversations for sensitive topics
9. **Unity** - Reach decisions everyone commits to ("disagree and commit")
10. **Love** - The culmination of all other keys

**Critical communication habits:**
- Speak objectively: "The spaceship idea" not "my idea"
- Phrase alternatives as questions: "What if we did B instead of A?"
- Never pretend to understand—keep asking until you do
- Write down decisions and reasons immediately
- Include everyone on all design communications

## Workflow 2: Creating Game Documentation

### The Purpose of Documents

Documents serve exactly two purposes:
1. **Memory** - Record decisions so you don't solve the same problems twice
2. **Communication** - Share decisions with the team and enable dialog

**Important:** There is no magic template. Each game needs different documents based on what must be remembered and communicated.

### Document Types and When to Create Them

See [game-documents-guide.md](game-documents-guide.md) for detailed descriptions of all 15 document types.

**Essential documents for most projects:**
- **Game design overview** (first document - for management and team alignment)
- **Detailed design document** (many small documents for subsystems, not one giant document)
- **Project schedule** (updated weekly or more)
- **Asset tracker** (maintain throughout project)

**Specialized documents (create as needed):**
- Technical design, pipeline overview, system limitations (engineering needs)
- Art bible, concept art overview (art consistency)
- Story overview, story bible, script (narrative-driven games)
- Game budget (funding and scope management)
- Tutorial and manual (player-facing documentation)

### How to Start Your Documentation

1. **Start simple:** Create a bullet list of ideas for your game
2. **Write down questions** as they arise (don't lose these—they're crucial!)
3. **Answer questions** and note decisions + reasons
4. **Organize naturally** as your list grows and falls into sections
5. **Keep it living:** Documents should evolve with the game

**Key insight from Jason VandenBerghe:**
> "GDDs are literally out of date the moment you write them. Design documents are an expression of your current theories about what will make your game good… but until you see those theories in practice, you cannot know."

Treat documents as theories, not blueprints. Update them when theories change or abandon them when the game itself becomes the primary reference (often happens midway through projects).

**Best practice from Stone Librande:**
Every game should be explainable in a **diagram on a poster** that shows how the whole game fits together (the "one-page design").

## Workflow 3: Planning and Running Playtests

### The Reality of Playtesting

Playtesting is essential but uncomfortable. It's like an invitation that reads: "You are cordially invited to tell me why I suck."

**Why it's necessary:**
- Makes clear that decisions you were comfortable with are completely wrong
- Gives you a chance to fix problems while there's still time
- Builds team confidence that you're making the right game

**Mindset shift:** If players don't like your game, that's great! You can ask why and fix it before launch.

### The Six Playtesting Questions Framework

Every playtest is defined by answering six questions. See [playtesting-framework.md](playtesting-framework.md) for complete guidance.

#### 1. WHY are we playtesting?

**Enter the playtest with specific goals.**

Bad question: "Is my game fun?"
Good questions:
- Do men and women play my game differently?
- Do players understand how to play without instructions?
- Is level three too long?
- Does the "A" button or "B" button feel right for jumping?
- Are players ever bored, confused, or frustrated? When and why?
- What strategies do players discover on their own?

**Prepare a list of questions before the playtest.** Use the design lenses throughout this book to generate good playtesting questions.

#### 2. WHO should we test with?

Match testers to your questions:

- **Developers** - Convenient, thoughtful feedback, but too close to the game
- **Friends and family** - Available and comfortable, but may bend truth to spare feelings
- **Expert gamers** - Technical feedback and comparisons, but may be too hardcore
- **Tissue testers** - Fresh eyes see what you've become blind to, but only good for first impressions

**Most games need some combination of all four types** at different stages of development.

#### 3. WHEN should we test?

**Answer: WUBALEW** (When Useful, But At Least Every Week)

You can test at any stage:
- Pre-idea: "What kind of games would you like to see?"
- Initial concept: "We're thinking about a game like this..."
- Paper prototype: Test with playtesters, not just developers
- Whitebox prototype: Test even without art
- Working prototype: Keep testing as late as possible
- Finished, shipped game: Games as a Service means ongoing testing and tuning

**Make weekly playtesting a habit.** You'll never go too far without real feedback.

#### 4. WHERE should we hold it?

Options and trade-offs:
- **In your studio** - Convenient, team can observe, but testers may feel uncomfortable
- **Playtesting lab** - Professional setup with cameras and experts, but expensive
- **Public venue** - Many testers, low cost, but may be distracted or wrong demographic
- **Playtester's home** - Natural habitat, real conditions, but logistically limited
- **Online** - Large scale, many configurations, but less insight per tester

Choose based on your "why" questions.

#### 5. WHAT will we look for?

**Two types of things:**

1. **Things you know you're looking for** - Answers to your "why" questions
2. **Things you don't know you're looking for** - Surprises that challenge your assumptions

**Key skill:** Be ready to jump on surprises. When anything unexpected happens (good or bad), investigate why. The insights from these surprises are "the sweetest fruit that grows on the playtesting tree."

#### 6. HOW will we conduct it?

**During the playtest:**
- **Where to look:** Watch player faces (emotions), not just the screen
- **What to tell them up front:** Be very careful—a few misplaced words can spoil the test. Write your intro speech down.
- **Data collection:** Log important events automatically if possible (time spent, choices made, scores, etc.)
- **Think-aloud protocol:** Can be useful but may taint natural behavior
- **Interrupting players:** Generally avoid, but sometimes necessary for critical insights

**After the playtest:**
- **Surveys:** Use 5-point labeled scales (Terrible/Pretty bad/So-so/Good/Excellent), keep short, use pictures
- **Interviews:** Have a script, interview privately, avoid memory tests, don't expect players to be designers
- **FFWWDD Questions** (use these six every time):
  1. Most **Frustrating** moment?
  2. **Favorite** moment?
  3. What did you **Want** to do but couldn't?
  4. Magic **Wand** to change anything?
  5. What were you **Doing**?
  6. How would you **Describe** this to friends?

**Video recording:** Set up cameras to capture game screen, player face, and player hands simultaneously for later review.

**Video closet technique (Barbara Chamberlin):** Private space with camera and whiteboard questions where players record individual responses. Counters groupthink and helps quiet players share perspectives.

## Common Patterns and Mistakes

### Pattern: Gradual Tutorial Refinement

**Opportunity:** When running multiple playtests, you'll naturally tune your introductory speech to players, trimming a word here and adding a phrase there.

**Action:** Write down this refined speech! It becomes the foundation of your in-game tutorial. Tutorials created this way have an "aura of excellence" because they've been iteratively tested.

### Pattern: Team Members Eating Separately

**Warning sign:** If artists eat separately from programmers, expect pipeline problems. If platform teams eat separately, expect porting problems.

**Why it matters:** Humans are selective about who they eat with. Separate meals signal low trust.

**Solution:** Give teams every opportunity to be together and communicate, even about non-work topics. Constant face-to-face communication builds trust.

### Mistake: Treating Documents as Blueprints

**Problem:** Team treats design documents as unchangeable specifications instead of evolving theories.

**Impact:** "Evil abounds when you have a document that some people think is a plan, some people think is a theory, and some people think is a blueprint."

**Solution:** Make it clear documents are living theories. Update them frequently or abandon them when the game itself becomes the reference.

### Mistake: Overtuning for Hardcore Players

**Example:** *The Sims Online* was expected to be a huge success but failed because it was overtuned for the most hardcore players.

**Lesson:** Expert gamers are valuable testers but represent a niche audience. Balance their feedback with input from your actual target demographic.

### Mistake: Designing in Isolation

**Problem:** Designer withdraws from team, ignores opinions, produces design independently.

**Impact:** Team's beautiful ideas get crushed, love dries up, implementation lacks unified vision.

**Solution:** Include the team whenever possible. Set ego aside. Most people just want their ideas heard, not to hijack the design.

## Tips for Success

1. **Never skip addressing love problems** - They only get worse with time
2. **Write everything down persistently** - Memory is terrible, documents enable collaboration
3. **Watch player faces during playtests** - Emotions reveal what surveys cannot
4. **Disagree and commit** - Team unity requires sometimes going down paths you don't fully agree with
5. **Start documentation simple** - Bullet lists evolve naturally into proper documents
6. **Test weekly (WUBALEW)** - Make it a habit so you never go too far without feedback
7. **One person disagrees?** - Two people make a disagreement. Work with them until meaningful compromise is found.
8. **Keep team physically together** - Open offices with constant face-to-face communication build trust faster
9. **Use FFWWDD questions** - These six questions consistently provide valuable insights
10. **Set your ego aside in interviews** - All that matters is how the playtester feels and why

## Measuring Success

**Team health indicators:**
- Team members talk about the game like children anticipating Christmas
- Design meetings focus on problem statements, not personal preferences
- Important decisions are written down and accessible to everyone
- One-on-one conversations reveal the same story as public meetings
- Team can reach "disagree and commit" consensus on difficult decisions
- Playtesters' feedback leads to rapid design improvements
- Weekly playtests feel routine and valuable, not terrifying

**Warning signs:**
- Team members avoid design discussions or meetings
- Different disciplines eat lunch separately
- Design documents created but never updated or referenced
- Playtests postponed repeatedly because "game isn't ready"
- Team can't make decisions without leadership intervention
- Players confused about basic game concepts during tests
