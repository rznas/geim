# Transformational Games Guide

## What Are Transformational Games?

The term "serious games" is dissatisfactory — it insults entertainment and implies players should not enjoy themselves. **Transformational games** is the better term: it encompasses all helpful games and speaks directly to their primary purpose — to change the player.

Transformational games include educational games, health games, behavior-change games, empathy games, and any game whose design intent is to leave the player meaningfully different than before they played.

---

## Four Categories of Games for Good

### 1. Emotional Maintenance

Games are tools that help maintain and control mood and emotional state. Players use them to:

| Purpose | Mechanism | Example Context |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Vent anger/frustration** | Cathartic action in a safe world | Sports, fast-action combat games |
| **Cheer up** | Whimsical situations, humor | *Cranium*, *Mario Party* |
| **Gain perspective** | Distance from real-world problems | Any absorbing game during stress |
| **Build confidence** | Mastery through successful outcomes | Games where choices lead to success |
| **Relax** | Force brain to engage with something unconnected to worries | Puzzle games, casual games |

**Design implication:** Even entertainment games serve emotional maintenance. This is not a lesser function — it is a genuine human need.

### 2. Connecting

Games act as a **social bridge** between people. They provide:
- Reasons to interact with each other
- Insight into how others respond to situations
- Topics of conversation
- Shared discoveries about what we have in common
- Shared memories that bond relationships

**Design implication:** Social features are not optional enhancements. They are one of the core ways games improve lives. Designing good social mechanics IS designing for impact.

### 3. Exercise

Games — particularly sports — give people reason and motivation to perform healthy physical and mental exercise.

- Physical exercise benefits are well established
- Mental exercise benefits are increasingly documented, especially for the elderly
- Problem-solving games are flexible tools for both types of exercise
- As technology becomes more portable, new exercise delivery methods will emerge

**Design implication:** Any game that makes movement or cognitive challenge feel fun is contributing to player health.

### 4. Education

Education is already a game — students just don't realize it:

| Education Element | Game Equivalent |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Assignments | Goals |
| Due dates | Time limits |
| Grades | Scores / feedback |
| Harder assignments | Escalating challenges |
| Final exam | Boss monster |
| Honor roll | Leaderboard |

**Why educational games haven't taken over classrooms:**
1. **Time constraints** — Meaningful educational games are often too long for classroom slots
2. **Variable pacing** — Games let players proceed at their own pace; classrooms require uniform pacing
3. **Generational gap** — Until recently, educational systems were run by people who didn't grow up with videogames
4. **Difficulty of creation** — Making something that delivers a complete, verifiable, assessable lesson while remaining engaging is genuinely hard

**Best use:** Games work as **tools** alongside skilled instructors, not as complete educational system replacements.

## Education Deep Dive: Giving the Brain What It Wants

Games keep the brain engaged because they deliver what it actually needs:

### Visible Progress
A tremendous human motivator. Game structures like leveling up and completing quests make progress clear and visible — something traditional education often lacks.

### Making the Abstract Concrete
The human brain struggles with abstract concepts but excels with concrete ones. Games are naturally manipulable and can make the abstract concrete.

**Example:** War games and simulations are essential parts of military education — abstract principles of strategy are meaningless without the concrete realities of the battlefield.

### Full Engagement
When one part of the brain is active (listening to a lecture) but others are idle (musical, social, kinesthetic), restlessness follows. Games excel at occupying eyes, ears, hands, and mind simultaneously. When every part of the mind is comfortable, education happens more easily.

### Lots of Little Goals
The brain loves clear, interesting, achievable goals. Good games are long chains of exactly these.

## What Games Teach Best

### Facts
Learning facts (state capitals, times tables, disease names) is dull and repetitive. Games integrate fact-learning into reward structures that make progress through boring material feel engaging. Visuals and meaningful context help retention.

### Problem Solving
Games are defined as problem-solving approached with a playful attitude. For teaching integrated problem-solving — where students must combine multiple skills in realistic settings — game-based simulations may eventually replace final exams in fields like:
- Police and rescue work
- Geology and architecture
- Management and logistics

### Systems of Relationships (Games' Greatest Strength)

Linear media (lectures, readings, videos) cannot effectively convey complex systems of relationships. **The only way to understand a complex system is to play with it.**

**Miller's Pyramid of Learning** places "doing" at the pinnacle of knowledge. Game-based learning is almost entirely focused on doing.

**Systems best understood through simulation:**
- The human circulatory system
- Traffic patterns in a major city
- A nuclear reactor
- The ecology of endangered species
- Climate and atmospheric systems

**The power of failure:** Simulations give permission to fail — and failure is incredibly educational. Players don't just see failures; they see WHY they happened, which leads to insight about how the whole system works.

**Case study — Peacemaker (Impact Games):**
A simulation of the Israeli/Palestinian conflict where players can play either side. Native players from these countries entered believing "if the other side would just do a few simple things, it would be over." Playing the opposing side showed them the complex pressures that make resolution genuinely difficult. Players then became deeply curious — first testing what causes all-out war, then tackling the real challenge: finding techniques that actually work toward peace.

**Note on imperfect simulations:** Simulations don't need to be perfect. Simulation loopholes can be more instructive than perfect accuracy — they cause players to ask "why doesn't this happen in the real world?" That question alone leads to deep insight.

### New Insights
Games create whole new realities with new rules where "you aren't you anymore." This perspective shift is powerful.

**Groundhog Day pattern:** Repeated exposure to a situation from different angles produces behavioral change. Games naturally create this through replay, alternate paths, and role-switching.

**Emerging applications:**
- Help children in low-income neighborhoods imagine success and make it feel achievable
- Help people understand how to escape abusive relationships
- Help people break addiction
- Make volunteering feel meaningful and connected

### Curiosity
"The cure for boredom is curiosity. There is no cure for curiosity." — Dorothy Parker

A curious student owns their learning, and owned learning is kept. With the Internet, curious people can become experts at anything. A **curiosity gap** is forming: curious minds will vastly outpace the incurious.

Recall the definition of play: "manipulation that indulges curiosity." Play-based education may be the best possible way to prepare children for the twenty-first century.

### Creating Teachable Moments
Knowledge cannot be poured into a mind like coffee into a cup. The mind must be put into a state of readiness where certain knowledge becomes suddenly useful and the mind hungrily reaches for it. Games — with their concrete situations and problems — are excellent tools for creating these moments of readiness.

## Seven Transformational Tips

### Tip #1: Define Your Transformation
State exactly what change will occur in the player and specifically how and why your game will foster that change. This is Lens #14 (Problem Statement) applied to transformation.

### Tip #2: Find Great Subject Matter Experts
Find two types: content experts (who know all the facts) and teaching experts (who know how to teach those facts). Listen to them about what is and isn't important.

### Tip #3: What Does the Instructor Need?
Design as a tool for the instructor, not a replacement. Make them a dungeon master who guides students through your game's scenarios. Talk to instructors first — find out what problems they have.

### Tip #4: Don't Do Too Much
Pick one key transformation and do it exceptionally well. If it works, people will ask for more. Most effective transformational games target exactly one type of transformation.

### Tip #5: Assess Transformation Appropriately
Five assessment levels (ascending rigor):
1. **Feels like it** — Subjective impression
2. **Anecdotes** — Individual stories (inspiring but not proof)
3. **SME approval** — Expert endorsement (strong indicator)
4. **Informal surveys** — In-game or post-play tests
5. **Scientific testing** — Formal statistical analysis (ideal, expensive)

Align the whole team on what level of rigor is necessary before starting.

### Tip #6: Choose the Right Venue
Where, how, and with whom players play makes a tremendous difference. Consider: location, alone vs. with others, time constraints, instructor presence, available devices.

### Tip #7: Accept the Realities of the Market
A transformational game that no one plays changes no one. Business savvy is just as important as game design when the goal is real-world impact. Understand what people will and will not pay for, and why.

## Quick Decision Framework

```
Is your goal to change players?
├── Yes → Define the specific transformation (Tip #1)
│   ├── Will an instructor be involved?
│   │   ├── Yes → Design as instructor tool (Tip #3)
│   │   └── No  → Design standalone, but acknowledge limitations
│   ├── How will you know it's working? → Choose assessment level (Tip #5)
│   └── Can you sustain this as a business? → Apply market realities (Tip #7)
└── No  → Still apply Lens #110: How might your game change players anyway?
```
