# Designer Purpose Guide

## The Deepest Theming

Throughout *The Art of Game Design*, Schell argues that knowing your game's theme makes it dramatically more powerful. But the deepest version of this question is not about your game — it is about you:

**What is YOUR personal theme? Why is game design important to YOU?**

Most designers never ask this question seriously. They assume the time for self-reflection can come later. It can't. Life is short. Time destroys everything and takes everything away.

---

## Finding Your Purpose

Your subconscious mind holds secrets you are not consciously aware of. You often do things — including designing games — without knowing the real reason why. Finding that reason is the most important creative work you can do.

### Why Purpose Matters

Once you identify your true purpose:
- **Conscious and subconscious motivations unite**
- Your work gains **passion, focus, and intensity** that cannot possibly be greater
- Every design decision has a clear north star
- The gap between "going through the motions" and "doing meaningful work" closes permanently

Without purpose, even technically excellent games lack the quality that makes them truly memorable and truly impactful.

### Possible Sources of Purpose

Purpose is deeply personal. Common patterns include:

- **A vision:** Something you see in your mind's eye that you believe will change someone's life
- **A gift:** A wonderful experience you had that you want to share with the world
- **A wound:** Something terrible that happened to someone you loved, and you want to be sure it never happens again
- **A question:** Something about human nature you are trying to understand through interactive experience
- **A belief:** A conviction that games can solve a problem no other medium can

No one can know your purpose but you. No one needs to know it but you.

### Reflection Exercises

**Exercise 1: The Why Chain**
Start with "I design games." Ask "why?" Write the answer. Ask "why?" again. Continue until you reach an answer that feels true and deep — not an answer you think sounds good, but one that resonates emotionally.

**Exercise 2: The Game That Changed You**
Think of a game — any game — that genuinely changed how you think, feel, or see the world. What did it do? Why did it matter? What does your desire to create something like it reveal about your purpose?

**Exercise 3: The Impossible Game**
If you could make any game — no constraints on budget, technology, or time — what would it be? Not what would sell. What would you make if you could make anything? The answer often points directly at your purpose.

**Exercise 4: The Last Game**
If you could only make one more game in your life, what would it be? This constraint strips away everything except what truly matters to you.

## The Two Critical Lenses

### Lens #112: The Lens of the Raven

Named after Poe's raven that mocks with "nevermore" — a reminder that time is finite and relentless.

**The question:** Is making this game worth my time?

**When to apply:** Before committing to any significant project. Not as a casual filter, but as a genuine reckoning with how you spend your limited life.

**The urgency:** You must run like death is behind you — because death IS behind you. Quick — do your important work now while you still can.

This does not mean every game must be world-changing. It means every game should connect to something you genuinely care about. A game made with purpose — even a small, joyful game — carries more weight than a technically brilliant game made from indifference.

### Lens #∞: The Lens of Your Secret Purpose

This is not numbered. It is infinite — because it encompasses all other lenses.

**The only question that matters:** Why am I doing this?

This lens is not meant to be answered once and filed away. It is meant to be revisited constantly — whenever your work feels hollow, whenever you face a difficult design decision, whenever you wonder if any of this matters.

## Violence: A Nuanced Analysis

### What Parents Actually Worry About

Parents are not concerned about abstract violence (chess captures, *Pac-Man* ghosts). The concern escalates with:

1. **Graphic bloodshed** — The "Mom Test": *Virtua Fighter* passed (no blood). *Mortal Kombat* failed (graphic blood). Same actions, completely different parental response. Blood makes the game feel "creepily real."

2. **Real-world proximity** — Pirate cannons shooting cartoon ships: no concern whatsoever. *Death Race* rewarding players for running down animated pedestrians: immediate parental protest. The danger of reckless driving is too real.

3. **Reward structure** — Violence as an obstacle (defeat the enemy to progress) reads differently than violence as the entertainment itself.

### The Compartmentalization Question

**How adults handle it:** The more someone plays a game, the more they see through the aesthetics and enter the pure problem-solving world of game mechanics. Even during a killing rampage, the player's thoughts are about perfecting skills, solving puzzles, and accomplishing goals — not rage or murder.

Despite millions of people playing violent games, stories of someone acting out game violence in real life are extremely rare. Most people compartmentalize well: they know a game is just a game.

**The real concern — children:** Children are still forming their views of the world. Are they able to safely compartmentalize? Research suggests some level of violent play is natural and even necessary for healthy psychological development (*Killing Monsters* by Gerard Jones). But there are limits — images and ideas children are not yet ready to deal with.

**The designer's position:** Psychology cannot give a definitive answer yet. So far, violent games don't appear to have damaged our collective psyche. But new technologies will make increasingly extreme violent play possible. Designers must stay alert. To say crossing a harmful line is impossible would be arrogant and irresponsible.

## Addiction: A Nuanced Analysis

### The Spectrum

"Addictive" is used as a compliment ("I love it, it's so addictive!") meaning engaging and compelling. But for some players, game engagement becomes self-destructive — interfering with school, work, health, and relationships.

This is not new. Historical examples:
- College students dropping out from playing bridge (Jesse Schell's grandparents' era)
- *Hearts in Atlantis* — students failing out from addiction to the card game Hearts
- 1970s *Dungeons & Dragons* overplay leading to poor academics
- *League of Legends* as an "uncontrollable temptation" for students today

### Why It Happens (Nicholas Yee's Framework)

Addiction is complex because different players are drawn to different aspects, to different degrees, and may be motivated by different external factors:

| Driver | What's Happening |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Game pulling player in** | The game's engagement systems are too compelling |
| **Real life pushing player in** | A real-world problem is using the game as an outlet |
| **Both simultaneously** | Most common case — game and life problems reinforce each other |

**There is no single treatment** because there are many reasons people become obsessed. If playing habits are causing real-life problems, professional counseling is appropriate.

### The Designer's Obligation

Some suggest designers should not create games that are "too engaging." This logic is flawed — it is like saying overeating is the fault of irresponsible bakers who make cake taste "too delicious."

The real obligation: **find ways to make game structures fit into a well-balanced life.** This responsibility belongs to game designers — not publishers, not platforms, not parents alone.

**Shigeru Miyamoto's approach:** When signing autographs for children, Miyamoto often writes: *"On a sunny day, play outside."*

This is not ironic. It is a designer acknowledging, with a light touch, that games should complement life — not replace it.

## Connecting Purpose to Practice

Once you have found your purpose, here is how it guides daily design work:

### For Every Design Decision
Ask: Does this serve my purpose? If not, does it at least not work against it?

### For Project Selection
Apply Lens #112 (The Raven): Is this game worth my finite time? Does it connect — even indirectly — to why I design?

### For Difficult Trade-offs
When commercial pressure conflicts with impact, your purpose tells you how to weigh the scales. Remember: a game no one plays changes no one. But a game that succeeds commercially AND serves your purpose is the highest achievement.

### For Sustaining Motivation
When the work is hard, when the game isn't coming together, when you feel invisible and unappreciated — return to Lens #∞. Why am I doing this? The answer should reignite the fire.

## The Closing Thought

> "We are just starting to understand how games can change us. It is imperative that we learn more about how they do, because the more we learn, the more we can use them not just as an amusement, but as a valuable tool for improving the human condition."

When you think about it: is there any human activity that cannot be viewed as a game, and therefore benefit from the principles of good game design? The medium you work in is not a niche. It is becoming the primary lens through which the next generation will understand the world.

Find your purpose. Wear the ring. Design with intention.
