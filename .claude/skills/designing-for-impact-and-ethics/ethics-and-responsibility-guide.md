# Ethics and Responsibility Guide

## The Core Truth About Responsibility

Corporations have no souls. They have bank accounts and legal responsibility, but no ethical responsibility. Their sole purpose is to generate money. Only **individuals** can take ethical responsibility for what they create.

No one is going to ask you to take responsibility for what you design. The obscurity of game design makes this easy to forget. But if you aren't willing to take personal responsibility for the games you make, you shouldn't be making them.

---

## The Danger of Obscurity

Game designers work in relative anonymity:
- Publishers prefer designers stay unknown (fame makes them expensive)
- The public doesn't care who made the games they love
- Screenwriters face the same problem — no one asks "who wrote that?"

**Why this is dangerous:** Because no one is watching, no one is going to hold you accountable. The temptation is to assume someone else — the publisher, the lawyers, the platform — will catch problems before they ship.

**The reality:** Corporations make mistakes all the time. Corporate managers probably won't take on personal ethical responsibility. There is only one person who can take responsibility for what you create: **you**.

## Being Accountable: Online Safety

Of all dangers games might contain, the most real and undeniable for online games is **the potential for players to meet dangerous strangers.**

### The Severity

When people think about making online games "safe," they usually focus on foul language. But foul language has nothing to do with safety. The real danger: online games can be a **mask of anonymity** that dangerous people use to take advantage of innocents.

This is one of the rare cases where game design choices could **cause lives to be saved or lost.**

### The Math

If there is a 1-in-a-million chance of something dangerous happening in your game, and your game is successful enough that 5 million people play it — that dangerous thing will happen **five times.**

Do not dismiss low-probability scenarios when the stakes are this high.

### The Time Magazine Cover Technique

One team felt so strongly about online safety that they asked their concept artist to create an image of what the cover of *Time* magazine would look like the week a child was abducted due to lack of communication safeguards in their game.

They never showed it outside the team. But every team member burned this image into their minds as a permanent reminder of the responsibility on their shoulders.

**How to use this technique:**
1. Identify the worst realistic outcome your game could enable
2. Visualize it vividly and specifically — not abstractly
3. Make the image concrete enough that it stays with you
4. Let it guide every safety-related design decision

### Accountability Checklist for Online Games

- [ ] Have I identified every communication channel between strangers?
- [ ] Have I imagined the worst-case scenario explicitly?
- [ ] Have I designed safeguards that go beyond what lawyers require?
- [ ] Am I personally satisfied with the safety measures — not just legally covered?
- [ ] Would I be comfortable if my name were publicly associated with any incident?

## Your Hidden Agenda

### The Question Nobody Asks

If it is possible to find a way for your game to do good — to make people's lives better — and you know this is possible but choose not to do it, isn't that just as bad as making a game that harms people?

### The Practical Reality

Management won't care about your goal to improve mankind. Their job is to serve the corporation, and the corporation only cares about making money. If you tell them your priority is using games to help people, they will think your priorities are out of whack.

**So don't tell them.**

### The Secret Strategy

Design your games so they improve people's lives — but do it quietly:

1. **Your game must first be commercially successful.** A game that is good for people but no one likes (the "broccoli smoothie") helps nobody. The only way your games can serve humanity is if as many people play them as possible.

2. **Find what you can embed invisibly.** Look for small design choices that nudge players toward better habits, more empathy, or deeper connection — without compromising the entertainment value.

3. **People like feeling cared for.** If you manage, through your game, to make players into better people, they will feel, appreciate, and remember that rare feeling that someone else cares what they become.

### Real-World Example: Toontown Online

The *Toontown Online* team designed a chat system where players could only communicate by picking phrases off a menu — mostly supportive and encouraging ("Thanks!" "Good job!"). This was meant as an aesthetic choice for the game's tone.

**The unintended result:** An adult MMO player who also played *Toontown* on the side found that his trash-talking habits from other MMOs gradually disappeared. He became more inclined to thank people who helped him. He was embarrassed — but grudgingly grateful — that a simple children's game had changed his communication patterns.

**The lesson:** Games change players through their systems and social structures, often in ways designers never anticipated. Understanding this power and using it intentionally is the hidden agenda at its best.

## The Secret Hidden in Plain Sight

Games are not trivial amusements. They are:
- A means of creating experiences
- Life itself is composed of nothing but experiences
- The experiences game designers create are ones where people **live out their fantasies and strive to become what they have always secretly wished to be**

### The Scale of Impact

- Fantasy worlds created for children become **modern mythology** — shared story worlds that stay with them as a guiding compass for life
- Games create **utopias**: ideal societies to which all nations are compared
- The medium a person is immersed in when young **defines how they will think for their entire lives**

### The Generational Responsibility

As you continue to invent and improve the medium of games, you are defining the thought process of the next generation. This is no trivial matter.

It is not enough to think of how games affect people today — we must consider how they will affect people tomorrow. You are working in, and inventing, **the medium that will subsume all others.**

## The Ring: Your Obligation

### The Engineer's Ritual

In 1922, Rudyard Kipling created a ritual for graduating engineers at the University of Toronto — a solemn ceremony reminding them of their obligation to help society. At its conclusion, each engineer receives an **iron ring** placed on the pinky finger of their dominant hand. A lifelong, physical reminder of obligation. Still practiced today.

### Your Invisible Ring

Game designers don't have this ritual yet. But you don't need to wait for one.

**Your obligation begins now — this minute.**

If you believe that games can help people, put on an invisible ring. Wear it as a reminder to let these responsibilities guide your hand. It doesn't come off.

The ring bears this inscription — Lens #111: The Lens of Responsibility:

> **Does my game help people? How?**

### What the Ring Means in Practice

Every design decision you make, ask yourself:
- Am I being accountable for what this creates?
- Have I considered who this might harm?
- Have I looked for ways this might help?
- Am I willing to put my name on the consequences?

The pinky finger guides the hand — feeling things out like a little antenna, safely guiding everything into position. Let responsibility be your pinky: always there first, quietly ensuring everything lands safely.
