# Designing for Impact and Ethics

Games are not trivial amusements — they are experiences that change people. This skill covers how to harness that power responsibly: creating games that genuinely transform players for the better, understanding the ethical obligations every designer carries, and finding your own purpose as a designer. Based on Jesse Schell's *The Art of Game Design* (Chapters 33–35).

## When to Use This Skill

- Designing a game intended to educate, heal, or change player behavior
- Evaluating whether a game could cause harm (violence, addiction, safety risks)
- Making design decisions that affect vulnerable populations (children, youth)
- Reflecting on your personal purpose and motivation as a designer
- Assessing whether a transformational game is actually working
- Deciding what "good" looks like for your game and its players
- Navigating the tension between commercial success and positive impact

---

## Core Framework: Three Layers of Designer Responsibility

Every game designer operates within three nested layers of responsibility:

<responsibility_layers>
1. TRANSFORMATION  → What change does your game create in players? (Ch 33)
2. ETHICS          → Are you accountable for the consequences?    (Ch 34)
3. PURPOSE         → Why are you designing at all?                 (Ch 35)
</responsibility_layers>

**The key insight:** Purpose powers everything. If you don't know your own purpose, your games lack the passion and focus that make them truly great — and truly meaningful.

## Workflow 1: Creating a Transformational Game

Use this when your game has an explicit goal of changing players — education, health, behavior change, empathy, skill-building.

### Step 1: Define the Transformation Clearly (Tip #1)

Do NOT state vague goals. Be specific about what changes and how.

**Bad:** "My game teaches math"
**Good:** "My game changes players by introducing them to factoring and builds familiarity by giving practice at breaking down composite numbers into base factors"

**Bad:** "My game gets people to exercise"
**Good:** "My game helps players develop a daily exercise habit by giving them small, achievable daily challenges"

This is Lens #14 (Problem Statement) applied to transformation. You cannot solve what you haven't clearly defined.

### Step 2: Find Subject Matter Experts (Tip #2)

Two kinds of experts are both essential:
1. **Content experts** — People who know every detail about the subject matter
2. **Teaching experts** — People who know the best ways to teach that subject

Find both. They are usually eager to collaborate. Listen to them about what is and isn't important.

### Step 3: Design as a Tool for Instructors (Tip #3)

**Default assumption:** Your game will be used alongside a human instructor, not as a replacement.

An instructor has a lifetime of experience. Your game cannot replace that. But it CAN magnify their abilities.

**Design the instructor as a "dungeon master"** who guides students through scenarios your game creates. This earns their gratitude and dramatically increases effectiveness.

**Exception:** When instructors are genuinely unavailable (self-paced, remote access), design for standalone — but acknowledge this is harder and less effective.

### Step 4: Do One Thing Exceptionally Well (Tip #4)

Pick **one key transformation** and execute it brilliantly. Do not try to replace an entire curriculum.

**Evidence:** The most effective transformational games each target exactly one type of transformation:
- *Peacemaker* — understanding geopolitical conflict
- *Dragon Box Plus* — algebra concepts
- *Zombie Division* — division skills
- *Papers Please* — moral decision-making under pressure

If it works, people will ask for more. Then plan the next step.

### Step 5: Choose the Right Venue (Tip #6)

Ask before designing:
- Where will players be when they play? (classroom, commute, home)
- Will they be alone or with others?
- Will they have unlimited time or a tight window?
- Will an instructor be present?
- What devices will be available?

The venue fundamentally shapes whether the transformation succeeds.

### Step 6: Assess Whether It's Working (Tip #5)

Align your team on the level of rigor needed. Five assessment levels (ascending):

| Level | Method | Strength |
|-------|--------|----------|
| 1 | Feels like it | Subjective impression only |
| 2 | Anecdotes | Inspiring stories, not proof |
| 3 | SME approval | Expert endorsement — strong indicator |
| 4 | Informal surveys | In-game or post-play tests measuring change |
| 5 | Scientific testing | Formal statistical analysis — ideal but expensive |

### Step 7: Accept Market Realities (Tip #7)

A transformational game that no one plays changes no one. Business savvy is just as important as good design when the goal is real-world impact.

See [transformational-games-guide.md](transformational-games-guide.md) for full detail on all seven tips, the four categories of games for good, and the education deep dive.

## Workflow 2: Ethical Self-Audit

Use this on any game — not just transformational ones. Every game changes players in some way.

### Step 1: Apply Lens #110 (Transformation)

Ask honestly:
- How might this game change players for the better?
- How might this game change players for the worse?

If you can answer "better" but not "worse," you haven't thought hard enough.

### Step 2: Assess Online Safety (if applicable)

If your game involves communication between strangers, this is **life-or-death design work.**

**The math:** If there is a 1-in-a-million chance of something dangerous happening, and 5 million people play your game, it WILL happen — five times.

**Design checklist:**
- [ ] Can dangerous strangers reach vulnerable players?
- [ ] What communication channels exist between unknown users?
- [ ] Are there safeguards that prevent dangerous contact?
- [ ] Have you imagined the worst-case scenario explicitly? (Time Magazine Cover technique — see [ethics-and-responsibility-guide.md](ethics-and-responsibility-guide.md))

### Step 3: Evaluate Violence Content

| Factor | Lower Risk | Higher Risk |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| Realism | Abstract (chess captures) | Graphic / realistic |
| Blood | No blood (*Fortnite*) | Graphic blood (*Mortal Kombat*) |
| Real-world proximity | Fantastical (pirates, aliens) | Realistic everyday scenarios |
| Reward structure | Violence as obstacle | Violence as the fun itself |

**Principle:** Adults compartmentalize well. Children are still forming their worldview — rating systems exist for this reason. See [designer-purpose-guide.md](designer-purpose-guide.md) for full violence and addiction analysis.

### Step 4: Evaluate Addiction Risk

- Does your game create compulsive behavior that damages real life?
- Have you considered that different players are vulnerable for different reasons? Some are pushed in by real-life problems; some are pulled in by the game.
- **Your obligation:** Find ways to make game structures fit into a well-balanced life. This is YOUR responsibility — not the publisher's, not the platform's.

### Step 5: Look for Hidden Good

Can you embed something genuinely good into a commercially successful game without announcing it? People like nothing more than feeling cared for. A game that quietly makes players better will be felt, appreciated, and remembered.

See [ethics-and-responsibility-guide.md](ethics-and-responsibility-guide.md) for the full hidden agenda framework.

## Workflow 3: Finding Your Designer Purpose

Use this when you feel disconnected from your work, or want to deepen the meaning behind what you create.

### Step 1: Listen to Yourself

The fifth kind of listening (from establishing-design-foundations) is the most important and the hardest. Your subconscious holds the answer to why you design.

**Questions to sit with:**
- Why is game design important to YOU specifically?
- What do you see in your mind's eye that you want to share with the world?
- Was there an experience that changed your life? Do you want others to have it?
- Is there something terrible you want to prevent from happening again?

See [designer-purpose-guide.md](designer-purpose-guide.md) for structured reflection exercises.

### Step 2: Align Conscious and Subconscious

Once you identify your purpose:
- Your work gains **passion, focus, and intensity** that cannot be manufactured
- Conscious and subconscious motivations unite
- Every design decision has a clear north star

### Step 3: Apply Lens #112 (The Raven)

Before committing to any project, ask: **"Is making this game worth my time?"**

Life is short. Work on what matters — now.

## Common Patterns and Mistakes

### Mistake: Vague Transformation Goals
**Problem:** "My game teaches science" tells you nothing about what change will occur.
**Fix:** State the exact cognitive or behavioral change, and the specific mechanism your game uses to produce it.

### Mistake: Trying to Replace the Instructor
**Problem:** Designer assumes their game can fully replace a human teacher.
**Fix:** Design as a tool that amplifies the instructor. Make them a dungeon master.

### Mistake: Ignoring Online Safety
**Problem:** "The lawyers will handle it" or "It's probably fine."
**Fix:** Take personal responsibility. Imagine the worst case explicitly. Design safeguards.

### Mistake: Assuming Violence Is the Only Risk
**Problem:** Focusing on violent content while ignoring subtler forms of harm.
**Why it matters:** *Toontown Online*'s polite chat system changed an adult MMO player's communication habits without anyone noticing during design. Games change players through mechanics, social systems, and communication patterns — not just through violent content.
**Fix:** Apply Lens #110 holistically across all systems.

### Pattern: The Secret Good
**Opportunity:** Embed positive transformation into a commercially successful game without announcing it.
**Why it works:** "One thing people like better than almost anything else is being cared for." A game that makes players better people will be felt and remembered.
**How:** Don't tell management. Design the good in quietly. If the game succeeds AND helps people, you've achieved the best possible outcome.

## Tips for Success

1. **Define transformation before designing anything** — You can't hit a target you haven't named
2. **Find both kinds of experts** — Content knowledge AND teaching knowledge
3. **Design for the instructor, not against them** — Amplify human wisdom, don't replace it
4. **One transformation, done brilliantly** — Breadth without depth helps no one
5. **Imagine the worst case explicitly** — Write it down. Burn the image in your mind.
6. **Violence isn't the only way games change people** — Communication patterns and habit loops matter too
7. **Your hidden agenda is your superpower** — Quietly embed good into great games
8. **Purpose unites conscious and subconscious work** — Find yours as soon as possible
9. **Business savvy serves impact** — A game no one plays changes nothing
10. **Ask "why am I doing this?" regularly** — Lens #∞ keeps you pointed at what matters
