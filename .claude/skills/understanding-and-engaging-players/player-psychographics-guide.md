# Player Psychographics Guide

## What Are Psychographics?

**Psychographics** focus on internal factors: what people enjoy, value, and seek. Unlike demographics (external traits like age and gender), psychographics reveal the pleasures and motivations that drive player behavior.

## Why Psychographics Matter

Ultimately, all human action traces back to some kind of pleasure seeking. Understanding the specific pleasures your players seek allows you to design experiences that deeply satisfy them.

**Key insight**: People have pleasure preferences. No one seeks only one type, but individuals have strong patterns in what they find most rewarding.

## LeBlanc's Taxonomy of Game Pleasures

Game designer Marc LeBlanc identified eight primary "game pleasures":

### 1. Sensation

**Definition**: Pleasures of using your senses—seeing beauty, hearing music, touching textures, tasting, smelling.

**In games**: Primarily delivered through aesthetics
- Beautiful visuals
- Great music and sound design
- Tactile feedback (controller rumble, touch)
- Satisfying audio cues

**Example**: Greg Costikyan's experience with *Axis & Allies*
- First version: Garish board, ugly cardboard counters → played once, put away
- Milton Bradley version: Elegant board, hundreds of plastic military pieces → played many times
- **Same game, different sensory pleasure**

**Key point**: Sensory pleasure is often the pleasure of the toy. Cannot make a bad game good, but can make a good game better.

**Who seeks this**: Players who appreciate art, music, production values, and "game feel"

### 2. Fantasy

**Definition**: The pleasure of inhabiting an imaginary world and imagining yourself as something you are not.

**In games**:
- Role-playing different identities
- Exploring impossible worlds
- Being powerful, heroic, or villainous
- Escapism from everyday life

**Related chapters**: Discussed in detail in Chapters 19 (Worlds) and 20 (Characters)

**Who seeks this**: Players who enjoy imagination, role-play, escapism, and alternate identities

### 3. Narrative

**Definition**: The pleasure of a dramatic unfolding of events—not necessarily linear story, but the emergence of a sequence of meaningful events.

**In games**:
- The story that emerges from play
- Dramatic tension and resolution
- Character arcs and development
- Memorable moments and "water cooler" stories

**Important distinction**: LeBlanc means emergent narrative from gameplay, not just prescribed story

**Related chapters**: Discussed in Chapters 16 (Interest Curves) and 17 (Story)

**Who seeks this**: Players who want their actions to create memorable stories

### 4. Challenge

**Definition**: The pleasure of problem-solving and overcoming obstacles.

**In games**:
- Puzzles to solve
- Enemies to defeat
- Skills to master
- Obstacles to overcome

**Key insight**: Challenge is a core pleasure of gameplay—every game has a problem to solve at its heart.

**Who seeks this**: Players for whom the core problem-solving loop is enough motivation

### 5. Fellowship

**Definition**: Everything enjoyable about friendship, cooperation, and community.

**In games**:
- Playing with friends
- Cooperative gameplay
- Community belonging
- Shared experiences
- Social bonding

**Importance**: For some players, this is the main attraction—the game is secondary to the social experience.

**Related chapters**: Discussed in Chapters 24 (Multiplayer) and 25 (Communities)

**Who seeks this**: Social players who value relationships and belonging

### 6. Discovery

**Definition**: The pleasure of seeking and finding something new.

**In games**:
- Exploring game worlds
- Uncovering secrets
- Finding clever strategies
- Discovering hidden features
- "Aha!" moments

**Breadth**: Very broad pleasure—encompasses physical exploration, intellectual discovery, strategic insight

**Who seeks this**: Curious players who love exploring and uncovering

### 7. Expression

**Definition**: The pleasure of creating things and expressing yourself.

**In games**:
- Character customization
- Level creation
- Building and crafting
- Sharing creations
- Personal style and flair

**Historical note**: Often neglected in past game design, now central to many games

**Key insight**: Expression often doesn't advance game goals—designing outfits doesn't help you win—but for some players, it's the primary reason to play

**Who seeks this**: Creative players who value self-expression and building

### 8. Submission

**Definition**: The pleasure of entering the magic circle—leaving the real world behind and entering a new set of rules and meaning.

**In games**:
- Becoming absorbed in the game world
- Suspending disbelief effortlessly
- Mind easily entering and staying in game world
- Losing awareness of real-world concerns

**Important**: All games involve submission, but some make it much easier and more pleasant than others

**Quality spectrum**:
- **Poor**: Forced to suspend disbelief, constantly reminded of reality
- **Great**: Disbelief suspends effortlessly, complete mental immersion

**Who seeks this**: Players seeking escape, flow, and immersion

## Bartle's Taxonomy of Player Types

Richard Bartle, who designed MUDs and online games, observed players fall into four main groups based on pleasure preferences. They use playing card suits as mnemonics:

### ♦ Achievers (Diamonds)

**Primary pleasure**: Challenge
**What they want**: To achieve the goals of the game
**Motivation**: Completing objectives, earning achievements, "beating" the game

**In games**:
- Focus on goals and completion
- Track achievements and milestones
- Value progression systems
- Want clear success criteria

**Design for Achievers**:
- Clear, measurable goals
- Achievement systems
- Progression tracking
- Difficulty tiers
- Completion percentages

### ♠ Explorers (Spades)

**Primary pleasure**: Discovery
**What they want**: To know the breadth of the game
**Motivation**: Uncovering everything the game has to offer

**In games**:
- Explore every corner of the world
- Discover secret areas
- Learn hidden mechanics
- Map the entire possibility space

**Design for Explorers**:
- Hidden areas and secrets
- Discoverable lore
- Complex systems to understand
- Easter eggs
- Comprehensive world design

### ♥ Socializers (Hearts)

**Primary pleasure**: Fellowship
**What they want**: Relationships with other people
**Motivation**: Social connections and community

**In games**:
- Chat and communicate constantly
- Form friendships and alliances
- Value shared experiences
- The game is a venue for socializing

**Design for Socializers**:
- Communication tools
- Cooperative gameplay
- Social spaces (hubs, taverns)
- Group activities
- Friend/guild systems

### ♣ Killers (Clubs)

**Primary pleasure**: Mix of competition and destruction (doesn't map cleanly to LeBlanc)
**What they want**: To compete with and defeat others
**Motivation**: Imposing themselves on others (both negatively and positively)

**In games**:
- Dominate other players
- PvP combat
- Rankings and leaderboards
- Also includes helping others (imposing benevolently)

**Note**: "Killers" includes both aggressive competitors and those who strongly help others—both are "acting on" other players

**Design for Killers**:
- PvP systems
- Competitive rankings
- Tools to affect other players
- Mentoring/helping mechanics

## Bartle's Graph: A Spatial Model

Bartle proposes a 2-axis model:

```
           Acting
             |
        ♣ Killers  ---  ♦ Achievers
             |              |
        (on Players)    (on World)
             |              |
      ♥ Socializers --- ♠ Explorers
             |
        Interacting
```

**Vertical axis**: Acting (top) vs Interacting (bottom)
**Horizontal axis**: Players (left) vs World (right)

- **Achievers**: Act on the world
- **Explorers**: Interact with the world
- **Socializers**: Interact with players
- **Killers**: Act on players

## Additional Pleasures

LeBlanc's and Bartle's taxonomies are useful but incomplete. Consider these additional pleasures:

### Anticipation
**Definition**: Waiting for a pleasure is itself pleasurable
**In games**: Waiting for expansions, countdown timers, teasing future content
**Design use**: Build excitement before releases, tease upcoming features

### Completion
**Definition**: The satisfaction of finishing something
**In games**:
- "Collect all the treasures"
- "Destroy all the bad guys"
- "Clear the level"
- 100% completion tracking

**Design use**: Progress bars, checklists, completion percentages

### Schadenfreude
**Definition**: Delight in another's misfortune (German: "damage-joy")
**When it occurs**: When an unjust person gets their comeuppance
**In games**: Seeing opponents fail, especially if they were cocky or unsportsmanlike
**Importance**: Crucial aspect of competitive games

### Gift Giving
**Definition**: The pleasure of making someone else happy through a surprise gift
**In games**:
- Sending gifts to friends
- Trading valuable items
- Surprising teammates with help
- The pleasure is that YOU made them happy

**Design use**: Gift systems, trading, surprising bonuses for teammates

### Humor
**Definition**: Two unconnected things unite through paradigm shift, causing laughter
**In games**: Funny characters, situations, dialogue, physics glitches, unexpected outcomes
**Design use**: Comedy timing, absurd situations, self-aware humor

### Possibility
**Definition**: The pleasure of having many choices and knowing you can pick any one
**In games**: Character creation screens, skill trees, multiple paths, shopping
**Design use**: Show options, "paradox of choice" can enhance or overwhelm

### Pride in Accomplishment (Naches)
**Definition**: Pleased satisfaction in an achievement, especially in others you care about (Yiddish)
**In games**: Completing difficult challenges, watching students succeed
**Duration**: Can persist long after the accomplishment
**Design use**: Difficult achievements, mentoring systems, spectator modes

### Surprise
**Definition**: The brain likes surprises—unexpected events capture attention
**In games**: Plot twists, unexpected mechanics, random events
**Design use**: Lens #4 Surprise—keep players on their toes

### Thrill
**Definition**: "Fear minus death equals fun" (roller coaster designer saying)
**In games**: Horror games, high-stakes moments, close calls
**Requirement**: Must feel secure in ultimate safety
**Design use**: Controlled danger, narrow escapes, tension

### Triumph Over Adversity (Fiero)
**Definition**: Accomplishing something you knew was a long shot (Italian: "fiero")
**Physical expression**: Shouts of personal triumph, arms raised
**In games**: Beating hard bosses, clutch victories, overcoming odds
**Design use**: Difficult challenges with dramatic payoff

### Wonder
**Definition**: Overwhelming awe and amazement
**Result**: Leads to curiosity ("wonder" double meaning)
**In games**: Epic vistas, impossible architecture, cosmic scale
**Design use**: Spectacle moments, reveal of scale, beauty

## Using Pleasure Taxonomies

### Step 1: Audit Your Game's Pleasures

Go through each pleasure and ask:
1. Does my game deliver this pleasure?
2. How well does it deliver it?
3. Could it deliver it better?
4. Is this pleasure missing? Should it be added?

### Step 2: Identify Your Core Pleasures

What 2-3 pleasures are central to your game?
- These should be your design pillars
- Everything should support these core pleasures
- Don't dilute focus by trying to deliver everything

### Step 3: Map Players to Pleasures

Different player types value different pleasures:
- **Achievers** → Challenge, Completion
- **Explorers** → Discovery, Wonder
- **Socializers** → Fellowship, Gift Giving
- **Killers** → Competition, Schadenfreude, Triumph

Know your target audience's pleasure preferences.

### Step 4: Look for Unique Pleasures

The most memorable games often deliver a unique pleasure not found elsewhere:
- What can your game do that others can't?
- Is there an unclassified pleasure your game delivers?
- Novel pleasure combinations can create unique experiences

## Context Sensitivity

**Critical insight**: Pleasure is context-dependent.

**Example**: Dancing
- At a party: Pleasurable
- At a job interview: Torturous

**Application**: The same mechanic can be pleasurable or painful depending on:
- When it happens
- Why the player is doing it
- What else is happening
- Player's current state of mind

## Lifestyle Psychographics

Beyond pleasure types, consider lifestyle choices:
- **Dog lover**: Values nurturing, companionship
- **Baseball fan**: Understands statistics, strategy, team loyalty
- **Hardcore FPS player**: Values twitch skill, spatial reasoning, competition

**Design implications**: If creating a game about dogs, baseball, or FPS, study that lifestyle group's preferences deeply.

## Practical Application

### For Broad Audiences
Deliver multiple pleasures to appeal to multiple player types:
- **Minecraft**: Survival (Challenge), Building (Expression), Exploring (Discovery), Multiplayer (Fellowship)
- **MMORPGs**: Questing (Challenge), Exploration (Discovery), Raiding (Fellowship), PvP (Competition)

### For Niche Audiences
Double down on specific pleasures:
- **Dark Souls**: Challenge, Triumph, Discovery
- **The Sims**: Expression, Narrative, Nurturing
- **Stardew Valley**: Completion, Nurturing, Expression

### Watch for Conflicts
Some pleasures can conflict:
- **Challenge** vs **Submission**: Frustration breaks immersion
- **Competition** vs **Fellowship**: Conflict can damage friendships
- **Discovery** vs **Efficiency**: Exploration slows achievement

Balance these tensions based on your priorities.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Pleasure is the ultimate goal**: Games exist to deliver pleasure
2. **Pleasures are diverse**: Don't assume everyone seeks the same pleasures
3. **Know your priorities**: Focus on 2-3 core pleasures
4. **Player types exist**: Achievers, Explorers, Socializers, Killers have different needs
5. **Context matters**: Same action can be pleasurable or painful in different contexts
6. **Look beyond lists**: Unique pleasures not on standard lists can make your game special
7. **Taxonomies are incomplete**: Use them as starting points, not comprehensive guides

## Remember

> "Ultimately, the job of a game is to give pleasure. By going through lists of known pleasures and considering how well your game delivers each one, you may be inspired to make changes that increase your players' enjoyment. Always be on the lookout for unique, unclassified pleasures not found in most games—for one of these might be what gives your game the unique quality it needs."

Apply **Lens #20: Pleasure** regularly:
- What pleasures does your game give to players? Can these be improved?
- What pleasures are missing from your experience? Why? Can they be added?
