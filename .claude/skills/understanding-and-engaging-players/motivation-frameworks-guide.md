# Motivation Frameworks Guide

## The Fundamental Truth

> **Games are not important.**

In the larger scheme of life, there's always something more important we could be doing. This is true for all entertainment.

**The goal of entertainment**: Take something unimportant (ball through hoop, imaginary story, card values) and make it feel vitally important.

**The magic of motivation**: During play, we care about trivial experiences as if they were matters of life and death. We're always aware "it's just a game," but something compels us to care deeply.

## The Challenge for Designers

Most designers create motivational systems through:
- Gut instinct
- Experimentation
- Occasionally stumbling into success

**Not** through masterful psychological understanding (though that helps!)

**This guide**: Collect useful insights about motivation to improve your instincts and experiments.

## Framework 1: Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

### The Pyramid (1943)

Abraham Maslow proposed a hierarchy of human needs:

```
        5. Self-Actualization
       (creativity, purpose, potential)

         4. Esteem Needs
      (achievement, recognition, mastery)

          3. Love/Belonging
       (relationships, friends, community)

           2. Safety Needs
         (security, stability, health)

     1. Physiological Needs
    (food, water, shelter, survival)
```

### The Core Principle

**People don't pursue higher needs until lower needs are satisfied**

**Examples**:
- Starving person: Food priority over safety
- Unsafe person: Safety priority over relationships
- Lonely person: Belonging priority over self-esteem
- Low self-esteem: Can't pursue talents and purpose

### Exceptions Exist

You can find exceptions, but overall the model is a very useful tool.

### Application to Games

**Map game activities to the hierarchy:**

Most game activities: **Level 4 (Esteem)** - Achievement and mastery

But some are lower:
- **Level 2 (Safety)**: Survival games, horror, "trying not to die"
- **Level 1 (Physiological)**: Rarely directly, but through fantasy (gathering food, building shelter)

Some are higher:
- **Level 5 (Self-Actualization)**: Creative games, self-expression, "finding yourself"

**Level 3 (Love/Belonging)** is crucial:
- **Why multiplayer games are so powerful**: They fulfill more basic needs than single-player
- **Social features have staying power**: More fundamental needs = stronger motivation

### Minecraft as Complete Pyramid Coverage

**Level 1-2 (Physiological/Safety)**: Fantasy context—gather resources, build shelter, survive
**Level 3 (Love/Belonging)**: Multiplayer—play with friends, build together
**Level 4 (Esteem)**: Mastery of systems, achievements
**Level 5 (Self-Actualization)**: Creativity, building anything you can imagine

**Result**: Tremendous appeal and staying power

### Levels Feed Into Each Other

Games that combine:
- Connect with other people (Level 3)
- Sense of accomplishment (Level 4)
- Build and create things (Level 5)
- Let you express yourself (Level 5)

**Result**: Fulfill three levels simultaneously = very powerful

**Example**: MMOs with communities and content creation tools

### Using Lens #22: Needs

Ask yourself:
- On which levels of Maslow's hierarchy is my game operating?
- Does it fill the needs of competence, autonomy, and relatedness?
- How can my game fill more basic needs than it already does?
- For needs my game fills, how can it fill those needs even better?

**Insight**: Games that fulfill needs better than others will win players

## Framework 2: Self-Determination Theory

### Modern Psychology: Mental Needs

Developed by Edward Deci and Richard Ryan

**Core premise**: Just as humans have physical needs, we have mental needs—actual needs, not just wants or desires.

**When mental needs are unmet**: We become mentally unhealthy

### The Three Mental Needs

#### 1. Competence
**Definition**: I need to feel good at something
**In games**: Mastery, skill progression, "getting better"
**Why games excel**: Designed specifically to make you feel mastery

#### 2. Autonomy
**Definition**: I need freedom to do things my own way
**In games**: Choice, different playstyles, freedom to explore
**Why games excel**:
- Give you freedom to play as you want
- Ultimate freedom: Can stop playing whenever you like (it's just a game)

#### 3. Relatedness
**Definition**: I need to connect with other people
**In games**: Multiplayer, communities, shared experiences
**Why games excel**: Most games designed for social connection

### Why Games Are So Appealing

Games fulfill all three mental needs:
- **Competence**: Designed for mastery
- **Autonomy**: Freedom of play
- **Relatedness**: Social connections

**Evidence**: Great body of research backs up these three needs

## Framework 3: Intrinsic vs Extrinsic Motivation

### Simple Definitions

**Intrinsic**: I feel like doing it (internal motivation)
**Extrinsic**: Someone pays me to do it (external motivation)

### The Complexity in Games

**Example**: Playing Pac-Man
- **Intrinsic**: Visceral thrill of racing through maze
- **Extrinsic**: Game gives me points
- **But what if**: I'm motivated by excitement of high score?
  - Is that intrinsic or extrinsic or both?

**Example**: Mountain Dew promotional game
- **Clearly extrinsic**: Points and prizes for drinking soda
- **But**: Becomes social game with friends, intrinsically fun experience
- **Result**: One-upping each other for points becomes intrinsically enjoyable

### The Continuum

Not binary (internal/external) but a gradient:

```
External ←―――――――――――――――→ Internal
(Pure external         (Pure internal
 reward)               motivation from
                       "true self")
```

**Key idea**: The more motivation comes from "your true self," the more internal it is

**Designer insight**: Different motivations in your game fall at different points on this continuum

### The Surprising Interaction: The Drawing Study

**Experiment setup**:
- Group 1: Children paid for each picture drawn
- Group 2: Children not paid

**Results during activity**:
- Paid group: Drew MORE pictures
- Paid group: LOWER quality (less interesting, less thoughtful)

**Results after activity** (researchers left room):
- Unpaid children: Kept drawing
- Paid children: Put crayons down, just waited

**Shocking conclusion**: Adding extrinsic motivation to intrinsically motivating activity slides it toward external, **draining intrinsic motivation**

### Implications for "Gamification"

**Common belief**: Add points, badges, rewards = more motivation

**Reality**: Can actually reduce intrinsic motivation if activity was already intrinsically motivating

**Design carefully**: Extrinsic rewards aren't always additive

### Motivations Can Grow on Each Other

**Key insight**: "One motivation can grow on another, like a vine growing on a trellis"

Savvy designers know extrinsic can support intrinsic (and vice versa) when used carefully

## Framework 4: Wanna vs Hafta

### Two Different Brain Systems

Neuroscience insight: Pleasure-seeking and pain-avoiding are **two different systems**, not a continuum from pain to pleasure.

**Common mistake**: Lump both into "motivation" and think no more about it

**Reality**: Separating them reveals important insights

### The Big Red Button Thought Experiment

**Product 1: Tax Preparation Software**
- Push button → Taxes instantly prepared, maximum refund
- **Reaction**: Amazing! Best product ever!

**Product 2: Angry Birds Game**
- Push button → Instantly win
- **Reaction**: Worst game ever

**What makes them different?**
- **Taxes**: "Hafta" (pain avoidance) - done to avoid penalties/jail
- **Games**: "Wanna" (pleasure seeking) - done because I enjoy it

### Wanna vs Hafta Defined

**Wanna (Pleasure-Seeking)**:
- Motivated by enjoyment
- No penalty for not doing it
- Pure desire to experience it

**Hafta (Pain-Avoidance)**:
- Motivated by avoiding negative consequences
- Suffer if you don't do it
- Not inherently enjoyable

### The Attitude Shift

**Critical insight**: Same activity can be wanna or hafta depending on attitude

**Example**: Friend who loves tax preparation
- For him: Taxes are "wanna"
- For him: Videogames are "hafta" (boring)

**Implication**: Nothing inherent in the activity itself—it's about mindset

### Both Exist in Games

Not all gameplay motivation is pleasure-seeking:
- **Pain avoidance**: Avoiding enemies, "trying not to die"
- **Pleasure seeking**: Collecting rewards, scoring combos

**Can work well in combination**, but balance matters

### When the Balance Breaks: Free-to-Play Example

**Early game** (Wanna):
- Big rewards
- Unexpected bonuses
- Exciting animations
- Pure pleasure-seeking

**Over time** (Hafta):
- Come back by certain time or lose points
- Invite friends or miss prizes
- Obligations build up
- Slide from pleasure-seeking to pain-avoidance

**Result**: Keep players coming back, but feel worse about it

**Consequence**: As designer Sheri Graner Ray says, "People don't just stop playing these games, they divorce them"

### World of Warcraft Guild Example

**Beginning** (Wanna):
- So many fun things to do
- Join guild for friendship and camaraderie
- Team play is enjoyable

**Guild pressure** (Hafta):
- Leaders pressure members to play more
- Avoid shame from guild
- Keep showing up out of obligation
- Playing becomes something you "hafta" do

### The Motivation Matrix

Map each motivation in your game on two axes:

```
        Wanna (Pleasure-seeking)
                |
                |
External ――――――――――――― Internal
                |
                |
        Hafta (Pain-avoiding)
```

**Reveals complexity of motivation**: Four quadrants, each with different character

**Example quadrants**:
- **External + Hafta**: Avoid penalty from system (least engaging)
- **External + Wanna**: Earn rewards from system
- **Internal + Hafta**: Avoid personal shame or guilt
- **Internal + Wanna**: Pure joy of activity (most engaging)

## Framework 5: Novelty

### The Unbeatable Power of New

> "Unquestionably, the popular thing in this world is novelty." — Mark Twain

**Reality**: If quality was primary concern, bookstores would be full of time-tested classics

**Actual reality**: Classics get dusty back shelf, new books dominate

**Even truer for games**: Conversation dominated by what's new and what's coming next

### Novelty's Dual Role in Games

#### 1. Novelty as Purchase Motivation

**Thirst for novelty** drives game purchases:
- Games costing hundreds of dollars today → pennies on eBay tomorrow
- Constant desire for the next new thing
- Older games feel "outdated" regardless of quality

#### 2. Novelty as Play Motivation

**Believing something new is on the next level** is powerful incentive to finish current level

### The Most Powerful Novelty: New Ways of Thinking

**Example**: Portal
- Mechanic: Shoot connected "holes" into surfaces
- Slogan: "Now you're thinking with portals" (very appropriate)
- Makes you think about the world in a completely new way
- Same old world, but new interaction method
- Never occurred to you because seemed impossible
- Suddenly possible AND urgent

**Reward**: Novel ways of thinking stretch our brains in rewarding ways

### The Danger of Too Much Novelty

**Problem 1**: Being ahead of your time
- Many fascinating games failed by being too novel
- Audiences weren't ready

**Problem 2**: Novelty without substance
- Game may have novelty but no other qualities
- Initial excitement drives early sales
- Players vanish when novelty wears off

### The Success Formula

**Successful games** = Mix of novel and familiar
- Novelty attracts and excites
- Familiarity provides comfort and accessibility
- Solid gameplay underneath ensures retention

### Don't Despair at Society's Novelty Obsession

**Why we seek novelty**: It's how humans explore what's possible and quest for a better world

**Designer approach**: Embrace it, give people what they want—something they've never experienced

**Critical addition**: When novelty wears off, ensure there's still something to care about

### Using Lens #24: Novelty

> "Different isn't always better, but better is always different." — Scotty Meltzer

Ask yourself:
- What is novel about my game?
- Does my game have novelties throughout or just at the beginning?
- Do I have the right mix of the novel and the familiar?
- When the novelty wears off, will players still enjoy my game?

## Framework 6: Judgment

### The Deep Need to Be Judged

Fourth level of Maslow's hierarchy (self-esteem) most intimately connected to games.

**Common misconception**: People hate being judged

**Truth**: People hate being judged **unfairly**

**Reality**: Deep inner need to know how we stack up

### Why We Seek Judgment

- Want to understand our performance
- Not happy with judgment → Work hard to improve
- Need objective evaluation of our abilities

### Games as Judgment Systems

**One of games' most appealing qualities**: Excellent systems for objective judgment

**Why players engage**: To be evaluated fairly and see how they measure up

### Using Lens #25: Judgment

Ask yourself:
- What does your game judge about the players?
- How does it communicate this judgment?
- Do players feel the judgment is fair?
- Do they care about the judgment?
- Does the judgment make them want to improve?

**Key insight**: Fair judgment drives improvement motivation

## Integrated Motivation Design

### Understanding Motivation Complexity

**Warning**: Beware of those who say motivation is simple

**Reality**: Richly complex and interesting

**Ignore complexity at your peril**

### Mapping Your Game's Motivation Ecosystem

Every game is a complex ecosystem of motivations. Consider:

1. **What needs does it fulfill?** (Maslow, Self-Determination Theory)
2. **Where are motivations on continua?** (Intrinsic/Extrinsic, Wanna/Hafta)
3. **What novelties attract and retain?**
4. **How does it judge players?**

### Motivation Interactions

**Supporting motivations**:
- Social play (Relatedness) + Competition (Esteem) = Multiplayer appeal
- Novelty (Discovery) + Mastery (Competence) = Learning curve satisfaction
- Autonomy (Choice) + Judgment (Fair evaluation) = Meaningful decisions

**Conflicting motivations**:
- External rewards can undermine intrinsic motivation
- Too many "hafta" obligations can kill "wanna" desire
- Unfair judgment damages esteem needs
- Excessive novelty can overwhelm need for competence

## Practical Application Guide

### Step 1: Audit Your Motivations

For each system/feature, ask:
- What motivates players to engage with this?
- Is it wanna or hafta?
- Is it intrinsic or extrinsic?
- What needs does it fulfill?
- Is it novel or familiar?
- How does it judge players?

### Step 2: Identify Conflicts

Look for:
- Extrinsic rewards added to intrinsically fun activities
- Too many "hafta" obligations piling up
- Unfair judgment systems
- Novelty wearing off with nothing underneath

### Step 3: Strengthen Support

Enhance:
- Intrinsic motivations with meaningful choices
- Fair judgment with clear feedback
- Novelty with solid core gameplay
- Social needs with community features

### Step 4: Test with Real Players

Observe:
- What actually motivates them?
- When do they feel obligated vs joyful?
- Do they care about your judgment systems?
- How long does novelty sustain them?

### Step 5: Iterate on Motivation Balance

Adjust based on:
- Player retention data
- Qualitative feedback
- Observation of play behavior
- Long-term engagement patterns

## Common Motivation Pitfalls

❌ **Assuming more motivation is always better**: Can conflict and cancel out
❌ **Over-relying on extrinsic rewards**: Drains intrinsic motivation
❌ **All novelty, no depth**: Players leave when newness fades
❌ **Unfair judgment**: Damages esteem and drives players away
❌ **Too many "hafta" obligations**: Turns play into work
❌ **Ignoring basic needs**: Missing foundation for higher needs
❌ **One-size-fits-all motivation**: Different players want different things

## Key Takeaways

1. **Games are "unimportant" but feel vital**: That's the magic of motivation
2. **Multiple frameworks reveal different insights**: Maslow, Self-Determination Theory, Intrinsic/Extrinsic, Wanna/Hafta, Novelty, Judgment
3. **Needs drive behavior**: Fulfill basic needs for powerful motivation
4. **Motivations interact**: Can support or conflict with each other
5. **Extrinsic can undermine intrinsic**: Be careful with rewards
6. **Wanna beats hafta**: Pleasure-seeking more sustainable than pain-avoiding
7. **Novelty attracts, depth retains**: Need both
8. **Fair judgment motivates**: Players want to be evaluated objectively
9. **Motivation is complex**: Respect its richness
10. **Test assumptions**: Player behavior reveals true motivations
