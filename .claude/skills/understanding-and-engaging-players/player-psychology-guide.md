# Player Psychology Guide

## The Mind: Where All Experiences Happen

Game experiences happen in one place: the human brain—the most complex object in the known universe. Most brain processes are hidden from conscious awareness, operating below the surface.

**Designer challenge**: Create great experiences in a system we barely understand and can't fully control.

**Designer advantage**: You have a brain too. Use self-observation and empathy to understand player minds.

## The Four Mental Abilities That Enable Gameplay

Four principal mental abilities make gameplay possible:
1. **Modeling** - Simplifying reality
2. **Focus** - Sustained attention
3. **Empathy** - Projecting into others
4. **Imagination** - Filling gaps and solving problems

### 1. Modeling: How the Brain Simplifies Reality

#### The Fundamental Insight

**Reality is too complex**. The brain copes by creating simplified mental models of reality.

**Critical understanding**:
- We don't experience reality itself
- We experience our brain's simplified model of reality
- Consciousness is an illusion that these models ARE reality
- The illusion is very good, but it's still an illusion

#### Examples of Mental Modeling

**Visual Modeling - Dots Changing Color**:
- Stare at a grid of dots
- They appear to change color as your eyes move
- In reality, the dots don't change color at all
- Your brain's model creates the illusion

**The Visible Light Spectrum**:
- Physics: Electromagnetic radiation exists on a smooth spectrum
- Our eyes: Only see a tiny fraction ("visible light")
- Infrared would be useful (see living things in dark) but would blind us (our eyeballs emit it)
- Result: Huge amounts of data not in our perceived reality

**Colors Aren't Real**:
- Physics: Smooth gradient of wavelengths
- Our perception: Distinct color groupings (red, orange, yellow, green, blue, violet)
- Sharp boundaries between colors (blue vs light blue vs green) are artifacts of retina construction
- "Colors" are useful models, not objective reality

**The Invisible World Around Us**:
- Bodies, homes, and food teem with microscopic bacteria and mites
- Demodex folliculorum lives in your eyelashes, pores, and hair follicles (up to 0.4mm—almost visible)
- Generally not part of our mental models because we don't need to think about them

#### Charlie Brown: A Case Study in Modeling

Look at a drawing of Charlie Brown. Seems normal at first, but analyze it:
- Head nearly as big as his body (not realistic)
- Fingers are little bumps (not realistic)
- Made of lines (nothing in reality is made of lines)

**Why we accept him as a person**:
- Matches our internal models enough
- Giant head: Brain stores more info about faces than bodies
- Lines: Brain draws lines around separate objects internally—cartoons are "predigested"
- Cartoons are soothing to look at because brain does less work

**If he had a tiny head and giant feet**: Would look ridiculous—doesn't match our models at all

#### How Stage Magicians Use Modeling

Magicians create experiences where mental models break:
- In our mind, models = reality
- Magician appears to do the impossible
- Audible gasp = sound of mental models tearing apart
- Only "it must be a trick" prevents us from believing in supernatural powers

#### Why This Matters for Game Design

**Games are like Charlie Brown**:
- They are predigested models
- Simple rules = easy to absorb and manipulate
- Less work for brain than real world = relaxing to play

**Abstract games** (tic-tac-toe, backgammon): Almost completely bare models

**Richly-themed games** (computer RPGs): Simple model coated with sugary aesthetics

**Real world**:
- Must work hard to figure out what the rules even are
- Work even harder to play by them properly
- Never sure if doing the right thing

**Games as practice**:
- Simpler models train us for complex ones
- Chess still taught at West Point
- Games let us experiment with digestible models
- Build competence for real-world complexity

#### Design Implication

If you understand how the illusion forms in the player's mind, you can create experiences that feel as real as (or more real than) reality itself.

### 2. Focus: Achieving Flow States

#### The Power of Selective Attention

Brain uses selective attention to make sense of the world:
- Ignores some things
- Devotes mental power to others

#### The Cocktail Party Effect

**Phenomenon**: In a room full of talking people, you can focus on one conversation and tune out all others

**Experimental evidence** (dichotic ear studies):
- Different audio to each ear (Shakespeare in left, numbers in right)
- Subjects can repeat one voice back accurately
- Afterward, have no idea what the other voice said
- Brain focused only on selected information, tuned out the rest

#### What Determines Focus?

Blend of:
- **Unconscious desires**: What your brain automatically attends to
- **Conscious will**: What you choose to focus on

#### The State of Flow

**Definition**: "A feeling of complete and energized focus in an activity, with a high level of enjoyment and fulfillment"

**Characteristics**:
- Rest of world falls away
- No intrusive thoughts
- Complete attention on current activity
- Lose track of time
- High enjoyment

**Researcher**: Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi (and many others)

**Designer goal**: Create experiences that put players into flow

#### Four Components Necessary for Flow

##### 1. Clear Goals

**Why**: When goals are clear, we can stay focused
**Problem**: Unclear goals make us uncertain if current actions are useful
**Result**: Can't get "into" the task

**In games**:
- Explicit objectives
- Clear win conditions
- Visible progress toward goals
- Understanding what "success" means

##### 2. No Distractions

**Why**: Distractions steal focus
**No focus = No flow**

**Two types of distraction**:
- **Mind wandering**: Menial labor with no thought
- **Hands fidgeting**: Just sitting and thinking

Both create "itchy" feelings that break focus

**In games**:
- Engage both mind AND hands
- Remove UI clutter during key moments
- Minimize interruptions
- No mandatory breaks in critical gameplay

##### 3. Direct Feedback

**Why**: Delayed feedback makes us lose focus waiting for results
**Immediate feedback = Easy to stay focused**

**In games**:
- Instant visual/audio response to actions
- Clear cause-and-effect
- Responsive controls
- See results of decisions immediately

##### 4. Continuously Challenging

**Human reality**: We love challenge
- **Must believe we can achieve it**: Or feel frustrated, seek different activity
- **Can't be too easy**: Or feel bored, seek more rewarding activity

**The Flow Channel**: Narrow margin between boredom and frustration

*Discussed more in Chapter 13: Balance*

#### The Flow Channel Diagram

```
High Skill
    |  Boredom
    |  ↗
    |   FLOW CHANNEL
    |     ↗
    | Frustration
    |_________________
       Challenge
```

**The dynamic**:
- As skill improves, must increase challenge
- Stay in the narrow channel between boredom and frustration
- Both extremes cause mind to seek new activity

#### Flow Channel Example: Tennis (Csikszentmihalyi)

**Alex learning tennis**:

**A1** (Beginning):
- Almost no skill
- Only challenge: Hit ball over net
- Not very difficult, but matches skill level
- **Result**: Flow (enjoying it)

**A2** (After practice):
- Skills have improved
- Still just batting ball over net
- **Result**: Boredom (too easy)

**A3** (Harder opponent):
- Faces much tougher challenges
- Skills haven't caught up
- **Result**: Anxiety (too difficult)

**Getting back to flow from A2 (bored)**:
- Must increase challenges
- Set harder goals (beat better opponent)
- Match challenge to improved skill
- **Result**: Back in flow at A4

**Getting back to flow from A3 (anxious)**:
- Must increase skills
- Could reduce challenge (back to A1), but difficult once aware harder challenges exist
- Better: Practice and improve
- **Result**: Back in flow at A4

**A4 vs A1**:
- Both are flow states
- A4 is more complex (greater challenge + greater skill)
- A4 not stable either—will lead to boredom or anxiety again
- Must keep moving up the channel

**The growth mechanism**:
- Can't enjoy same thing at same level for long
- Boredom or frustration motivates seeking higher flow
- Pushes to stretch skills or discover new opportunities
- **This dynamic explains why flow activities lead to growth and discovery**

#### Challenge Progression in Videogames

**Traditional games**: Challenge comes from tougher opponents

**Videogames with levels**:
- Sequence of levels with gradually increasing difficulty
- Self-balancing: Skilled players move through easy levels quickly
- Most players eventually reach a level where they get frustrated and quit
- Debate: Is this bad (frustration) or good (accomplishment is special)?

#### Movement Patterns Through Flow Channel

**Straight up the channel**:
```
    ↑ Skill
    ↑
    ↑
    ↑
   Challenge →
```
Better than ending in anxiety or boredom, but...

**Cyclical pattern (better)**:
```
    ↗ (challenge increases)
    ↘ (power/reward, easier period)
    ↗ (challenge ramps up again)
    ↘ (reward)
```

**Example**:
- Gun that needs 3 shots to kill enemies
- Enemies become more numerous (harder)
- Beat them, get gun that needs only 2 shots (easier, rewarding)
- New enemies appear that need 4 shots even with new gun (harder again)

**Result**: "Tense and release, tense and release"

**Why it works**:
- Too much tension = wear out
- Too much relaxation = bored
- Oscillation = enjoy excitement AND relaxation
- Plus: Pleasure of variety and anticipation

#### Using Lens #21: Flow

Ask yourself:
- Does my game have clear goals? If not, how can I fix that?
- Are the goals of the player the same goals I intended?
- Are there parts that distract players from their goal? Can distractions be reduced or tied into game goals?
- Does my game provide steady stream of not-too-easy, not-too-hard challenges, accounting for improving player skills?
- Are player skills improving at the rate I hoped? If not, how can I change that?

#### Testing for Flow

**Challenges**:
- Won't see it in 10 minutes
- Must observe longer periods
- A game that creates flow initially may later become boring or frustrating

**What to watch for**:
- **Solo games**: Quiet, possibly muttering, withdrawn, slow to respond to questions, possibly irritated by interruptions
- **Multiplayer games**: Enthusiastic communication, constantly focused on game

**Critical observation**:
- Watch for the moment they LEAVE flow
- Identify the event that moved them out
- Fix it in next prototype

#### Apply Flow to Yourself

Use Lens #21 on your own design process:
- Times of flow = most productive design time
- Organize design time to reach flow frequently
- Maximize your own flow state as a designer

### 3. Empathy: Projecting into Others

#### The Power of Empathy

**Definition**: Projecting yourself into the place of others—thinking their thoughts, feeling their feelings

**Importance**:
- Hallmark of human understanding
- Integral to gameplay

#### The Actor Exercise

**Setup**:
- Group 1: Each chooses an emotion, projects it through attitude/walk/facial expression
- Group 2: No chosen emotion, just walk randomly, establish eye contact

**Result**:
- Group 2 discovers they automatically take on emotions when making eye contact
- Happens without conscious willing
- Shows how powerful and automatic empathy is

#### Empathy's Scope

**Can empathize with**:
- Happy people (feel their joy)
- Sad people (feel their pain)
- Characters in stories
- Animals (especially dogs—they evolved expressive faces to capture our empathy)

**Empathy is easily tricked**:
- Brain empathizes with mental models, not real beings
- Photos, drawings, videogame characters can capture empathy
- Cinematographers manipulate empathy constantly

#### Empathy in Games

**Shared with other media**:
- Feeling for characters
- Emotional engagement
- Story investment

**Unique to games**:
- Empathic problem-solving: Imagine yourself as another to make better decisions
- Project entire decision-making capacity into character
- Become the character in ways impossible in non-interactive media

*Discussed more in Chapter 20: Characters*

### 4. Imagination: Filling Gaps and Solving Problems

#### The Everyday Miracle

Not talking about creative fantasy imagination—talking about the mundane imagination everyone uses constantly for communication and problem-solving.

#### The Automatic Gap-Filling

**Example**: "The mailman stole my car yesterday."

Your brain automatically imagined:
- What the mailman looked like
- What neighborhood the car was in
- What color the car was
- What time of day
- How he stole it
- Why he stole it

**You made up all these details** to more easily think about the story.

**If given new info** ("It wasn't a real car, but an expensive model toy car"):
- Brain quickly reformulates imaginary image
- Updates all answers to fit new information

#### Design Implication

**Critical insight**: Games don't need to provide every detail—players fill in the rest

**The art**: Knowing what to show and what to leave to imagination

#### Imagination's Incredible Power

Brain can manipulate simplified models into impossible situations:
- Imagine armchair in different color
- Imagine armchair made of oatmeal
- Imagine armchair walking around

**How we use it**:
- **Communication/storytelling**: Understanding what others tell us
- **Problem-solving**: "How can I change this light bulb without a stepladder?" → Immediately imagine possible solutions

#### Imagination in Game Design

**Two crucial functions**:
1. **As storytelling partner**: Players fill in narrative gaps, co-create the story world
2. **For problem-solving**: What problems can players imagine solving? What's beyond their imagination?

**Designer must understand**:
- What gaps players can fill
- What gaps require explicit information
- What problems players can imagine solving
- What's too abstract or complex to imagine

## Integrating the Four Abilities

The four abilities work together to create game experiences:

**Modeling** → Players simplify game rules into manageable mental representations
**Focus** → Players achieve flow when challenge matches skill
**Empathy** → Players project themselves into characters and situations
**Imagination** → Players fill gaps and solve problems creatively

## Using Your Own Mind to Understand Players

**The meta-insight**: You have these same abilities

**Application**:
- Use your own modeling to understand how players simplify your game
- Experience your own flow to recognize it in players
- Use your empathy to project into player perspectives
- Use your imagination to predict what players will imagine

**Self-listening** becomes the key to listening to your audience.

## Practical Applications

### For Modeling
- Design games as elegant models of experiences
- Strip away unnecessary complexity
- Make rules simple and clear
- Use familiar patterns (leverage existing models)
- Test: Can players explain the game simply?

### For Focus and Flow
- Set clear, specific goals
- Remove distractions and clutter
- Provide immediate feedback
- Balance challenge progression
- Watch for boredom and frustration
- Use tense/release pacing

### For Empathy
- Create characters players can project into
- Give emotional hooks for engagement
- Use faces and expressions (highly empathetic)
- Let players make meaningful choices for characters
- Design avatars as either ideal forms or blank slates

### For Imagination
- Leave room for player interpretation
- Don't over-explain everything
- Trust players to fill narrative gaps
- Present problems players can imagine solving
- Use suggestion and implication

## Common Design Mistakes

❌ **Over-explaining everything** → Trust player imagination
❌ **No clear goals** → Can't achieve flow
❌ **Too much complexity** → Mental models can't form
❌ **Constant interruptions** → Break flow
❌ **No emotional hooks** → Empathy has nothing to latch onto
❌ **Delayed feedback** → Can't maintain focus
❌ **Straight difficulty curve** → Exhausting, no release
❌ **Treating players as passive** → Underutilizing imagination

## Key Takeaways

1. **Reality is modeled**: Players experience your game through simplified mental models
2. **Flow is fragile**: Must carefully balance between boredom and frustration
3. **Empathy is automatic**: Players will project into anything you give them hooks for
4. **Imagination fills gaps**: Don't over-specify; let players co-create the experience
5. **These abilities work together**: Great games engage all four simultaneously
6. **You have these too**: Use self-observation to understand player psychology

## Remember

The human mind is the most fascinating, complex thing we know. We may never fully understand it. But the more we know, the better chance we have of creating great experiences—because the mind is where all game experiences take place.

**Final insight**: You're equipped with one yourself. Use your own powers of modeling, focus, empathy, and imagination to understand how these powers work in your players' minds.
