## Step 1: Know Your Competition

Your game doesn't go straight to consumers — it must **battle dozens of competitors** for attention first. Competitive positioning matters as much as game quality.

### Gap Analysis Process
1. List all games in your genre / platform / audience segment
2. For each competitor, identify: strengths, weaknesses, audience served
3. Find **gaps** — things players want that nobody currently provides
4. Position your game to fill the most valuable gap
5. Document your gap analysis for the pitch — it dramatically strengthens your business case

### Key Questions
- Is your game the only one of its kind, or one of dozens?
- Are you positioning as best-in-breed or low-budget alternative?
- What specific feature or experience are you providing that's missing from the market?
- If a funder does their own research, will your gap still hold up?

## Step 2: Understand How Your Audience PAYS

Knowing how your audience *plays* isn't enough — you must understand how they *pay*. This knowledge can shape your game more than anything else.

### Payment Psychology Questions
- What price point feels **fair** to this audience?
- How do they feel about free-to-play? (Some see F2P as cheap; others see a premium price as a barrier)
- What features trigger a purchase decision?
- Do they self-identify as "gamers"?
- What makes them feel **cheated** vs. **rewarded** when paying?

### Hardcore vs. Casual Economics

| | Hardcore Gamers | Casual Players |
|--|----------------|----------------|
| **Self-identity** | "I'm a gamer" | Play games but don't self-identify as gamers |
| **Willingness to pay** | High — for deep, well-crafted content | Low — mostly playing to kill time |
| **Market size** | Smaller | ~100x larger |
| **Discoverability** | Responds well to quality and depth | Requires viral moment or massive marketing to break through |
| **Best approach** | Premium price, deep content, word-of-mouth | F2P or very low price, massive reach needed |
| **Examples** | Dark Souls, Elden Ring | Angry Birds, Subway Surfer |

**Both markets are valid** — but they require completely different strategies. The casual market's sheer size is appealing, but breaking into it without luck or enormous marketing spend is extremely difficult.

### The Designer's Advantage
Use your understanding of player psychology to see what financial analysts cannot. If you can devise a pricing model that players find genuinely fair — based on insight into their hearts and minds — you will have more impact on profitability than a hundred financial analysts.

## Step 3: Analyze the Top Sellers

### Why This Matters
The game industry is **hit-driven**. Publishers make money off big hits and study them obsessively to understand the patterns behind financial success.

### Exercise: Test Your Market Awareness
1. For your target platform, list the **top 10 money-making games** of the past year — from memory
2. Look up the actual list
3. Analyze the gaps between your list and reality:
   - Did you overlook franchise or movie tie-ins?
   - Did you forget about F2P games?
   - Did you assume your personal favorites = everyone's favorites?
   - Did you miss a platform or demographic entirely?

### Why This Matters for Pitching
Any funder you pitch to can name the top 10 games in their segment. Demonstrating that **you** can too — and understanding *why* they succeeded — builds instant credibility. If you have special insights about why certain designs made money, money people will want to hear you out.

### Analytical Approaches
- Study hit games feature-by-feature: which features correlated with financial success?
- How much does multiplayer contribute? Hours of gameplay? Franchise recognition?
- Use this analysis to build a common vocabulary with business stakeholders

## Step 4: Identify Barriers to Entry

### Red Ocean vs. Blue Ocean

| | Red Ocean | Blue Ocean |
|--|-----------|------------|
| **Competition** | Fierce, crowded | Few competitors |
| **Profitability** | ~10% of games profitable | ~90% of games profitable |
| **Prices** | Driven toward zero | Supported by scarcity |
| **Example** | iOS app store (general games) | Slot machine industry |
| **Strategy** | Be the biggest fish OR find a blue ocean | Protect your position before competitors arrive |

**The counterintuitive truth:** Most people avoid barriers when they see them — they prefer following the crowd. But following the crowd means eventually fighting the crowd. **The road less traveled often leads to the greatest success.**

### Seven Types of Barriers

| Barrier Type | Description | Game Example |
|--------------|-------------|--------------|
| **Technical** | Special algorithms, novel look/feel, unique server technology that others can't easily replicate | Games built on proprietary engines or algorithms that create unique gameplay |
| **Hardware** | New platform or patented hardware others can't quickly match | Skylanders — competitors couldn't quickly create similar toy-game integration |
| **Expertise** | Deep domain knowledge in a niche that others lack | Early childhood social-emotional learning games — requires specialized understanding few possess |
| **Sales & Distribution** | Existing relationships with specific markets or channels | Training games for firefighters — need precinct-by-precinct sales relationships already in place |
| **Imagination** | First to see a viable market model that others overlooked | Minecraft — simple mechanics, but only one person saw that a flat-fee sandbox could work at scale |
| **Relationship** | Exclusive license or franchise partnership with a gatekeeper | Movie tie-ins where the licensor only works with trusted partners |
| **Uncertainty** | Jumping on a new platform when most developers are skeptical | Early Microsoft Kinect developers — took the gamble, reaped benefits when the platform unexpectedly succeeded |

### Barrier Analysis Checklist
- [ ] What barriers already exist in my target market?
- [ ] Which barriers protect my position from competitors?
- [ ] Can I CREATE new barriers? (technical innovation, exclusive partnerships, first-mover advantage)
- [ ] Am I entering a red ocean or blue ocean?
- [ ] If red ocean: what's my differentiation strategy to stand out?
- [ ] If blue ocean: what's my plan before competitors inevitably enter?
- [ ] Which barrier type gives me the strongest long-term advantage?

## Putting It All Together: Business Viability Assessment

Before pitching, prepare evidence-based answers to ALL of these:

| Question | What to Prepare |
|----------|-----------------|
| **Market fit** | Gap analysis showing what's missing and why players want it |
| **Audience** | Who plays? Who pays? How much? What price feels fair? |
| **Competition** | Competitive landscape with your positioning clearly defined |
| **Business model** | Complete money flow — where every dollar goes and why |
| **Barriers** | What protects this game's market position long-term |
| **Risks** | What could go wrong + mitigation plan for each risk |
| **Comparable titles** | How similar games performed financially — with data |

Money people will ask about every one of these. A strong pitch includes credible evidence for all seven. Weakness in any single area can derail an otherwise compelling pitch.
