# Twelve Pitch Tips Guide

A pitch is a **negotiation of power**. Both sides are trying to get what they want. The foundation of any successful pitch: know what YOU want AND know what THEY want (keeping in mind each party has three layers of desire).

## The Hierarchy of Ideas

Ideas are not judged on overall merit — they are judged by **how useful they are right now** to the person you're pitching to. A brilliant idea rejected today may land a deal six months later when it becomes useful. Don't grind your teeth on rejection — take it to someone who can use it, or file it for when its time comes.

---

## Tip 1: Get in the Door

- Use **back doors**, not front doors. Know someone on the inside who can vouch for you
- Most game deals happen through mutual introductions
- Attend industry events (GDC, IGDA meetings) to build contact networks
- Even internal pitches benefit: busy decision-makers take people they already know and trust more seriously
- A publisher who ignores your email won't ignore the email of someone they work with regularly

### Action
Build your network before you need it. Every industry event is an investment in future pitches.

---

## Tip 2: Show You Are Serious

- A working **prototype** is now expected — sketches and descriptions are no longer enough
- Show you've given serious thought to the game, its market, and how it works
- A detailed design document signals seriousness — "no one will read it, but they will weigh it"
- Prove the game will be fun AND monetize — belief alone isn't enough

### The Disney Open Forum Lesson
A designer presented two half-baked ideas without prototypes. A panel member responded: "If you don't care enough about these ideas to try them out, why should we?" The lesson: preparation signals respect for both the idea and the people you're pitching to.

---

## Tip 3: Be Organized

- Organization = lower perceived risk = more trust
- Test ALL equipment in advance (projectors, cables, internet connections)
- Have paper handouts ready and accessible in sufficient quantity
- Confirm every logistical detail: date, time, location, attendees
- Being organized keeps you calm, which keeps you in control

---

## Tip 4: Be Passionate

- You must be **genuinely** excited — faking passion is immediately obvious
- Passion communicates drive and commitment to deliver a quality game
- Funders need to see commitment before entrusting money
- If you're not excited about your game, no one else will be
- DO NOT try to maintain composure at the expense of enthusiasm

---

## Tip 5: Assume Their Point of View

This is the single most important mindset shift in pitching. Selling is not about pushing — it's about solving their problems.

### Before the Pitch
- **Research them:** Learn what they need, what fits their portfolio
- **Confirm fit:** If your game isn't right for them, don't waste their time — or yours

### During the Pitch
- **Avoid jargon:** They've never seen your game — explain it simply
- **Respect their time:** Get to the point; skip points they seem bored by; elaborate on what interests them
- **Start with platform, audience, genre** — immediately. No suspense, no backstory first. They need to quickly decide if they even care
- **Provide "handles":** Short memorable phrases that summarize the concept
  - "It's a bowling RPG!"
  - "It's Pokémon for grownups!"
  - "It's Nintendogs, with a whole zoo!"
- **Use Lens #83 (Fantasy):** Tell them what fantasy the game fulfills — not the backstory of the fictional world
- **Show, don't tell:** Working prototype > video > slideshow > verbal description

### After the Pitch (Best Case: They Love It)
They probably can't make a final decision alone — they need to pitch it internally. Make this easy:
- Create **self-explanatory leave-behind materials** (key images, short videos, bullet lists)
- These must be compelling without you in the room doing a song and dance
- Arm them to be your advocate

---

## Tip 6: Design the Pitch

The pitch IS an experience. Apply the same design thinking you'd use on your game.

- Create a good **interest curve:** hook → build → tension/release → climax
- Favor **images over words** in presentation slides
- Focus on what's **unique** about your game and why it fits this client
- Look for **flourishes of showmanship:**
  - Detailed animations or sound effects
  - Humorous or surprising images
  - Physical props
  - A surprising venue (a pitch about mummies was given in the Egyptian wing of the Metropolitan Museum of Art — the publisher was dazzled, and the project got funded)
- **Plan every moment** of the encounter: introductions, prototype reveal timing, Q&A flow
- "Overplanning" doesn't kill energy — it frees your mind to focus on delivery

---

## Tip 7: Know All the Details

Experienced publishers will interrupt your presentation with detailed questions. Have answers ready across four categories:

### Design Details
- How many hours of gameplay?
- How long does it take to finish a level?
- How does multiplayer work?
- For anything you haven't fully designed: have a confident guess ready

### Schedule Details
- Timeline to each milestone: design doc → prototype → alpha → beta → launch
- Post-launch update frequency
- Be **realistic** — you'll be held to your answers
- Prepare for: "What's the fastest you could get this done?"

### Financial Details
- Total development cost (team size × duration + other costs)
- Revenue projections based on comparable titles — give a **range**
- Ensure the minimum of your range still makes the game profitable
- Prepare for: "How much money will this make?"

### Risks
- State the biggest risks clearly with mitigation plans for each
- Categories: technical, gameplay, aesthetic, marketing, financial, legal

### The Bengal Tiger Principle
Anticipate what questions are coming and prepare perfect answers. Legend has it Joe Rohde was pitching Disney's Animal Kingdom to CEO Michael Eisner, who kept wavering. After Eisner said "I still don't see what's exciting about live animals," Rohde walked out and returned leading a Bengal tiger into the room. "This," he declared, "is what is so exciting about live animals." The park got funded.

---

## Tip 8: Exude Confidence

- You're selling **yourself** as much as the idea
- Nervousness undermines everything you say
- **Magic word: "Absolutely"** — use it for questions like "Can you make a kid's version?" It sounds far more confident than "yes" or "probably." Back it up afterward.
- When showing something impressive, act like it was easy
- Answer questions as a team — each member confident about their domain
- If you feel nervous: **visualize a past moment of peak confidence.** Putting yourself in that memory helps you access the feeling

### Handshakes
A confident handshake is a subconscious signal of personality. If yours isn't confident, practice until it is. Two pumps and a firm squeeze — being in sync for that brief moment sets the tone for the entire relationship.

---

## Tip 9: Be Flexible

- Expect curveballs: they might hate your concept, shorten your meeting, change requirements entirely
- Stay objective — your goal is to get a game published, not necessarily THIS game

### The Magic: The Gathering Story
Richard Garfield pitched RoboRally (an elaborate board game) to a publisher. After a patient listen, the publisher said: "We can't use this — it's too big. We're looking for small, portable games. Got anything like that?"

Garfield could have walked out insulted. Instead, he stayed calm, mentioned he had a card game idea, and came back to pitch it. That game became **Magic: The Gathering** — one of the best-selling card games in history.

### Practical Flexibility
- Keep alternative ideas ready
- If your time gets cut, have a compressed version of the pitch
- If they hate the concept, pivot to what they ARE looking for
- Never let ego override strategy

---

## Tip 10: Rehearse

- Planning is good; rehearsing is **better**
- Practice anywhere: on friends, coworkers, family
- Goal: not memorizing words, but making the chain of ideas flow naturally — like a favorite song
- **Never pitch while playing your own game** — it makes you sound unfocused and wastes time
- Have a **colleague play** while you narrate and answer questions
- Don't expect executives to play your prototype unless they're already excited — too much risk of embarrassment

---

## Tip 11: Get Them to Own It

The ideal outcome: they leave thinking of the game as **"their game."**

- Find or cultivate an **inside advocate** — someone presold on the concept who will defend it internally
- **Integrate their previously expressed interests** into your pitch. If they once said "I love helicopters" — make sure helicopters appear
- Incorporate their questions **on the fly** into your explanations ("Could it have giant rats?" → "So, say you come upon a room full of giant rats…")
- The easier they can imagine it as their game, the closer they are to accepting your proposal

---

## Tip 12: Follow Up

- After the pitch, don't wait for them to contact you
- Follow up within a few days with a **natural excuse:** "You had asked about the texture manager — I wanted to get back to you on that"
- Keep following up **periodically** — not too often, not too pushy
- Be patient: they may need time to discuss internally or review competing proposals
- Never get frustrated by silence — the timing may simply not be right yet
- It is **not uncommon** to hear back 6 months later: "Remember that pitch? We want to talk about it"

## Crowdfunding Strategy (Kickstarter & Similar)

All 12 pitch tips apply equally to crowdfunding video pitches. Additionally:

| Rule | Why It Matters |
|------|----------------|
| It's a LOT of work | Budget: 1 month prep + 1 month campaign + 1 month fulfillment |
| It's really a preorder system | People fund because they want the product early at a good price — not out of charity |
| Ask for as little as possible | Lower target = higher hit rate. Kickstarter: miss target = get nothing |
| Shorter campaigns win | Most funding arrives in first and last week. 30 days > 60 days |
| Keep rewards simple | Early access at a good price is the main draw; extras add work with minimal funding impact |
| Show, don't tell | A slice of gameplay in action beats 5 minutes of talking about what the game might be |
| Hustle constantly | Tweet, update, set small intermediate goals, engage influential people |
| Use stretch goals | Give backers a reason to spread the word ("Hit $50K and we add a blue laser level!") |
| Be famous | Already having a following is the strongest predictor of crowdfunding success |

**Best fit for crowdfunding:** Games with physical components or high fixed price ($20+).
**Poor fit:** Free-to-play mobile games (why would someone pay $25 before playing a game that will be free?).
