# Managing Business and Stakeholders

Covers the business side of game design: working with clients, pitching ideas to funders/publishers, and understanding the economics that shape design decisions. Based on Jesse Schell's *The Art of Game Design* (Chapters 30–32).

## When to Use This Skill

- Preparing to pitch a game concept to a publisher, funder, or executive
- Navigating client feedback or difficult stakeholder suggestions
- Evaluating whether a game concept is commercially viable
- Choosing or switching business models (retail, digital, F2P)
- Performing competitive and market analysis
- Building a business case for design decisions

---

## Core Framework: Three Stakeholder Interactions

Every game designer navigates three key business interactions in sequence:

<interaction_flow>
1. CLIENT RELATIONSHIP → Understand what clients truly want (3 layers of desire)
2. PITCH            → Convince stakeholders your game is worth funding (12 pitch tips)
3. PROFIT           → Ensure the game can succeed commercially (business models + barriers)
</interaction_flow>

## Workflow

### Phase 1: Understand Your Client (Lens #107)

Before any pitch or negotiation, map the client's three layers of desire:

```
Words  → What they SAY they want  (stated requirements)
Mind   → What they THINK they want (unstated agenda)
Heart  → What they REALLY want    (deep motivation)
```

**When handling bad suggestions:**
1. NEVER agree blindly — it's a disservice to client and game
2. NEVER immediately counter — it usually backfires
3. ALWAYS ask: "What problem are you trying to solve?" (Lens #14: Problem Statement)
4. Find a solution that addresses the underlying problem better than their suggestion

**"Not That Rock" pattern** — when a client doesn't know what they want:
- Ask probing questions about purpose and use case
- Present curated options, not open-ended requests
- Guide them through discovery — helping a client find what they want IS part of the design job

See [client-relationship-guide.md](client-relationship-guide.md) for full techniques including the Michelangelo method.

### Phase 2: Craft and Deliver the Pitch (Lens #108)

A pitch is a **negotiation of power**. Know what YOU want AND what THEY want.

**Hierarchy of Ideas:** Ideas are judged not on overall merit — but on how useful they are **right now** to the person you're pitching to. A brilliant idea rejected today may land a deal six months later.

**Pitch Preparation Checklist:**
```
- [ ] Research the target (their needs, portfolio, decision process)
- [ ] Define what YOU want from this pitch
- [ ] Build materials: prototype, presentation deck, demo video
- [ ] Lead with: platform → audience → genre  (NOT backstory)
- [ ] Provide "handles" (e.g., "Pokémon for grownups")
- [ ] Anticipate tough questions with prepared answers
- [ ] Rehearse with colleagues unfamiliar with the game
- [ ] Prepare leave-behind materials they can use to pitch internally
- [ ] Follow up within days after the pitch (find a natural excuse)
```

See [twelve-pitch-tips-guide.md](twelve-pitch-tips-guide.md) for all 12 tips with examples, plus crowdfunding strategy.

### Phase 3: Evaluate Business Viability (Lens #109)

**Step 1 — Trace the money flow.** Understand where every dollar goes in your business model.

| Model | Key Dynamic | Developer Takes |
|-------|------------|-----------------|
| Retail ($50) | Publisher + retailer absorb most; high volume needed | ~$8–10/unit |
| Direct Download ($10) | Platform takes 30%; marketing cost falls on developer | ~$7/unit minus marketing |
| Free-to-Play | Revenue from fraction of players; LTV vs acquisition cost | Highly variable |

**Step 2 — Analyze competition.** Perform gap analysis: what's missing in the market that players actually want?

**Step 3 — Understand audience payment psychology.** Design payment moments that feel rewarding, not exploitative. The D&D Online example: paying $5 for an *adventure* (earn weapon as treasure) feels heroic. Paying $5 for a *weapon* when stuck feels like cheating. Same money, completely different player experience.

**Step 4 — Identify barriers to entry.** Seek blue oceans (few competitors, high profitability) over red oceans (crowded, prices driven to zero). Seven barrier types to evaluate — see [market-analysis-framework.md](market-analysis-framework.md).

**Step 5 — Learn the business language.** Master key terms so you can have credible conversations with money people. See [business-models-and-terminology.md](business-models-and-terminology.md) for the full glossary.
