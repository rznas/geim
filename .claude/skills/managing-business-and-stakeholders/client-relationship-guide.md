# Client Relationship Guide

## The Three Layers of Desire

Every client has three layers. Satisfying deeper layers builds stronger relationships and better outcomes.

### Layer 1: Words (Stated Requirements)
What the client explicitly says they want. Take it seriously — it defines scope — but never assume it tells the whole story.

### Layer 2: Mind (Unstated Agenda)
What the client actually thinks will happen. Often a more specific or different direction than what they stated. May include personal preferences, political considerations, or a pre-formed vision they haven't shared.

### Layer 3: Heart (Deep Motivation)
The emotional core driving the client. Often has nothing to do with the game itself — could be about reputation, creative identity, proving something, or personal legacy.

**Example from the book:**
- **Words:** "Make an algebra game for eighth graders for the Rittenhouse Foundation"
- **Mind:** "Actually I want a space-themed geometry game — I have it all planned out. I'm only going along with the algebra thing because the funders want it"
- **Heart:** "I'm tired of being the financial person. I want people to see I have a creative side"

**Design principle:** Fulfilling a client's Heart desire earns you a lifelong ally. Always look for ways to satisfy all three layers simultaneously — this is non-trivial but achievable with skill.

## Coping with Bad Suggestions

When a client gives a suggestion that seems wrong or foolish:

### The Wrong Responses
1. **Agree blindly** — A disservice to both client and game. The suggestion may solve the wrong problem entirely.
2. **Immediately reject** — Usually backfires. Makes the client feel dismissed and defensive.

### The Right Response: Problem Statement (Lens #14)
Ask: **"What problem are you trying to solve?"**

Most bad suggestions are attempted solutions to unstated problems. People's brains jump to solutions before fully defining the problem. Once you identify the real problem, you can often find a better solution the client will actually prefer.

**Case study — "These cars need more chrome":**
- **Client's suggestion:** Add chrome to racing cars (would strain performance budget)
- **Real problem (uncovered by asking "why?"):** Cars didn't feel fast enough
- **Actual solution:** Increase car speed + lower camera angle for perceived motion (easy changes)
- **Result:** Client was thrilled, felt like a creative partner, understood the design better

**The magic words:** "What problem are you trying to solve?" — use them every time a bad suggestion lands.

## The "Not That Rock" Pattern

When a client doesn't know what they want, interactions become a frustrating loop of rejected proposals.

### The Loop
```
Client: Bring me a rock.
Designer: [presents option]
Client: No, not that one.
Designer: [presents another]
Client: No, not that either.
(Repeat endlessly)
```

After 20 rounds, designers typically conclude: "This client has no idea what they want!" — and that may be true. But if they already knew, the game would already be designed.

### Breaking the Loop
A core part of the designer's job is helping the client discover what they want:

1. **Ask about purpose:** "What are you going to use this for?"
2. **Narrow the scope:** "What kind of [X] are you looking for?"
3. **Present curated choices:** Bring 2–3 well-chosen options based on what you've learned
4. **Educate as you go:** Help them understand the design space so they can articulate preferences

**Correct version of the exchange:**
```
Client: Bring me a rock.
Designer: What kind of rock?
Client: I'm not sure... I don't know much about rocks.
Designer: Well, what are you going to do with it?
Client: Oh... I was going to put it by my driveway and paint a house number on it.
Designer: Ah... I think I know a good one. Let me bring you a few to choose from.
```

When you guide a client to discover what they want, you are designing AND empowering them. Done well, the client feels smart, and the game meets their needs perfectly.

## The Michelangelo Method: Making Clients Feel Like Partners

The deepest client relationship technique: give clients a sense of creative ownership without compromising your vision.

### The David and Soderini Story
Michelangelo was sculpting David when Mayor Soderini (the client/funder) visited and complained: "The nose is too big."

Soderini was standing too close — everyone's nose looks too big when viewed from below. But Michelangelo understood the real issue wasn't the nose. Soderini wanted **creative ownership** — to feel like a partner on the project.

**What Michelangelo did:**
1. Invited Soderini up the scaffold (met him where he was, didn't lecture)
2. Pretended to chisel the nose while scooping marble dust in his pocket
3. Dropped the dust to simulate actual sculpting happening
4. Stepped back: "Look at it now"
5. Soderini: "I like it better — you've made it come alive!"

**Result:** Soderini felt ownership. He defended the statue from any future criticism.

### Applying This to Game Design
- Find ways for clients to feel creative partnership **without changing your core vision**
- Integrate their ideas where possible — even small elements they previously expressed interest in
- During meetings, let them experience the impact of changes in real-time when feasible
- Remember: clients offer more than funding — connections, business expertise, audience understanding
- **If you truly listen to clients, they will listen to you**

The goal is not deception — it is finding genuine ways to make the client feel like a valued creative contributor.

## Pre-Interaction Client Research Checklist

Before any significant client interaction:
- [ ] What does the client say they want? (Layer 1)
- [ ] What might they actually be thinking/planning? (Layer 2)
- [ ] What deeper motivation might be driving this? (Layer 3)
- [ ] Do they care more about quick profit or long-term reputation?
- [ ] Are they entering a new market or capitalizing on an existing one?
- [ ] What do they think makes a good game?
- [ ] How can I make them feel like a creative partner?
- [ ] What connections or expertise do they bring beyond funding?
