# Business & Stakeholder Lenses (#107, #108, #109)

These three lenses form a sequential chain: you understand your client (#107), then pitch them (#108), then ensure the game is profitable (#109). Apply them in order. Each lens feeds into the next.

---

## Lens #107: The Lens of the Client

**Core principle:** If you are making a game for someone else, you must know what they actually want — which is rarely exactly what they say they want.

### Questions to Ask
- What does the client **say** they want? (Words — stated requirements)
- What does the client **think** they want? (Mind — unstated agenda, possibly a pre-formed vision)
- What does the client **really** want, deep down in their heart? (Heart — the emotional core driving the project)

### When to Apply
- Before any meeting, review, or negotiation with a client
- When a client's behavior seems contradictory or hard to predict
- When you receive feedback that conflicts with earlier instructions
- When a client rejects something without clear reasoning

### How to Use It
1. **Research first.** Do your homework personally and professionally. Do they want quick profit or long-term reputation? New market entry or capitalizing on existing position? What do they think makes a good game?
2. **Map all three layers.** Write them out explicitly — don't leave any layer as "unknown." Use inference, past conversations, and context clues.
3. **Design for all three simultaneously.** This is the highest skill. Fulfilling someone's Heart earns a lifelong ally. Fulfilling only Words satisfies the contract; fulfilling Mind and Heart builds the relationship.
4. **When stuck:** Use Lens #14 (Problem Statement). Ask: "What problem are you trying to solve?" Most confusing client behavior dissolves when you identify the unstated problem.

### Connection to Next Lens
Once you understand what the client truly wants, you can craft a pitch that speaks to all three layers — not just the surface request.

## Lens #108: The Lens of the Pitch

**Core principle:** A pitch is a negotiation of power. Power = the ability to get what you want. The foundation of any successful pitch: know what YOU want AND know what THEY want.

### Questions to Ask
- **Why** are you pitching this game to **this** client? (Alignment check — wrong client = wasted effort)
- What will you consider "a successful pitch"? (Define your goal before entering)
- What's **in it for** the people you are pitching to? (Their motivation, not yours)
- What do the people you are pitching to **need to know** about your game? (Their information gaps, not your desire to explain everything)

### When to Apply
- Before preparing any pitch (internal or external)
- When deciding whether to pitch at all (does the idea fit this client right now?)
- When a pitch has been rejected (re-evaluate through these questions)
- When designing the pitch experience itself

### How to Use It
1. **Start with the alignment question.** If you can't clearly answer "Why this game to this client?", either reframe or find a better-fit client. The Hierarchy of Ideas is real: ideas are judged by how useful they are RIGHT NOW to the person hearing them, not on overall merit.
2. **Define success explicitly.** "They fund us" is one definition. "They become an advocate" is another. "We learn what they're looking for" is a third. Know your goal.
3. **Reverse-engineer from their needs.** What problem does this client have that your game solves? Structure the entire pitch around that answer.
4. **Design the pitch as an experience.** Apply the same design thinking you'd use on your game: interest curve, accessibility, surprises, aesthetic, showmanship. Plan every moment.
5. **Arm them to pitch internally.** Best case, they love it — but they can't say yes alone. Give them self-explanatory materials that work without you in the room.

### The Hierarchy of Ideas (Critical Concept)
```
Ideas judged by:  How useful is this RIGHT NOW to the person I'm pitching to?
NOT by:           Overall merit, creativity, or how cool it is
```
A brilliant idea rejected today may land a deal six months later when it becomes useful. Don't grind your teeth — file it for when its time comes.

### Connection to Next Lens
If pitching to an executive, the single most important question they will have is: "Can this game make money, and how much?" That leads directly to Lens #109.

## Lens #109: The Lens of Profit

**Core principle:** Profits keep the game industry alive. Understanding business gives designers more creative control — because "form follows funding."

### Questions to Ask
- **Where does the money go** in my game's business model? Why? (Trace every dollar)
- **How much will it cost** to produce, market, distribute, and maintain this game? Why? (Full cost picture, not just development)
- **How much money will this game make?** Why do I think that? (Evidence-based projection, not hope)
- **What are the barriers to entry** in the market for this game? (What protects your position?)

### When to Apply
- When evaluating whether a game concept is commercially viable
- When choosing or switching business models
- When preparing financial projections for a pitch
- When a money person challenges your design decisions
- When positioning your game in a competitive market

### How to Use It
1. **Follow the money.** For your chosen business model, trace where every dollar goes. If baffled by a new model, find a money person and ask: "Can you show me where the money goes?"
2. **Cost everything.** Development is only part of the cost. Add marketing, distribution, platform fees, post-launch support. The full picture often surprises designers.
3. **Base projections on comparable titles.** Don't guess — research how similar games performed. Give a range, not a single number. The minimum of your range must still be profitable.
4. **Identify your ocean.** Red ocean = crowded, prices driven to zero, ~10% profitable. Blue ocean = few competitors, ~90% profitable. Barriers (technical, hardware, expertise, sales, imagination, relationship, uncertainty) are what create blue oceans.
5. **Learn the language.** Business terminology isn't intimidating once you see it as shorthand for concepts you already understand. Speak it fluently, and money people will defer to your design judgment.

### The Designer's Leverage
> "If you can explain why your precious feature will make more money, in terms they'll understand and believe, you've got a much better shot at having the game turn out the way you know is best."

Design decisions impact profitability. Business decisions impact design. The designer who understands both sides holds disproportionate influence. This is the strategic advantage of mastering Lens #109.

## Applying All Three Lenses Together

### Pre-Pitch Workflow
```
1. Lens #107 → Map client's three layers of desire
2. Lens #108 → Align game concept with client's right-now needs
3. Lens #109 → Build the business case that answers "Will it make money?"
4. Combine → Craft a pitch that satisfies Words (what they asked for),
              Mind (what they're actually planning), and
              Heart (what will make them feel like a creative partner)
```

### When a Pitch Fails — Diagnostic Questions
```
Did the idea not fit this client RIGHT NOW?        → Lens #108 (Hierarchy of Ideas)
Did you misunderstand what they actually wanted?   → Lens #107 (Three Layers)
Could you not answer "Will it make money?"         → Lens #109 (Profit)
Was the pitch itself poorly designed?              → Lens #108 (Design the Pitch)
Did you fail to show you were serious?             → Lens #108 (Tip #2)
```

### Quick Decision Framework: "Should I Pitch This?"
| Check | Question | If No → |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Lens #107 | Do I understand what this client truly wants? | Do more research first |
| Lens #108 | Is this idea useful to them RIGHT NOW? | Find a better-fit client or wait |
| Lens #109 | Can I answer "Where does the money go?" credibly? | Do the business model analysis first |
| All three | Can I satisfy all three layers of desire? | Refine the pitch until you can |
