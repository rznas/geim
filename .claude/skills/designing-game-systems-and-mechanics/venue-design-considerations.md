# Venue Design Considerations - Matching Mechanics to Venues

This guide provides detailed information on understanding game venues and how to match game mechanics and design to different play spaces, based on Chapter 3 of *The Art of Game Design*.

---

## Understanding Venues

### The Core Principle
**Look past the technologies (ephemeral) to see the venues (persistent)**

Technologies come and go:
- Arcade gaming → PC gaming → Console gaming → PC gaming returns → Mobile/Tablet rises

But **venues** (the places in our lives where we use technologies) remain constant and shape what works.

### Why Venues Matter More Than Platforms
- **Platforms change:** New hardware every few years
- **Venues persist:** Unchanged for thousands (or millions) of years
- **Design mistake:** Focus too much on shiny new technology
- **Design wisdom:** Focus on familiar, invisible patterns of where and how we use technology

## Private Venues

**Core Characteristic:** Privacy aids gameplay—safe place to immerse in fantasy world, alone or with trusted people.

### 1. The Hearth

**Description:**
- TV screen replaced the fireplace
- Central gathering place in home
- Light in darkness, family storytelling

**Evolution:**
- Pre-fire: Lived like animals
- Fire mastery: Cultural/psychological/physical change
- Modern: Fireplace obsolete but homes still built with them
- Current: TV screen is the modern hearth

**Characteristics:**
- Right size for group viewing
- Gives light, flickers
- Tells its own stories
- Around-the-clock focus point

**Best Game Types:**
- **Multi-player games** that whole family can play
- **Watchable games** - entertaining for spectators
- **Physical involvement** games (visible to all)
- **Singing/dancing** games (around the fire for million years)

**Successful Examples:**
- Nintendo Wii: Family play + entertaining to watch + physical involvement
- SingStar, Dance Central: Singing and dancing around hearth

**Failures:**
- Wii U: Separate handheld screen only one player could see (broke hearth model)

**Design Considerations:**
- Can multiple people play or watch?
- Is it entertaining for spectators?
- Does it involve physical activity visible to all?
- Does it bring family together?
- Can people of different skill levels participate?

**The Future of Hearth:**
- "End of television" or "end of consoles" often predicted
- But hearth has existed since dawn of humanity
- Will continue to change and evolve but won't vanish

---

### 2. The Workbench

**Description:**
- Private space for working on hard problems
- Actual workbench, sewing machine, desk in quiet corner
- Solitary, quiet, often messy
- Where computers naturally went when entering homes

**Characteristics:**
- Intense and solitary work/play
- Quiet, no interruptions
- Not for guests
- "Lean forward" posture
- Privacy essential

**Best Game Types:**
- **Difficult and intense** games
- **Long play sessions** (hours at a time)
- **Competitive** online play with like-minded players
- **Not family-oriented**
- **Deep strategic** games
- **VR experiences** (intensity + privacy)

**Successful Examples:**
- MOBAs: League of Legends
- MMOs: World of Warcraft
- Steam network (owns the workbench)
- Head-mounted VR displays

**Why These Games Stay at Workbench:**
- Too intense for casual hearth play
- Require dedicated attention
- Long sessions incompatible with family time
- Solitary or online-social (not local-social)

**Design Considerations:**
- Does game require intense focus?
- Can it be played in long uninterrupted sessions?
- Is it too complex for casual players?
- Does it require privacy from family?
- Is online community more important than local?

---

### 3. The Reading Nook

**Description:**
- Private, comfortable, quiet space
- Bedroom, quiet chair/couch away from TV
- "Lean back" activity (vs. workbench "lean forward")
- Where we read books and relax

**Characteristics:**
- Portable preferred (can read anywhere, but have preferences)
- Not hearth (too noisy, interruptions)
- Not workbench (uncomfortable, too "work-oriented")
- Comfortable, relaxed posture
- Peaceful and quiet

**Platform Evolution:**
- PC gaming: NO (workbench)
- Game consoles: NO (hearth)
- iPad announcement: Game industry not impressed
  - Not as convenient as mobile phone
  - No control system for hearth/workbench experiences
  - Industry dismissed it
- iPad success: Perfect for reading nook
  - Reading and watching videos
  - Significant force in gaming

**Best Game Types:**
- **Easier, simpler, more relaxing** than console/PC games
- **Peaceful gameplay** like reading a book
- **Touch-based** controls
- **Shorter sessions** or easy to pause
- **Comfortable to play** in bed/couch/quiet corner

**Successful Examples:**
- Tablet games (iPad, Android tablets)
- Casual puzzle games
- Story-driven relaxing games
- Reading nook-appropriate mobile games

**Design Considerations:**
- Is game relaxing and low-stress?
- Can it be played lying down or reclining?
- Does it work with touch controls?
- Is it easy to pause and resume?
- Does it create peaceful state like reading?

## Public Venues

**Core Principle:** Give them something they can't get at home.

This rule has worked for thousands of years across all location-based entertainment (LBE):
- Taverns
- Theaters
- Restaurants
- Theme parks
- Video arcades

### 4. The Theater

**Description:**
- Large number of people witness something together
- Plays, movies, music, sporting events, planetarium shows

**The Magic of Theaters:**
- Crowd focuses on events simultaneously
- Subconsciously sense how others feel about performance
- Helps focus our own feelings
- Appreciating experience together feels satisfying
- Why TV shows feature laugh tracks

**The Theater Problem for Games:**
- **Games want to be unique for each player** (interactive)
- **Crowds want to experience together** (passive)
- **Fundamental tension** between these needs

**Failed Attempts:**
- Theatrical experiences where everyone participates
- No long-term successes yet

**The Challenge:**
- Joy of interactivity watered down proportionately by number of seats
- Either: Experience gets tiresome quickly
- Or: Theater so small as to be unprofitable

**Potential Future:**
- Someone may use technology to overcome this
- Solution will have to be very clever indeed

**Design Considerations:**
- Can interaction scale to hundreds of people?
- Is it fun when choice is divided among many?
- Can you create shared experience with personalized elements?
- Is there clever technology solution?

---

### 5. The Arena

**Description:**
- Specialized fields for competitive play
- Large, outdoors, designed for specific gameplay
- Public place for witnessing competitions
- Public record of who wins/loses

**Examples:**
- Baseball diamonds
- Golf courses
- Soccer fields
- Tennis courts
- Horse tracks
- Even courtrooms (!)

**The Arena + Theater Combo:**
- People get excited watching these games
- Theater forms around arena
- Two venues in one

**Digital Impact:**
- Little impact on traditional outdoor sports arena (so far)
- But digital gameplay takes on arena characteristics:
  - Multiplayer FPS: Players physically in workbench/hearth, but minds in arena
  - Esports tournaments: Millions of simultaneous viewers
  - YouTube/Twitch: Virtual theaters around digital play
  - Growing spectator culture

**The Future:**
- Lines are blurring
- New mobile and augmented reality technology
- Traditional sporting arenas becoming more digital

**Design Considerations:**
- Is competition core to the game?
- Is it exciting to watch?
- Does it create public record of success?
- Can it support spectator experience?
- Does it work for esports/streaming?

---

### 6. The Museum

**Description:**
- Break from day-to-day life
- Examine exotic things and places
- Dose of variety, expand knowledge
- Return home with new perspective

**What Counts as Museum:**
- Actual museums
- Zoos and aquariums
- Sightseeing (city as museum)
- Shopping trips to unusual stores

**Games in Museums:**
- Many games right at home here
- Museums incorporate gameplay to introduce information
- Video arcade has museum-like quality:
  - Pass from game to game
  - Try each one
  - Think about which you like best

**Design Considerations:**
- Does game showcase exotic content?
- Is it educational or informative?
- Can it be experienced in short bursts?
- Does it work alongside other exhibits?
- Is it self-explanatory for new visitors?

## Half Private/Half Public Venues

**Core Characteristic:** Exist on boundary between public and private, or in both simultaneously.

### 7. The Gaming Table

**Description:**
- Special face-to-face intimacy
- Players sit like gods over toy world they control
- Can happen in home (with guests) or public places (bars, casinos)

**Characteristics:**
- Special intensity in players
- Both sandwich and sushi roll invented in 18th century by obsessed table gameplayers who needed to eat without stopping

**Traditional Games:**
- Board games
- Card games
- Dice games

**Digital Challenges:**
- Reliance on vertical screens
- Not much digital presence yet
- Exception: Some experimental board games

**The Future:**
- Cheaper, larger touch screens
- Augmented reality glasses
- Potential new world of digital table games

**Design Considerations:**
- Does it work on horizontal surface?
- Can players see each other's faces?
- Does it support social interaction?
- Can it use AR or large touch screens?
- Is there face-to-face intimacy?

---

### 8. The Playground

**Description:**
- Domain of children (adults can forget)
- Outdoor play with friends
- Improvisational play

**Examples:**
- Play equipment at public parks
- Backyards
- Streets
- Vacant lots
- Caves in the woods

**Importance:**
- Children always will play this way
- Must play this way—important for development

**Video Game Industry Status:**
- Largely ignored so far
- Not feasible to create games that work there
- Technology not rugged or mobile enough

**The Future:**
- Technology becoming more rugged and mobile
- Will likely change

**Design Considerations:**
- Is it rugged enough for outdoor use?
- Can children play without adult supervision?
- Does it encourage improvisational play?
- Is it physically active?
- Is it social and collaborative?

---

### 9. Anywhere

**Description:**
- Games that don't care where they're played
- Fill spare minutes throughout the day

**Traditional Examples:**
- Crossword puzzles
- Sudoku
- Word Find games
- Paper-based portable games

**Smartphone Revolution:**
- Vastly richer set of games
- Changed "anywhere" venue forever
- Can be played any time, any place

**Characteristics Due to Venue:**
- **Interruptibility:** Must be playable in tiny bites
- **Screen size:** Very simple interfaces and stories
- **Time commitment:** Filling minutes here and there
- **Pricing:** Not willing to pay much up front, prefer free
- **Session length:** Few minutes at a time
- **Complexity:** Must be simple, immediately understandable

**Design Considerations:**
- Can game be paused instantly?
- Does it work in 30-second to 5-minute sessions?
- Is interface simple enough for small screen?
- Can player pick up where they left off easily?
- Is it free-to-play compatible?
- Does it respect interruptions (phone calls, etc.)?

## Venue Analysis Framework

### Lens #3: The Lens of the Venue

**Purpose:** Make sure you aren't designing in a vacuum.

**Questions to Ask:**
1. **What type of venue best suits the game I'm trying to create?**
   - Consider natural fit
   - Where will players actually play?
   - What venue characteristics align with game design?

2. **Does my venue have special properties that will influence my game?**
   - Privacy requirements
   - Social context
   - Physical constraints
   - Time constraints
   - Posture (lean forward vs. lean back)
   - Screen size/type
   - Interruption likelihood

3. **What elements of my game are in harmony with my venue? What elements are not?**
   - Identify conflicts
   - Adjust design to venue
   - Or reconsider venue choice

## Venue Mixed and Matched

**Important Reality:** Easy to find gaps and overlaps.

**Examples of Complexity:**
- Pinball at restaurant: What venue?
- Bowling alley: What venue?
- Casino: Museum? Arena? Gaming Table? Something else?

**Success Story: Nintendo Switch**
- Works in multiple venues:
  - Hearth (docked mode)
  - Reading Nook (handheld mode)
  - Gaming Table (tabletop mode)
  - Anywhere (portable)
- Remarkable because of versatility

**The Point:**
- Don't need perfect taxonomy
- Important to see past games and platforms
- Be aware of venues and their properties
- **Games and technologies change constantly**
- **Venues change very little**

## Matching Mechanics to Venues

### Hearth Mechanics
**✓ Works Well:**
- Local multiplayer
- Spectator-friendly actions
- Physical motion controls
- Simple, accessible gameplay
- Party games
- Shared screen experiences

**✗ Doesn't Work:**
- Single-player focus
- Complex, hard-to-watch gameplay
- Individual screens that exclude others
- Headphones-required sound

### Workbench Mechanics
**✓ Works Well:**
- Deep strategic systems
- Complex controls
- Competitive online multiplayer
- Long play sessions
- High difficulty
- Intense concentration requirements
- VR immersion

**✗ Doesn't Work:**
- Casual, drop-in gameplay
- Family-oriented content
- Short sessions
- Simple mechanics

### Reading Nook Mechanics
**✓ Works Well:**
- Touch controls
- Relaxing gameplay
- Story-driven experiences
- Easy to pause
- Comfortable posture support
- Peaceful aesthetics
- Shorter sessions

**✗ Doesn't Work:**
- Intense action
- Complex button controls
- Stressful gameplay
- Requires sitting upright

### Theater Mechanics
**✓ Works Well:**
- Passive viewing
- Shared narrative
- Minimal individual interaction
- Synchronized experience

**✗ Doesn't Work:**
- High individual agency
- Personalized choices
- Unique player paths

### Arena Mechanics
**✓ Works Well:**
- Competitive gameplay
- Spectator appeal
- Clear win conditions
- Skill-based challenges
- Public performance

**✗ Doesn't Work:**
- Purely cooperative
- Anti-social gameplay
- Uninteresting to watch
- Unclear outcomes

### Gaming Table Mechanics
**✓ Works Well:**
- Face-to-face social
- Turn-based play
- Strategic depth
- Horizontal board/play space
- Cards, dice, tokens

**✗ Doesn't Work:**
- Vertical screen dependence
- Real-time twitch gameplay
- Requires privacy
- Individual focused

### Anywhere Mechanics
**✓ Works Well:**
- Instant pause/resume
- Short sessions (30 sec - 5 min)
- Simple controls
- Small screen optimization
- Free-to-play model
- Pick up and play

**✗ Doesn't Work:**
- Long required sessions
- Complex tutorials
- Large screen needed
- Premium pricing

## Design Process: Starting with Venue

### Step 1: Identify Target Venue
- Where will players actually play your game?
- What venue characteristics matter most?
- Are there multiple venues to consider?

### Step 2: Understand Venue Constraints
- Privacy vs. public
- Social vs. solitary
- Screen type and size
- Posture (lean forward/back, lying down)
- Session length
- Interruption likelihood
- Noise/quiet requirements

### Step 3: Design Mechanics for Venue
- Choose mechanics that harmonize with venue
- Avoid mechanics that conflict
- Consider venue-specific opportunities

### Step 4: Test in Actual Venue
- Playtest in the real environment
- Observe natural player behavior
- Identify venue-specific issues

### Step 5: Iterate Based on Venue Reality
- Adjust mechanics based on observations
- Double-down on what works in venue
- Remove or redesign what conflicts

## Case Studies

### Case Study 1: Wii vs. Wii U
**Wii Success:**
- Venue: Hearth
- Mechanics: Physical motion, local multiplayer, spectator-friendly
- Harmony: Perfect alignment with hearth venue

**Wii U Failure:**
- Venue: Attempted hearth
- Mechanics: Individual screen only one player could see
- Conflict: Broke hearth social model
- Result: Largely disappointing

**Lesson:** Even small venue misalignment can break successful formula

### Case Study 2: iPad Gaming
**Initial Skepticism:**
- Game industry dismissed iPad
- Not as convenient as mobile phone
- No controls for console-style games

**Unexpected Success:**
- Found natural venue: Reading nook
- Peaceful, relaxing gameplay
- Perfect platform for reading nook games
- Different games than console/PC

**Lesson:** New technology may create new venue opportunities

### Case Study 3: Esports
**Virtual Arena Creation:**
- Players physically in workbench/hearth
- Minds in the arena
- Spectators watching in virtual theater
- Millions of simultaneous viewers

**Venue Innovation:**
- Combined multiple venues
- Arena + Theater hybrid
- Digital enables what physical couldn't

**Lesson:** Digital can blend venue characteristics in new ways

## Common Venue Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Ignoring Venue Entirely
- **Problem:** Design in vacuum, focus only on mechanics
- **Result:** Great mechanics that don't fit where played
- **Solution:** Always consider actual play context

### Mistake 2: Wrong Venue for Game Type
- **Problem:** Complex strategy game designed for "anywhere"
- **Result:** Players can't engage properly
- **Solution:** Match complexity to venue

### Mistake 3: Fighting Venue Constraints
- **Problem:** Trying to force long sessions in "anywhere" venue
- **Result:** Player frustration, abandoned sessions
- **Solution:** Embrace venue characteristics

### Mistake 4: Following Technology Instead of Venue
- **Problem:** "VR is new, let's make VR game for everyone"
- **Result:** Workbench game marketed to hearth audience
- **Solution:** Understand venue first, technology second

## Future of Venues

### What Will Change
- **Technologies:** Constantly evolving
- **Platforms:** New ones every few years
- **Specific games:** Come and go

### What Won't Change
- **Basic venues:** Been with us for millennia
- **Human needs:** Privacy, social connection, exploration
- **Fundamental patterns:** Where and why we play

### Emerging Trends
- **AR Glasses:** May enable new gaming table experiences
- **Rugged Mobile:** May open playground venue
- **VR Advancement:** Currently workbench, may expand
- **Venue Blending:** Technology enables hybrid venues

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Venues persist, platforms don't:** Focus on where people play, not what they play on
2. **Each venue has characteristics:** Privacy, social context, posture, time, interruptions
3. **Mechanics must match venue:** Harmony = success, conflict = failure
4. **Don't design in vacuum:** Always consider actual play context
5. **Multiple venues possible:** Some games work in several venues
6. **LBE rule:** Give them something they can't get at home
7. **Test in real venue:** Playtesting in natural environment reveals truth
8. **Venues change slowly:** Hearth, workbench, table have existed for centuries
9. **Technology enables new combinations:** But doesn't change fundamental venues
10. **See past the technology:** Focus on human patterns and places

## Practical Application Checklist

When designing your game, ask:

**Venue Identification:**
- [ ] What venue(s) is my game for?
- [ ] What are characteristics of that venue?
- [ ] Are there multiple venues to consider?

**Venue Harmony:**
- [ ] Do my mechanics fit the venue?
- [ ] What venue constraints must I respect?
- [ ] What venue opportunities can I leverage?

**Venue Conflicts:**
- [ ] What mechanics conflict with venue?
- [ ] Can I adjust them or must I change venue?
- [ ] Am I fighting venue characteristics?

**Venue Testing:**
- [ ] Have I playtested in the actual venue?
- [ ] Do players naturally play as I expect?
- [ ] What venue-specific issues emerge?

**Venue Future:**
- [ ] Is my target venue stable or changing?
- [ ] What technology changes might affect it?
- [ ] Am I following venue or following tech?
