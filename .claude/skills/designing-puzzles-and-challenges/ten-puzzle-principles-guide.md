# The Ten Puzzle Design Principles - Detailed Guide

Complete reference for applying Jesse Schell's ten puzzle design principles.

---

## Principle #1: Make the Goal Easily Understood

**Core Concept:** Players must know what they're trying to achieve.

### Why It Matters
If players don't understand the goal, they'll lose interest quickly—unless discovering the goal is intentionally part of the fun (use this sparingly; it appeals mainly to hardcore puzzle fans).

### Examples

**Clear Goal (Good):**
- Jigsaw puzzle: Complete the picture
- Disk-on-shaft puzzle: Remove the disk from the shaft
- 15 Puzzle: Arrange tiles in numerical order 1-15

**Unclear Goal (Problematic):**
- Hasbro's Nemesis Factor: 100 puzzles with different, unclear goals
- Abstract colored object with no obvious objective

### Application Guidelines
1. Make the victory condition immediately visible or easy to describe
2. If goal discovery IS the puzzle, ensure it's still fun (not frustrating)
3. Use familiar patterns when possible (players recognize puzzle types)
4. State the goal explicitly in text if visual clarity isn't enough

## Principle #2: Make It Easy to Get Started

**Core Concept:** Once players understand the goal, they need to know how to begin.

### Why It Matters
Even with a clear goal, players will abandon a puzzle if they can't figure out initial steps.

### "Build a Good Toy First"
> "To design a good puzzle, first build a good toy." — Scott Kim

Players should be drawn to manipulate the puzzle naturally:
- Rubik's Cube: People want to touch it, hold it, twist it
- 15 Puzzle: Sliding tiles is obviously how you interact
- Physical puzzles: Tactile appeal invites experimentation

### Examples

**Easy to Start (Good):**
- 15 Puzzle: Goal is clear (numerical order), and sliding tiles is obvious
- Rubik's Cube: Twisting faces is immediately apparent
- Crossword: Pick any clue and start guessing

**Hard to Start (Problematic):**
- Cryptarithmetic puzzles (SEND + MORE = MONEY): Most players have no idea where to begin
- Abstract logical puzzles with no intuitive entry point

### Application Guidelines
1. First few moves should be obvious or intuitive
2. Make the puzzle feel like something familiar
3. Create tactile or interactive appeal (especially for physical puzzles)
4. Use Lens #17: The Toy - Does it invite manipulation?

### Related Lens
**Lens #17: The Toy** - Good toys make manipulation obvious and appealing.

## Principle #3: Give a Sense of Progress

**Core Concept:** Players need to feel they're getting closer to the solution.

### Progress vs. Riddles
- **Riddles:** Just think and guess (right or wrong). No progress. Frustrating.
- **Puzzles:** Manipulate and see progress bit by bit. Hopeful.

### The Power of Progress
- **Rubik's Cube:** Completing one side feels like huge progress (even though it's only 1/6 done)
- **Twenty Questions:** Each question narrows the space of answers (220 = 1 million+ possibilities)
- **Jigsaw Puzzle:** Frame completed → sections assembled → final pieces

### Turning Riddles into Puzzles
Twenty Questions transforms a riddle into a puzzle by adding:
- Incremental information gathering
- Visible narrowing of possibilities
- Clear steps toward the answer

### Application Guidelines
1. Break puzzles into sub-goals with visible completion
2. Show partial solutions or partial credit
3. Use meters, counters, or visual indicators of advancement
4. Avoid pure "guess until you get it right" mechanics
5. Create milestone celebrations for significant progress

### Broader Application
Visible progress matters beyond puzzles:
- Games: Level progression, skill trees, achievements
- Workplace: The primary driver of mood is visible progress
- Economics: Inflation exists partly because people want to see yearly raises (progress)

## Principle #4: Give a Sense of Solvability

**Core Concept:** Players must believe the puzzle CAN be solved.

### Why It Matters
If players suspect a puzzle is unsolvable, they'll fear wasting time and quit in disgust.

### Rubik's Cube's Elegant Solution
When purchased, Rubik's Cube is already solved. You scramble it yourself (usually ~12 twists).

**What this communicates:**
- Proof it's solvable (you saw it solved)
- Should be solvable in ~12 moves backward (spoiler: it's not that simple!)
- Players may get frustrated but never doubt it CAN be done

### Other Approaches
- State explicitly: "This puzzle has a solution"
- Show visible progress (see Principle #3)
- Provide examples of similar puzzles being solved
- Show partial solutions or completed sections
- Include a "solutions" section (even if players try not to look)

### Application Guidelines
1. Let players see the solved state first (then scramble)
2. Use explicit statements if needed
3. Provide social proof (other players solved it)
4. Consider difficulty ratings to set expectations
5. Never present potentially unsolvable puzzles

## Principle #5: Increase Difficulty Gradually

**Core Concept:** Difficulty should ramp up through a series of increasingly challenging actions.

### The Jigsaw Puzzle Model

**Natural difficulty progression:**
1. Flip pieces picture-side up (mindlessly easy)
2. Find corner pieces (very easy)
3. Find edge pieces (easy)
4. Connect edge pieces into frame (slight challenge, rewarding)
5. Sort remaining pieces by color (easy)
6. Assemble obvious sections (moderate challenge)
7. Assemble pieces that could go anywhere (significant challenge)

**What makes this work:**
- Each step builds on previous ones
- Players get frequent small wins
- Difficulty increases naturally
- Players control the pace

### Breaking the Model
"One Tough Puzzle" eliminates steps 1-6, making it immediately difficult. Result: Interesting as a novelty but lacks the satisfying gradual progression.

### Player Control Over Difficulty
**Crossword puzzles:**
- Dozens of questions at various difficulty levels
- Players naturally start with easy ones they know
- Each answer provides hints for harder questions
- Players create their own difficulty curve

This leads directly to Principle #6 (Parallelism).

### Application Guidelines
1. Design a chain of goals with rising difficulty
2. Give players choice in ordering challenges when possible
3. Reward intermediate steps, not just final solution
4. Test the difficulty curve with actual players
5. Avoid sudden difficulty spikes

## Principle #6: Parallelism Lets the Player Rest

**Core Concept:** Multiple related puzzles prevent frustration by allowing breaks.

### The Danger of Single-Path Puzzles
Players who get stuck on one puzzle with no alternatives will:
- Bang their head against it repeatedly
- Grow frustrated
- Abandon the game entirely

### The Power of Parallelism
Give players several related puzzles simultaneously:
- Stuck on one? Try another for a while
- Taking a break from the first puzzle
- Return with renewed vigor
- "A change is as good as a rest"

### Natural Parallelism Examples
- **Crossword puzzles:** Dozens of clues; work on any order
- **Sudoku:** Multiple rows/columns to focus on
- **RPGs:** Multiple quests; switch when one becomes frustrating
- **Open-world games:** Dozens of challenges available simultaneously

### Application Guidelines
1. Identify bottlenecks where players might get stuck
2. Provide at least 2-3 parallel challenges
3. Make parallel challenges meaningfully different (not just variations)
4. Consider connecting challenges (progress on one helps others)
5. Allow players to choose their path

### Related Lens
**Lens #56: Parallelism** - Use to prevent frustration bottlenecks.

**Questions to ask:**
- Are there bottlenecks where players can't proceed without solving a specific challenge?
- Can I add parallel challenges when they get stuck?
- Are my parallel challenges different enough to provide variety?
- Can parallel challenges be connected so progress on one helps others?

## Principle #7: Pyramid Structure Extends Interest

**Core Concept:** Small puzzles feed clues to larger puzzles, creating short- and long-term goals.

### The Jumble Example

**Structure:**
1. Four small scrambled words (short-term goals)
2. Each unscrambled word reveals letters for a larger phrase
3. The phrase is the punch line to the cartoon joke (long-term goal)

**Why it works:**
- Multiple levels of satisfaction
- Clear singular goal at the top (the punch line)
- Gradual difficulty increase
- Meaningful interconnection

### Pyramid Properties
- **The Point:** A singular, clear, compelling final goal
- **Foundation:** Multiple smaller, accessible challenges
- **Hierarchy:** Intermediate layers build toward the top
- **Motivation:** The top draws players upward through the layers

### Application Guidelines
1. Design a compelling final challenge (the "point")
2. Break it into sub-challenges that feed into it
3. Create hierarchies: small puzzles → medium puzzles → large puzzle
4. Ensure the top challenge is worth the effort
5. Make connections between layers clear

### Related Lens
**Lens #57: The Pyramid** - Structure challenges with a singular peak.

**Questions to ask:**
- Can all pieces feed into a singular challenge at the end?
- Can I have a hierarchy of puzzle elements leading to a final challenge?
- Is the challenge at the top interesting, compelling, and clear?

## Principle #8: Hints Extend Interest

**Core Concept:** Well-timed hints can prevent abandonment while preserving satisfaction.

### The Hint Paradox
**Objection:** "What's the point of a puzzle if we give hints?"

**Reality:** Solving with a hint is better than not solving at all.

### When Hints Matter
- Player is about to give up in frustration
- Frustration has reached a breaking point
- Progress has completely stalled

### Hint System Design

**Hasbro's Nemesis Factor approach:**
1. Press "hint" button → Brief cryptic hint ("staircase" or "music")
2. Press again → Less cryptic hint
3. Slight point penalty for using hints
4. Players prefer penalty over abandonment

**Mobile "Escape the Room" approach:**
- Games are free to play
- Hints cost money
- Monetization + frustration management

### Modern Context: Walk-Throughs
With online walk-throughs available, in-game hints might seem obsolete. But:
- Solving with a hint is more satisfying than copying someone else's answer
- Keeps players in your game (not browsing the web)
- You control hint quality and pacing
- Can integrate hints into fiction/narrative

### Application Guidelines
1. Design multi-level hints (cryptic → clear)
2. Add cost or penalty to maintain challenge respect
3. Time hints to prevent abandonment, not trivialize the puzzle
4. Consider diegetic hints (within the game world/fiction)
5. Test hint effectiveness with players

## Principle #9: Give the Answer!

**Core Concept:** The "Aha!" experience comes from seeing the answer, not just solving it yourself.

### The Pleasure of Solving

**Question:** What makes puzzle-solving pleasurable?

**Common answer:** The "Aha!" moment when you figure it out.

**Reality:** The "Aha!" is triggered by SEEING the answer, whether you solved it or not.

### Mystery Novel Analogy
Mystery novels are "big puzzles in book form":
- Sometimes readers guess the ending (satisfying)
- More often readers are surprised: "Oh! The butler did it! I see now!"
- The surprise can be MORE pleasurable than solving it yourself
- The brain was primed by considering the problem

### Implementation in Games
**Internet age reality:**
- Puzzle answers are posted online quickly
- Players will find walk-throughs if desperate
- You don't control whether they can see answers

**Take control:**
- Provide in-game answer reveals
- Better experience than leaving your game to search
- Maintain narrative/fiction integration
- Control the timing and presentation
- Players appreciate the option

### Application Guidelines
1. Consider an "answer" system after players have tried enough
2. Add cost or delay to preserve challenge value
3. Make seeing the answer satisfying (presentation matters)
4. Recognize that "Aha!" from seeing ≈ "Aha!" from solving
5. Don't force players to leave your game for answers

### The Brain Science
If a player has genuinely considered a problem:
- Their brain is primed for insight
- Seeing the answer triggers pleasure
- The rush comes from understanding, not just discovery
- Some shame if they didn't solve it, but still pleasurable

## Principle #10: Perceptual Shifts Are a Double-Edged Sword

**Core Concept:** "You either get it or you don't" puzzles are risky.

### The Matchstick Puzzle Example
**Puzzle:** Arrange six matchsticks to form four equilateral triangles.

**Possible outcomes:**
1. You've seen it before → No "Aha!" (just smugness)
2. You made the perceptual shift → Very exciting!
3. Someone told you the answer → Bit of "Aha!" + bit of shame
4. You gave up in frustration → Shame, no satisfaction

*Note: The solution requires thinking in 3D—build a tetrahedron (pyramid).*

### The Problem
**Perceptual shift puzzles:**
- No gradual progress possible
- No intermediate steps
- Almost like riddles (not real puzzles)
- Lot of staring and hoping for inspiration
- Binary outcome: breakthrough or frustration

### When They Work
**Occasional use in specific contexts:**
- Audience expects sudden insights (puzzle enthusiasts)
- Part of a larger set of varied puzzles
- Optional challenges (not required for progress)
- When failure doesn't block the player

### When They Don't Work
**Avoid in these situations:**
- Required for game progress
- General audience (not puzzle experts)
- No alternative challenges (see Principle #6)
- No hint system available
- Player expects continuous progress

### Application Guidelines
1. Use perceptual shift puzzles sparingly
2. Make them optional when possible
3. Combine with parallelism (multiple paths forward)
4. Provide hint systems for perceptual puzzles
5. Know your audience (puzzle fans vs. general players)
6. Consider time limits or "give up" options

### The Spectrum
- **Many perceptual shifts:** Frustrating for most players
- **Occasional perceptual shifts:** Exciting variety
- **No perceptual shifts:** May feel too predictable

Find the right balance for your audience and genre.

## Summary: Applying All Ten Principles

### Quick Decision Tree

**For every puzzle, ask:**
1. Is the goal clear? (Principle #1)
2. Can players start easily? (Principle #2)
3. Will they see progress? (Principle #3)
4. Do they believe it's solvable? (Principle #4)
5. Does difficulty ramp gradually? (Principle #5)

**For sequences and systems:**
6. Are there parallel alternatives? (Principle #6)
7. Do small puzzles build to big ones? (Principle #7)
8. Is there a hint system? (Principle #8)
9. Can players eventually see answers? (Principle #9)
10. How many perceptual shifts? (Principle #10 - use sparingly)

### The Goal
Create puzzles that:
- Engage players mentally
- Feel fair and solvable
- Provide satisfying "Aha!" moments
- Don't create frustration bottlenecks
- Integrate smoothly into your game

**Remember:** Modern puzzle design favors integration over separation. The best puzzles don't feel like "stopping to do a puzzle"—they feel like natural challenges within the game world.
