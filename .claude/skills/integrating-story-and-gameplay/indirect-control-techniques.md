# Indirect Control Techniques

Indirect control is the art of giving players the **feeling** of freedom while guiding them toward optimal experiences. This solves the fundamental conflict between story (needs structure) and gameplay (needs freedom).

## The Core Principle

**You don't always need to give players true freedom—you only need to give them the *feeling* of freedom.**

**Why this works:** All that's real is what you feel. If players feel free, they ARE free, experientially.

**The benefit:** Players get wonderful feeling of control + Designer gets optimal interest curve and event sequence.

## The Six Methods

### Method #1: Constraints

**Principle:** Limit choices to manageable, attractive options.

#### The Psychology

**Compare these requests:**

**Request 1:** Pick a color: ________________
- Millions of possible answers
- Daunting freedom
- Forces imagination to work hard

**Request 2:** Pick a color: (a) red (b) blue (c) green
- Three choices
- Still freedom (you choose)
- Much easier to decide
- People who wanted red/blue/green won't notice limitation

**Key insight:** People who would have chosen within your constraints don't notice the constraint.

#### Example: The Candy Store

**First attempt:** "What flavors do you have?"
- Recite all 60 flavors
- Customer eyes widen with fear
- Around flavor #32: "Stop! Stop! That's enough!"
- **Result:** Overwhelmed, frustrated, no sale

**Second attempt:** "Name the flavors you'd like—we have them all"
- Impressive freedom initially
- Then: "Uh... cherry? No wait... peppermint? No... forget it"
- **Result:** Too much work, walked away frustrated

**Winning approach:** "We have every flavor, but our most popular are cherry, blueberry, lemon, root beer, wintergreen, and licorice"
- Feeling of freedom (can imagine beyond the six)
- Clear, attractive choices
- Most customers chose from the "popular six" (a list changed frequently)
- **Result:** High sales, happy customers

#### In Games: The Two-Door Room

**Setup:** Player in empty room with two doors

**What happens:**
- Player will almost certainly go through one door
- Which one? Unknown
- But definitely through one

**Why it works:**
- Door = message "open me"
- Players are naturally curious
- Nowhere else to go
- Two options = choice (feels free)
- Limited options = predictable behavior

**Contrast:** Player in open field, city street, or shopping mall
- Far more options
- Unpredictable behavior
- Harder to design around
- Unless you use other methods...

#### When to Use Constraints

✓ **Too many choices overwhelm players**
✓ **Need predictable behavior for pacing**
✓ **Want players to notice certain options**
✓ **Limited development resources**
✓ **Tutorial/introduction sections**

#### Implementation Techniques

**Physical constraints:**
- Walls, locked doors, impassable terrain
- Linear corridors opening to wider spaces
- Blocked paths that unlock later

**Contextual constraints:**
- Time limits
- Resource limits
- Character abilities
- Equipment requirements

**Choice constraints:**
- Multiple choice dialogs
- Limited action palette
- Specific interaction points
- Discrete options instead of open-ended

#### Pitfall to Avoid

**Too obvious constraints feel oppressive:**
- Invisible walls
- Artificial restrictions
- "You can't go there yet" with no reason

**Better: Make constraints feel natural**
- Logical barriers
- Diegetic restrictions
- Story-justified limits

### Method #2: Goals

**Principle:** Clear objectives guide player behavior predictably.

#### The Basic Mechanism

**Without goal:**
- Player in room with two doors
- Could go through either
- Unpredictable

**With goal:**
- "Find all the bananas"
- One door clearly has bananas visible
- Player goes to that door
- **Behavior now predictable**

#### Goals Shape Exploration

**Example: Racing Game**

**Goal:** Race to finish line as fast as possible

**Design implication:**
- Don't need complete street map
- Just need fastest route clearly marked
- Can add some side streets (feeling of freedom)
- Shortcuts enhance goal pursuit
- Players won't explore every alley

**Result:** Goal indirectly controls where players go, so you only build what they'll see.

#### The Confidence Multiplier

**High player confidence:**
- Clear goals
- Confident ability to interact
- **Result:** Powerful indirect control (players trust instincts)

**Low player confidence:**
- Confused about goals
- Doubtful about interactions
- **Result:** Weak indirect control (players second-guess everything)

**Lesson:** Make goals crystal clear for maximum control effect.

#### Real-World Example: Schiphol Airport Urinals

**Problem:** Sloppy aim = excessive janitorial needs

**Solution:** Etched fly in urinal surface

**How it works:**
- Creates implicit goal: Hit the fly
- Placed in optimal position (center, slightly to side)
- Users aim there without thinking
- Bathrooms stay cleaner

**Key points:**
- Users retain complete freedom
- Behavior indirectly controlled
- Optimal result for designers
- Users barely notice

#### When to Use Goals

✓ **Need players to go specific places**
✓ **Want consistent pacing**
✓ **Teaching mechanics**
✓ **Structuring open worlds**
✓ **Creating sense of purpose**

#### Types of Goals

**Explicit goals:**
- Quest markers
- Waypoints
- Clear objectives ("Collect 10 items")

**Implicit goals:**
- Visible rewards
- Interesting landmarks
- Obvious dangers to avoid
- Mysteries to solve

**Emergent goals:**
- Player-created objectives
- "I want to see if I can..."
- Experimental play
- Mastery challenges

#### Best Practices

1. **Make primary goals obvious**
2. **Use landmarks as goal targets**
3. **Ensure goals align with player motivation**
4. **Layer multiple goals (short/medium/long term)**
5. **Reward progress toward goals constantly**

### Method #3: Interface

**Principle:** Physical and virtual interfaces secretly define what players expect they can do.

#### The Interface Assumption

Players set expectations based on interface. They don't consciously think about it—they just assume possibilities.

#### Example: Rock Star Game

**Plastic guitar interface:**
- Players expect to play guitar
- Won't occur to them to do other rock star activities
- Interface silently limits expectations

**Gamepad interface:**
- Players wonder about other instruments
- Might expect stage dives
- Might think about band management
- Many more imagined possibilities

**Lesson:** Physical interface invisibly constrains thought.

#### Example: Pirates Attraction

**Wooden ship's wheel + heavy aluminum cannons:**
- No guest ever asked about sword fighting
- Interface defined the experience
- Options outside interface didn't enter minds

**If we'd used different interface:**
- Motion controls = might expect acrobatics
- Handheld controllers = might expect boarding actions
- Sword-shaped controllers = completely different expectations

#### Virtual Interface Power

**Avatar choice:**

**Human adventurer:**
- Players try human activities
- Walking, talking, fighting, climbing
- Social interactions

**Dragonfly:**
- Players try flight-based activities
- Maneuverability tests
- Different exploration style

**Elephant:**
- Players try strength-based activities
- Trampling, carrying
- Different problem-solving

**Sherman tank:**
- Players try destruction activities
- Focus on firepower
- Less concern for finesse

**Lesson:** Avatar IS part of interface, defines player expectations.

#### When to Use Interface Control

✓ **Defining scope of gameplay**
✓ **Teaching without explicit tutorial**
✓ **Matching expectations to mechanics**
✓ **Preventing impossible requests**
✓ **Creating focused experiences**

#### Implementation Techniques

**Physical interface:**
- Custom controllers imply specific actions
- Limited buttons = limited action expectations
- Shape/form suggests usage

**Virtual interface:**
- Avatar appearance
- UI elements visible
- Control scheme revealed
- Camera perspective

**Action palette:**
- Visible verbs
- Context-sensitive actions
- Tool/weapon selection

#### Best Practices

1. **Interface should match fantasy**
2. **Don't show unavailable options**
3. **Make controls discoverable but not overwhelming**
4. **Use interface to guide, not frustrate**
5. **Consider what interface implies**

### Method #4: Visual Design

**Principle:** Layout controls where guests look, and where they look determines where they go.

#### The Fundamental Rule

**Control attention = Control behavior**

If you can control where someone looks, you can control where they go.

#### Example: Contrasting Layouts

**Figure 1: Center Focus**
```
→ → → [CENTER] ← ← ←
Eyes drawn to middle
Guest examines center first
Edge of frame ignored
```

**Figure 2: Edge Focus**
```
← [LEFT]    [CENTER]    [RIGHT] →
Eyes drawn to edges and beyond
Guest tries to push past borders
Center ignored
```

**Application:** Same content, different layout = completely different behavior.

#### Disneyland Castle Example

**The problem:** Guests enter, mill about at entrance, unsure where to go

**The solution:** Castle placed to draw eyes immediately upon entry

**How it works:**
- Visual landmark dominates view
- Eyes drawn to it (like Figure 1 center focus)
- Feet follow eyes
- Guests move quickly to Disneyland hub

**At the hub:** Multiple landmarks visible in different directions (like Figure 2 edges)

**Result:**
- Fast movement to center
- Then random branching to areas
- No explicit directions needed
- Feels like total freedom

**Disney term:** "Visual weenie" (from dog training—hold hot dog to control where dog looks)

#### Example: Aladdin VR Throne Room

**Goal:** Players should fly to Sultan's throne and listen to him

**Initial expectation:** Little Sultan in white hopping on throne would be enough

**What actually happened:**
- Players on flying carpets
- Wanted to explore ceiling, pillars, everything
- Implicit goal: Fly and have fun
- Sultan ignored

**Wrong solution considered:** Seize control, force players to throne, lock them there

**Problem with that:** Robs feeling of freedom

**Breakthrough solution:** Paint single red line on floor leading to throne

**Result:**
- Over 90% of players followed line
- Moved right up to Sultan
- Waited to hear him talk
- Felt totally free

**Post-interview:**
- "Why did you follow the red line?"
- "What red line?"
- **Not in conscious memory**

**How it worked:** Red line so visually dominant it prevented players from noticing ceiling/pillars, so flying around didn't occur to them.

#### Example: Four-Player Version

**New goal:** Split players—some to throne, some to side doors

**Problem:** Tyrannical red line made all four go to Sultan

**Solution:** Split the red line

```
    Door (left) ← ← line
            ↓
    line → Throne ← line
            ↓
    line → → Door (right)
```

**Result:**
- Two players to throne
- One player to left door
- One player to right door
- Beautiful distribution

#### Quilt Design Principle

**Good quilt:** Eye flits continuously around, never rests

**Application:** Control how eyes move = control how players explore

#### When to Use Visual Design

✓ **Guiding without waypoints**
✓ **Teaching through environment**
✓ **Pacing exploration**
✓ **Level design**
✓ **Creating flow**

#### Visual Control Techniques

**Lines and paths:**
- Roads, trails, rivers
- Architectural lines
- Light beams
- Contrasting materials

**Landmarks:**
- Towers, mountains, monuments
- Visible from distance
- Distinctive silhouettes
- "Weenies" that pull attention

**Lighting:**
- Bright areas draw attention
- Dark areas hide distractions
- Contrast creates focus
- Color temperature guides emotion

**Color:**
- Saturated colors attract
- Complementary colors guide
- Color trails lead players

**Framing:**
- Doorways, arches, windows
- Natural frames focus attention
- Depth creates interest

**Composition:**
- Rule of thirds
- Leading lines
- Depth layers
- Negative space

#### Best Practices

1. **Test where players actually look**
2. **Remove visual noise from critical paths**
3. **Use contrast to guide**
4. **Make landmarks visible from far away**
5. **Layer multiple techniques**
6. **Consider camera angles**

### Method #5: Characters

**Principle:** When players care about characters, they can be motivated to do (or not do) specific things.

#### The Mechanism

If storytelling makes players care about characters, those characters become tools to control player behavior:

- **Obey them** (trust their guidance)
- **Protect them** (keep them safe)
- **Help them** (fulfill their requests)
- **Destroy them** (seek revenge)

#### Example: Mr. Sardonicus (1961)

**The setup:**
- Interactive movie by William Castle
- Narrator: "The ending is up to you"
- Audience gets thumbs-up/thumbs-down cards
- Should villain suffer or be forgiven?

**Castle's confidence:** Audiences would choose punishment

**His action:** Never filmed the "forgiveness" reel

**Result:** No audience ever noticed

**Lesson:** When you understand character relationships, you can predict player behavior.

#### Example: Animal Crossing - Happy Room Academy (HRA)

**What it is:** Council that evaluates house decoration

**What it does:** Awards points for good decoration

**Why players care:**
- Partly: It's a goal
- Mostly: Embarrassing to think someone looks at your house and shakes their head in disgust
- Even though they're imaginary

**Lesson:** Players respond to implied social judgment.

#### Example: Ico's Princess Protection

**Mechanic:** Princess travels with you

**Timer:** Evil spirits appear if you stay still, try to drag princess into hole

**Technical facts:**
- Spirits can't hurt her unless successful
- Takes time to drag her away
- Not immediate danger

**Player response:** Snap into action instantly

**Why:** Idea of spirits touching her feels like letting her down

**Lesson:** Emotional connection drives urgent action.

#### The Empathy Key

First, make players care. Then characters become powerful motivators.

**How to create care:**
- Develop personality
- Show vulnerability
- Create relationship
- Give characters clear emotions
- Make their needs clear

#### Lens #80: The Lens of Help

Everyone wants to be helpful. Use this to create engaging gameplay.

**Questions:**

1. **Within the context of the game, who is the player helping?**
   - Specific character? Group? Yourself?
   - Is this clear?

2. **Can I make the player feel more connected to the characters who need help?**
   - More personality?
   - Shared experiences?
   - Better communication?

3. **Can I better tell the story of how meeting game goals helps someone?**
   - Make connection explicit
   - Show impact of actions
   - Create emotional payoff

4. **How can the helped characters show their appreciation?**
   - Thank players sincerely
   - Change behavior based on help
   - Remember what player did

#### When to Use Character Control

✓ **Creating emotional investment**
✓ **Motivating specific actions**
✓ **Teaching through friendly NPCs**
✓ **Punishing through antagonists**
✓ **Creating moral choices**
✓ **Pacing revelation of information**

#### Character Control Techniques

**Companion characters:**
- Travel with player
- Need protection/help
- Provide guidance
- Create attachment

**Quest-givers:**
- Likeable personalities
- Clear needs
- Appropriate rewards
- Memorable presentation

**Antagonists:**
- Create desire for revenge
- Motivate opposition
- Raise stakes personally

**Mentors:**
- Earn player trust
- Provide wisdom
- Guide without dictating

**Helpless NPCs:**
- In danger
- Need rescue
- Create urgency
- Appeal to protective instinct

#### Best Practices

1. **Develop memorable personalities**
2. **Show, don't tell, relationships**
3. **Make characters react to player actions**
4. **Use voice/animation to convey emotion**
5. **Give characters clear wants/needs**
6. **Make helping feel rewarding**

### Method #6: Music

**Principle:** Music affects mood, which affects behavior, without players realizing it.

#### The Restaurant Technique

**Fast music during lunch rush:**
- Makes people eat faster
- Faster eating = more table turnover = more profit
- Customers don't realize

**Slow music at 3pm:**
- Empty restaurant = looks bad
- Slow music makes diners linger
- Consider ordering more
- Creates appearance of busy restaurant
- Customers don't realize

**Lesson:** If it works for restaurant managers, it can work for game designers.

#### Music as Emotional Guide

Music is the "language of the soul"—it speaks on deep level that:
- Changes moods
- Shifts desires
- Modifies actions
- All without conscious awareness

#### What Music Can Make Players Do

**Look around for something hidden:**
- Mysterious, quiet music
- Occasional curious notes
- Builds anticipation

**Destroy everything without slowing down:**
- High-energy, aggressive music
- Fast tempo
- Heavy rhythm

**Realize they're heading the wrong way:**
- Dissonant, unsettling music
- Creating unease
- "Something's wrong here"

**Move slowly and carefully:**
- Sparse, quiet music
- Building tension
- Every sound matters

**Worry about innocent bystanders:**
- Gentle, emotional music
- Reminds of consequences
- Creates moral weight

**Go as far and fast as possible:**
- Triumphant, racing music
- Never stops
- Creates momentum

#### When to Use Music Control

✓ **Setting emotional tone**
✓ **Pacing player actions**
✓ **Creating tension/release**
✓ **Signaling danger/safety**
✓ **Teaching through emotion**
✓ **Enhancing narrative moments**

#### Music Control Techniques

**Tempo:**
- Fast = urgency
- Slow = deliberation
- Variable = unpredictability

**Volume:**
- Loud = confident action
- Quiet = careful exploration
- Dynamic range = emotional impact

**Mood:**
- Major keys = positive/triumphant
- Minor keys = sad/tense
- Modal = mysterious/ancient

**Instrumentation:**
- Heavy = power
- Delicate = fragility
- Familiar = comfort
- Strange = unease

**Adaptive music:**
- Changes with player action
- Reinforces correct behavior
- Warns of mistakes
- Rewards success

#### Best Practices

1. **Match music to desired player behavior**
2. **Use silence strategically**
3. **Test different music tracks**
4. **Consider cultural associations**
5. **Let music ebb and flow**
6. **Make transitions smooth**

## Advanced Technique: Collusion

**Definition:** Characters serve dual goals—their in-world objectives AND designer objectives.

### The Concept

**Characters have two simultaneous goals:**

1. **In-world goal:** What makes sense for the character (fight player, protect treasure, etc.)
2. **Designer goal:** Guide players to optimal experience (lead to interesting places, maintain pacing, etc.)

### Example: Pirates Treasure Game

**Challenge:** 5-minute experience, need excellent interest curve

**Initial approach:** Players battle enemies on open ocean

**Problem:**
- Fun for 2 minutes 20 seconds
- Then players ask "is this all we do?"
- Unacceptable interest curve

**Wanted:** Interesting scenarios near islands

**Tried:** Visual landmarks (islands) would draw players

**Failed:** Players surrounded by enemy ships with bright sails, islands barely noticeable, totally ignored

**Considered:** Force players onto fixed path

**Nobody liked that:** Robs freedom

**Breakthrough solution:**

**New enemy ship behavior:**
1. Attack players initially (in-world goal: fight)
2. Then flee (designer goal: lead players somewhere)
3. Flee toward random island (designer goal: structured experience)
4. Players pursue (feels natural)
5. By time players sink ships, they're at island
6. Players look up, discover interesting scenario
7. Complete scenario, new ships appear
8. New ships flee to different island
9. Repeat

**Results:**
- Feeling of total freedom
- Actually very structured experience
- Perfect pacing: battle → scenario → battle → scenario
- Players never noticed manipulation

### Example: Façade

**What it is:** Interactive dinner party with married couple (Grace and Trip)

**Your role:** Guest at their anniversary party

**Interface:** Mostly typing (tremendous freedom)

**Situation:** Only guest, couple constantly bickering, trying to get you to take sides

**The innovation:**

Grace and Trip are AI characters with:
1. **Character goals:** Personal relationship drama they're trying to resolve
2. **Story goals:** Aware they're in story, trying to make it interesting

**Decision-making:**
- What to say based on character motivation
- But also: Is tension appropriate for this part of story?
- Encoded ideal tension curve over time

**Result:**
- Characters make sense
- Story stays interesting
- Player has freedom
- Experience remains engaging
- Each playthrough unique (only ~10% of dialog heard per session)

### How to Implement Collusion

1. **Define ideal player experience flow**
   - What sequence creates best pacing?
   - Where should high/low points be?
   - What should players see?

2. **Give characters in-world goals that make sense**
   - Can't be purely designer servants
   - Must feel authentic
   - Have logical motivations

3. **Add secret designer goal layer**
   - Lead players to interesting places
   - Maintain tension curve
   - Pace revelations
   - Create variety

4. **Test for naturalness**
   - Does it feel contrived?
   - Do players notice?
   - Is it TOO obvious?

### Lens #82: The Lens of Collusion

**Questions:**

1. **What do I want the player to experience?**
   - Ideal pacing?
   - Key moments?
   - Emotional journey?

2. **How can the characters help fulfill this experience, without compromising their goals in the game world?**
   - What behaviors serve both?
   - Where can dual goals align?
   - How to hide designer intent?

### When to Use Collusion

✓ **Need structured experience with freedom feeling**
✓ **Pacing is critical**
✓ **Linear sequence in non-linear world**
✓ **Teaching without explicit tutorial**
✓ **Creating dynamic narrative**

## Combining Methods

### Layering for Power

**Most effective:** Use multiple methods together

**Example: Linear Level**
- Constraints: Walls funnel players
- Goals: Clear objective visible ahead
- Interface: Movement abilities match path
- Visual: Lighting guides forward
- Characters: Companion suggests direction
- Music: Tempo matches desired pace

**Result:** Very strong control, still feels free

### Example: Open World Gentle Guidance

- Constraints: Edges of playable area
- Goals: Quest markers (optional)
- Interface: Map shows possibilities
- Visual: Landmarks attract attention
- Characters: NPCs provide rumors
- Music: Adaptive to player discoveries

**Result:** Freedom preserved, guidance available

### The Balance

**Too much control:**
- Feels oppressive
- Players notice
- Rebellion or frustration

**Too little control:**
- Players lost
- Experience unfocused
- Weak pacing

**Right amount:**
- Players feel free
- Experience is optimal
- Control invisible

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake #1: Too Obvious

**Problem:** Players notice the control
**Example:** Invisible walls, forced cutscenes, obvious breadcrumb trails
**Fix:** Make control feel natural, diegetic, motivated

### Mistake #2: Fighting Player Desires

**Problem:** Trying to control players against their instincts
**Example:** Slow-paced puzzle during action climax
**Fix:** Work with player momentum, not against it

### Mistake #3: Inconsistent Control

**Problem:** Sometimes guide, sometimes force, no pattern
**Example:** Some objectives optional, identical-looking ones required
**Fix:** Establish consistent language of control

### Mistake #4: Forgetting to Give Freedom

**Problem:** All control, no genuine choice
**Example:** Pure string of pearls with no player input
**Fix:** Create spaces of genuine freedom between controlled sections

### Mistake #5: Inducing Wrong Desires

**Problem:** Design accidentally makes players want wrong things
**Example:** Circular theater with chairs that strap in, but doesn't spin (alien encounter story)
**Fix:** Use Lens #81 to check for unintended suggestions

## Lens #81: The Lens of Indirect Control

**Use this lens to ensure proper control:**

**Questions:**

1. **Ideally, what would I like the players to do?**
   - Be specific
   - Sequence matters
   - Include timing

2. **Can constraints get players to do it?**
   - Limit options?
   - Funnel paths?

3. **Can goals get players to do it?**
   - Clear objectives?
   - Visible rewards?

4. **Can interface get players to do it?**
   - Control scheme?
   - Avatar choice?

5. **Can visual design get players to do it?**
   - Landmarks?
   - Lines?
   - Lighting?

6. **Can game characters get players to do it?**
   - Companions?
   - Quest-givers?
   - Empathy?

7. **Can music or sound get players to do it?**
   - Tempo?
   - Mood?
   - Adaptive response?

8. **Is there some other method I can use to coerce players toward ideal behavior without impinging on their feeling of freedom?**
   - Novel approaches?
   - Combinations?

9. **Is my design inducing desires I'd rather the player NOT have?**
   - Unintended suggestions?
   - False expectations?
   - Wrong goals?

## The Lao Tzu Principle

> "When the best leader's work is done, the people say 'We did it ourselves!'"

The goal of indirect control is for players to feel total agency while having optimal experiences.

**If they notice the control, it's too strong.**
**If the experience is chaotic, it's too weak.**
**If they feel free and satisfied, it's perfect.**
