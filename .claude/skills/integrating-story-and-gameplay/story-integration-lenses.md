# Story Integration Design Lenses

This guide compiles all design lenses related to integrating story and gameplay, including story-specific lenses and indirect control lenses.

Use these lenses throughout development to ensure story and gameplay support each other effectively.

## Story-Focused Lenses

### Lens #73: The Story Machine

**Purpose:** Make your game generate better player stories

A good game is a machine that generates stories when people play it. To make your story machine as productive as possible, ask:

1. **When players have different choices about how to achieve goals, new and different stories can arise. How can I add more of these choices?**
   - Are there multiple paths to objectives?
   - Can players choose different strategies?
   - Do choices lead to meaningfully different outcomes?
   - Can I add more decision points?

2. **Different conflicts lead to different stories. How can I allow more types of conflict to arise from my game?**
   - What conflicts are possible?
   - Can I add more conflict types?
   - Do systems create emergent conflicts?
   - Are conflicts interesting enough to talk about?

3. **When players can personalize the characters and setting, they will care more about story outcomes, and similar stories can start to feel very different. How can I let players personalize the story?**
   - Character customization?
   - Naming things?
   - Aesthetic choices?
   - Strategic/playstyle choices?
   - Does personalization affect outcomes?

4. **Good stories have good interest curves. Do my rules lead to stories with good interest curves?**
   - Natural rise and fall of tension?
   - Climactic moments possible?
   - Pacing feels right?
   - Do stories have satisfying conclusions?

5. **A story is only good if you can tell it. Who can your players tell the story to that will actually care?**
   - Other players?
   - Friends/family?
   - Online communities?
   - Social media?
   - Are stories memorable and shareable?

**When to use:**
- Designing emergent narrative systems
- Creating sandbox/simulation games
- Building replayable experiences
- Reducing prescripted content

**Related to:** Story machine approach (see [story-game-integration-methods.md](story-game-integration-methods.md))

### Lens #74: The Obstacle

**Purpose:** Ensure obstacles create meaningful challenges and drama

A goal with no obstacles is not worth pursuing. Use this lens to make sure your obstacles are ones that your players will want to overcome.

1. **What is the relationship between the main character and the goal? Why does the character care about it?**
   - Personal stakes?
   - Emotional investment?
   - Logical motivation?
   - Does player understand why this matters?

2. **What are the obstacles between the character and the goal?**
   - List them explicitly
   - Are they varied?
   - Do they make sense?
   - Are they interesting to overcome?

3. **Is there an antagonist who is behind the obstacles? What is the relationship between the protagonist and the antagonist?**
   - Personal connection?
   - Opposing philosophies?
   - History between them?
   - Does antagonist have clear motivation?

4. **Do the obstacles gradually increase in difficulty?**
   - Natural progression?
   - Appropriate pacing?
   - Too easy/too hard anywhere?
   - Good difficulty curve?

5. **Some say "the bigger the obstacle, the better the story." Are your obstacles big enough? Can they be bigger?**
   - Raising stakes possible?
   - More dramatic challenges?
   - Higher consequences?
   - Don't be timid?

6. **Great stories often involve the protagonist transforming in order to overcome the obstacle. How does your protagonist transform?**
   - Character arc?
   - Skill progression?
   - Emotional growth?
   - Does transformation feel earned?

**When to use:**
- Designing main story path
- Creating boss encounters
- Structuring narrative beats
- Balancing challenge progression

**Related to:** Goals, obstacles, and conflicts (see [ten-story-tips-guide.md](ten-story-tips-guide.md))

### Lens #75: Simplicity and Transcendence

**Purpose:** Achieve the powerful combination that makes game worlds appealing

Game worlds offer simplicity (simpler than real world) and transcendence (more powerful than in real world). To ensure right mix:

1. **How is my world simpler than the real world? Can it be simpler in other ways?**
   - What complexities removed?
   - Rules straightforward?
   - Fewer variables?
   - Easy to understand?
   - Can simplify further?

2. **What kind of transcendent power do I give to the player? How can I give even more without removing challenge from the game?**
   - What powers/abilities?
   - Feeling of godlike capability?
   - More powerful than reality?
   - Can increase without breaking balance?
   - Push boundaries of power fantasy?

3. **Is my combination of simplicity and transcendence contrived, or does it provide my players with a special kind of wish fulfillment?**
   - Feels natural?
   - Resonates emotionally?
   - Fulfills deep desires?
   - Would players want to be in this world?
   - Does combination make sense?

**When to use:**
- Designing game worlds
- Choosing settings
- Balancing complexity
- Creating power fantasy

**Common combinations:**
- Medieval: Simple tech + magic (transcendence)
- Post-apocalyptic: Simple society + advanced tech
- War: Simple rules + powerful weapons
- Modern: Simple situation + breaking rules/powers

**Related to:** Story world creation (see [ten-story-tips-guide.md](ten-story-tips-guide.md))

### Lens #76: The Hero's Journey

**Purpose:** Use as diagnostic tool for heroic stories

Many heroic stories share similar structure. Use this lens to ensure you haven't missed elements that might improve your story.

1. **Does my story have elements that qualify it as a heroic story?**
   - Hero present?
   - Journey/quest?
   - Transformation?
   - Heroic themes?

2. **If so, how does it match up with the structure of the hero's journey?**
   - Map to 12 steps (ordinary world, call to adventure, refusal, mentor, threshold, tests, cave, ordeal, reward, road back, resurrection, return with elixir)
   - What's present?
   - What's missing?
   - Does structure serve your story?

3. **Would my story be improved by including more archetypical elements?**
   - Missing mentor? Would one help?
   - No threshold crossing? Should there be?
   - Weak ordeal? Make stronger?
   - Opportunities for archetype inclusion?

4. **Does my story match this form so closely that it feels hackneyed?**
   - Too formulaic?
   - Beats too obvious?
   - Need to subvert expectations?
   - Is it fresh or stale?

**When to use:**
- Designing heroic narratives
- Structuring main quest
- Creating character arcs
- Diagnosing weak story

**Important:** This is a diagnostic tool, not a formula. Use AFTER writing story, not as starting point.

**Related to:** Hero's journey (see [ten-story-tips-guide.md](ten-story-tips-guide.md))

### Lens #77: The Weirdest Thing

**Purpose:** Manage surreal elements effectively

Weird things can capture interest and give meaning to unusual mechanics, but too much weirdness makes stories inaccessible.

1. **What's the weirdest thing in my story?**
   - Identify explicitly
   - Is it weird enough to be interesting?
   - Is it TOO weird to understand?
   - Clear identification?

2. **How can I make sure that the weirdest thing doesn't confuse or alienate the player?**
   - Introduce it carefully?
   - Make it normal in your world?
   - Give it clear rules?
   - Explain how it works?
   - Use familiar frame of reference?

3. **If there are multiple weird things, should I maybe get rid of, or coalesce, some of them?**
   - Too many weird things?
   - Can combine related weird things?
   - Would reducing weirdness help?
   - One special thing > ten confusing things?

4. **If there is nothing weird in my story, is the story still interesting?**
   - Sometimes weird = memorable
   - But familiar can be powerful too
   - Need for surprise?
   - Balance of familiar and novel?

**When to use:**
- Introducing surreal elements
- Designing unusual mechanics
- Creating unique worlds
- Making strange things accessible

**Technique:** Introduce weird thing as normal in your world (e.g., "Hamster Cannons on sale!" signs)

**Related to:** Making story accessible (see [ten-story-tips-guide.md](ten-story-tips-guide.md))

### Lens #78: Story

**Purpose:** General-purpose lens for any story element

Use this lens to examine story elements across the elemental tetrad.

1. **Does my game really need a story? Why?**
   - Purpose of story?
   - Does it enhance experience?
   - Would game work without it?
   - Clear justification?

2. **Why will players be interested in this story?**
   - Compelling hook?
   - Relatable characters?
   - Interesting premise?
   - Emotional investment?

3. **How does the story support the other parts of the tetrad (aesthetics, technology, gameplay)? Can it do a better job?**
   - Story justifies aesthetics?
   - Story explains technology?
   - Story motivates gameplay?
   - Integration opportunities?

4. **How do the other parts of the tetrad support the story? Can they do a better job?**
   - Gameplay reinforces narrative?
   - Aesthetics support mood?
   - Technology enables storytelling?
   - Better alignment possible?

5. **How can my story be better?**
   - Surprise?
   - Emotion?
   - Clarity?
   - Unity?
   - Accessibility?

**When to use:**
- Any story design decision
- Reviewing existing story
- Integrating story with other elements
- General story health check

**Remember:** Story should serve experience, not constrain it

## Indirect Control Lenses

### Lens #79: Freedom

**Purpose:** Ensure players feel as free as possible (whether or not they actually are)

Feeling of freedom is what separates games from other entertainment. To make sure players feel free:

1. **When do my players have freedom of action? Do they feel free at these times?**
   - Identify free moments
   - Do players recognize freedom?
   - Does freedom feel meaningful?
   - Are choices clear?

2. **When are they constrained? Do they feel constrained at these times?**
   - Identify constrained moments
   - Do constraints feel oppressive?
   - Are constraints justified?
   - Can constraints be hidden?

3. **Are there any places I can let them feel freer than they do now?**
   - Opportunities for more perceived freedom?
   - Remove unnecessary constraints?
   - Add choices?
   - Better communication of options?

4. **Are there any places where they are overwhelmed by too much freedom?**
   - Decision paralysis?
   - Too many options?
   - Unclear choices?
   - Need more guidance/constraints?

**When to use:**
- Balancing freedom and control
- Level design
- Interaction design
- Pacing experiences

**Key insight:** Feeling of freedom ≠ actual freedom. Indirect control gives feeling without reality.

**Related to:** All indirect control methods (see [indirect-control-techniques.md](indirect-control-techniques.md))

### Lens #80: Help

**Purpose:** Channel players' natural helpful spirit into engaging gameplay

Deep down, everyone wants to be helpful. To channel this:

1. **Within the context of the game, who is the player helping?**
   - Specific character?
   - Group/faction?
   - Themselves?
   - Clear beneficiary?

2. **Can I make the player feel more connected to the characters who need help?**
   - More personality development?
   - Shared experiences?
   - Better emotional bonds?
   - More interaction?

3. **Can I better tell the story of how meeting game goals helps someone?**
   - Make connection explicit?
   - Show impact of actions?
   - Create emotional payoff?
   - Visualize help provided?

4. **How can the helped characters show their appreciation?**
   - Thank players sincerely?
   - Change behavior based on help?
   - Remember player actions?
   - Express gratitude meaningfully?

**When to use:**
- Designing quest structures
- Creating companion characters
- Motivating player actions
- Building emotional investment

**Application:** Characters as indirect control method #5

**Related to:** Characters method (see [indirect-control-techniques.md](indirect-control-techniques.md))

### Lens #81: Indirect Control

**Purpose:** Ensure you're guiding players toward ideal behavior without impinging on freedom

Every designer has a vision of ideal play experience. To help ensure players do these things of their own free will:

1. **Ideally, what would I like the players to do?**
   - Be specific about desired behavior
   - Sequence matters
   - Timing important
   - Clear vision?

2. **Can constraints get players to do it?**
   - Limit options to guide?
   - Funnel paths?
   - Two-door room approach?
   - Physical restrictions?

3. **Can goals get players to do it?**
   - Clear objectives leading that way?
   - Visible rewards?
   - Implicit goals?
   - Meaningful targets?

4. **Can interface get players to do it?**
   - Control scheme suggests it?
   - Avatar choice implies it?
   - UI communicates it?
   - Physical interface guides it?

5. **Can visual design get players to do it?**
   - Landmarks attract attention?
   - Lines guide movement?
   - Lighting draws eyes?
   - Color attracts?
   - Composition leads?

6. **Can game characters get players to do it?**
   - Companions suggest?
   - Quest-givers motivate?
   - NPCs need help there?
   - Empathy drives action?

7. **Can music or sound get players to do it?**
   - Tempo affects pacing?
   - Mood guides behavior?
   - Adaptive audio reinforces?
   - Sound attracts attention?

8. **Is there some other method I can use to coerce players toward ideal behavior without impinging on their feeling of freedom?**
   - Novel approaches?
   - Combinations of methods?
   - Genre-specific techniques?
   - Experimental methods?

9. **Is my design inducing desires I'd rather the player NOT have?**
   - Unintended suggestions?
   - False expectations?
   - Wrong goals activated?
   - Contrary impulses created?

**When to use:**
- Any time you want specific player behavior
- Level design
- Pacing experiences
- Tutorial design
- Structuring exploration

**Lao Tzu principle:** "When the best leader's work is done, the people say 'We did it ourselves!'"

**Related to:** All six methods (see [indirect-control-techniques.md](indirect-control-techniques.md))

### Lens #82: Collusion

**Purpose:** Ensure characters serve both in-world goals and designer goals

Characters should fulfill their roles in game world but also serve as designer's minions, working toward engaging player experience.

1. **What do I want the player to experience?**
   - Ideal pacing?
   - Key moments?
   - Emotional journey?
   - Specific sequence?
   - Tension curve?

2. **How can the characters help fulfill this experience, without compromising their goals in the game world?**
   - What dual-purpose behaviors possible?
   - Where do character and designer goals align?
   - How to hide designer intent?
   - Make it feel natural?
   - Characters still believable?

**When to use:**
- Designing AI behavior
- Creating dynamic narratives
- Pacing player experiences
- Guiding without forcing
- Emergent storytelling with structure

**Examples:**
- Enemy ships that flee toward interesting locations
- NPCs that reveal story at optimal times
- Companions that suggest next steps naturally
- Façade's tension-aware characters

**Related to:** Collusion technique (see [indirect-control-techniques.md](indirect-control-techniques.md))

## How to Use These Lenses

### During Design

**Early phase (concept/prototype):**
- Lens #78: Story (does game need story?)
- Lens #75: Simplicity and Transcendence (world appeal)
- Lens #73: Story Machine (if emergent narrative)

**Mid phase (production):**
- Lens #74: Obstacle (meaningful challenges)
- Lens #76: Hero's Journey (if heroic story)
- Lens #79: Freedom (player agency)
- Lens #81: Indirect Control (guiding players)

**Late phase (polish):**
- Lens #77: Weirdest Thing (accessibility)
- Lens #80: Help (emotional investment)
- Lens #82: Collusion (dynamic pacing)

### During Review

**Story review:**
- Run through Lenses #73, #74, #75, #76, #77, #78
- Check for surprise (Lens #4)
- Check for emotion (Lens #1)

**Player experience review:**
- Run through Lenses #79, #80, #81, #82
- Check interest curve (Lens #69)
- Check goals (Lens #32)

### Iteration

**If story feels weak:**
- Apply Lens #78 (general story health)
- Apply Lens #74 (obstacles compelling?)
- Check surprise and emotion

**If players seem lost:**
- Apply Lens #81 (indirect control)
- Apply Lens #79 (feeling of freedom)
- Increase guidance without removing freedom

**If pacing is off:**
- Apply Lens #82 (collusion to manage pacing)
- Apply Lens #73 (story machine interest curves)
- Check interest curve lens

**If world feels hollow:**
- Apply Lens #75 (simplicity + transcendence)
- Apply Lens #77 (weirdest thing accessible?)
- Make it real (Story Tip #4)

## Lens Combinations

### For Strong Narrative Integration

Use together:
- Lens #78 (Story) - Does story serve experience?
- Lens #74 (Obstacle) - Obstacles = gameplay challenges?
- Lens #81 (Indirect Control) - Guiding through narrative?

### For Emergent Narrative

Use together:
- Lens #73 (Story Machine) - Generate good stories?
- Lens #79 (Freedom) - Players feel free?
- Lens #75 (Simplicity + Transcendence) - World appeals?

### For Character-Driven Games

Use together:
- Lens #80 (Help) - Helping characters?
- Lens #82 (Collusion) - Characters guide?
- Lens #74 (Obstacle) - Character relationships compelling?

### For Open World Guidance

Use together:
- Lens #81 (Indirect Control) - Multiple methods?
- Lens #79 (Freedom) - Balance right?
- Lens #73 (Story Machine) - Emergent stories happening?

## Quick Reference: When to Use Which Lens

| Situation | Primary Lens | Supporting Lenses |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Story too weak | #78 Story | #74 Obstacle, #4 Surprise, #1 Emotion |
| Players feel railroaded | #79 Freedom | #81 Indirect Control, #82 Collusion |
| Emergent narrative lacking | #73 Story Machine | #75 Simplicity/Transcendence, #79 Freedom |
| Pacing problems | #82 Collusion | #81 Indirect Control, #69 Interest Curve |
| Weird elements confusing | #77 Weirdest Thing | #78 Story, #75 Simplicity/Transcendence |
| Players not emotionally invested | #80 Help | #74 Obstacle, #1 Emotion |
| Heroic story feels flat | #76 Hero's Journey | #74 Obstacle, #78 Story |
| World feels hollow | #75 Simplicity/Transcendence | #78 Story |
| Obstacles feel arbitrary | #74 Obstacle | #32 Goals, #78 Story |

## The Integration Principle

**Story lenses + Indirect control lenses = Cohesive experience**

Story lenses help you craft the narrative
Indirect control lenses help you merge it with gameplay
Together, they create experiences where story and game are one

**Test:** Can you remove the story without changing gameplay? If yes, they're not integrated. Use these lenses to fuse them.

---

**Remember:** These lenses are diagnostic tools, not formulas. Apply them to find opportunities for improvement, not to rigidly follow templates.
