# Ten Story Tips for Game Designers

These tips help you craft story elements that enhance your game rather than fight against it. They're practical, battle-tested techniques from veteran game designers.

## Story Tip #1: Respect the Story Stack

**See [story-stack-framework.md](story-stack-framework.md) for complete details.**

**Quick summary:**
- Never start by writing the story
- Design sequence: Fantasy → Action → Economy → World → Story
- Story is most flexible, should come LAST
- If you hear "we can't do that—it goes against the story," story has enslaved you

**Why this is Tip #1:** Because violating the story stack **ruins more games than any other mistake**.

## Story Tip #2: Put Your Story to Work!

### The Principle

Story is remarkably flexible—it can be changed with just a few words. Use this superpower to support gameplay, technology, and aesthetics.

**Story changes:** Minutes
**Gameplay balancing:** Weeks
**Technology changes:** Months

### Example 1: The Green Fog Problem (3DO Game)

**Situation:**
- Flying spaceship game over planet
- Need fog to hide pop-in of distant terrain
- Technical limitation: Only weird green fog available
- Looks completely unrealistic

**Wrong response:** "Scrap the fog, find another solution"
**Right response:** "How can story make this work?"

**Story solution:**
Evil aliens shrouded planet in toxic gas during takeover

**Results:**
- Technical approach now viable
- Gameplay mechanic supported
- Story actually IMPROVED (more dramatic)
- Side benefit: Alien takeover feels more menacing

### Example 2: Mordak's Revenge Board Game

**Initial design:**
- Players collect 5 keys
- Travel to evil wizard Mordak's stronghold
- Unlock stronghold, battle him

**Better gameplay:**
- Mordak should come to player (more immediate)
- Battle could happen in variety of terrains
- More exciting conclusion

**Story obstacle:** "But that doesn't make sense!"

**Story solution:**
- Changed keys to "summoning stones"
- Mordak has secret stronghold no one can find
- Five stones summon him out of hiding
- Forces him to battle player in player's current terrain

**Results:**
- Desired gameplay achieved
- Story more novel than "villain in castle" trope
- Mechanic makes narrative sense
- Better game overall

### How to Put Story to Work

1. **Identify gameplay/technical needs**
   - What do you want players to do?
   - What technical limitations exist?
   - What feels fun but seems illogical?

2. **Ask: "How can story explain this?"**
   - What narrative makes this make sense?
   - Can we add one story element that justifies it?
   - What's the simplest story fix?

3. **Don't fight story changes**
   - Story is pliable—use that
   - A few words can solve big problems
   - Don't be precious about narrative

### Red Flags

- ❌ "We can't change the story, it's already written"
- ❌ "That gameplay breaks the narrative"
- ❌ "The story doesn't allow that"

### Green Lights

- ✓ "How can we adjust the story to support this?"
- ✓ "What simple narrative change makes this work?"
- ✓ "Story is flexible—let's use that"

## Story Tip #3: Goals, Obstacles, and Conflicts

### Hollywood Maxim

Main ingredients for story:
1. A character with a **goal**
2. **Obstacles** that prevent reaching the goal

### Why This Works

- **Problem-solving** → interesting
- **Conflicts** → unpredictable results (surprise) → interesting
- **Big obstacles** → potential for dramatic change → interesting

### In Games, This Is Even More Important

**Character goal = Player goal**
- The goal drives them along the string of pearls (if you use that method)
- Obstacles = challenges the player faces
- Conflicts create drama AND gameplay

### The Critical Alignment

**Strong integration:** Gameplay challenges = Story obstacles
- Player defeating challenge = Character overcoming obstacle
- Game and story fuse into one
- Player feels like part of the story

**Weak integration:** Gameplay challenges unrelated to story obstacles
- Ludonarrative dissonance
- Player doing random things between story beats
- Experience feels disconnected

### Example: Character's Quest

**Character's story goal:** Rescue the princess from the tower

**Weak integration:**
- Gameplay: Solve color-matching puzzles to progress
- Why? Because game designers needed puzzles
- Result: Feels arbitrary

**Strong integration:**
- Gameplay: Battle guards, climb walls, navigate fortress
- Why? These are actual obstacles to rescuing princess
- Result: Every action reinforces story goal

### Creating Strong Obstacles

**Lens #74: The Obstacle**

Questions to ask:

1. **What is the relationship between the main character and the goal? Why does the character care about it?**
   - Weak: "The game told me to do this"
   - Strong: "This matters personally to my character"

2. **What are the obstacles between the character and the goal?**
   - List them explicitly
   - Make sure they're meaningful

3. **Is there an antagonist behind the obstacles? What is the relationship between protagonist and antagonist?**
   - Personal connection increases stakes
   - Antagonist motivation matters

4. **Do the obstacles gradually increase in difficulty?**
   - Rising challenge = rising drama
   - Pacing matters in story AND gameplay

5. **Some say "the bigger the obstacle, the better the story." Are your obstacles big enough? Can they be bigger?**
   - Don't be timid
   - Big obstacles = big satisfaction when overcome

6. **Great stories often involve the protagonist transforming to overcome the obstacle. How does your protagonist transform?**
   - Character arc and player skill progression can align
   - Transformation = growth = satisfying narrative

### Conflicts

**Conflict = Two characters with opposing goals**

This creates:
- Unpredictability (who will win?)
- Drama (stakes are clear)
- Player investment (take a side)

**In multiplayer:** Players naturally create conflict
**In singleplayer:** Antagonist should have clear, opposing goal

## Story Tip #4: Make It Real

> "Know your world as God knows this one." —Robert McKee

### The Difference

**Plotting:** List of characters, events, rules
**Real world:** Place you can see in your mind as if it exists

### How to Get There

Think about your world constantly. Imagine being a character living in it.

**Questions to answer:**
- What does a typical day look like here?
- What do people eat?
- What do they value?
- What makes them laugh?
- What do they fear?
- What happened 100 years ago?
- Why are things the way they are?

### The Disney Secret

Every Disney attraction has elaborate backstory never shared with public:
- Haunted Mansion: Complete history of the house and its owners
- Splash Mountain: Extended character relationships and motivations

Designers know these stories and use them to keep everything consistent.

**Guests don't see it directly, but they feel its presence.**

### Tolkien's Method

Tolkien spent years imagining and documenting Middle-earth:
- Languages
- History
- Genealogies
- Geography
- Cultures

Before stories took shape, the world was REAL to him.

### The Test

Can you answer basic questions about:
- The history of your world?
- The motivations of your characters?
- Why things are the way they are?

If not, it will show. People will recognize it as unimaginative.

### The Golden Rule

**If it's not real to you, it's not real to them.**

## Story Tip #5: Provide Simplicity and Transcendence

### The Powerful Combination

Game worlds typically offer:
- **Simplicity:** World is simpler than reality
- **Transcendence:** Player is more powerful than in reality

This combination explains why certain settings appear repeatedly in games.

### Common Settings and Why They Work

#### Medieval (Swords & Sorcery)

**Simplicity:**
- Primitive technology
- Straightforward social structures
- Clear good vs evil

**Transcendence:**
- Magic powers
- Legendary abilities
- Hero status

**Why it endures:** Combines simple and transcendent in primal way

#### Futuristic (Post-Apocalyptic)

**Simplicity:**
- Society collapsed (no complex rules)
- Frontier setting (lawless)
- Clear survival goals

**Transcendence:**
- Advanced technology
- Superhuman abilities
- Power over life and death

**Why NOT realistic future:** Real future = suburban sprawl, complicated phones, more bureaucracy (neither simple nor transcendent)

#### War

**Simplicity:**
- Normal rules suspended
- Clear objectives
- Binary outcomes

**Transcendence:**
- Powerful weapons
- Godlike power (life or death)
- Extraordinary circumstances

**Note:** Horror in reality, but in fantasy provides both elements

#### Modern (with a twist)

**Usually needs something extra to work:**

**Grand Theft Auto:**
- Simplicity: Life simpler when you ignore laws
- Transcendence: More powerful when you ignore laws

**The Sims:**
- Simplicity: Dollhouse version of human life
- Transcendence: Godlike control over characters

#### Abstract (Minecraft, etc.)

**Simplicity:**
- Simpler than real world
- Simpler than average videogame

**Transcendence:**
- Godlike power to create and destroy
- Total freedom

**Notch's discovery:** This combination is incredibly powerful

### Lens #75: Simplicity and Transcendence

Questions to ensure right mix:

1. **How is my world simpler than the real world? Can it be simpler in other ways?**
   - Identify what you've simplified
   - Consider further simplification

2. **What kind of transcendent power do I give to the player? How can I give even more without removing challenge from the game?**
   - List player powers
   - Push boundaries of power fantasy
   - Balance with challenge

3. **Is my combination of simplicity and transcendence contrived, or does it provide my players with a special kind of wish fulfillment?**
   - Does it feel natural?
   - Does it resonate emotionally?
   - Would players want to be in this world?

### The Balance

Too much simplicity = boring
Too much transcendence = no challenge
The right mix = powerful wish fulfillment

## Story Tip #6: Consider the Hero's Journey

### What It Is

Joseph Campbell's monomyth—underlying structure found in most mythological hero stories.

**Influenced:** Star Wars (George Lucas), The Matrix (Wachowskis), countless others

**Practical guide:** Christopher Vogler's "The Writer's Journey" makes it accessible

### Vogler's 12 Steps

1. **Ordinary world** - Hero's normal life established
2. **Call to adventure** - Challenge disrupts ordinary life
3. **Refusal of the call** - Hero makes excuses
4. **Meeting with the mentor** - Wise figure gives aid
5. **Crossing the threshold** - Leaves ordinary world for adventure world
6. **Tests, allies, enemies** - Minor challenges, makes friends/foes
7. **Approaching the cave** - Encounters setbacks, tries new approach
8. **The ordeal** - Faces peak life-or-death crisis
9. **The reward** - Survives, overcomes fear, gets reward
10. **The road back** - Returns to ordinary world, problems not all solved
11. **Resurrection** - Greater crisis, uses everything learned
12. **Returning with the elixir** - Journey complete, improves everyone's lives

**Note:** You don't need all twelve, or this exact order. It's a form, not a formula.

### Why It's Relevant to Games

Many videogames revolve around theme of heroism. The structure naturally fits.

### How to Use It (Bob Bates' Advice)

**Don't use it as:**
- ❌ Starting point for writing
- ❌ Box of tools to fix every problem
- ❌ Mandatory formula

**Do use it as:**
- ✓ Diagnostic tool
- ✓ Circuit tester for "mythical current"
- ✓ Lens to examine existing story

### The Process

1. Write your story first
2. Notice commonalities with monomyth
3. Consider whether following archetypes more closely would improve it
4. Make informed adjustments

### Lens #76: The Hero's Journey

Questions to ask:

1. **Does my story have elements that qualify it as a heroic story?**
   - Is there a hero?
   - Is there a journey/quest?
   - Is there transformation?

2. **If so, how does it match up with the structure of the hero's journey?**
   - Map your story to the 12 steps
   - Identify what's present and missing

3. **Would my story be improved by including more archetypical elements?**
   - Missing mentor? Would one help?
   - No clear threshold crossing? Should there be?
   - Weak ordeal? Make it stronger?

4. **Does my story match this form so closely that it feels hackneyed?**
   - Too formulaic?
   - Beats too obvious?
   - Need to subvert expectations?

### Connection to Interest Curves

View the Hero's Journey through Lens #69: Interest Curve
- You'll see a familiar rising/falling pattern
- The structure naturally creates good pacing

## Story Tip #7: Keep Your Story World Consistent

### The French Saying

> "If you add a spoonful of wine to a barrelful of sewage,
> you get a barrelful of sewage.
>
> If you add a spoonful of sewage to a barrelful of wine,
> you get a barrelful of sewage."

### The Fragility of Story Worlds

One small inconsistency breaks the reality of the world—forever.

**Damage is permanent:**
- Breaks suspension of disbelief
- Taints past, present, and future
- Can never be fully repaired

### Example: "Jumping the Shark"

**Origin:** *Happy Days* TV show

**What happened:**
- Successful season finale: Fonzie jumps school buses on motorcycle
- Next season: Trying to repeat success, Fonzie jumps shark on water skis
- So ridiculous and out of character that fans were repulsed

**The problem:**
- Not just that one episode was bad
- The character and world were forever tainted
- Could never be taken seriously again

**Term now means:** Point where show/franchise deteriorates irreversibly

### Example: Mass Effect 3 Ending Controversy

**What happened:**
Developers announced patch to change the ending

**Why players were outraged:**
Quality of story matters less than illusion it is REAL. Changing it destroys that illusion.

**The lesson:**
Perceived reality > narrative quality

### Example: Inconsistent Item Size

**Setup:** In your world, you can put microwave in pocket

**Player reaction:** "Weird, but okay—magic pockets"

**Later:** "That ironing board is too big to carry"

**Player reaction:**
- Frustration
- World no longer taken seriously
- Stops projecting imagination into it

**What happened:**
In one moment, your world changed from real place to broken toy.

### The Rule

If you establish rules for how things work in your world:
1. **Stick with them**
2. **Take them seriously**
3. **Never break them**

### Prevention

- Document your world rules explicitly
- Share them with entire team
- Review content for inconsistencies
- Playtest with consistency in mind
- Fix inconsistencies immediately

## Story Tip #8: Make Your Story World Accessible

### The Verne Example

**From the Earth to the Moon (1865):**
- Three men travel to moon
- Launched from giant cannon

**Modern reaction:** "That's ridiculous—cannon blast would kill them"

**But Verne knew about rockets:**
- Rockets existed (see "Star-Spangled Banner," 1814)
- He had scientific mind
- He knew rockets were more realistic

**So why use cannon?**

### Accessibility > Accuracy

**Context: 1860s technology progress**

**Rockets:**
- 1812: Congreve's rockets (6.5", 42 lbs, 2-mile range)
- 1840: Hale's rockets (same size, slightly more accurate)
- **30 years, almost no growth**

**Cannons:**
- 1855: Dahlgren's gun (100-lb shell, 3-mile range)
- 1860: Rodman's Columbiad (1000-lb shell, 6-mile range)
- **5 years, 10x increase in power**

**With American Civil War making headlines:**
- Easy to imagine even larger cannons
- Small leap to "cannon that reaches moon"
- **Rockets seemed stagnant, cannons seemed limitless**

**Verne's choice:** The story that audiences could **believe** and enjoy, not the technically accurate one.

### The Principle

**When telling story:**
- Facts aren't always your friends
- What player will believe and enjoy > physical accuracy
- Accessible > realistic

### Example: Pirates of the Caribbean Game

**Problem 1: Boat Speed**

**Realistic:** Historically accurate slow speed
**Result:** Players bored (felt too slow from camera height)
**Fix:** Made boat go at speed that **felt** realistic and exciting
**Lesson:** Perceived speed > actual speed

**Problem 2: Wind Direction**

**Look at the ships:** Wind behind all of them (impossible in reality)

**Why?** Players familiar with cars/motorboats, not sailing
**Result:** Players never questioned it
**When flags pointed "correct" way:** Players complained they looked wrong
**Fix:** Made flags point "wrong" way (like car antenna)
**Result:** Players stopped asking about it

**Lesson:** What players are familiar with > what's physically accurate

### Example: Hamster Cannon

**Situation:** Student game about hamsters in pet store trying to meet

**Element:** Hamster cannon to launch boy hamster to girl's cage

**Problem:** "There's no such thing as hamster cannon—story seems strange"

**Solution options:**
1. Change cannon to something else (hamster wheel)
2. Make cannon normal in this world

**They chose option 2:**
- Establishing shots feature signs: "Special! Hamster Cannons on sale!"

**Results:**
- Intriguing hook
- Introduced strange item before it appeared
- Made it feel natural in this unusual world
- No longer strange when players encountered it

### Making the Strange Accessible

**When you need weird/surreal elements:**

1. **Call special attention to them**
2. **Explain what they are and how they work**
3. **Introduce them early**
4. **Make them feel normal in your world**

### Lens #77: The Weirdest Thing

Questions to manage weirdness:

1. **What's the weirdest thing in my story?**
   - Identify it explicitly
   - Is it weird enough to be interesting?
   - Is it too weird to understand?

2. **How can I make sure the weirdest thing doesn't confuse or alienate the player?**
   - Introduce it carefully
   - Make it normal in your world
   - Give it clear rules

3. **If there are multiple weird things, should I maybe get rid of, or coalesce, some of them?**
   - Too much weird = confusion
   - One or two weird things = special
   - Ten weird things = incomprehensible

4. **If there is nothing weird in my story, is the story still interesting?**
   - Sometimes weird is memorable
   - But familiar can also be powerful
   - Find your balance

### The Balance

**Familiar + Novel = Success**

Too familiar = boring
Too novel = inaccessible
The right mix = comprehensible yet surprising

## Story Tip #9: Use Clichés Judiciously

### The Problem with Clichés

Videogame stories overuse tropes:
- Save world from evil aliens (again)
- Wizard vs evil dragon (again)
- Fight zombies with shotgun (again)

Becomes tedious.

### The Overreaction

Some designers avoid anything done before.
Result: So offbeat players can't understand or relate.

### The Truth About Clichés

**Advantage:** Familiar = understandable and comprehensible

**The Formula:** Successful games combine something **familiar** with something **novel**

### The Ninja Examples

**Avoid because done to death?**
"Ninjas have been done to death—won't make ninja game"

**Or find the novel angle:**
- Lonely ninja
- Incompetent ninja
- Ninja dog
- Robotic ninja
- Third-grade girl with secret ninja life

**Result:**
- New and different
- Hook into world player already understands
- Best of both worlds

### The Balance

**Error 1:** Overuse clichés (boring)
**Error 2:** Exile clichés completely (inaccessible)
**Right approach:** Use clichés as foundation, add novel twist

### Judicious Use

**Clichés are useful for:**
- Establishing familiar baseline
- Quick communication (player instantly understands)
- Foundation for innovation

**Avoid clichés when:**
- They're the entire concept
- They add nothing new
- They feel tired even to you

## Story Tip #10: Sometimes a Map Brings a Story to Life

### Robert Louis Stevenson's Discovery

**Context:** Rainy vacation, entertaining a schoolboy

**Activity:** Taking turns drawing pictures

**The moment:**
- Drew map of fanciful island on a whim
- Map took on life of its own
- Characters appeared visually in the woods
- They "peeped out from unexpected quarters"
- Fighting, hunting treasure on "these few square inches"

**Result:**
- Found himself writing list of chapters
- Became *Treasure Island*, his greatest work

**His realization:** Story emerged from PLACE, not from plot

### Why This Works for Games

**Most videogames happen in physical place, not world of words.**

By making sketches and drawings:
- Story naturally takes shape
- You consider who lives there
- What they do
- Why things are the way they are

### The Process

1. **Draw the place**
   - Map, blueprint, sketch
   - Don't worry about art quality
   - Focus on layout and relationships

2. **Let the place speak**
   - Who would live here?
   - What would happen here?
   - Why is it laid out this way?
   - What conflicts would arise?

3. **Follow what emerges**
   - Don't force predetermined story
   - Let geography suggest narrative
   - Use environment as storytelling

### When to Use This

- Level-based games
- Open world games
- Location-focused narratives
- When stuck on story
- When world feels generic

## Story Tip #11: Surprise and Emotion

(Bonus tip, though chapter says ten!)

### The Core Problem

**When stories are dull, it's because they lack:**
1. Surprise
2. Emotion
3. (Worst case) Both

### The Solution

**Use your lenses:**
- Lens #1: Emotion
- Lens #4: Surprise

**Point them at your story.**
**You might be surprised and emotional at how much they help.**

### Quick Fixes

**For lack of surprise:**
- Add unexpected twist
- Subvert player expectations
- Create genuine choices
- Use emergent systems

**For lack of emotion:**
- Connect player to characters
- Raise stakes personally
- Create moments of triumph/loss
- Use music and pacing
