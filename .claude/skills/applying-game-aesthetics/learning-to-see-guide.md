# Learning to See: Visual Observation for Game Designers

## The Challenge

It's difficult to actually see things as they really are. For efficiency, our brains categorize things before they enter our consciousness - we see a dog and our left brain slaps a label on it rather than deeply observing its unique qualities.

**The Goal:** Learn to get your left brain to take a break and let your right brain observe details that labels miss.

## Deep Seeing vs. Categorizing

### Categorizing (Left Brain - Typical)
"That's a salt shaker."
- Quick label
- Efficient for daily tasks
- Misses unique qualities
- Prevents deep understanding

### Deep Seeing (Right Brain - Artistic)
Observe the salt shaker's:
- **Shapes** - Cylindrical body, conical top, geometric relationships
- **Colors** - Base white with subtle cream undertones, metallic silver cap
- **Proportions** - Height-to-width ratio, cap size relative to body
- **Shadows** - Where light falls, darkness patterns, shadow direction
- **Reflections** - How surfaces catch and bounce light
- **Textures** - Smooth glass vs. rough metal, surface irregularities
- **Relationships** - Position relative to pepper shaker, distance from table edge
- **Function** - How holes are sized, grip considerations, refill method
- **Meaning** - Cultural associations, design era indicators

## Visual Equivalent of Deep Listening

Just as game designers practice "five kinds of listening" (to team, audience, game, client, self), visual observation requires similar depth:

**Listen to the Object:**
- What is it trying to communicate?
- What is its essential nature?
- What details give it character?

**Listen to the Environment:**
- How does it relate to surrounding objects?
- How does light interact with it?
- What context does placement provide?

**Listen to the User:**
- How would people interact with it?
- What emotional response does it evoke?
- What expectations does it set?

## Techniques for Developing Visual Observation

### Exercise 1: Timed Observation
1. Select an everyday object
2. Set timer for 5 minutes
3. Write or speak continuous observations
4. **Rule:** No labels - only describe what you see
5. When you think you've seen everything, look again

**Example:**
- ❌ "It's a coffee mug with a logo"
- ✅ "Cylindrical form curves outward at rim, white ceramic surface with hairline crack near handle attachment, C-shaped handle with smooth interior and ridged exterior for grip, blue geometric pattern wraps 40% around circumference..."

### Exercise 2: Upside-Down Drawing
From Betty Edwards' *Drawing on the Right Side of the Brain*:

1. Find a line drawing or photograph
2. Turn it upside down
3. Draw what you see (not what you know it is)
4. Focus only on lines, shapes, angles, relationships
5. Don't label parts as "nose" or "tree" - just observe shapes

**Why it works:** Disorientation defeats left-brain labeling, forcing right-brain observation.

### Exercise 3: Negative Space Study
1. Choose an object with interesting gaps (chair, scissors, vase)
2. Draw ONLY the spaces around and between elements
3. Ignore the object itself
4. Focus on the shapes of emptiness

**Result:** You see spatial relationships invisible to categorizing mind.

### Exercise 4: Five-Minute Sketch Loop
Rapid observation practice:

1. **Minute 1:** Sketch object's basic proportions
2. **Minute 2:** Add primary shapes and major forms
3. **Minute 3:** Observe and add one surprising detail you initially missed
4. **Minute 4:** Add shadows and value relationships
5. **Minute 5:** Final refinement

**Purpose:** Forces rapid deep seeing under time pressure.

### Exercise 5: Comparative Observation
1. Find two similar objects (two chairs, two trees, two characters)
2. List 20 differences
3. Go beyond obvious (color, size)
4. Find subtle distinctions in proportion, texture, character, function

**Goal:** Train eye to see uniqueness, not just categories.

## Application to Game Art

### When Evaluating Reference Materials

**Before briefing artists:**
1. Spend 5 minutes deeply observing reference images
2. Identify specific details that matter to your vision
3. Note what makes the reference work (or not work)
4. Articulate observations without jargon

**Instead of:** "Make it look cool like this cyberpunk image"

**Try:** "Notice how the neon signs reflect in wet pavement creating light pools, the vertical emphasis of architecture draws eyes upward, the color palette alternates cool blues with warm oranges in 70/30 ratio, and fog diffuses distant elements creating depth layers..."

### When Reviewing Concept Art

**Ask yourself:**
- What specific shapes create the character's silhouette?
- Where does light naturally fall given the environment?
- What textures would feel right to touch?
- How do proportions affect emotional response?
- What details attract the eye first? Second? Third?

**Don't just accept or reject** - deeply see what's there, then articulate specifically what works and what doesn't.

### When Designing Game Spaces

**Observe your reference spaces:**
- How do landmarks organize the area?
- What sight lines exist?
- Where do shadows create mystery or drama?
- How do colors guide or confuse navigation?
- What textures signal different functional zones?

## The Virtuous Circle

**Deep Seeing ↔ Drawing**

- Really seeing helps you draw properly
- Drawing helps you see properly
- Each reinforces the other

**Even if you can't draw well:**
Attempting to sketch forces observation. The goal isn't beautiful art - it's training your eye to notice details you'd otherwise miss.

## Practical Recommendations

### For Non-Artists
1. **Sketch rough thumbnails** during design discussions
2. **Trace over reference images** to understand construction
3. **Keep a visual notebook** with observations
4. **Practice 5-minute observation daily** on any object
5. **Study why things work** in games you admire

### For Art-Directed Designers
1. **Build visual reference libraries** organized by quality you're observing
2. **Annotate references** with specific observations
3. **Create comparison sheets** (good vs. bad examples with notes)
4. **Practice articulating** what you see without art jargon
5. **Draw during meetings** to ground abstract discussions

### For Everyone
1. **Slow down** - Observation requires time
2. **Suspend judgment** - See first, evaluate later
3. **Question labels** - "It's a forest" reveals nothing useful
4. **Share observations** - Verbalizing trains the eye
5. **Draw constantly** - Even bad drawings build seeing skills

## Key Takeaway

**You cannot create great game aesthetics without learning to see.** The categorizing mind creates generic, forgettable visuals. The observing mind creates specific, memorable experiences.

Practice seeing deeply, and your aesthetic decisions will transform from vague preferences ("make it look cool") to specific, articulable vision ("use vertical emphasis, 70/30 cool-to-warm color ratio, wet surface reflections for depth...").
