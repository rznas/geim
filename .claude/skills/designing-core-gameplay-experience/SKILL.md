# Designing Core Gameplay Experience

## Overview

This skill guides you through designing the core experience that players will have with your game. The game itself is merely an artifact—what matters is the experience it creates in the player's mind. This skill covers:

- **Essential Experience**: Identifying and capturing the essence of what you want players to feel
- **Unified Themes**: Creating resonant themes that strengthen all design decisions
- **Creative Ideation**: Brainstorming and working with your creative subconscious
- **Iterative Refinement**: Using the Rule of the Loop to improve through rapid prototyping
- **Interest Pacing**: Structuring experiences with compelling interest curves

## Dependencies

**Required:** `establishing-design-foundations` - You need the lens framework and understanding of the Elemental Tetrad first.

## Core Principles

### The Game Is Not the Experience

**Critical understanding:** The game is just a means to an end. Players don't care about your game—they care about the experience they have while playing it.

- The game (artifact) enables the experience (what matters)
- You can never directly create experiences—only artifacts that generate them
- This makes game design like "building a ship in a bottle"—far removed from what you're creating
- Success is measured by the quality of the imaginary experience, not the tangible game

### The Rule of the Loop

> **The more times you test and improve your design, the better your game will be.**

This is an absolute truth with no exceptions. Your goal is to:
1. **Make every loop count** through risk assessment
2. **Loop as fast as possible** through rapid prototyping

## Step 1: Define Your Essential Experience

Before designing mechanics or features, clearly articulate the experience you want to create.

### Identifying Essential Experience

Ask yourself:
- **What experience do I want the player to have?**
- **What is essential to that experience?**
- **How can my game capture that essence?**

**Example - Snowball Fight Game:**

Essential elements:
- "It was so cold" → Visual: breath condensation, shivering animation; Audio: whistling wind; Mechanics: better snowballs without gloves, but cold hands force gloves on
- "The snow was just right for packing" → Mechanic: perfect snow makes better snowballs
- "We couldn't stop laughing" → Audio: character laughter; Social: play with friends

Non-essential elements (skip these):
- "I was wearing corduroy pants"
- "A man walking his dog looked at us"

**Key insight:** The essential experience can be delivered in forms very different from reality. Abstract what matters emotionally.

### Using Introspection

To understand experiences deeply:

**Defeating Heisenberg (Observing Without Spoiling):**
1. **Analyze memories**: Play/experience something, then analyze the memory immediately after
2. **Two passes**: Experience it once naturally, then replay analyzing everything
3. **Sneak glances**: Quick mental observations during experience ("Exciting enough? Yes.")
4. **Observe silently**: Advanced—watch yourself experiencing with a "still mind" while engaged

**Dissect your feelings:**
- Don't just know if you like something
- Clearly state what you like, don't like, and why
- Put words to emotions—feelings are abstract, words are concrete

See [essential-experience-guide.md](essential-experience-guide.md) for detailed techniques.

## Step 2: Create a Unifying Theme

A strong theme ties all game elements together, making every design decision reinforce a single goal.

### Two Steps to Thematic Power

1. **Figure out what your theme is**
2. **Use every means possible to reinforce that theme**

### Types of Themes

**Experience-Based Themes:**
Deliver a specific essential experience.

*Example:* "The fantasy of being a pirate" (Pirates of the Caribbean VR)
- Reinforcements: ship-shaped room, motion platform, ship's wheel controller, real cannons, feeling of freedom in gameplay, treasure rendering, water-reflected lighting, sea breeze from AC vents

**Truth-Based Themes:**
Express a deeply held personal or universal truth.

*Example:* "Love is more important than life and stronger than death" (Titanic)
*Example:* "War changes a world" (Risk: Legacy with permanent board modifications)
*Example:* "If you are virtuous enough, you can defeat death" (Hercules myth)

### Finding Resonance

Resonant themes tap into:
- Universal fantasies and desires
- Hidden truths we believe but rarely express
- Conflicts central to human experience (work vs. play in Toontown Online)

**Ask yourself:**
- What feels powerful and special about my game?
- What ideas get people really excited when I describe them?
- What instincts are driving my design choices?

See [theme-design-guide.md](theme-design-guide.md) for detailed thematic development.

## Step 3: Generate and Select Ideas

### Working with Your Creative Subconscious

Your subconscious is your "silent partner"—treat it as a separate person who:
- Communicates through imagery and emotions (not words)
- Is impulsive and lives in the moment
- Is deeply emotional
- Is playful and loves wordplay
- Is irrational but sometimes brilliantly insightful

**Five tips for partnership:**

1. **Pay attention**: Listen to hunches, feelings, emotions, dreams. Consider "crazy" ideas seriously for a few moments.

2. **Record your ideas**: Voice recorder or notebook. Frees mental space and signals respect for ideas.

3. **Manage its appetites**: Can't do creative work if obsessing about basic needs (food, safety, relationships). Satisfy these judiciously.

4. **Sleep**: Not optional. Subconscious works during sleep, sorts and reorganizes. Insufficient sleep = subconscious "not around."

5. **Don't push too hard**: Like trying to remember a name—pushing harder slows it down. State the problem, then let it work.

### Brainstorming Essentials

**State the problem first:**
- Clear goal + constraints
- Example: "How can I make a browser-based experience teenagers really like?"
- Avoid falling in love with solutions—fall in love with problems

**16 Brainstorming Tips (Quick Reference):**
1. Write everything down
2. Write or type (whatever works—handwriting can be more creative)
3. Sketch ideas visually
4. Bring toys to the table
5. Change your perspective (stand on chair, go to different locations)
6. Immerse yourself in the problem space
7. Crack jokes (loosens thinking)
8. Spare no expense on materials
9. Use walls/whiteboards/large Post-Its
10. The space remembers (spatial memory is strong)
11. Write everything (even stupid ideas first)
12. Number your lists (adds dignity, easier to reference)
13. Destroy your assumptions (list assumptions, then break them)
14. Mix and match categories (use Elemental Tetrad)
15. Talk to yourself (say ideas out loud)
16. Find a partner (small groups of 2-4, individual brainstorming first)

**Look for inspiration everywhere EXCEPT games:**
- Real-life experiences you want to share
- Ballet, geese taking off, machinery, nature
- Books, movies, music from other genres
- The key emotional heart that drives the art direction

See [Lens #13: Infinite Inspiration](#) in your lens reference.

## Step 4: Iterate Through Prototyping

### The Eight Filters

Every design must pass through eight filters. When it fails one, change it and test all eight again:

1. **Artistic impulse**: Does this feel right to me and my team?
2. **Demographics**: Will the intended audience like this enough?
3. **Experience design**: Is this well-designed (aesthetics, balance, interest curves)?
4. **Innovation**: Is this novel enough?
5. **Business/marketing**: Will this be profitable and marketable?
6. **Engineering**: Is it technically possible to build?
7. **Social/community**: Does it meet our social goals?
8. **Playtesting**: Do playtesters enjoy it enough?

### Risk Assessment and Mitigation

**For each major design decision, list risks:**

*Example - Parachuting Cat Game:*
- Risk: Bubble collecting mechanic might not be fun → Mitigation: Build 2D abstract prototype first (1-2 weeks)
- Risk: Engine can't handle city + bubbles + vultures → Mitigation: Technical stress test with placeholder art
- Risk: 30 houses too much art time → Mitigation: Build one house, time it, adjust design now
- Risk: Players won't like characters/story → Mitigation: Concept art bulletin board, show to target audience

**Prioritize prototypes:**
- Biggest risks first
- Upstream dependencies first (results make other prototypes meaningless)
- Parallelize when possible (art, tech, gameplay prototypes simultaneously)

### Ten Prototyping Tips (Quick Reference)

1. **Answer a question**: State question clearly. Resist overbuilding.
2. **Forget quality**: Fast and ugly > slow and polished. Problems show in rough prototypes.
3. **Don't get attached**: Plan to throw it away. It's practice for the real thing.
4. **Prioritize prototypes**: Biggest risks first.
5. **Parallelize productively**: Multiple independent prototypes simultaneously.
6. **Doesn't have to be digital**: Paper prototypes are fast. Even FPS can be paper prototyped.
7. **Doesn't have to be interactive**: Animations can test concepts.
8. **Pick fast-loop engines**: Write code while game is running (scripting languages).
9. **Build the toy first**: Make something fun to play WITH before adding goals.
10. **Seize opportunities**: When unexpected time appears, iterate more.

**Paper Prototyping:**
- Tetris: Cardboard pieces, drawn board, assistant drops them
- Halo: Graph paper, game pieces, metronome (5-second ticks), one person per character

See [iteration-and-prototyping-guide.md](iteration-and-prototyping-guide.md) for detailed process.

## Step 5: Shape with Interest Curves

Interest curves chart how captivating your experience is over time.

### Classic Interest Curve Shape

```
Interest
   |           G (climax)
   |          /|\ H (resolution)
   |    E    / | \
   |   /\   /  |  \___
   |  C  \ /   |
   | /    D    F
B (hook)
|/
A (initial interest)
|________________________ Time
```

**Key points:**
- **A**: Initial interest from expectations (packaging, ads, friends)
- **B**: Hook that grabs attention immediately (opening number, first level, intro movie)
- **C, E**: Interest peaks
- **D, F**: Brief rests (lower but not boring)
- **G**: Climax exceeding all before
- **H**: Satisfying resolution, "leave them wanting more"

### Fractal Structure

Interest curves repeat at multiple scales:

**Level 1 - Overall Game:**
- Intro movie (hook)
- Series of levels with rising challenge
- Final boss battle (climax)

**Level 2 - Each Level:**
- New aesthetics/challenges (hook)
- Series of encounters
- Boss battle (level climax)

**Level 3 - Each Encounter:**
- Interesting introduction
- Rising challenge within encounter
- Satisfying conclusion

### Three Components of Interest

**1. Inherent Interest:**
- Risk > safety
- Unusual > ordinary
- Dramatic change and potential for change
- Base instincts (survival, curiosity)
- Higher instincts (love, justice, beauty)

**2. Poetry of Presentation:**
- Beauty of writing, music, art, cinematography
- Aesthetic quality of all elements
- How elements compose together

**3. Projection:**
- Player puts themselves in the experience
- Empathy with characters (strangers → friends → yourself)
- Imagination filling in a consistent world
- Interactive advantage: YOU are the main character, events happen to YOU

**Examples:**
- Chainsaw juggling: High inherent interest, low poetry, medium projection
- Violin concerto: Low inherent interest, high poetry, low projection
- Tetris: Low inherent interest, low poetry, HIGH projection (you make all decisions)

### Designing Your Interest Curve

**Process:**
1. Identify your top 10 moments
2. Chart their approximate interest levels over time
3. Look for problems: flat sections, wrong peaks, missing climax, poor hook
4. Restructure ordering or cut weak sections
5. Test with players, compare observed vs. expected interest

**Tools:**
- Draw curves for different player types/demographics
- Color-code by interest type (inherent, poetry, projection)
- Compare different difficulty levels
- Track player deaths/failures as interest proxy

See [interest-curves-guide.md](interest-curves-guide.md) for detailed techniques.

## Workflow: Designing a Core Experience

### Phase 1: Vision (Before Prototyping)

1. **Introspection**: What experiences excite you? What do you want to share?
2. **Essential experience**: What feelings/sensations are core? Write them clearly.
3. **Theme identification**: What unifies these experiences? Experience-based or truth-based?
4. **Resonance check**: Does this theme tap into universal desires/truths? Does it excite people?

### Phase 2: Ideation (Problem → Ideas)

1. **State the problem**: Clear goal + constraints. Use Elemental Tetrad to check freedom.
2. **Gather inspiration**: OUTSIDE games. Real experiences, other art forms.
3. **Brainstorm widely**: 50-100+ ideas. Mix and match categories. Write everything.
4. **Quick selection**: Pick one, commit, see what becomes clear. Reverse if wrong.

### Phase 3: Iteration (Loops)

**Loop structure:**
1. State current problem (gets more specific each loop)
2. Choose solution
3. List risks
4. Build prototypes to mitigate risks (parallel if possible)
5. Test prototypes
6. Pass through eight filters
7. State new problems → back to step 1

**Loop velocities:**
- Pre-production: Many fast loops (days to weeks)
- Mark Cerny's rule: "Done" when 2 publishable levels complete (30% budget spent)
- 50% rule: All core gameplay playable at 50% schedule mark

### Phase 4: Pacing (Interest Curves)

1. **Identify top moments**: List the 10 best moments in your game
2. **Chart overall curve**: Hook, rising action, climax, resolution
3. **Fractal detail**: Chart curves for each level, each encounter
4. **Component analysis**: Label inherent interest, poetry, projection
5. **Compare to observed**: Playtest, watch player engagement, adjust structure

### Phase 5: Passion Check (After Each Sprint)

Ask:
- Am I filled with blinding passion about how great this game will be?
- If I've lost the passion, can I find it again?
- If passion isn't coming back, shouldn't I be doing something else?

**Passion is irrational but crucial**—it's your subconscious saying this matters.

## Common Pitfalls

**Confusing game with experience:** Remember, the game is just the means. The experience is what matters.

**Theme as afterthought:** Define theme early. Every decision should reinforce it.

**Loving your solution:** Fall in love with your problem, not your solution. Be ready to pivot.

**Too few loops:** More loops = better game. Find ways to loop faster.

**Ignoring the subconscious:** Your "silent partner" is your most powerful tool. Listen to it.

**Flat interest curves:** No hook, wrong ordering of moments, missing climax. Chart it visually.

**Pushing quality too early:** Rough prototypes find problems faster. Polish hides issues.

**Skipping risk assessment:** Face problems immediately, not when budget is 80% spent.

**Forgetting passion:** If you've lost it, something is deeply wrong. Fix it or move on.

## Examples

### Example 1: Wii Sports Baseball
- **Essential experience**: Swinging the controller like a bat (most unique/exciting part)
- **Decision**: Cut other details (9 innings, stealing bases) to focus on core essential experience
- **Result**: Simpler game, stronger experience

### Example 2: James Bond 007 RPG
- **Essential experience**: Exciting spy movie feeling (not war game tactics)
- **Theme solution**: Hero Points - budget of points to alter dice rolls in dramatic moments
- **Result**: Players feel like Bond, spectacular events feel earned not random

### Example 3: Toontown Online
- **Subthemes identified**: Fun with friends, escaping reality, simplicity + transcendence
- **These three = PLAY**: Realized these describe "play" itself
- **Truth-based theme**: "Work wants to destroy play, but play must survive"
- **Execution**: Robot executives (Cogs) vs. colorful Toons with gags

### Example 4: Risk Legacy
- **Assumption destroyed**: "One game does not affect next one"
- **Result**: Permanent modifications (stickers, permanent marker, ripped cards)
- **Theme**: "War changes a world" - reinforced by never-reversible mechanics
