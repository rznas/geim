# Essential Experience Guide

## Understanding Essential Experience

The essential experience is the core feeling, sensation, or emotion that defines your game. Everything else is in service of delivering this experience.

## The Experience vs. Artifact Distinction

**The hard truth:**
- The game (artifact) is just cardboard, bits, rules
- The experience (what matters) exists only in the player's mind
- You can never directly create experiences
- You can only create artifacts that hopefully generate experiences

**Why this is hard:**
"Like building a ship in a bottle"—you're far removed from what you're actually trying to create. You create an artifact, a player interacts with it, and you cross your fingers that the experience that emerges is enjoyable.

**This is why deep listening is essential for game design.**

## Introspection: Your Primary Tool

### The Challenge of Introspection

**Peril #1: False conclusions about reality**
- What feels true isn't necessarily objectively true
- Scientists reject introspection for this reason
- **BUT** game designers care about what "feels true," not what is objectively true
- For human heart/mind/experience questions, introspection is incredibly powerful

**Peril #2: What's true for you may not be true for others**
- "I like it, therefore it's good" is sometimes very wrong
- Solution: Observe your experiences AND others' experiences
- Develop a picture of how your experiences differ from your audience
- Like a cultural anthropologist, put yourself in their place

### Four Techniques for Effective Introspection

#### 1. Analyze Memories

**Why:** The part of your mind used for analysis is normally focused on the experience itself. Memory analysis is easier.

**How:**
- Play/experience something with intention to analyze after (not during)
- Analyze immediately after while memory is fresh
- Focus on powerful experiences (remembered more vividly)
- Just having the intention to analyze helps you remember more details

**Trick:** Tell yourself "I'll analyze this later" before experiencing—helps memory without interfering.

#### 2. Two Passes

**Why:** First pass is untainted, second pass allows deep analysis.

**How:**
- **Pass 1**: Just have the experience. Don't stop to analyze.
- **Pass 2**: Do it again. Pause. Take notes. Relive it with analysis mind active.

**You have the untainted experience fresh in mind while you analyze.**

#### 3. Sneak Glances (Intermediate)

**Why:** Quick observations don't significantly degrade the experience.

**How:**
- "Mental glances" at your experience while it's happening
- Like glancing at a stranger—short looks go unnoticed, staring catches attention
- **Quick mental check:** "Exciting enough? Yes." Then immediately back to experience.
- Avoid interior mental dialog (questions/answers interrupt experience)
- With practice, becomes habitual and less intrusive

**This takes practice but is very effective.**

#### 4. Observe Silently (Advanced)

**Why:** Continuous observation without degradation—the gold standard.

**How:**
- Almost like you have two minds:
  - One moving, engaged in experience
  - One still, silently observing
- Similar to Zen self-observation or watching your own breathing
- You observe your natural process without disturbing it

**This is difficult and takes extensive practice:**
- Practice anywhere: TV, work, playing, doing anything
- Won't get it right at first
- Keep experimenting
- Tremendous power when mastered

**Like observing your breathing:** Normally unconscious, but with practice you can observe it without disturbing the natural rhythm.

## Dissecting Your Feelings

### Why Dissection Matters

It's not enough to have a general sense of liking/not liking. You must clearly state:
- **What** you like/don't like
- **Why** you like/don't like it

**Bad example:**
- "The pizza was bad."
- "Bad how?"
- "Just... bad."

**Good example:**
- "The pizza was bad."
- "The crust was soggy, the sauce was too sweet, and there wasn't enough cheese."

### The Dissection Process

**For any experience:**

1. **Identify the emotion**
   - Happy? Frustrated? Excited? Bored? Anxious? Satisfied?
   - Don't settle for "good" or "bad"—be specific

2. **Locate the source**
   - What specifically triggered this feeling?
   - Was it visual, audio, tactile, conceptual?
   - Was it the thing itself or how it was presented?

3. **Put words to it**
   - Feelings are abstract, words are concrete
   - You need concreteness to communicate with others
   - Write it down—makes it real

4. **Consider the why**
   - Why did this trigger that emotion?
   - Is it universal or personal to you?
   - Does it connect to memories, fears, desires?

### Practice Exercise: Daily Dissection

**Pick one experience per day:**
- A meal
- A conversation
- A piece of music
- A moment in a game
- Anything at all

**Spend 5 minutes dissecting:**
- What emotions did I feel?
- What caused each emotion?
- What was essential vs. incidental?
- How would I describe this to someone else?

**The more you practice, the better you become at understanding experience.**

## Identifying Essential Elements

### The Snowball Fight Example

**The memory:**
- "There was so much snow, school was canceled"
- "We played right in the street"
- "The snow was just right for packing"
- "It was so cold, but sunny—sky was so blue"
- "There were kids everywhere"
- "We built this huge fort"
- "Fred threw one high—when I looked up, he hit me!"
- "We couldn't stop laughing"

**Essential elements** (define the experience):
- Cold weather
- Perfect packing snow
- Strategic fort building
- Surprise attacks
- Laughter/joy

**Non-essential elements** (forgettable details):
- "I was wearing corduroy pants"
- "I had mints in my pocket"
- "A man walking his dog looked at us"

### The Translation Process

**For each essential element, ask: How can a game convey this?**

**Example: "It was so cold"**

**Artwork options:**
- Characters breathe condensation puffs
- Shivering animation
- Blue color palette for environment

**Audio options:**
- Whistling wind sound effect (might not have been there, but captures essence)
- Crunching snow sounds

**Mechanic options:**
- Can make better snowballs without gloves
- But hands get cold, must put gloves on
- Risk/reward decision around cold

**Note:** Might not have happened in real experience, but game rule delivers the essence.

### Essential Experience Extraction Process

**Step 1: Recall the experience**
- Close your eyes
- Relive it in as much detail as possible
- What do you see, hear, feel?

**Step 2: List everything you remember**
- Write it all down
- Don't filter yet

**Step 3: Mark the essential**
- Go through the list
- Ask: "If this element was missing, would the experience be fundamentally different?"
- If yes → essential
- If no → non-essential

**Step 4: Prioritize essentials**
- Some essential elements are MORE essential than others
- Rank them
- Top 3-5 are your core targets

**Step 5: Brainstorm implementations**
- For each essential element, list 5+ ways to convey it through:
  - Mechanics (rules, systems, interactions)
  - Story (narrative, characters, events)
  - Aesthetics (art, sound, feel)
  - Technology (what's technically possible)

**Step 6: Playtest and refine**
- Build prototypes
- Do players feel the essential elements?
- What's working? What's not?
- Iterate until essence captured

## Wii Sports Baseball: A Case Study

**Initial vision:**
- Make baseball as realistic as possible
- Bonus: swing controller like bat

**During development:**
- Realized: won't have time for every baseball aspect
- Decision point: What's MOST essential?

**Essential experience identified:**
- Swinging the controller like a bat
- THIS is the most unique part
- THIS is what makes the experience special

**Radical decision:**
- Cut non-essential details
  - Nine innings → simplified
  - Stealing bases → removed
  - Complex rules → streamlined
- Focus ALL attention on the swing

**Result:**
- Simpler game structure
- Much stronger essential experience
- Huge success

**Lesson:** Sometimes capturing the essence means cutting things that seem important.

## James Bond 007 RPG: A Case Study

**Problem identified:**
- Previous spy RPGs (Top Secret) felt like war games
- Essence of spy movies was missing
- **What's the essential experience of being James Bond?**

**Essential elements:**
- Exciting, risky actions
- Spectacular, dramatic events
- Success through cleverness and boldness
- Superhuman but not unlimited power

**Previous approach (didn't work):**
- Game master calculates probability
- Player rolls dice
- Success or failure determined

**Problem:**
- If probability too low → players won't risk
- If probability too high → players act like superheroes constantly

**Essential experience solution: Hero Points**
- Players get a budget of Hero Points per adventure
- Can spend to alter dice rolls in their favor
- Limited number forces careful choice
- When used, enables spectacular Bond-like feats

**Result:**
- Players feel tension (when to spend points?)
- When spent, truly spectacular events
- Captures essence of Bond films perfectly

**Lesson:** The right mechanic can deliver an essential experience that description alone cannot.

## Practical Checklist: Essential Experience

**For your current game:**

- [ ] I can clearly state the experience I want players to have (in 1-2 sentences)
- [ ] I have identified 3-5 essential elements of that experience
- [ ] For each essential element, I have listed multiple ways to convey it
- [ ] I have prototyped the top essential elements
- [ ] Playtesters report feeling the essential experience
- [ ] I have cut non-essential elements that distract from the core
- [ ] Every major system/feature reinforces the essential experience
- [ ] I can explain why each game element is essential to the experience

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Confusing detailed realism with essential experience**
- Realism is a means, not an end
- What matters is the feeling, not perfect simulation
- Example: Wii Sports cut realistic baseball for essential swing experience

**Mistake 2: Including everything from the source experience**
- Not everything is essential
- Corduroy pants don't make the snowball fight
- Cut ruthlessly to what matters

**Mistake 3: Not articulating the experience clearly**
- "Fun game about pirates" is too vague
- "The fantasy of being a pirate" is better
- "The fantasy of throwing aside rules and being free" is specific and actionable

**Mistake 4: Designing before experiencing**
- Experience something memorable first
- Then extract the essence
- Don't invent essence without lived experience

**Mistake 5: Ignoring player feedback about the experience**
- You know what you intended
- Players know what they experienced
- If there's a gap, the players are right

## Integration with Other Skills

**With Theme Design:**
- Essential experience often becomes your theme
- Or informs what your theme should be
- Theme is "what game is about," essential experience is "what players feel"

**With Iteration:**
- Each prototype should test: "Did we capture the essential experience?"
- If not, what's missing? What needs to change?
- Essential experience is a filter for all design decisions

**With Interest Curves:**
- Your top moments should embody the essential experience
- Climax should deliver the essential experience most powerfully
- If moments don't connect to essential experience, cut or change them

## Exercise: Essential Experience Extraction

**Pick a memorable game experience:**
1. Close your eyes and relive it for 2 minutes
2. Write down everything you remember (5 minutes)
3. Mark essential vs. non-essential elements (3 minutes)
4. For top 3 essential elements, brainstorm 5 ways each could be implemented in a game (10 minutes)
5. Prioritize: which implementations best capture the essence? (2 minutes)

**Do this regularly to build your essential experience extraction skills.**
