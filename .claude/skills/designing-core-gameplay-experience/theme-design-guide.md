# Theme Design Guide

## Understanding Theme

**Theme is what your game is about.** It's the idea that ties your entire game together—the idea that all elements must support.

**Why theme matters:**
- All elements reinforce one another (working toward common goal)
- Easy method for deciding if something belongs
- Stronger, more cohesive experiences
- Memorable and meaningful to players

## The Two-Step Theme Process

### Step 1: Figure Out What Your Theme Is

Sometimes themes emerge during creation. Sometimes you choose them upfront. The sooner you have a theme, the easier design becomes.

**Theme acts as a filter:**
- If it reinforces the theme → keep it
- If it doesn't → remove it or change it

### Step 2: Use Every Means Possible to Reinforce That Theme

"Every means" literally means everything:
- Core mechanics
- Art style
- Sound design
- Interface design
- Physical components (controllers, boards)
- Room lighting
- Even air conditioning

## Two Types of Themes

### Experience-Based Themes

**Definition:** The goal is to deliver a specific essential experience to the player.

**Structure:** Usually phrased as "The [experience/fantasy] of [doing something]"

**Examples:**
- "The fantasy of being a pirate"
- "The thrill of street racing"
- "The terror of survival horror"
- "The satisfaction of building an empire"

**Characteristics:**
- Player-centric (about what player feels/does)
- Can be tested directly ("Did you feel like a pirate?")
- Often obvious from gameplay

### Truth-Based Themes

**Definition:** A statement of a deeply held personal or universal truth.

**Structure:** Usually a declarative statement about life, humanity, or the world

**Examples:**
- "Love is more important than life and stronger than death" (Titanic)
- "War changes a world" (Risk: Legacy)
- "If you are virtuous enough, you can defeat death" (Hercules)
- "Work wants to destroy play, but play must survive" (Toontown Online)

**Characteristics:**
- Philosophical or emotional statement
- Not scientifically provable, but "feels true"
- Often hidden (players may feel it without articulating it)
- More powerful and resonant when done well

**Important:** Players don't need to state the theme. They just know they like the game but can't say why. Theme operates at subconscious level.

## Finding Resonant Themes

### What Is Resonance?

**Resonant themes touch players deeply.**

They tap into:
- Fantasies everyone has had (being free, being powerful, being loved)
- Hidden truths we believe but rarely express
- Universal human desires (safety, belonging, meaning)
- Fundamental conflicts (freedom vs. security, individual vs. group)

**When you tap into resonance:**
- Experience becomes transcendent and transforming
- Players remember your game for years
- Word of mouth spreads naturally
- Connection goes beyond entertainment to something meaningful

### How to Find Resonant Themes

**Questions to ask yourself:**

1. **What feels powerful and special?**
   - Not "cool" or "fun"
   - Powerful—like it reaches something deep
   - Trust your gut feeling

2. **What gets people really excited?**
   - When describing your game, what makes eyes light up?
   - What do they remember and repeat?
   - What do they add their own ideas to?

3. **If you had no constraints, what would this be?**
   - Remove all technical, budget, scope limits
   - What would you really make?
   - That unconstrained vision often reveals true theme

4. **What's driving your instincts?**
   - When you insist something must be a certain way, why?
   - What feels "right" or "wrong" about design choices?
   - Your instincts are hints from your subconscious about theme

**The Lens of Resonance is quiet and delicate.** It requires deep self-listening.

### Testing for Resonance

**Show the concept (not the game) to people:**
- Describe the theme or core idea
- Watch their reactions
- Do they lean forward?
- Do they immediately start imagining it?
- Do they tell you their own related stories?

**Resonance reveals itself through:**
- Strong emotional reactions
- Personal connections ("That reminds me of...")
- Instant understanding ("Oh, I get it!")
- Desire to experience it ("When can I play this?")

## Pirates of the Caribbean: A Comprehensive Case Study

This is one of the best examples of unified theming in game design history.

### The Journey to the Theme

**Initial assignment:**
- Create VR game adaptation of Pirates of the Caribbean ride
- CAVE environment at DisneyQuest
- Five-minute experience
- That's it. No storyline. No game goals.

**Beginning of theme (too broad):**
- "About pirates"
- But which point of view?
  - Historical documentary?
  - Battle between pirate ships?
  - Search for treasure?
  - Pirates as villains to destroy?
- Still don't have a real theme

**Research phase:**
- Read pirate history → Good details, no theme
- Studied other pirate video games → Good ideas, no theme
- Talked to original ride creators → Good insights, no theme
- Rode the ride dozens of times → Scribbled notes, took pictures, lots of details

**Key insight (in the car heading home):**
- Couldn't help humming: "Yo ho, yo ho, a pirate's life for me"
- Suddenly clear: Not ABOUT pirates
- **About BEING a pirate!**

**Final theme:**
"The fantasy of being a pirate"

More specifically: "The fantasy of throwing aside the rules of society and just being a pirate"

### Using Every Means to Reinforce Theme

**CAVE Shape:**
- Previous: Square and hexagonal CAVEs
- Pirates: Created NEW four-screen CAVE shape suited to pirate ship simulation
- **Why:** Physical space reinforces "being on a ship"

**Stereoptics (3D):**
- Not every CAVE experience uses it
- Pirates: Used it for sense of depth
- Eyes focus on infinity → feels like being out at sea
- **Why:** Physical sensation reinforces being at sea

**Modified 3D Glasses:**
- Off-the-shelf glasses have blinders on sides (for movies)
- **Problem:** Blinders reduce peripheral vision
- Peripheral vision crucial for sense of motion/sailing
- **Solution:** Had manufacturer cut off blinders
- **Why:** Detracting from theme (not enough sense of sea)

**Motion Platform:**
- Wanted rocking, swaying boat feeling
- Tested various platforms
- Custom-built pneumatic platform
- **Why:** Felt most like ship at sea (not just motion, but right motion)

**Interface:**
- Could have used joysticks (off-the-shelf)
- Pirates: Ship's wheel for steering, real metal cannons for aiming/firing
- **Why:** Pirate fantasy is about steering ships and firing cannons

**Visuals:**
- "Hyperreal" look (like the ride)
- High-end graphics hardware
- Rich textures and models
- **Why:** Pirates ride has this look; connects game to ride emotionally

**Music:**
- Got permission to use music from the ride (required effort)
- **Why:** Captures theme perfectly, creates nostalgic connection

**Audio:**
- Custom ten-speaker surround system
- Sounds from all directions
- Some speakers only for cannon blasts
- Placed at precise distance so waveform collapses in stomach
- **Why:** Don't just hear cannons, FEEL them physically

**Feeling of Freedom:**
- Piracy is about freedom
- Gameplay lets players sail wherever they want
- But ensures exciting time (indirect control)
- **Why:** Core to pirate fantasy

**Death Handling:**
- Tradition: die, penalty, respawn
- **Problem:** Doesn't fit pirate fantasy
- Also: would break dramatic interest curve (5-minute experience)
- **Solution:** Players invulnerable during most of game
- Only if take too many hits: ship sinks dramatically at end of final battle
- **Why:** In the fantasy you don't die, or die dramatically. Respawning breaks immersion.

**Treasure:**
- Collecting treasure is essential pirate fantasy
- **Problem:** Piles of gold hard to render convincingly
- **Solution:** Special technique making flat hand-painted treasure seem solid/dimensional
- Prominently displayed on ship's deck
- **Why:** Pirate fantasy requires seeing your plunder

**Lighting:**
- Room needed lighting
- Could have used standard lights
- **Solution:** Filters making light look like water reflection
- **Why:** Themed even the ambient lighting

**Air Conditioning:**
- Facilities asked about AC vent placement
- First thought: "Who cares?"
- **Solution:** Vents at front of ship, blowing back
- **Why:** Feel breeze while sailing

**Storage:**
- Players need place for bags/purses
- Could have used shelf
- **Solution:** Bags made from fishing nets
- **Why:** Look like they belong on boat

**The Eyes of Bluebeard:**
- One thing team couldn't figure out: theming 3D glasses
- Tried pirate hats, bandanas—didn't work
- Gave up
- **Surprise:** Cast member improvised: "Before ye board, ye must wear the Eyes of Bluebeard"
- Not in official script!
- **Lesson:** Strong unifying theme makes it easy for everyone on team to contribute

**Result:** Every single element reinforced "being a pirate." Massive success.

## Toontown Online: Finding Theme Through Iteration

### Starting Point (Unclear Theme)

**Initial research:**
- Studied "Who Framed Roger Rabbit?" movie
- Studied Toontown section of Disneyland
- **Discovery:** Toontown poorly defined but powerful
- Everyone seemed to know what it was instinctively

**First theme attempts (subthemes):**
1. Having fun with your friends
2. Escaping from reality
3. Simplicity and transcendence (simpler world, but you're more powerful)

**Problem:** These are subthemes, not a clear main theme.

### The Breakthrough

**Realization:** Those three things together strongly characterize PLAY
- Fun with friends → play
- Escaping reality → play
- Simpler world with more power → play world

**But:** "Play" alone isn't powerful enough theme. Needs more bite. Needs conflict.

**Solution:** Play's natural opponent: WORK

**Final theme:**
"Work wants to destroy play, but play must survive"

Or more powerfully: "Slavery vs. Freedom" (work = slavery, play = freedom)

**Why it works:**
- Strong conflict (work vs. play)
- Resonates with kids AND parents
- Central conflict in their lives
- Can be explored playfully

### Theme Implementation

**Story:**
- Robot executives (the Cogs) trying to turn colorful Toontown into dingy office park
- Toons team up to fight Cogs
- Weapons: Gags and practical jokes (play)
- Cog weapons: Office supplies (work)

**Reception:**
- Strange enough to raise eyebrows inside company
- But confident it would work
- **Why:** Theme resonates deeply with audience

**Lesson:** Sometimes finding theme requires exploring what feels right until deeper truth emerges.

## Unifying Theme Checklist

### Initial Theme Development

- [ ] Theme clearly stated in one sentence
- [ ] Theme is specific (not "about pirates" but "fantasy of being a pirate")
- [ ] Team can articulate theme without looking it up
- [ ] Theme fits one of the two types (experience-based or truth-based)

### Testing Theme

- [ ] When I describe theme to people, they get excited
- [ ] Theme feels powerful, not just "cool"
- [ ] Can imagine many ways to reinforce theme
- [ ] Theme helps me make design decisions (keep or cut features)

### Theme Reinforcement

- [ ] Core mechanics reinforce theme
- [ ] Art style reinforces theme
- [ ] Audio/music reinforces theme
- [ ] Interface reinforces theme
- [ ] Story/narrative reinforces theme
- [ ] Even minor details considered for theming (lighting, AC, storage)
- [ ] When in doubt, team asks: "Does this reinforce the theme?"

### Resonance Check

- [ ] Theme taps into universal fantasy or truth
- [ ] Players describe game using words from the theme
- [ ] Theme operates at emotional/subconscious level
- [ ] Even players who don't articulate theme feel its power

## Common Theme Mistakes

**Mistake 1: No clear theme**
- "It's a fun game" isn't a theme
- Results: inconsistent design, elements don't reinforce each other
- Solution: State theme clearly before major development

**Mistake 2: Theme too broad**
- "About space" or "About pirates"
- Doesn't help make decisions
- Solution: Narrow to specific experience or truth

**Mistake 3: Theme as afterthought**
- Add theme after game mostly designed
- Results: theme doesn't actually unify anything
- Solution: Define theme early, use it throughout

**Mistake 4: Not actually using theme**
- Have theme on paper
- Don't actually use it for decisions
- Solution: Every design meeting, ask "Does this reinforce the theme?"

**Mistake 5: Confusing theme with story**
- Theme is what game is about (deeper meaning)
- Story is sequence of events
- Can have same story, different themes
- Solution: Articulate theme separately from plot

**Mistake 6: Theme doesn't resonate**
- Intellectual theme that doesn't touch emotions
- No one gets excited about it
- Solution: Keep searching until you find theme that moves people

**Mistake 7: Inconsistent reinforcement**
- Some elements reinforce theme, others don't
- Weakens overall impact
- Solution: Audit all elements, fix or cut inconsistencies

## Theme Workshop Exercise

**For your current game concept:**

### Part 1: Theme Discovery (20 minutes)

1. **Freewrite:** What excites you about this game? (5 min)
2. **Identify patterns:** What words/ideas repeat? (3 min)
3. **Draft themes:** Write 3-5 possible theme statements (5 min)
4. **Test resonance:** Which one gives you chills? (2 min)
5. **Refine:** Rewrite chosen theme until it feels perfect (5 min)

### Part 2: Reinforcement Brainstorm (30 minutes)

For your chosen theme, list ways to reinforce through:
- Mechanics (5 ways minimum)
- Story (5 ways minimum)
- Aesthetics (5 ways minimum)
- Technology (3 ways minimum)
- Minor details (10 ways—get creative!)

### Part 3: Decision Filter (10 minutes)

List 5 design decisions you're uncertain about. For each:
1. Does it reinforce the theme?
2. If yes, how can it reinforce more strongly?
3. If no, cut it or change it to reinforce theme

## Integration with Other Skills

**With Essential Experience:**
- Essential experience often IS your theme
- Or theme encompasses multiple essential experiences
- Theme provides "why," essential experience provides "what"

**With Interest Curves:**
- Theme should be most powerful at climax
- Hook should hint at theme
- Every peak moment should reinforce theme
- If moments don't serve theme, reconsider them

**With Iteration:**
- Theme helps prioritize what to prototype
- Test: "Does this reinforce theme effectively?"
- Each iteration should strengthen theme reinforcement

## Analyzing Existing Games for Theme

**Practice: Pick a successful game**

1. What is the theme? (Try to state in one sentence)
2. Is it experience-based or truth-based?
3. How do mechanics reinforce theme?
4. How do aesthetics reinforce theme?
5. What details reinforce theme?
6. Where does theme break down?
7. How could theme be stronger?

**Games to analyze:**
- Journey (experience-based, highly resonant)
- Dark Souls (truth-based, resonant)
- Portal (experience-based with truth elements)
- The Last of Us (truth-based, powerful)
- Tetris (experience-based, minimal)
