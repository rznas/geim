# Interest Curves Guide

## What Is an Interest Curve?

**An interest curve charts how captivating your experience is over time.**

**The quality of an entertainment experience** can be measured by the extent to which its unfolding sequence of events is able to hold a guest's interest.

**Note:** We use "guest" instead of "player" because this applies to all entertainment, not just games.

## The Classic Interest Curve

### Anatomy of a Good Interest Curve

```
Interest Level
      |              G (Climax)
      |             /|\  H (Resolution)
      |       E    / | \
      |      /\   /  |  \____
      |  C  /  \ /   |
      | / \/    D    F
  B (Hook)
  |/
A (Initial)
__|________________________________ Time
```

**Point A: Initial Interest**
- Guest comes in with some level of interest
- Based on preconceived expectations
- Influenced by: packaging, ads, friends' advice
- Want this high to get guests in the door
- **But:** Over-inflating can hurt overall experience

**Point B: The Hook**
- Grabs attention and excites
- **Musical:** Opening number
- **Song:** Beatles' "Revolution" screaming guitar riff
- **Play:** Ghost appearing in Hamlet
- **Videogame:** Often intro movie
- **Purpose:** Hints at what's to come, sustains focus during slower beginning

**Points C and E: Interest Peaks**
- Temporary peaks as experience unfolds
- Excitement, revelation, action
- Higher than surrounding moments

**Points D and F: Rest Periods**
- Interest drops temporarily
- But NOT below interest threshold
- Guests catch their breath
- Anticipation of next peak

**Point G: Climax**
- Peak of entire experience
- Exceeds everything before it
- Most dramatic moment

**Point H: Resolution**
- Story resolved
- Guest satisfied
- Experience over
- Hopefully leave with interest left over
- "Leave them wanting more"

### The Bad Interest Curve

```
Interest Level
      |
      |                    d (too late)
  a   |            c      / \
  |\_                    /   \
     \__        Threshold_____\________
         \______/              e
         b (disappointment)
__|________________________________ Time
```

**Point a: Initial Interest**
- Guest comes in with expectations

**Point b: No Hook**
- Immediately disappointed
- Interest begins to wane

**Point c: Interest Threshold Crossed**
- Guest becomes SO disinterested they:
  - Change the channel
  - Leave theater
  - Close book
  - Shut off game

**Point d: Too Late**
- Something interesting finally happens
- But guest already gave up
- Doesn't matter anymore

**Point e: Peters Out**
- No climax
- Just ends
- Not that it matters (guest is gone)

## Using Interest Curves in Design

### Identifying Your Moments

**First question:** What are the top 10 moments in your experience?

**This takes thought and preparation.** But you can't chart an interest curve without knowing your best moments.

**Moments are:**
- Most powerful events
- Most memorable
- Most exciting/dramatic
- Most emotionally resonant
- Stars that make up the constellation of your curve

**For each moment, ask:**
- How powerful is this moment?
- Where does it fall in the timeline?
- What comes before/after it?
- How does it relate to theme and essential experience?

### Charting Your Curve

**Step 1: List your moments**
- Write down top 10-20 moments
- Don't worry about order yet

**Step 2: Plot them on timeline**
- X-axis: Time (beginning to end)
- Y-axis: Interest level (subjective)
- Place each moment roughly where it occurs

**Step 3: Draw the curve**
- Connect the dots
- Add the rests between peaks
- Identify hook, climax, resolution

**Step 4: Analyze the shape**
- Does it match the good curve pattern?
- Or does it look like the bad curve?
- What needs to change?

### Common Problems and Solutions

**Problem: Flat beginning (no hook)**
- **Solution:** Move exciting moment to start OR create new hook
- **Example:** Juggling show moved ring routine to beginning

**Problem: Wrong ordering (best moment too early)**
- **Solution:** Rearrange moments so they build
- **Example:** Juggling show moved club passing to end (climax)

**Problem: Flat section in middle**
- **Solution 1:** Cut the flat section entirely
- **Solution 2:** Add shortcut past it
- **Example:** Aladdin VR added shortcut past flat section

**Problem: No clear climax**
- **Solution:** Identify/create moment that exceeds all others
- Place at end
- Make sure it truly is climactic

**Problem: Too many high peaks**
- **Solution:** Not all peaks can be climax
- Lower some peaks to create contrast
- Climax loses impact if everything is equally intense

**Problem: Crosses interest threshold**
- **Solution:** Identify where/why interest drops too low
- Either cut that section or boost its interest

## The Fractal Nature of Interest Curves

**Key insight:** Interest curve patterns repeat at multiple scales.

**Each long peak can have internal structure that mirrors overall pattern.**

### Three Typical Levels in Video Games

**Level 1: Overall Game**
- Intro movie (hook)
- Series of game levels (rising interest)
- Final boss/conclusion (climax)
- Credits and epilogue (resolution)
- Duration: Hours to tens of hours

**Level 2: Each Game Level**
- New aesthetics/challenges (level hook)
- Series of encounters/puzzles
- Boss battle or major challenge (level climax)
- Duration: 30 minutes to 2 hours

**Level 3: Each Encounter**
- Interesting setup (encounter hook)
- Rising challenge as you work through it
- Satisfying conclusion
- Duration: 30 seconds to 5 minutes

**Visualization:**
```
Overall Game
/\  /\  /\  /\  /\  /\  /\
||  ||  ||  ||  ||  ||  ||
Zoom into one peak:

One Level
/\/\/\/\/\/\
||||||||||||
Zoom into one peak:

One Encounter
/\/\/\
```

### Designing Fractal Interest

**Advantage:** Keeps interest high at all scales
- Moment-to-moment interesting
- Level-by-level interesting
- Overall arc interesting

**Process:**
1. Design overall interest curve (hours)
2. For each major peak, design sub-curve (level design)
3. For each level peak, design encounter curves (minute-by-minute)

**Check at each level:**
- Does this have a hook?
- Does it build?
- Does it have a climax?
- Does it have rest periods?

## The Three Components of Interest

Interest is not monolithic. It can be broken down into three factors.

### Factor 1: Inherent Interest

**Definition:** Some events are simply more interesting than others.

**Generally:**
- Risk > safety
- Fancy > plain
- Unusual > ordinary
- Dramatic change > status quo
- Potential for change > static

**Examples:**
- Man wrestling alligator > man eating cheese sandwich
- Explosion > nothing happening
- Surprise twist > predictable outcome

**Connected to base instincts:**
- Survival
- Curiosity
- Social status
- Reproduction
- Safety/danger

**Connected to higher instincts:**
- Love and connection
- Justice and fairness
- Beauty and transcendence
- Meaning and purpose

**Questions to ask:**
- What aspects of my game immediately capture interest?
- Does my game let players see/do something they've never experienced?
- What base instincts does it appeal to?
- What higher instincts does it appeal to?
- Does dramatic change happen? Can it be more dramatic?

**Note:** Events don't stand alone. They build on one another (story arc).

**Example:** Goldilocks and the Three Bears
- Eating porridge, sitting in chairs, napping (not inherently interesting)
- But these boring events enable the interesting part (bears discover disturbance)
- Setup makes payoff possible

### Factor 2: Poetry of Presentation

**Definition:** The aesthetics of the entertainment experience.

**Includes:**
- Writing quality
- Music composition and performance
- Visual design and art
- Acting and performance
- Cinematography
- Comedy timing
- Any aesthetic quality

**The more beautiful the artistry, the more interesting and compelling.**

**Best case:** Beautiful presentation of inherently interesting event.

**Questions to ask:**
- What elements make up my game?
- How can each one be more beautiful?
- What combinations of elements are poetic/beautiful?
- What does beauty mean within my game's context?

**Example:** Violin concerto
- Events (two sticks rubbed together) not inherently interesting
- Projection usually not notable
- **Poetry must carry the experience**
- If not beautifully played, not interesting
- But beautiful music alone can sustain interest

### Factor 3: Projection

**Definition:** The extent to which you compel the guest to use empathy and imagination to put themselves into the experience.

**Why it matters:**
- Events that happen to us are more interesting than events that happen to others
- Stranger winning lottery: mildly interesting
- Friend winning lottery: more interesting
- YOU winning lottery: extremely interesting

**The Projection Spectrum:**
```
Stranger → Acquaintance → Friend → You
(Low projection)        (High projection)
```

**Two mechanisms for projection:**

**1. Empathy**
- Guests put themselves in place of characters
- Start as strangers
- Become like friends as you know them
- Sometimes you mentally become them (peak projection)

**Storyteller's advantage:**
- Create characters guests can empathize with easily
- The more empathy, the more interesting events become

**2. Imagination**
- Entertainment creates little world in imagination
- World doesn't have to be realistic
- MUST be internally consistent
- When world is consistent and compelling, guest enters it mentally
- "Immersion" in the story world

**Suspension of disbelief is fragile:**
- One contradiction can break immersion
- "Takes them out" of the experience
- Brings them back to reality

**Interactive entertainment's advantage:**
- Guest CAN BE the main character
- Events ACTUALLY HAPPEN to the guest
- Overlap between perception and imagination
- Can directly manipulate and change the world

**Why videogames can work with low inherent interest/poetry:**
- Make up for it with projection
- Player makes all decisions
- Success/failure contingent on player's performance
- Shortcut traditional storytelling cannot take

**Episodic entertainment advantages:**
- Characters and world persist across episodes
- Returning guests already familiar
- Each episode grows projection
- Fantasy world becomes "more real" over time

**But fragile:**
- One bad episode can spoil entire series
- Compromised characters seem phony going forward
- Hard to sustain projection after contradiction

**Transmedia advantages:**
- Toys and games based on movies/TV
- Not just gimmicks to make money
- Provide new ways to access fantasy world
- More time in world = greater projection
- More projection = more meaningful experience

**Questions to ask:**
- What can players relate to in my game?
- What captures player imagination?
- Are there places players have always wanted to visit?
- Does player get to be a character they could imagine themselves to be?
- Are there other characters players would want to meet (or spy on)?
- Do players get to do things they'd like to do in real life but can't?
- Is there an activity that once players start, it's hard to stop?

## Half-Life 2: A Case Study

**Game:** Half-Life 2, Episode 1
**Duration:** Average 5 hours 39 minutes
**Data:** Player death tracking

**Death curve by difficulty:**
```
Deaths
  |        /\
  |       /  \
  |      /    \
  |  /\ /      \  /\
  | /  ∨        \/  \
  |/                 \__
  |_____________________
       Time (5h 39m)
```

**Three lines for three difficulty settings, all similar shape.**

**Interpretation:**
- Number of deaths = challenge level
- Challenge level connected to interest
- Shape matches classic interest curve:
  - Hook at beginning
  - Rising challenges with rest periods
  - Major climax
  - Resolution

**Lesson:** Even 5+ hour experiences can follow interest curve pattern.

## Practical Application

### For New Designs

**Step 1: Brainstorm moments (15 minutes)**
- List 20-30 potential moments
- Don't filter yet

**Step 2: Identify top moments (10 minutes)**
- Mark 10-15 best moments
- Rate each for excitement/power

**Step 3: Plot initial curve (10 minutes)**
- Place moments on timeline (X-axis)
- Rate interest level (Y-axis)
- Draw curve connecting them

**Step 4: Analyze shape (10 minutes)**
- Does it match good curve pattern?
- Identify problems:
  - Weak hook?
  - Wrong ordering?
  - Weak climax?
  - Flat sections?
  - Too many peaks?

**Step 5: Redesign (20 minutes)**
- Rearrange moments
- Cut weak moments
- Strengthen hook and climax
- Add rest periods if needed
- Redraw curve

**Step 6: Detail fractal levels (30 minutes)**
- For each major peak (level), sketch sub-curve
- For key encounters, sketch micro-curve
- Ensure all levels have good shape

### For Existing Games

**Step 1: Play through and mark moments (varies)**
- Play your game
- Note when interest peaks
- Note when it dips
- Write down all moments

**Step 2: Chart observed curve (15 minutes)**
- Plot moments on timeline
- Draw interest curve
- Be honest about flat sections

**Step 3: Playtest with others (varies)**
- Watch players play
- Observe their engagement:
  - Leaning forward?
  - Eyes wide?
  - Smiling?
  - Distracted?
  - Bored?
- Mark when their interest peaks/dips

**Step 4: Compare curves (10 minutes)**
- Your expected curve
- Your experienced curve
- Playtesters' curves
- Where do they differ?

**Step 5: Identify problems (15 minutes)**
- Where does observed interest drop below expected?
- Where do playtesters lose interest?
- What's causing the gap?

**Step 6: Fix problems (varies)**
- Rearrange content
- Cut weak sections
- Boost flat sections
- Strengthen climax
- Add shortcut past problems

**Step 7: Test again (varies)**
- Iterate until curves match

## Demographics and Interest Curves

**Different players have different interests.**

**Age differences:**
- Children: faster pacing, more color, simpler stories
- Adults: slower pacing, complex stories, subtlety

**Gender differences (generalizations):**
- Different inherent interests
- Different projection targets
- Different aesthetic preferences

**Player type differences:**
- Hardcore gamers: high challenge, complex systems
- Casual players: accessibility, immediate fun

**Creating multiple curves:**
- Chart interest curve for each demographic
- Ensure game appeals to intended audiences
- May discover unintended audiences

**Example approach:**
```
Interest (Kids)
  |   /\    /\    /\    /\
  |  /  \  /  \  /  \  /  \
  |_/    \/    \/    \/    \_

Interest (Adults)
  |           /\
  |          /  \
  |       /\/    \  /\
  |    /\/        \/  \
  |_/\/                \__
```

**Kids:** More frequent peaks, less buildup
**Adults:** Longer buildup, bigger climax

## Integration with Other Skills

**With Essential Experience:**
- Top moments should deliver essential experience
- Climax should deliver it most powerfully
- If moments don't connect to essential experience, reconsider

**With Theme:**
- Moments should reinforce theme
- Climax should be most thematic moment
- Pattern of curve can itself express theme (e.g., chaos vs. order)

**With Iteration:**
- Chart curves for each prototype
- Test: Does observed interest match expected?
- Iterate based on gaps

- Deaths/failures (challenge proxy)

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Never charting curve**
- Design blind without seeing overall shape
- Miss obvious pacing problems
- Solution: Chart curve early and often

**Mistake 2: Charting but not acting**
- Draw curve, see problems, ignore them
- "It'll be fine"
- Solution: If curve shows problem, fix it

**Mistake 3: Only charting overall curve**
- Miss fractal structure
- Moment-to-moment can be boring even if overall arc is good
- Solution: Chart at multiple scales

**Mistake 4: Optimizing for wrong audience**
- Chart what YOU find interesting
- Target audience has different curve
- Solution: Playtest with target demographic

**Mistake 5: All peaks, no rests**
- Exhausting
- Numbing (constant excitement becomes boring)
- Climax has no contrast
- Solution: Intentionally design rest periods

**Mistake 6: Too much reliance on one factor**
- All inherent interest, no poetry or projection
- All poetry, no inherent interest or projection
- All projection, no inherent interest or poetry
- Solution: Balance all three factors
