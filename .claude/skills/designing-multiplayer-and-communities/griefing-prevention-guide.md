# Griefing Prevention Guide

## Introduction

Griefing is when players derive enjoyment from teasing, tricking, and torturing other players rather than from the game itself. Griefers are the "Joker" in Bartle's player type system—they see themselves as higher status because they can spoil a game that others care about and they don't.

**Core Principle**: The best approach is to **make griefing boring** rather than policing it after the fact.

---

## Understanding Griefers

### Griefer Psychology

**Status Motivation (Lens #90):**
- Griefers see themselves as higher status than other players
- Power comes from spoiling game that others care about
- Indifference to game makes them feel superior
- Exploiting loopholes demonstrates intelligence/superiority

**What Griefers Enjoy:**
- Making other players upset or frustrated
- Demonstrating power over other players
- Finding and exploiting loopholes
- Ruining experiences people care about
- Getting reactions from victims
- Feeling clever or superior

**What Makes Griefing Fun:**
- Visible negative reactions from victims
- Sense of power and control
- Exclusive knowledge (exploit others don't know)
- Disrupting order and systems
- Attention (even negative attention)

**What Makes Griefing Boring:**
- No reaction from victims (no feedback)
- Too difficult to execute
- No visible impact
- Gets them banned quickly
- Everyone can do it (not special)
- Victims don't care

---

## Anti-Griefing Principle

**Traditional Approach (Bad):**
- Allow griefing-friendly systems
- Create anti-griefing policies
- Police griefing behavior
- Maintain "court of law" to decide cases
- Distinguish intentional abuse from "fooling around"
- Ugly, difficult, resource-intensive

**Better Approach:**
- Avoid game systems that make griefing easy
- Design so griefing is boring or ineffective
- Remove griefer tools at design level
- Eliminate loopholes quickly
- Provide no feedback for griefing attempts

**Key Insight**: An ounce of prevention is worth a pound of cure. Design systems that don't enable griefing rather than trying to moderate it after.

## Common Griefing Vectors and Solutions

### 1. Player vs. Player (PvP) Combat

**The Problem:**

Unrestricted PvP is easiest system for griefers to exploit:

**Classic Griefing Pattern:**
1. Befriend a player
2. Build trust over time
3. Unexpectedly kill them
4. Steal their inventory
5. Enjoy their frustration

**Why It's Toxic:**
- Creates environment where players fear strangers
- Destroys trust and community
- Victims feel betrayed and violated
- Generally done for torture, not game advantage

**When PvP Makes Sense:**
- Core activity of game (FPS, fighting games, battle royale)
- Clear competitive purpose
- Expected by all players
- Balanced and fair

**When PvP Damages Community:**
- Not core activity of game
- Used primarily for griefing
- Creates constant threat feeling
- Prevents trust and socialization

**Solutions:**

#### Confine PvP to Specific Areas
- **PvP zones**: Clearly marked dangerous areas
- **PvP flags**: Players opt-in to PvP
- **Safe zones**: Cities, newbie areas are protected
- **Dueling systems**: Consensual PvP only
- **Arenas**: Dedicated PvP spaces

**Example**: World of Warcraft PvE servers (PvP only in specific zones or by consent)

#### Level/Skill Restrictions
- Can't attack players much weaker
- Level bracketing for PvP areas
- Scaling systems that balance levels
- Penalties for attacking lower-level players

#### Consequences for Unprovoked Attacks
- Criminal status (guards attack on sight)
- Reputation penalties
- Loot penalties (get less from victims)
- Time-out penalties
- Not allowed in certain areas while flagged

#### Full Loot Prevention
- Can't loot players in PvP
- Insurance systems (get items back)
- Bind-on-pickup important items
- Death penalties don't include losing items

### 2. Stealing and Looting

**The Problem:**

Ability to rob others of items/power is extremely attractive to griefers:

**Types of Stealing:**
- **Pickpocketing**: Direct theft from inventory
- **Kill looting**: Taking items from defeated players
- **Kill stealing**: Stealing final blow to get all rewards
- **Ninja looting**: Taking group rewards unfairly
- **Resource stealing**: Taking gathering nodes others were heading toward

**Why It's Toxic:**
- Being stolen from feels violating
- Griefers love that feeling of violation
- Destroys trust in community
- Makes players paranoid and defensive

**EverQuest Kill Stealing Example:**
- Only player with final blow got experience
- Griefers would wait nearby battles
- Sneak in killing blow at last second
- "Steal" all the experience
- Few did this as strategy, many for griefing

**Solutions:**

#### Prevent Direct Theft
- No pickpocketing other players
- No looting other players' bodies
- Bind important items to player
- Bank systems for secure storage
- Death doesn't drop items to others

#### Shared Kill Credit
- Everyone who damages enemy gets credit
- Experience shared among contributors
- Rewards based on contribution percentage
- No benefit to "stealing" kills
- Group automatically shares everything

#### Fair Loot Distribution
- Round-robin looting in groups
- Need/greed roll systems
- Personal loot (everyone gets own drops)
- Transparent trade windows
- Can't misrepresent items in trades

#### Resource Sharing
- Gathering nodes appear for each player
- Mining/harvesting creates copy for everyone
- Timed exclusivity (first player has 30 seconds)
- Shared node depletion (everyone can gather)

---

### 3. Unfair Trading

**The Problem:**

Trading systems allow misrepresentation and scamming:

**Griefing Patterns:**
- Misrepresenting item quality or stats
- Bait-and-switch (changing item at last second)
- Exploiting new players' ignorance
- Scam trades ("Trust trade" cons)
- Currency conversion scams

**Solutions:**

#### Total Information Transparency
- Both players see exactly what they're getting
- Clear display of all item stats
- Can't modify after both accept
- Confirmation screen showing final trade
- No hidden properties

#### Trade Windows with Confirmation
- Both must accept
- Change resets acceptances
- Final confirmation required
- Clear "You give / You receive" display
- Timer to review before finalizing

#### Auction House Systems
- Standardized pricing
- Transparent bid history
- Buyout prices clearly shown
- Can't back out of accepted bids
- System-mediated (no direct player trading)

#### No "Trust Trades"
- All trades through secure system
- No need to drop items
- No middleman trades
- System holds items during trade
- Automatic exchange on acceptance

**Example**: World of Warcraft trade window shows everything clearly, requires multiple confirmations, resets if changed.

---

### 4. Obscene Language and Harassment

**The Problem:**

Griefers enjoy using shocking, disturbing language:

**Why Traditional Filters Fail:**

**Black List (Forbidden Words):**
- Griefers find creative misspellings
- Human pattern recognition beats machine
- Becomes game to beat the filter
- Always ways around (l33t speak, symbols)

**White List (Only Allowed Words):**
- Too restrictive for normal conversation
- Still beatable with creative combinations
- Frustrates legitimate players

**Solutions:**

#### Automated Filter + Player Reporting
- Automated filter catches obvious cases
- Players report sophisticated griefing
- Human review of reports
- Penalties for confirmed abuse
- Works better than either alone

#### No Feedback for Filtered Content
**Key Technique**: Let griefer see obscenity on their screen, filter it for everyone else

**Why This Works:**
- Griefer doesn't know if filter worked
- Can't iterate to beat filter
- Much more work to find workarounds
- Much less fun (no visible reaction)
- Still possible to beat, but boring

**Example**: Griefer types offensive message → sees it normally → everyone else sees "[filtered]" → griefer doesn't know it was filtered → can't easily test what works

#### Voice Chat Challenges
- Harder problem than text
- Voice recognition improving
- **NBA 2K14**: Technical fouls for spoken obscenities
- Mute and report systems
- Optional voice chat (opt-in)

#### Positive Community Tools
- Player commendations
- Honor systems
- Reputation scores
- "Good player" badges
- Silence toxic, reward positive

### 5. Blocking Pathways

**The Problem:**

Simple but extremely annoying: standing in doorways or paths to prevent movement.

**Griefing Pattern:**
- Find chokepoint (doorway, bridge, narrow path)
- Block with character(s)
- Prevent other players from passing
- Enjoy their frustration
- Especially effective with larger avatars

**Solutions:**

#### Allow Passthrough
- Players can walk through each other
- Temporary collision only (can push through)
- Collision disabled in cities/safe zones
- No collision with idle players

**Trade-off**: Less physical presence, but prevents griefing

#### Wide Chokepoints
- Design doorways wide enough for multiple players
- Multiple paths to destinations
- No mandatory single-point passages
- Teleportation options

#### Push Mechanics
- Players can push other players
- Slow push (must hold button)
- Only works on idle players
- Makes blocking difficult

**Toontown Online Example:**
- Chose push mechanic
- Griefers adapted: found "abandoned avatars" (player stepped away)
- Slowly pushed them down street into battles
- Even solutions have griefing potential

**Lesson**: No perfect solution, minimize griefability

### 6. System Loopholes

**The Problem:**

Griefer's greatest joy: finding exploits that let them do things they shouldn't.

**Common Loopholes:**

**Disconnect Exploits:**
- Disconnect during battle to deny rewards
- Disconnect to avoid death
- Disconnect to duplicate items
- Disconnect to escape consequences

**Physics Exploits:**
- Jumping in corners crashes server
- Getting stuck in geometry
- Terrain clipping
- Collision boundary exploits

**Economy Exploits:**
- Item duplication glitches
- Infinite money bugs
- Trade window exploits
- Auction house manipulation

**Social Exploits:**
- Arranging furniture to spell obscenities
- Names that look like system messages
- Impersonating staff
- Abusing friend/block systems

**Why Loopholes Are Appealing:**
- Feel powerful and important
- Exclusive knowledge
- Can vandalize or annoy
- Show superiority over other players
- Especially if others don't know how

**Solutions:**

#### Extensive Testing
- Beta testing with large groups
- Dedicated exploit testing
- Adversarial testing (try to break game)
- Automated testing for edge cases
- Community bug reports

#### Quick Patching
- Fast response to discovered exploits
- Rollback affected accounts/items
- Clear communication about fixes
- Thank reporters (encourage reporting)

#### Design for Failure States
- Graceful disconnect handling
- Server-authoritative systems (client can't cheat)
- Validation of all player actions
- Sanity checks on values
- Impossible actions rejected

#### Monitoring and Analytics
- Track unusual patterns
- Flag impossible statistics
- Monitor economy for manipulation
- Alert on suspicious behavior
- Data-driven exploit detection

#### Clear Consequences
- Ban for deliberate exploit abuse
- Temporary ban for minor cases
- Removal of ill-gotten gains
- Public acknowledgment of action
- But: forgiveness for accidental discovery

**Important Balance:**
- Don't punish players for discovering bugs
- Punish deliberate exploitation
- Reward responsible disclosure
- Create bug bounty programs

## Lens #99: The Lens of Griefing

To make sure griefing in your game is minimized, ask yourself these questions:

**What systems in my game are easy to grief?**
- PvP combat?
- Stealing/looting?
- Trading?
- Communication?
- Blocking movement?
- Resource systems?

**How can I make my game boring to grief?**
- No feedback for griefing attempts?
- No visible impact on victims?
- Too difficult or time-consuming?
- Doesn't grant power or status?
- Everyone can do it (not exclusive)?

**Am I ignoring any loopholes?**
- Disconnect exploits?
- Physics glitches?
- Economy manipulation?
- Social engineering?
- Edge cases in systems?

---

## Lens #95½: The Lens of Cheatability

No one wants to worry about being beaten by a cheater—it makes you feel like a chump. To make sure your players trust your game, ask yourself these questions:

**Can players cheat at my game? How?**
- Client-side manipulation?
- Network exploits?
- Input spoofing?
- Information exposure?
- Automation/botting?

**If players can cheat, will anyone notice?**
- Is cheating detectable?
- Are outcomes too good to be possible?
- Suspicious patterns emerge?
- Other players can tell?
- System flags unusual behavior?

**Do players trust my game?**
- Believe others are playing fair?
- Feel matches are balanced?
- Trust anti-cheat systems?
- Report suspicious behavior?
- Continue playing despite concerns?

**Why Cheatability Matters:**

If players realize cheating is possible and can't tell who's cheating:
- Non-cheaters worry they can't win without cheating
- No one wants to lose to a cheater
- Drives away honest players
- Leaves only cheaters
- Destroys game community

**Solutions:**
- Server-authoritative systems
- Anti-cheat software
- Replay analysis
- Statistical anomaly detection
- Player reporting systems
- Transparent ban policies
- Regular communication about anti-cheat efforts

## Griefing Prevention Checklist

Use this during design to minimize griefing potential:

**PvP Systems:**
- [ ] Is PvP core to the game or optional?
- [ ] Can PvP be confined to specific areas/modes?
- [ ] Are there level/skill restrictions?
- [ ] Can players opt out?
- [ ] Are there consequences for unprovoked attacks?
- [ ] Is full looting prevented or restricted?

**Stealing/Looting:**
- [ ] Can players steal from each other? (Consider removing)
- [ ] Is kill credit shared among contributors?
- [ ] Are loot rolls transparent and fair?
- [ ] Are important items protected (bind-on-pickup)?
- [ ] Can players grief by "ninja looting"?

**Trading:**
- [ ] Are all trades completely transparent?
- [ ] Can items be misrepresented?
- [ ] Multiple confirmations required?
- [ ] Clear display of what each side gets?
- [ ] Are "trust trades" necessary? (Eliminate them)

**Communication:**
- [ ] Automated filtering in place?
- [ ] Player reporting system exists?
- [ ] No feedback for filtered messages?
- [ ] Voice chat moderation plan?
- [ ] Positive behavior rewards?

**Movement/Blocking:**
- [ ] Can players block pathways?
- [ ] Players can pass through each other?
- [ ] Multiple routes to destinations?
- [ ] Wide enough chokepoints?
- [ ] Push mechanics considered?

**Loopholes:**
- [ ] Extensive exploit testing done?
- [ ] Fast patching process in place?
- [ ] Server-authoritative for critical systems?
- [ ] Monitoring for unusual patterns?
- [ ] Clear exploit reporting process?
- [ ] Consequences for deliberate exploitation?

**Anti-Cheat:**
- [ ] Server validates all critical actions?
- [ ] Anti-cheat systems in place?
- [ ] Cheating detectable and reported?
- [ ] Players trust fairness of game?
- [ ] Regular communication about anti-cheat?

## Case Studies

### Toontown Online: Cannot Heal Self

**Problem**: Wanted to encourage cooperation and communication

**Solution**: Players cannot heal themselves during battle—only heal other players

**Concern**: Players would find this frustrating

**Result**:
- Forced communication ("I need a Toon-up!")
- Encouraged helping behavior
- Created interdependence
- Minimal frustration

**Unexpected Griefing**:
- Push mechanic to prevent blocking
- Griefers pushed "abandoned avatars" into battles
- Even good solutions have griefing potential

**Lesson**: Encouraging cooperation can create positive community, but test for edge cases

---

### EverQuest: Kill Stealing

**Problem**: Only final-blow player got experience

**Griefing Pattern**:
- Wait near other players' battles
- When enemy nearly dead, sneak in final blow
- Steal all experience
- Mainly for griefing, not strategy

**Solution**: Shared kill credit in later games
- Everyone who damages gets experience
- Contribution-based rewards
- No incentive to "steal" kills

**Lesson**: Design systems so griefing provides no advantage

---

### Eve Online: Embracing Conflict

**Different Approach**: Integrate "griefing" as game mechanic

**How**:
- Scamming, stealing, betrayal are allowed
- But within structured systems
- Consequences exist (reputation, bounties)
- Part of core game fantasy (cutthroat space capitalism)

**Why It Works**:
- Players expect and accept it
- Part of core experience, not griefing
- Still has rules and limits
- Reputation systems create consequences

**Lesson**: If "griefing" is core to game fantasy, make it official mechanic with structure and consequences. Don't allow it in games where it's not expected.

---

## Key Principles

1. **Design Out Griefing**: Prevention better than punishment

2. **Make Griefing Boring**: Remove power, feedback, and fun from griefing

3. **No Feedback Loop**: Don't let griefers know their griefing worked

4. **Trust Over Policing**: Build systems that create trust, not systems that require policing

5. **Consider Lens #80 (Help)**: Make helping more rewarding than harming

6. **Fast Response**: Patch loopholes quickly when discovered

7. **Server Authority**: Never trust client for critical decisions

8. **Transparency in Combat**: Clear, fair systems reduce cheating accusations

9. **Community Self-Regulation**: Empower players to avoid griefers (block, report)

10. **Four Times the Effort**: Remember multiplayer is 4x harder—griefing is part of why

---

## Final Thoughts

**Griefing is Inevitable**: Some people will always try to spoil others' fun.

**Design's Role**: Make griefing as difficult and unrewarding as possible.

**Community's Role**: Empowered to avoid, report, and ostracize griefers.

**Your Role**: Vigilant testing, quick patching, and systems that reward cooperation over harm.

**Remember**: "An ounce of prevention is worth a pound of cure." Design systems that don't enable griefing rather than trying to police it afterward.

The best anti-griefing design makes cooperation more fun, more rewarding, and more powerful than griefing—so even potential griefers choose to be helpful instead.
