# Ten Tips for Strong Communities

## Introduction

Community is complex and involves many interrelated psychological phenomena, but these ten practical tips provide concrete ways to foster community around your game.

**Four Elements of Community (Psychological Model):**
1. **Membership**: Something distinct makes it clear you're part of this group
2. **Influence**: Being part gives you power over something
3. **Integration and fulfillment of needs**: Being part does something for you
4. **Shared emotional connection**: Guaranteed sharing of emotions about events

**Amy Jo Kim's Definition**: "A group of people with a shared interest, purpose, or goal who get to know each other better over time."

---

## Tip #1: Foster Friendships

Friendships are the glue that holds communities together. Online friendships require three things:

### 1. The Ability to Talk

**Problem**: Surprisingly many online games offer no communication ability, hoping nonverbal play will suffice.

**Solution**: Players must be able to speak to each other freely for community to form.

**Implementation:**
- Text chat (global, team, private messages)
- Voice chat (opt-in for comfort)
- Emotes and quick communication tools
- Translation tools for international players
- Permanent chat logs players can review
- Thread-based conversations for asynchronous play

### 2. Someone Worth Talking To

**Problem**: You can't assume strangers will want to talk to each other (like strangers on a bus won't mingle).

**Who Players Want to Talk To:**
- **Their existing friends** (most important for social media)
- **Celebrities or interesting people** (influencers, skilled players)

**Age-Based Patterns:**
- **Adults**: Others who relate to their problems, similar interests
- **Teenagers**: Members of another gender, people more interesting than regular friends
- **Children**: Generally little interest in strangers—prefer real-life friends

**Questions to Answer:**
- Do your players seek competitors? Collaborators? Assistants?
- Quick chats or long-term relationships?
- Similar-skill players or mentors/students?

**Implementation:**
- Robust matchmaking (skill, playstyle, preferences)
- Friend finding tools (search, recommendations)
- Guild/clan recruitment systems
- Mentor/student pairing programs
- Social spaces where interesting people congregate
- Celebrity/influencer integration

### 3. Something Worth Talking About

**Problem**: First two items could be satisfied by a chat room. Games need content to discuss.

**Sources of Discussion:**
- **Gameplay variety**: Different content every time (Words with Friends, Draw Something)
- **Strategic depth**: Complex strategies to discuss (chess communities)
- **Events and updates**: Introduced over time (MMOs, CCGs)
- **Rule changes**: Balance patches, new content
- **Shared experiences**: "Did you see that?!" moments

**Balance**: "Good online games are more community than game" is FALSE. They need solid balance of both:
- Not interesting enough game → community has nothing to discuss
- Not good enough community support → players enjoy but wander off

**Implementation:**
- Regular content updates and events
- Strategic complexity worth analyzing
- Replay systems for discussing gameplay
- Patch notes and developer communication
- Emergent gameplay worth sharing stories about
- Procedural content creating unique experiences

### The Three Phases of Friendship

Your game must support each phase for friendships to blossom and survive:

#### Phase 1: Breaking the Ice

**Challenge**: Meeting people for the first time is awkward.

**Solution**: Low social pressure engagement that allows self-expression.

**Implementation:**
- Easy discovery of compatible players
- Low-stakes initial interactions
- Optional interactions (not forced)
- Shared objectives in non-competitive settings
- Icebreaker mechanics (cooperative tutorial)
- Profile systems showing interests/playstyle
- Spectating before joining

#### Phase 2: Becoming Friends

**The Moment**: Mysterious and subtle, but almost always involves conversation about something both care about.

**Catalyst**: Often conversation about a gameplay experience just shared.

**Solution**: Opportunities to chat after intense shared experiences.

**Implementation:**
- Post-match chat lobbies
- Victory/defeat celebration screens with time to talk
- "Add friend" prompts after good matches
- Friend-making rituals (formal friend requests)
- Commendation systems ("honor opponent" features)
- Shared achievement celebrations
- Team formation mechanics

#### Phase 3: Staying Friends

**Challenge**: Meeting and making friends is one thing—maintaining them is another.

**Solution**: Give people ways to find each other again.

**Implementation:**
- Robust friends lists
- Guild/clan systems
- Memorable nicknames and avatars
- Last-online indicators
- Scheduled events (raid times, tournaments)
- Friend activity feeds
- Notification when friends come online
- Cross-game friend systems
- Import friends from social media

**Different Age Groups Want Different Friendships:**
- **Adults**: Making friends with similar interests
- **Kids**: Playing games with real-life friends

## Tip #2: Put Conflict at the Heart

**Principle**: Conflict is at the heart of all communities.

**Examples:**
- Sports team → conflict with other teams
- Parent/teacher association → fighting for better schools
- Vintage car fanatics → shared battle against entropy

**In Games:**

Conflict must either:
1. **Spur players to prove they're better** (conflict against other players)
2. **Require cooperation to resolve** (conflict against the game)

**Not All Conflict Creates Community:**
- Solitaire conflict doesn't create community
- The conflict must be shared or competitive against others

**Best Games Have Both:**

**Collectible Card Games:**
- Competitive: Being the best player in community
- Cooperative: Strategies so complex players share and discuss

**Minecraft:**
- Cooperative: Working together to build
- Competitive: Being the best builder in community

**Implementation:**
- Clear common enemies or challenges
- Competitive rankings and status
- Strategic complexity worth discussing
- Cooperative challenges requiring teamwork
- Guild vs. guild competition
- Server-wide events against game threats
- Leaderboards creating friendly rivalry

## Tip #3: Use Architecture to Shape Your Community

**Real-World Example:**

Some neighborhoods foster community, others don't—usually due to design:

**Community-Building Architecture:**
- Walkable with meaningful destinations
- Dead-end streets (you recognize passersby)
- Frequent opportunities to see same people
- Time and space to talk during encounters

**Online World Translation:**

Create places where people see each other repeatedly and have time to talk:

**Implementation:**
- Social hubs (main cities, gathering spaces)
- Bottleneck locations (everyone passes through)
- Waiting areas (before matches, during queues)
- Guild halls and private spaces
- Casual congregation areas
- Regular routes to important destinations
- Friend-finding spaces (newbie areas, help zones)
- Buddy lists make "neighbors" findable

**MMO Pattern:**
- Areas where players casually congregate and chat
- Often at places players pass regularly
- On route to important game business
- Natural waiting points (auction houses, quest hubs)

## Tip #4: Create Community Property

**Principle**: Things owned by groups (not individuals) encourage banding together.

**Examples:**

**Ships in Naval Game:**
- Individual can't afford to buy ship
- Group teams up to own it together
- Instant community (must communicate and be friendly)

**Eve Online Success:**
- Largely based on community property
- Territory, stations, capital ships
- Requires coordination and cooperation

**Guild Status:**
- Type of community property
- Shared reputation and achievements
- Everyone benefits from guild success

**Implementation:**
- Guild bases/halls that require collective resources
- Shared bank systems
- Community-funded projects
- Territory control systems
- Group achievements and titles
- Collaborative building (shared fortresses, bases)
- Co-owned vehicles, equipment, businesses
- Guild reputation systems
- Shared progression (unlock features for whole guild)

**Design Considerations:**
- How do groups make decisions about shared property?
- What happens when members leave?
- How do new members gain stake in property?
- Can individuals harm shared property (prevent or allow)?

## Tip #5: Let Players Express Themselves

**Importance**: Self-expression is very important in any multiplayer game.

**Why It Matters:**

You're creating a fantasy world where players can be whatever they like—why not let them express that?

**Expression Through Gameplay vs. Identity:**
- Gameplay strategies and styles (good, but not enough)
- Visual identity and customization (crucial for community)

**Popular Expression Systems:**
- **Rich avatar creation**: Beloved by online game players
- **Communication customization**: Emotion, colors, text styles
- **Vanity items**: Key monetization (League of Legends, Color Switch)

**Not Limited to Online Games:**

**Charades/Pictionary:**
- Expressive power in performance

**Board Game Example:**
- Game about kids trying to stay clean
- Color dirt onto character card when dirty
- Players create stories about how they got dirty
- Color characters to match stories

**Monopoly:**
- 12 different pieces for 2-8 players
- Easy way to ensure player expression and choice

**Implementation:**
- Avatar creation systems (face, body, appearance)
- Cosmetic customization (skins, outfits, colors)
- Emotes and animations
- Player-created content
- Custom emblems and symbols
- Player housing/base decoration
- Titles and badges
- Victory poses and celebrations
- Communication style options
- Playstyle statistics and identity

**Monetization Opportunity:**
- Vanity items are key revenue source
- Players pay to express unique identity
- No pay-to-win concerns
- Supports continued development

## Tip #6: Support Three Levels

**Principle**: You're designing three separate games for players at different experience levels.

### Level 1: The Newbie

**Their Game**: Learning to play IS the game

**Challenge**: Overwhelmed by just learning, not yet by gameplay

**Obligation**: Design learning process to be rewarding

**Risk**: Newbies give up before getting into it, limiting audience

**Best Practice**: Create meaningful interactions with experienced players

**Battletech Online Example:**
- Experienced players = generals recruiting armies
- Newbies = honored to be recruited
- Placed on front line (dangerous but exciting)
- Generals get "cannon fodder"
- Newbies get immediate action
- Win-win despite newbies getting slaughtered

**Implementation:**
- Rewarding tutorials
- Mentor systems (rewards for helping newbies)
- Protected newbie zones
- Gradual introduction of complexity
- Quick early victories
- Experienced player interactions
- Clear progression feedback
- Guidance from community members

### Level 2: The Player

**Their Game**: Main game activities and mastery

**Focus**: Completely understand game, immersed in mastery

**Design**: Most development aims at this group

**Challenge**: Keeping engagement through middle game

**Implementation:**
- Core progression systems
- Mastery challenges
- Social play with peers
- Skill development paths
- Competitive and cooperative content
- Clear goals and milestones

### Level 3: The Elder

**Their Game**: Something entirely different from main game

**Challenge**: Game no longer interesting—secrets discovered, pleasures squeezed dry

**Risk**: Players leave seeking new games

**Benefit**: Elders are vocal advertisers and expert advisors

**Elder Games:**

#### A. More Difficult Game
- Different, much harder game for high-level players
- So difficult no one consistently masters it
- **Toontown Online**: "Cog Headquarters" with new platform gameplay and battle system
- **Some games**: Rise from soldier to general
- **Others**: Shift from vs. computer to vs. players

**Problem**: What when elders get tired of this too?

#### B. Governance Privileges
- Elder players get rule-making responsibilities
- **MUDs**: Special powers to decide game rules
- **CCG Communities**: Formal tests to become official tournament judges
- Makes elders feel special and involved

**Risk**: Too much control can be dangerous

#### C. The Joy of Creation
- Players who love game fantasize about extending it
- **The Sims, Skyrim**: Strong communities from player-created content
- Elders play occasionally, spend most time creating
- **New game**: Status competition (most popular/respected designer)

#### D. Guild Management
- Groups benefit from organizers
- Elders will do this naturally
- **Better**: Give strong tools to run guilds
- Makes activity more appealing

#### E. A Chance to Teach
- Experts enjoy teaching (in games and real world)
- Give permission and encouragement
- **Entropia Universe**: Special status noting experts/teachers
- Source of pride for elder players

**Implementation:**
- Endgame content (raids, nightmare modes)
- Moderation and governance systems
- Content creation tools
- Guild management features
- Mentor programs with rewards/status
- Ranked competitive play
- Meta-game activities (theorycrafting, community building)

### Easter Egg Hunt Example (Simple Implementation)

**Level 1 (Ages 2-5, Newbies):**
- Separate area from older kids
- Eggs in plain sight (not hidden)
- Navigating space and picking up eggs is enough challenge
- Plenty of eggs, no pushy big kids

**Level 2 (Ages 6-9, Players):**
- Standard egg hunt over large area
- Eggs hidden in tricky places
- Enough eggs for everyone
- Need to move fast and look carefully

**Level 3 (Ages 10-13, Elders):**
- Task of hiding eggs for younger kids
- Very proud of this job
- Challenging and fun responsibility
- Status compared to younger kids
- Often enjoy giving hints to struggling kids

## Tip #7: Force Players to Depend on Each Other

**Problem**: Designers conditioned to make games playable solo, even in multiplayer ("don't exclude solo players").

**Valid concern**, but:
- Games that can be mastered solo diminish community value
- No dependence = no community necessity

**Solution**: Create situations where players must communicate and interact to succeed

**Often Involves Counterintuitive Step**: Taking something away from players

**Spaceteam Example:**
- Each player has information vital to mission
- Only others can act on that information
- Guarantees constant communication

**Toontown Online Example:**
- **Rule**: Players cannot heal themselves during battle
- Can only heal other players
- **Concern**: Players would find rule frustrating
- **Result**: Achieved objectives well
  - Forced communication ("I need a Toon-up!")
  - Encouraged helping each other
  - Created dependency

**Lens #80: Help**
- People want to help each other
- Helping is deeply satisfying (even in videogames)
- Often shy about helping (fear of insulting)
- Create situations where:
  - Players need each other's help
  - Can easily ask for it
  - Others quickly come to aid
  - Community strengthens

**Implementation:**
- Complementary abilities (can't do everything alone)
- Class-based systems (tank, healer, DPS)
- Information asymmetry (everyone sees different things)
- Resource sharing requirements
- Puzzles requiring multiple players
- Combat requiring coordination
- Buffs that can only be given to others
- Revival/rescue mechanics
- Trade and specialization systems

**Balance Solo Play:**
- Don't force multiplayer on everyone
- Create separate modes when appropriate
- But make multiplayer clearly more rewarding
- Show what players gain from cooperation

## Tip #8: Manage Your Community

**Problem**: Crossing fingers and hoping community happens doesn't work

**Modern Expectation**: Games updated based on player feedback continuously

**Solution**: Active community management

**Gardener Metaphor:**
- Don't create communities directly
- Plant seeds and encourage growth
- Observe and cater to specific needs
- Role of nurturing, listening, encouraging
- Balance "hands-on" and "hands-off"

**Tools and Systems:**
- Create appropriate communication tools
- Build organization systems
- Professional community managers (if needed)

**Community Manager Role:**
- Build and maintain designer-player feedback loop
- Listen to community needs
- Encourage positive behavior
- Address problems early
- Celebrate community achievements
- Connect designers with player insights

**Amy Jo Kim's Advice:**
- *Community Building on the Web* book
- Carefully manage online communities
- Strike right balance between hands-on/hands-off
- Don't over-moderate or under-moderate

**Implementation:**
- Regular communication (dev blogs, patch notes)
- Community forums and discussion spaces
- Official social media presence
- Response to player concerns
- Transparency about decisions
- Community events and initiatives
- Feedback collection systems
- Moderation tools and policies
- Community highlighting (player spotlights)

## Tip #9: Obligation to Others Is Powerful

**Cultural Context:**

Parts of aboriginal Australia: Rude to give unexpected gifts because it creates burden to give return gift.

**Universal Truth**: Obligation to others is deeply felt in all cultures.

**In Games:**

Create situations where players can:
- **Make promises**: "Let's meet at 10 p.m. Wednesday to fight trolls"
- **Owe favors**: "That healing spell saved my life! I owe you one!"

**Players Take These Seriously:**

**World of Warcraft Example:**
- Obligation to guild = strongest force for regular play
- Partly: want high status in guild
- Partly: avoid low status by not failing commitments

**Facebook Games:**
- Gifting responsible for massive viral spread
- Obligation to return gifts drove engagement

**Lens #25: Judgment**
- No one wants negative judgment from others
- Failing to live up to commitments = quick way to lose respect
- Strong motivator for regular play

**Implementation:**
- Scheduled group events (raids, team matches)
- Guild commitment systems
- Favor/debt tracking
- Gift exchange mechanics
- Team roles with responsibilities
- Appointment mechanics (tend farm, collect rewards)
- Public promises (sign up for raid slots)
- Reputation systems
- Trade agreements
- Alliance and treaty systems

**Design Carefully:**
- Don't make obligations feel like work
- Balance engagement with respect for player time
- Allow graceful ways to bow out
- Make fulfilling obligations rewarding
- Create positive social pressure, not negative

## Tip #10: Create Community Events

**Principle**: Almost all successful communities are anchored by regular events

**Real World Events:**
- Meetings
- Parties
- Competitions
- Practice sessions
- Awards ceremonies

**Virtual World**: Pretty much the same

**Event Purposes:**

1. **Give players something to look forward to**
   - Creates anticipation
   - Reason to log in

2. **Create shared experience**
   - Makes players feel connected
   - "Were you there when...?"

3. **Punctuate time**
   - Give players something to remember
   - Mark seasons and eras

4. **Guarantee opportunity to connect**
   - Know friends will be there
   - Scheduled socializing

5. **Create check-in habit**
   - Want to find out about upcoming events
   - Frequent checking becomes routine

**Player-Created vs. Designer-Created:**

**Players Often Create Their Own:**
- Guild events
- Community tournaments
- Roleplay gatherings
- Trading meetups

**Why Create Your Own:**
- Ensure quality and inclusiveness
- Reach broader audience
- Integrate with game systems
- Drive specific engagement goals
- Can be simple (goal + mass email)

**Types of Events:**

**Competitive:**
- Tournaments
- Races
- Contests
- Ranked seasons

**Cooperative:**
- Server-wide raids
- Community goals
- Charity drives
- Collaborative building

**Social:**
- Holiday celebrations
- Awards ceremonies
- Developer Q&As
- Player spotlights

**Special:**
- Limited-time content
- Seasonal events
- New content launches
- Anniversary celebrations

**Implementation:**
- Event calendar (upcoming schedule)
- Automated event triggers
- Special rewards for participation
- Event-specific content
- Announcements and hype building
- Post-event recaps and highlights
- Regular cadence (weekly, monthly, seasonal)
- Variety (don't repeat same event type)

**Online Game Ease:**
- Simple goals for players
- Mass email announcement
- Automatic tracking and rewards
- Lower overhead than real-world events

## Summary Checklist

Use this checklist when designing community features:

- [ ] **Foster Friendships**
  - [ ] Communication tools (chat, voice)
  - [ ] Ways to find compatible players
  - [ ] Content worth discussing
  - [ ] Support for breaking ice
  - [ ] Friend-making rituals
  - [ ] Systems to reconnect with friends

- [ ] **Put Conflict at Heart**
  - [ ] Common enemies or challenges
  - [ ] Competitive rankings
  - [ ] Cooperative difficult content
  - [ ] Guild vs. guild systems

- [ ] **Use Architecture**
  - [ ] Social hubs and gathering spaces
  - [ ] Routes that create repeated encounters
  - [ ] Friend-finding locations

- [ ] **Create Community Property**
  - [ ] Guild bases or shared spaces
  - [ ] Collective resources
  - [ ] Shared progression systems

- [ ] **Enable Expression**
  - [ ] Avatar customization
  - [ ] Cosmetic options
  - [ ] Creative tools
  - [ ] Identity badges and titles

- [ ] **Support Three Levels**
  - [ ] Rewarding newbie experience
  - [ ] Core player progression
  - [ ] Elder meta-games (governance, creation, teaching)

- [ ] **Force Dependence**
  - [ ] Class/role systems
  - [ ] Complementary abilities
  - [ ] Information asymmetry
  - [ ] Cooperative challenges

- [ ] **Manage Community**
  - [ ] Community manager role
  - [ ] Feedback loops
  - [ ] Moderation tools
  - [ ] Regular communication

- [ ] **Create Obligations**
  - [ ] Scheduled events
  - [ ] Guild commitments
  - [ ] Favor systems
  - [ ] Public promises

- [ ] **Hold Events**
  - [ ] Regular event calendar
  - [ ] Variety of event types
  - [ ] Shared experiences
  - [ ] Celebration and recognition

## Key Takeaway

**Community building is intentional design work**, not something that happens by accident. Each tip addresses a specific psychological or social need. Implement all ten for the strongest possible community.

**Remember**: Players come for the game but stay for the community. Strong community can extend a game's life from months to decades.
