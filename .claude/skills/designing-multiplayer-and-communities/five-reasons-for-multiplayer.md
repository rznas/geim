# Five Reasons We Play with Others

## Introduction

While many game pleasures (challenge, flow, rewards, transcendence) can be experienced alone, multiplayer games tap into specifically *social* motivations. Understanding these five core reasons helps you design meaningful multiplayer experiences rather than tacking on social features as an afterthought.

---

## 1. Competition

**What It Provides:**

Competition is usually the first thing that comes to mind with multiplayer games—for good reason. It simultaneously fulfills multiple needs:

- **Balanced game on a level playing field** (Lens #37: Fairness)
  - Human opponents create natural balance
  - No need for complex AI balancing
  - Both players have equal opportunities

- **Worthy opponent** (Lens #38: Challenge, Lens #43: Competition)
  - Humans provide unpredictable, intelligent opposition
  - Difficulty automatically scales to player skill
  - Victory feels meaningful against real people

- **Interesting problem to solve** (Lens #8: Problem Solving)
  - Human psychology creates complex problems
  - No two matches are identical
  - Constantly evolving strategies

- **Determine skill level relative to others** (Lens #25: Judgment, Lens #90: Status)
  - Fulfill deep inner need to know where you stand
  - Social circle context makes comparison meaningful
  - Status hierarchy emerges naturally

- **Complex strategy and psychology** (Lens #39: Meaningful Choice, Lens #34: Skill, Lens #79: Freedom)
  - Human intelligence enables sophisticated gameplay
  - Bluffing, reading opponents, mind games
  - Emergent complexity from player interaction

**Design Implications:**

- Ensure competitive modes are truly fair (symmetrical or balanced asymmetry)
- Provide clear status feedback (rankings, leaderboards, visible skill)
- Create opportunities for psychological gameplay (bluffing, deception, reading opponents)
- Allow skill expression that observers can recognize and appreciate
- Balance to prevent dominant strategies (rock-paper-scissors dynamics)

**Examples:**
- Fighting games (Street Fighter, Smash Bros)
- RTS games (StarCraft, Age of Empires)
- MOBAs (League of Legends, Dota 2)
- Sports games (FIFA, NBA 2K)
- Card games (Hearthstone, Magic: The Gathering)

## 2. Collaboration

**What It Provides:**

The opposite of competition, collaboration offers its own unique pleasures:

- **Impossible-with-one-person gameplay**
  - Team sports make no sense with one person (baseball, soccer)
  - Coordinated tactics requiring multiple roles
  - Complementary abilities working in concert

- **Deep pleasures of group problem solving**
  - Presumably evolved trait for survival
  - Satisfaction of being part of successful team
  - Shared victories feel different from solo achievements

**Collaboration + Competition:**

Most collaborative games follow team sports model, combining both pleasures:
- Collaborate with teammates
- Compete against opposing team
- Best of both motivations simultaneously

**Design Implications:**

- Create roles that are meaningfully different (tank, healer, DPS)
- Design problems that genuinely require coordination
- Enable player specialization (different builds, classes, roles)
- Provide clear communication tools for strategy
- Reward team play over individual heroics
- Make assists and support actions feel valuable

**Examples:**
- Team sports (basketball, soccer, baseball)
- Cooperative board games (Pandemic, Forbidden Island)
- MMO raids (World of Warcraft, Final Fantasy XIV)
- Co-op shooters (Left 4 Dead, Deep Rock Galactic)
- Team tactical games (Rainbow Six Siege, Overwatch)

---

## 3. Meeting Up

**What It Provides:**

Games give us convenient reasons to be together:

- **Socially convenient gathering catalyst**
  - Removes awkwardness of "just showing up"
  - Shared activity provides focus
  - Won't make anyone uncomfortable

- **Something to share and focus on**
  - Like food at a dinner party
  - Gives hands something to do
  - Provides conversation topics

- **Relationship maintenance**
  - Great way for kids and parents to spend time together
  - Weekly game nights hold friendships together
  - Regular ritual creates commitment

**Design Implications:**

- Support scheduled play times (appointments, game nights)
- Enable casual, low-stakes play that allows conversation
- Create games that don't require constant intense focus
- Allow players to drop in/out gracefully
- Design for living room spectation (pass-and-play, party games)
- Keep learning curve gentle for mixed-skill groups

**Examples:**
- Chess, golf, tennis, bridge, bingo
- Basketball, bowling leagues
- League of Legends, Call of Duty
- Words with Friends, Draw Something
- Board game nights (Catan, Ticket to Ride)
- Party games (Jackbox, Mario Party)

---

## 4. Exploring Our Friends

**What It Provides:**

Games reveal truth about people in ways conversation cannot:

- **See beyond filtered conversation**
  - Conversation is filtered through "what they think we want to hear"
  - Gameplay reveals unvarnished truth
  - Actions speak louder than words

- **Observe genuine behavior**
  - See them solving problems under pressure
  - Watch tough decisions being made
  - Witness when they show mercy or ruthlessness
  - Learn who to trust and who to watch

- **Deep character revelation**
  - "You can learn more about a man in an hour of play than a year of conversation" —Plato
  - See true priorities under stress
  - Understand values through choices
  - Recognize patterns in behavior

**Design Implications:**

- Create meaningful decisions that reveal character
- Include moral choices with consequences
- Enable betrayal/cooperation dilemmas (prisoner's dilemma situations)
- Make resource allocation decisions visible to others
- Allow players to help or hinder each other
- Design situations with genuine stakes

**Examples:**
- Social deduction games (Among Us, Mafia, Werewolf)
- Negotiation games (Diplomacy, Cosmic Encounter)
- Survival games with friendly fire (Rust, DayZ)
- Cooperative games with resource sharing decisions
- Games with optional betrayal mechanics

## 5. Exploring Ourselves

**What It Provides:**

Alone, games test ability limits. With others, we explore our social selves:

- **Discover behavior in social situations**
  - How do I behave under pressure?
  - Do I let friends win or crush them unconditionally?
  - Who do I prefer to team with and why?

- **Emotional self-awareness**
  - How do I feel when publicly defeated?
  - How do I cope with visible failure?
  - What's my reaction to victory vs. defeat?

- **Strategic self-knowledge**
  - How do my strategies differ from others?
  - Who do I choose to imitate?
  - What patterns emerge in my choices?

- **Social identity formation**
  - Close to the heart of how we see ourselves
  - Core of how we relate to other people
  - These are not trivial—they're fundamental questions

**Design Implications:**

- Create situations with social pressure and observation
- Make victories and defeats publicly visible
- Allow different strategic approaches to succeed
- Enable role-playing and identity exploration
- Provide feedback on playstyle and patterns
- Create opportunities for heroism and failure
- Allow players to define their own values through play

**Examples:**
- Competitive ranked modes (public ladder climbing)
- Social strategy games (Diplomacy, Resistance)
- Role-playing games (D&D, MMORPGs)
- Any game with public performance (streaming, tournaments)
- Games that track and display personal statistics

## The Spectator Motivation (Bonus)

**Watching is Also Social:**

It's not even necessary for everyone to play—there can be strong social connection just watching:

- **Living room spectation**
  - Watching is better than TV if the game is compelling
  - Laughing, commenting, helping solve problems
  - Kibbutzing creates shared experience

- **Streaming and esports**
  - Remote spectation through video streams
  - Parasocial relationships with streamers
  - Shared community of viewers

**Design for Spectators:**
- Make actions clear and readable
- Create dramatic moments worth watching
- Enable helpful commentary from observers
- Provide spectator-specific camera angles or information
- Design for "couch multiplayer" with interesting screen content
- Consider streaming appeal in competitive games

## Combining Multiple Motivations

**Most Successful Multiplayer Games Combine Several:**

**Team Sports** (e.g., Basketball, Soccer):
- ✓ Competition (vs. other team)
- ✓ Collaboration (with teammates)
- ✓ Meeting up (scheduled games, team gatherings)
- ✓ Exploring friends (see how they perform under pressure)
- ✓ Exploring self (discover your competitive nature)

**MMORPGs** (e.g., World of Warcraft):
- ✓ Collaboration (raids, dungeons, guilds)
- ✓ Meeting up (scheduled raid nights, guild events)
- ✓ Exploring friends (see who's reliable, who helps others)
- ✓ Exploring self (define your social role in guild)

**Social Deduction** (e.g., Among Us, Werewolf):
- ✓ Competition (find impostor or deceive crew)
- ✓ Exploring friends (who lies well, who's trustworthy)
- ✓ Exploring self (are you good at deception or detection?)
- ✓ Meeting up (party game, social gathering)

## Design Exercise

For your multiplayer game, identify:

1. **Primary motivation**: Which of the five is most central?
2. **Secondary motivations**: Which others does it support?
3. **Missing opportunities**: Are there motivations you could add?
4. **Conflicts**: Do any motivations work against each other?

**Example Analysis:**

**Game: Competitive FPS (Call of Duty)**
- Primary: Competition (core ranked play)
- Secondary: Exploring self (how you perform under pressure)
- Present: Meeting up (playing with friend groups)
- Weak: Collaboration (team modes exist but often feel like parallel play)
- Missing: Exploring friends (limited meaningful revelation of character)

**Improvement Ideas:**
- Strengthen collaboration through role specialization
- Create more cooperation-requiring objectives
- Add social deduction elements in new modes

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Each motivation requires different design support** - Competition needs fairness, collaboration needs interdependence, meeting up needs casual accessibility, exploration needs meaningful choices.

2. **Combine motivations for depth** - The richest multiplayer experiences fulfill multiple social needs simultaneously.

3. **Consider all five during design** - Don't default to competition only. Ask: "Which social needs does this feature fulfill?"

4. **Spectation matters** - Design for observers as well as players, both local and remote.

5. **Social beats mechanics** - Players will tolerate imperfect gameplay for strong social experiences, but perfect mechanics won't sustain engagement without social motivation.

**Remember**: The reason to add multiplayer isn't "because multiplayer is cool"—it's because you've identified specific social needs your game will fulfill better than solo play.
