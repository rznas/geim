# Christopher Alexander's Fifteen Properties of Living Structures

## Overview

Christopher Alexander identified fifteen fundamental properties shared by things that have "the nameless quality"—that special feeling of life, wholeness, comfort, and rightness. These properties originally applied to architecture but translate powerfully to game design.

Things with the nameless quality feel:
- **Alive** - as if they hold energy
- **Whole** - like nothing is missing
- **Comfortable** - pleasing to be around
- **Free** - not constrained unnaturally
- **Exact** - just how they're supposed to be
- **Egoless** - connected to the universe
- **Eternal** - as if they always have been and always will be
- **Free from inner contradictions**

## The Fifteen Properties

### 1. Levels of Scale

**Definition:** Multiple levels of size working together harmoniously, from large to small.

**In Architecture:** A building might have city-scale presence, building-scale form, room-scale spaces, furniture-scale elements, and detail-scale ornaments.

**In Game Design:**

- **Telescoping Goals:** Short-term goals lead to midterm goals leading to long-term goals
  - *Example:* Defeat this enemy → Clear this room → Complete this level → Finish this world → Beat the game

- **Fractal Interest Curves:** Large pacing structures contain smaller pacing structures
  - *Example:* A game session has tension/release, a level has tension/release, individual encounters have tension/release

- **Nested World Structures:** Overworld contains towns contains buildings contains rooms
  - *Example:* RPGs with outdoor space → town icons → separate interior spaces

- **Spore:** Symphony of levels of scale—cell → creature → tribe → civilization → space

**Application:** Ensure your game has goals and structures at multiple time scales, each meaningful and engaging.

### 2. Strong Centers

**Definition:** Clear focal points that organize everything around them.

**In Architecture:** A fireplace as the heart of a room, a plaza as the center of a town.

**In Game Design:**

- **Avatar Strength:** Strong, well-defined avatars > weak, undefined ones
  - Players prefer characters with clear identity and capabilities

- **Clear Purpose:** Well-defined goals that organize all gameplay
  - *Example:* "Save the princess" centers all of Super Mario Bros

- **Visual Layout:** Important UI elements and focal points clearly centered
  - HUD elements that draw attention to what matters

- **Story Structure:** Protagonist at center of game universe
  - World revolves around and responds to player

**Application:** Identify the "center" of each aspect of your game (gameplay, story, UI) and make it strong and clear.

### 3. Boundaries

**Definition:** Clear edges that define and protect what's inside.

**In Game Design:**

- **Territorial Games:** Many games are primarily about boundaries
  - *Go, Risk, Splatoon* - defining and defending territory

- **Rules as Boundaries:** Every game rule creates boundaries
  - What you can/can't do
  - A game with no rules is no game at all

- **Game Space Boundaries:** Walls, edges, invisible barriers
  - Define where gameplay can occur

- **Time Boundaries:** Turn limits, time limits, phases
  - Structure when actions can occur

- **Resource Boundaries:** Limited inventory, energy, ammunition
  - What you can carry or use

**Application:** Examine all boundaries in your game. Are they clear? Do they create interesting constraints? Do players understand them?

### 4. Alternating Repetition

**Definition:** Patterns that alternate between two or more states in a pleasing rhythm.

**In Game Design:**

- **Chessboard Pattern:** Visual appeal of alternating colors
  - Black/white squares create harmony

- **Level/Boss/Level/Boss:** Classic game structure
  - Exploration/Challenge/Exploration/Challenge rhythm

- **Tension/Release Cycles:** Emotional pacing
  - Stress/Relief/Stress/Relief creates satisfaction

- **Day/Night Cycles:** Environmental variation
  - Different gameplay modes or aesthetics

- **Combat/Exploration:** Action game pacing
  - Intense moments alternating with calm moments

**Application:** Look for rhythmic patterns in your game. Create pleasing alternation between contrasting states.

### 5. Positive Space

**Definition:** Foreground and background both have beautiful, complementary shapes (like yin and yang).

**In Architecture:** The space between buildings is as carefully designed as the buildings themselves.

**In Game Design:**

- **Balanced Strategies:** Multiple viable approaches that complement each other
  - Rock/Paper/Scissors has this quality
  - Each strategy has beautiful shape relative to others

- **Symmetrical Game Modes:** Attack and defense are both meaningful
  - Tower defense: Waves have shape, tower placement has shape

- **Negative Space in UI:** Empty space is as important as filled space
  - Minimalist UIs where emptiness creates focus

**Application:** Don't just design the "active" elements. Design the spaces between them. Ensure all strategies and options have elegance.

### 6. Good Shape

**Definition:** Pleasing, coherent forms that feel "right."

**In Game Design:**

- **Visual Elements:** Sprites, models, UI elements with strong silhouettes
  - Recognizable shapes that are aesthetically pleasing

- **Level Design:** Well-shaped levels that feel solid
  - "This level has a good curve" - organic flow

- **Rule Sets:** Elegant rules that fit together
  - Simple, memorable, internally consistent

- **Progression Curves:** Smooth, well-shaped difficulty curves
  - Not jagged or arbitrary

**Application:** When something feels "off" in your game, examine its shape. Could it be more coherent, more elegant?

### 7. Local Symmetries

**Definition:** Multiple small internal symmetries rather than one overall symmetry.

**In Architecture:** A cathedral might have symmetrical chapels, symmetrical arches, symmetrical windows—but the overall layout is organic.

**In Game Design:**

- **Zelda Architecture:** Each room/area feels symmetrical internally
  - But connections between rooms are organic
  - *The Wind Waker* exemplifies this throughout

- **Rule Systems:** Individual subsystems are balanced
  - Combat system balanced, magic system balanced, economy balanced
  - But they connect in asymmetric, organic ways

- **Character Design:** Body parts have internal symmetry
  - Two arms, two legs, symmetrical face
  - But poses and actions are asymmetrical

**Application:** Create symmetry at local levels while keeping overall structure organic and surprising.

### 8. Deep Interlock and Ambiguity

**Definition:** Elements so tightly intertwined they define each other; removing one makes the other incomplete.

**In Game Design:**

- **Go:** Pieces are only meaningful relative to opponent's pieces
  - Position has no intrinsic value, only relational value

- **Two-Player Games:** Your strategy defined by opponent's strategy
  - Rock/Paper/Scissors - each choice exists only in relation to others

- **Interconnected Systems:** Mechanics that deeply depend on each other
  - In *Dark Souls*, combat + stamina + weight are deeply interlocked

- **Story and Gameplay:** When narrative and mechanics cannot be separated
  - *Undertale* - mercy mechanic is inseparable from the story

**Application:** Look for opportunities to make systems define each other rather than existing independently.

### 9. Contrast

**Definition:** Strong differences that make each element more meaningful.

**In Game Design:**

- **Opponent Contrast:** You vs. enemy with clearly different capabilities
  - Small fast player vs. large slow boss

- **Controllable vs. Uncontrollable:** What you can affect vs. what you can't
  - Your units vs. random events

- **Reward vs. Punishment:** Victory vs. defeat strongly contrasted
  - Meaningful consequences create engagement

- **Light vs. Dark:** Visual and thematic contrast
  - *Ico* - bright outdoors vs. shadowy castle

- **Loud vs. Quiet:** Audio contrast in intensity
  - Explosions vs. silence

**Application:** Strengthen contrasts in your game. Weak opposites make for weak experiences.

### 10. Gradients

**Definition:** Qualities that change gradually and smoothly.

**In Game Design:**

- **Difficulty Curves:** Challenge that increases gradually
  - Players learn and improve at smooth rate

- **Probability Curves:** Smooth distributions of random outcomes
  - Gaussian distributions feel more natural than uniform

- **Distance Falloff:** Effects that diminish with distance
  - Sound, light, explosion damage

- **Learning Curves:** Skill acquisition that ramps smoothly
  - Not sudden skill walls

**Application:** Wherever you have progression or change, consider whether a gradient would feel better than discrete steps.

### 11. Roughness

**Definition:** Imperfection that gives character and prevents sterile perfection.

**In Game Design:**

- **House Rules:** Player-created variations
  - Make games feel more alive and personal

- **Procedural Variation:** Small random differences
  - Hand-placed trees look better than perfect grids

- **Handcrafted Feel:** Deliberately imperfect elements
  - *Minecraft* blocks feel handmade, not machine-perfect

- **Player Expression:** Systems that allow personalization
  - Builds, strategies, play styles that are never exactly the same

**Application:** Perfect is often sterile. Add controlled roughness to make your game feel more alive and human.

### 12. Echoes

**Definition:** Pleasing, unifying repetition across different scales.

**In Game Design:**

- **Visual Echoes:** Boss monsters share elements with their minions
  - Color palette, shapes, patterns repeated

- **Mechanical Echoes:** Similar mechanics at different scales
  - Jump in gameplay echoed in narrative (character's metaphorical jump)

- **Fractal Interest Curves:** Same tension/release pattern at multiple scales
  - Moment-to-moment, level-to-level, game-to-game

- **Thematic Echoes:** Theme expressed in multiple ways
  - *Portal* - "thinking with portals" in puzzles, story, humor

**Application:** Look for your game's core pattern. Echo it throughout at different scales.

### 13. The Void

**Definition:** A profound emptiness at the heart, surrounded by richness.

**In Architecture:** The hollow interior of a cathedral, an empty courtyard in a busy market.

**In Game Design:**

- **Boss Arenas:** Large empty spaces for important battles
  - *Shadow of the Colossus* - vast emptiness surrounding epic encounters

- **Quiet Moments:** Emotional space after intense action
  - *The Last of Us* - walking and talking after combat

- **Central Mysteries:** Empty space in understanding that pulls players in
  - What happened to this world? (environmental storytelling)

- **Minimalist Design:** Empty space that lets essential elements breathe
  - *Journey* - vast deserts emphasize loneliness and discovery

**Application:** Don't fill every space with content. Create meaningful voids that give weight to what surrounds them.

### 14. Simplicity and Inner Calm

**Definition:** Essential simplicity with perfect balance.

**In Game Design:**

- **Simple Rules, Emergent Complexity:** Small rule sets that create depth
  - *Go* - simple rules, infinite depth
  - *Chess* - six piece types, endless strategy

- **Balanced Systems:** Well-tuned mechanics that feel settled
  - No runaway dominance, no useless options
  - Everything has its place

- **Elegant Solutions:** Designs that solve problems with minimal complexity
  - One mechanic serving multiple purposes

- **Clear Core Loop:** Simple, repeatable, satisfying actions
  - *Tetris* - perfect inner calm in its core mechanic

**Application:** Simplify ruthlessly. Seek the fewest rules that create the richest experience.

### 15. Not-Separateness

**Definition:** Elements deeply connected to their surroundings, feeling like natural parts of the whole.

**In Architecture:** A building that feels like it grew from the landscape rather than being imposed upon it.

**In Game Design:**

- **Unified Theme:** Every element reflects the theme
  - *Lens #11: Unification*
  - Nothing feels tacked on or out of place

- **Integrated Mechanics:** Each rule fits naturally with others
  - Mechanics feel inevitable, not arbitrary

- **Environmental Storytelling:** Story woven into environment
  - Not separate cutscenes but integrated narrative

- **Contextual Tutorials:** Learning integrated into play
  - Not separate from game but part of natural flow

- **Holistic Design:** Everything serves the whole
  - Music, art, mechanics, story all support same experience

**Application:** For each element, ask: "Does this belong?" If anything feels separate or arbitrary, redesign it to connect naturally with the whole.

## Using These Properties

### Diagnostic Approach

When something feels "wrong" in your design:

1. Review the fifteen properties
2. Identify which are missing or weak
3. Redesign to strengthen those properties

### Generative Approach

When creating something new:

1. Choose 3-5 properties most relevant to your design challenge
2. Use them as constraints and guides
3. Iterate until those properties are strong

### Quality Assessment

When evaluating your design:

- Count how many properties are strongly present
- More properties = higher quality, more "alive"
- Even 5-6 properties can create something special
- All fifteen creates something timeless

## Remember

These properties describe what makes living things feel alive. As living beings, we're drawn to designs with these qualities. They're not rules to follow mechanically but patterns to understand and embody in your work.

**Aspire to the nameless quality in all your designs.**
