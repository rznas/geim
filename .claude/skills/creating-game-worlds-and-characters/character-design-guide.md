# Character Design Guide: Ten Character Tips

## Overview

This guide provides ten practical techniques for creating compelling game characters based on methods from scriptwriting, storytelling, and improvisational theater.

**Remember**: Game characters differ from novel and film characters. They are primarily involved in physical conflicts, exist in fantasy worlds, and tend to be simpler. But this doesn't mean they must be shallow—these tips help add depth while supporting gameplay.

---

## Character Tip #1: List Character Functions

### The Technique

In creating a story, one frequently invents characters as the storyline demands them. But what about when the **game** demands them? A very useful technique is:

1. List all the **functions** that characters need to fulfill
2. List the **characters** you had been thinking of putting in the game
3. See how they match up
4. Get creative with the mapping

### Example: Action Platform Game

**Character Functions**:
1. Hero: the character who plays the game
2. Mentor: gives advice and useful items
3. Assistant: gives occasional tips
4. Tutor: explains how to play the game
5. Final boss: someone to have the last battle against
6. Minions: bad guys
7. Three bosses: tough guys to battle against
8. Hostage: someone to rescue

**Characters from Your Imagination**:
1. Princess Mouse: beautiful, but tough and no nonsense
2. Wise Old Owl: full of wisdom, but forgetful
3. Silver Hawk: angry and vengeful
4. Sammy Snake: amoral and full of wry humor
5. Rat Army: hundreds of rats with evil red eyes

### Creative Mapping Exercise

**Traditional Mapping**:
- Princess Mouse = Hostage (obvious, boring)
- Rat Army = Minions (obvious, boring)

**Creative Alternatives**:

**Princess Mouse as...**
- **Mentor**: Tough, no-nonsense mentor who teaches through action
- **Hero**: Play as the princess saving someone else
- **Final Boss**: Princess has been corrupted and must be defeated

**Wise Old Owl as...**
- **Mentor**: Traditional wise guide (obvious)
- **Final Boss**: Twist! Your mentor was the villain all along
- **Tutor + Mentor**: Combine roles to save character development cost

**Rat Army as...**
- **Minions**: Evil enemies (traditional)
- **Hostages**: They've been hypnotized by evil Princess Mouse and need rescue
- **Helpers**: They assist you after you break the spell

**Silver Hawk as...**
- **Hostage**: Sends telepathic messages from captivity, mentoring you from afar
- **Assistant**: Shows up periodically with tips
- **Boss**: One of the three tough battles

**Sammy Snake as...**
- **Assistant + Tutor**: Gives tips with wry humor throughout
- **Unreliable Helper**: Helps sometimes, betrays you other times based on self-interest

### Benefits of This Technique

1. **Ensures All Functions Are Covered**: You won't forget needed roles
2. **Enables Creative Combinations**: Unexpected mappings create interesting dynamics
3. **Saves Development Cost**: One character filling multiple roles
4. **Creates Surprising Stories**: Non-traditional assignments lead to memorable moments
5. **Clarifies Character Purpose**: Every character has a job to do

### When to Use

- Early in character development
- When your character list feels too long or too short
- When characters feel generic or interchangeable
- When you're not sure what a character's purpose is

## Character Tip #2: Define and Use Character Traits

### The Problem with Flat Dialog

**Bad Example** (expositional, reveals nothing about characters):

> LESTER: Sabu!
>
> SABU: What is it?
>
> LESTER: Someone has stolen the king's crown!
>
> SABU: Do you realize what this means?
>
> LESTER: No.
>
> SABU: It means the Dark Arrow has returned. We must stop him!

This tells us about the situation (missing crown) and villain (Dark Arrow), but nothing about Sabu or Lester as people.

### The Solution: Define Core Traits

Create a small, distilled list of traits that encapsulate each character. Choose traits that:
- Will stay with the character through many situations
- Really define them as a person
- Can be a little contradictory (real people have contradictions)

**Example Traits**:
- *Sabu: trustworthy, short tempered, valiant, a fiery lover*
- *Lester: arrogant, sarcastic, spiritual, impulsive*

### Rewriting with Traits

**Better Example** (same information, but characters come alive):

> LESTER (exploding into the room): By the Gods! Sabu, I have news! *(impulsive and spiritual)*
>
> SABU: (covering herself): How dare you intrude on my privacy! *(short tempered)*
>
> LESTER: Whatever. Maybe you don't care that the king's crown has been stolen? *(arrogant and sarcastic)*
>
> SABU (a faraway look in her eye): This means I must do what I promised… *(trustworthy and valiant)*
>
> LESTER: I pray to Vishnu this is not another story of an old flame… *(spiritual and sarcastic; hints at Sabu's fiery lover trait)*
>
> SABU: Silence! The Dark Arrow broke my heart, and the heart of my sister—I promised her that if he ever returned, I would risk my life to destroy him. Prepare the chariot! *(short tempered, fiery lover, trustworthy, valiant)*

### Applying Traits Beyond Dialog

Traits should show in **everything**:

**Actions**:
- If your character is sneaky, does it show in their jump animation?
- If your character is depressed, does it show when they run?
- Maybe a depressed character shouldn't run, but only walk

**Appearance**:
- Clothing choices
- Posture
- Facial expressions
- Color scheme

**Animation**:
- Movement speed
- Gesture style
- Combat animations
- Idle animations

### Elegance: Multiple Traits at Once

Try to express more than one trait at a time (remember Lens #49: Elegance).

**Example**: "By the Gods! Sabu, I have news!"
- Spiritual (invokes gods)
- Impulsive (bursts in with excitement)

### Creating a Character Bible vs Distilled Traits

**Character Bible Approach**:
- List everything about the character
- Loves, hates, dress style, food preferences, where they grew up
- Can be a useful exercise
- But ultimately you want to boil it down

**Distilled Traits Approach**:
- 3-5 core traits
- Encapsulate the essence
- Easy to remember and apply
- Keep consistent across all character moments

### When to Use

- When writing any dialog
- When designing character animations
- When creating character appearance
- When planning character actions in cutscenes
- When making any creative decision about a character

## Character Tip #4: Make a Character Web

*See [character-relationships-guide.md](character-relationships-guide.md) for complete details*

### Quick Summary

For each character, write down what that character thinks of all the other characters. Explore not just how they feel, but **why**.

**Benefits**:
- Raises questions about character interactions you might not have thought about
- Gives characters more depth
- Reveals asymmetries (A likes B, but B doesn't like A)
- Helps write more authentic interactions

**See the full guide for a complete worked example using Archie Comics characters.**

## Character Tip #5: Use Status

*See [character-relationships-guide.md](character-relationships-guide.md) for complete details*

### Quick Summary

Status is a hidden negotiation that constantly takes place when people interact: who is in charge of the current interaction?

**Key Points**:
- Status is something you **DO**, not who you **ARE**
- We negotiate status almost entirely subconsciously
- High status: relaxed, strong eye contact, head doesn't move while speaking
- Low status: fidgeting, avoiding eye contact, touching own face, tense
- Status is **relative** (Darth Vader is high status with Leia, low status with the Emperor)

**See the full guide for detailed status behaviors and game examples.**

## Character Tip #6: Use the Power of the Voice

### Why Voice Matters

The human voice is incredibly powerful and can affect us at a deep subconscious level. This is why talking pictures elevated cinema from a novelty to the dominant art form of the twentieth century.

### The Problem in Games

Voice acting in games often seems primitive compared to films. Two reasons:

**1. Inexperience**: Game developers often lack experience directing voice actors. It's a delicate art that takes years of practice.

**2. Backwards Process**: We do it backwards compared to animated films.

#### Film Process (Effective)

1. Script is written
2. Voice actors record it
3. Lines are changed, improvisations made, good ones incorporated
4. Characters get designed (often incorporating facial features of actors)
5. Animation commences

**Result**: Voice actor is central to creative process; character design incorporates their performance.

#### Game Process (Less Effective)

1. Characters designed and modeled
2. Script written
3. Basic animations created
4. Voice acting added last

**Result**: Voice actor is peripheral; just trying to imitate what they see instead of expressing how they truly feel their character would act.

### Why We Do It Backwards

Game development is so volatile that it's expensive to create characters around voices, since the script continues to change throughout the entire process.

### Better Approach

**Example: Naughty Dog** (Uncharted, The Last of Us)
- Dozens of recording sessions that spanned the length of the projects
- Iterative work with voice actors throughout development
- Made a real difference in emotional power

### Design Recommendations

**Early Involvement**:
- Bring voice actors in earlier
- Let them influence character design
- Allow improvisation and iteration

**Script Flexibility**:
- Plan for script changes based on performance
- Capture the magic of actor interpretation
- Design characters that can accommodate voice performance

**Director Training**:
- Invest in learning to direct voice actors
- Study film voice direction
- Practice the craft

### When Voice Acting Isn't Possible

**Alternatives**:
- Strong visual characterization
- Text that conveys personality
- Sound effects and musical cues
- Physical performance and animation

**Some games succeed without voice**: Early Zelda, many indie games. But when you use voice, use it well.

## Character Tip #7: Use the Power of the Face

### Why Faces Matter

A huge percentage of our brains is taken up with custom hardware for processing facial expression. We have the most complex and expressive faces in the entire animal kingdom.

**Unique Human Features**:
- **Eye whites**: Other animals don't have visible eye whites—we evolved them as a method of communication
- **Blushing**: Only animal that blushes
- **Crying**: Only animal that cries

### The Problem

Few videogames give facial animation the attention it deserves. Game designers focus on actions and give little thought to emotions.

### When Games Get It Right

Games with meaningful facial animation garner a lot of attention:
- *Legend of Zelda: The Wind Waker*: Expressive cartoon faces
- *The Last of Us*: Realistic emotional performances

### The OnLive Traveler Lesson

**Experiment**: Early 3D chat room with strict polygon budgets
- Tested prototypes iteratively
- Each time asked users: "Do the characters need more details?"
- Every time: "Yes—in the face"
- After 5-6 rounds: Bodies dwindled to nothing, leaving eerie floating heads
- **Users preferred this**: Activity was about self-expression, and faces are the most expressive tool

**Lesson**: For self-expression and social interaction, face is paramount.

### Facial Animation Doesn't Have to Be Expensive

Tremendous power from:
- Simple animated eyebrows
- Eye shapes
- Mouth positions

**Requirement**: Character face must be visible to the player.

### The Avatar Face Problem

**Challenge**: Avatar faces generally are not visible to the player (first-person or behind-the-camera third-person).

**Doom's Solution**:
- Put small picture of avatar's face on bottom of screen
- Facial expression corresponded to health meter
- Players got sense of injury without taking eyes off enemies
- **Why it works**: We can notice facial expression in peripheral vision more easily than we can discern numbers

### Focus on the Eyes

**"The eyes are the windows to the soul"** - but so often, videogame characters have dull, dead eyes.

**If you can bring the eyes to life, the whole character will come alive.**

**Applications**:
- **Evil villain**: Focus on the eyes
- **Creepy zombie**: Give him creepy eyes
- **Cute penguin**: Focus on cute eyes

**Family Identification**: We use eyes to tell who is related
- Example: *The Simpsons*—each family has one set of eyes
- Eyes hold many secrets

**Priority**: Give them proper attention, and your characters will thank you by coming to life.

### *The Simpsons* Eye Example

FIGURE 20.6 shows how each family has distinctive eyes:
- Simpson family: Round eyes with specific style
- Other families: Different eye styles that mark family resemblance

**Design Principle**: Consistent eye design within character families creates visual cohesion and identity.

## Character Tip #8: Powerful Stories Transform Characters

### The Core Principle

> "People do not change, they are merely revealed." —Anne Enright

One distinguishing feature of great stories is how their characters change. Video game designers seldom consider this, to their detriment.

### The Problem

Tendency to treat game characters as fixed types:
- The villain is always the villain
- The hero was born a hero
- Nobody grows or changes

**Result**: Very boring storytelling.

### Games That Got It Right

**Examples**:
- *Fable*: Player character can become good or evil
- *Star Wars: Knights of the Old Republic*: Major character transformations based on choices

**What They Did**: What nearly every successful movie or book does—let events change the main character over time.

### Not Every Game Can Do This

Meaningful character change is not possible for the main character in every game.

**Alternatives**:
- Character change for sidekick
- Character change for villain
- Character change for supporting cast
- Environmental storytelling showing past changes

### The Character Transformation Chart

**Tool**: Visualize potential for character change

**Structure**:
- Characters on the left side
- Different sections of your story along the top
- Mark where each character undergoes change

#### Example: Cinderella

| Character | Opening | Ball Invitation | Preparation | Ball | Midnight | Slipper Search | Resolution |
|-----------|---------|----------------|-------------|------|----------|----------------|------------|
| **Cinderella** | Sad, oppressed | Hopeful | Excited, transformed | Confident, happy | Desperate | Waiting | Joyful, free |
| **Stepmother** | Cruel, controlling | Spiteful | Sabotaging | Absent | Angry | Desperate | Defeated |
| **Fairy Godmother** | Absent | Appears | Magical, kind | Absent | Absent | Absent | Satisfied |
| **Prince** | Bored | Dutiful | Absent | Smitten | Desperate | Determined | In love |
| **Stepsisters** | Mean, vain | Excited | Primping | Rejected | Smug | Confused | Humiliated |

### Types of Transformation

**Temporary and Small**:
- Cinderella excited for ball (returns to sad after)
- Prince bored → smitten (but still prince)

**Great and Permanent**:
- Cinderella sad, oppressed → joyful, free
- Stepmother controlling → defeated

**Surprising**:
- Fairy Godmother appears out of nowhere
- Prince goes from bored to desperate in love

### Design Benefits

By looking at each character over time instead of just the story thread:
- Get unique perspective on character arcs
- Better understand characters
- Identify missing transformation opportunities
- Ensure transformations are meaningful

### Making the Most of Transformation

**Communicate Clearly**:
- Visual changes (appearance, posture, color)
- Behavioral changes (how they move, fight, speak)
- Relationship changes (how they treat others)
- Environmental reactions (how others treat them)

**Make It Believable**:
- Transformations need proper setup
- Events must justify the change
- Change should feel earned, not arbitrary

## Character Tip #9: Let Your Characters Surprise Us

### The Fitzgerald Principle

From *Tender is the Night* by F. Scott Fitzgerald, a conversation between Rosemary (actress) and Dick:

> **Dick**: "In the theatre, all the best comediennes have built up their reputations by burlesquing the correct emotional responses—fear and love and sympathy."
>
> **Dick**: "The danger to an actress is in responding. Let's suppose somebody told you, 'Your lover is dead.' In life you'd probably go to pieces. But on the stage you're trying to entertain—the audience can do the 'responding' for themselves."
>
> **Dick**: "First the actress has lines to follow, then she has to get the audience's attention back on herself. So she must do something unexpected. If the audience thinks the character is hard she goes soft on them—if they think she's soft she goes hard. You go all OUT of character."
>
> **Dick**: "You do the unexpected thing until you've manoeuvred the audience back from the objective fact to yourself. Then you slide into character again."

### Why This Seems Wrong

This advice seems completely contrary to everything we know about storytelling and acting. We expect characters to behave realistically and believably.

### But It's Totally True

When you know to look for it, you can see it everywhere:
- Comedies
- Dramas
- The classics
- Everywhere

**Example**: *Breaking Bad* was practically founded on this principle.

### The Mechanism

**When characters have an emotional reaction that isn't what we expect, we pay attention.**

**Connection to Fun**: Fun is just pleasure with surprises, right?

**Application**: If you want players to enjoy playing with your characters, design each one the same way you would design a toy. Look for ways to let your characters surprise your players.

**Result**: Players will hang on your characters' every word and action.

### Practical Applications

**Setup Expectations, Then Subvert**:
- Show character is tough → have them show vulnerability
- Show character is cowardly → have them be brave in a crisis
- Show character is selfish → have them make a sacrifice

**Timing**:
- Let players get to know the "normal" character
- Then surprise them at key moments
- Return to normal, but players remember the surprise

**Not Random**:
- Surprises should reveal deeper truth about character
- Should be believable in hindsight
- Should add dimension, not contradict core traits

## Character Tip #10: Avoid the Uncanny Valley

### The Concept

Japanese roboticist Masahiro Mori noted something interesting about human response to robots and artificial characters.

**Expected Pattern**:
- The closer something is to seeming human, the more we empathize with it
- Linear increase in empathy as human-likeness increases

**Actual Pattern**:
- Empathy increases as expected
- But then there's a **sudden dip** when things get "too human"
- A valley of revulsion
- Then empathy increases again if you reach perfect human replication

### The Graph

**Horizontal Axis**: Human Likeness (left = not human, right = perfectly human)
**Vertical Axis**: Empathy/Comfort

**The Valley**: When artificial humans are almost but not quite right, we find them repulsive.

**Possible Cause**: Our brains register them as "diseased people" who might be dangerous to be around.

**Canonical Example**: Zombies are creepy things at the bottom of the uncanny valley.

### The Valley in Games and Animation

**Films That Fell In**:
- *Final Fantasy* (2001): Every frame looks gorgeous and natural, but in motion, something's not quite right
- *The Polar Express*: Computer-generated humans many people find creepy

**Why They Failed**: Characters got too close to the valley and fell in. They don't move quite right.

**Films That Avoided It**:
- Pixar films: Cartoony characters (fish, toys, cars, robots)
- No problem generating empathy
- Stay to the left side of the valley, where the puppy dog is

### Application to Games

Videogame characters can easily have the same problems—especially in games that try to mimic reality.

### Design Strategies

**Strategy 1: Stay Left (Stylized)**
- Cartoon-like characters
- Exaggerated features
- Obviously not human
- **Safe and effective**

**Strategy 2: Go Right (Photorealistic)**
- Aim for perfect human replication
- Very expensive and difficult
- Need perfect animation, skin shaders, expressions
- **Risky but potentially powerful**

**Strategy 3: Avoid the Middle**
- Don't be "almost human"
- The valley is dangerous
- Pick a side

### Current State of Technology

**The Day May Come**: Where videogame characters are so human looking that they can safely exist on the right side of the valley.

**Until Then**: Use caution—it's a long way down.

### Practical Checklist

**If going stylized**:
- Embrace cartoon aesthetics
- Exaggerate features
- Don't worry about realism
- Focus on expressiveness

**If going realistic**:
- Ensure animation quality matches visual fidelity
- Pay attention to eyes and facial expression
- Test with players for "creepy" reactions
- Be prepared for high cost

**Warning Signs You're in the Valley**:
- Playtesters say characters are "creepy" or "unsettling"
- Eyes look dead despite realistic model
- Movements feel wrong or uncanny
- Players actively avoid looking at characters

## Summary: Ten Tips at a Glance

1. **List Character Functions**: Separate functions from characters, then map creatively
2. **Define and Use Character Traits**: 3-5 core traits expressed in everything
3. **Use the Interpersonal Circumplex**: Graph relationships on two axes
4. **Make a Character Web**: Write how each character feels about every other
5. **Use Status**: Characters negotiate dominance/submission in every interaction
6. **Use the Power of the Voice**: Involve voice actors early in the process
7. **Use the Power of the Face**: Focus on eyes and facial animation
8. **Powerful Stories Transform Characters**: Show how events change characters
9. **Let Your Characters Surprise Us**: Do the unexpected to keep engagement
10. **Avoid the Uncanny Valley**: Stay stylized or go fully realistic—avoid the middle
