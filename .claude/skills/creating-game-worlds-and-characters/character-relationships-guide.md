# Character Relationships Guide

## Overview

Three powerful tools for designing and visualizing character relationships:

1. **Interpersonal Circumplex**: Graph-based visualization of relationship dynamics
2. **Character Web**: Written exploration of how characters feel about each other
3. **Status**: Understanding the hidden negotiation in every interaction

**Why These Matter**: Characters won't be alone—they're going to interact with each other. These tools help you design those interactions to be rich, interesting, and authentic.

---

## Tool 1: The Interpersonal Circumplex

### Origin

Brought from social psychology into game design by Katherine Isbister (author of *Better Videogame Characters by Design*).

### The Basic Graph

**Two Axes**:
- **Horizontal**: Hostile (left) ↔ Friendly (right)
- **Vertical**: Submissive (bottom) ↔ Dominant (top)

**Result**: Four quadrants representing different relationship types

### The Complete Diagram

The full interpersonal circumplex shows where many traits lie on this graph:

**Upper Right (Dominant + Friendly)**:
- Leading
- Directive
- Sociable
- Jovial

**Upper Left (Dominant + Hostile)**:
- Controlling
- Aggressive
- Quarrelsome
- Mistrusting

**Lower Left (Submissive + Hostile)**:
- Withdrawn
- Sullen
- Detached
- Inhibited

**Lower Right (Submissive + Friendly)**:
- Trusting
- Warm
- Agreeable
- Unassuming

### How to Use It

**Step 1: Choose a Central Character**
- Friendliness and dominance are **relative characteristics**
- Always make them relative to a particular character
- Place this character in the center of the graph

**Step 2: Plot Other Characters**
- Position each character based on how they relate to the central character
- Distance from center can indicate strength of relationship
- Position indicates type of relationship

**Step 3: Analyze Patterns**
- Look for gaps
- Identify extremes
- Note clustering

### Example: Star Wars Characters Relative to Han Solo

**Central Character**: Han Solo (at origin point)

**Plot Positions**:

**Upper Right (Friendly + Dominant)**:
- **Leia**: Slightly friendly, slightly dominant
  - She's friendly but also bossy toward Han
  - Close to center because they're equals

**Upper Left (Hostile + Dominant)**:
- **Darth Vader**: Extreme hostile, extreme dominant
  - Very far from center
  - Enemy with absolute power over Han

**Lower Right (Friendly + Submissive)**:
- **Chewbacca**: Extreme friendly, moderate submissive
  - Best friend but defers to Han
  - Very far right (maximum friendliness)
- **Luke**: Moderate friendly, moderate submissive
  - Friend who looks up to Han as mentor
- **C-3PO**: Moderate friendly, extreme submissive
  - Friendly but very subservient
  - Far down on submissive axis

**Upper Middle (Neutral Friendly, Dominant)**:
- **Obi-Wan**: Moderate friendly, dominant
  - Mentor figure, friendly but in charge

### What the Graph Reveals

**Extreme Characters Make It Interesting**:
- Darth Vader (extreme hostile + dominant)
- Chewbacca (extreme friendly + submissive)
- C-3PO (extreme submissive)
- These extremes are part of what makes them memorable

**Communication Patterns**:
- People he communicates with most are **closest to him on the graph**
- Leia, Luke, Chewbacca all near center
- Darth Vader far away (antagonist, not regular interaction)

**Gap Analysis**:
- **Lower Left Quadrant**: Empty (no hostile + submissive characters)
- What does this tell us about Han? He doesn't deal with cowardly enemies or withdrawn allies
- This reflects his character: straightforward, confrontational

**Contrast with Other Characters**:
- Graph for **Luke** would look very different
  - Obi-Wan might be center or upper right
  - Han would be upper left (friendly but challenging Luke)
- Graph for **Darth Vader** would be completely different
  - Almost everyone would be submissive
  - Han would be lower left (hostile + submissive from Vader's perspective)

### Design Questions the Circumplex Prompts

1. **Are there gaps in the chart?**
   - Why are they there?
   - Would it be better if gaps were filled?
   - Does the gap tell us something about the central character?

2. **Are there extreme characters?**
   - Extremes create memorable dynamics
   - If not, would it be better if there were?
   - Extremes provide contrast

3. **Are the character's friends in the same quadrant or different?**
   - Same quadrant: Similar relationship dynamics
   - Different quadrants: Varied relationship types
   - What if that were different?

4. **Where are the most interactions?**
   - Characters closest to center = most communication
   - Far characters = infrequent or tense interactions

5. **How does the graph change over time?**
   - Characters can move on the graph as relationships evolve
   - Tracking movement shows relationship arcs

### Limitations

The circumplex is not a be-all or end-all tool. It focuses on only two dimensions of relationships. But it's useful for:
- Quick visualization
- Identifying patterns
- Prompting design questions
- Ensuring variety in relationships

## Tool 2: The Character Web

### What It Is

For each character, write down what that character thinks of all the other characters. Explore not just **how** they feel, but **why**.

### Why It's Powerful

- Takes time, but well worth the effort
- Raises questions about character interactions you might not have thought about
- Reveals asymmetries in relationships
- Gives characters psychological depth
- Helps write authentic dialog

### Example: Archie Comics Universe

#### Archie's Perspective

**About Veronica**:
- Archie is lured by her elegance and beauty
- Though she is rich, Archie doesn't care much about that
- *Reveals*: Archie values personality over wealth

**About Betty**:
- Archie's true love
- But her insecurity constantly gives him mixed signals
- So he doesn't pursue her as forcefully as he might
- *Reveals*: Archie can be indecisive when confused

**About Reggie**:
- Archie shouldn't trust Reggie, but he often does
- Because Archie always tries to be a nice guy
- And Archie is kind of gullible
- *Reveals*: Archie's niceness is also a weakness

**About Jughead**:
- Archie's best friend
- What they have in common: they are both underdogs
- *Reveals*: Archie identifies with underdog status

#### Veronica's Perspective

**About Archie**:
- Veronica finds Archie attractive
- But sometimes seeks to date him to frustrate Betty
- And because she can always feel superior around him
- *Reveals*: Veronica is competitive and insecure about status

**About Betty**:
- Veronica trusts Betty as a friend (friends as little girls)
- Likes how she can always feel superior to Betty (wealth and class)
- But really frustrated that Betty is a better person
- *Reveals*: Veronica has internal conflict between friendship and competition

**About Reggie**:
- Attractive buffoon who appreciates wealth
- But Veronica gets frustrated that Reggie doesn't really respect or love her
- *Reveals*: Veronica wants genuine affection, not just admiration

**About Jughead**:
- A nauseating freak—can't understand why Archie is friends with him
- Often bribes him with food to get what she wants
- *Reveals*: Veronica is pragmatic and manipulative

#### Betty's Perspective

**About Archie**:
- Her true love
- Shy about telling him how she really feels
- Because Betty has low self-esteem
- *Reveals*: Betty's main obstacle is internal

**About Veronica**:
- Betty's BFF
- Can be mean sometimes, too money crazy
- But friends are friends forever, so Betty stays with Veronica
- *Reveals*: Betty values loyalty above all

**About Reggie**:
- Intimidated by his wealth and showy attempts at class
- Feels like she is supposed to like him
- But secretly repelled by him
- *Reveals*: Betty has hidden strength of conviction

**About Jughead**:
- Thinks he's cute and funny
- Glad that he's such a good friend to her true love
- *Reveals*: Betty is generous and supportive

#### Reggie's Perspective

**About Archie**:
- Reggie's archrival
- Can't imagine what anyone sees in such a dopey nice guy
- Occasionally envies Archie's popularity
- But always thinks he can find a tricky way to outdo Archie
- *Reveals*: Reggie is arrogant and scheming

**About Veronica**:
- Finds her attractive and rich
- Likes the power of her wealth
- *Reveals*: Reggie values status and power

**About Betty**:
- Finds her attractive
- Though her low self-esteem is a turnoff
- To be able to win her would show his superiority over Archie
- *Reveals*: Reggie sees relationships as competitions to win

**About Jughead**:
- Sees him as a total loser who deserves to be bullied
- Especially since he is friends with Archie
- *Reveals*: Reggie is a bully who targets the weak

#### Jughead's Perspective

**About Archie**:
- Jughead's best friend
- The only one who understands and appreciates Jughead's love of food
- *Reveals*: Jughead values acceptance

**About Veronica**:
- The mean girl that Archie likes
- *Reveals*: Jughead is simple and straightforward

**About Betty**:
- The nice girl that Archie likes
- *Reveals*: Jughead observes but doesn't judge

**About Reggie**:
- A bully
- *Reveals*: Jughead is a victim but accepts it

### What the Character Web Reveals

**Asymmetries**:
- Betty loves Archie, but Archie is torn between Betty and Veronica
- Reggie bullies Jughead, but Jughead just sees him as "a bully" (no personal hatred)
- Veronica wants respect from Reggie, but Reggie only values her wealth

**Hidden Motivations**:
- Veronica dates Archie partly to frustrate Betty
- Reggie wants Betty partly to beat Archie
- Betty stays friends with Veronica out of loyalty to childhood friendship

**Character Flaws**:
- Archie is gullible
- Veronica is manipulative
- Betty has low self-esteem
- Reggie is arrogant
- Jughead is passive

**Relationship Complexity**:
- Veronica both trusts Betty and competes with her
- Archie loves Betty but her mixed signals confuse him
- Betty feels she should like Reggie but is secretly repelled

### How to Create Your Own Character Web

**Step 1: List All Characters**

**Step 2: For Each Character, Write About Every Other Character**

Ask:
- How does this character feel about the other?
- Why do they feel this way?
- What do they want from this relationship?
- What do they fear from this relationship?
- What history do they have?
- What misconceptions might they have?

**Step 3: Look for Asymmetries**
- Where do feelings not match?
- Who loves whom more?
- Who trusts whom more?
- Who needs whom more?

**Step 4: Identify Unaccounted Connections**
- Are there characters who never interact?
- Why not? Could that change?
- What would happen if they did interact?

**Step 5: Check for Too Many Similar Connections**
- Do all relationships feel the same?
- How can they be more different?
- What variety is missing?

### Using the Web in Your Game

**Dialog**:
- Every line of dialog reflects the web
- Characters speak differently to different people
- Underlying feelings color word choices

**Actions**:
- Characters act differently toward different people
- Help some, hinder others, ignore still others
- Actions reveal true feelings

**Player Choices**:
- In branching narratives, web shows consequences
- Who will be angry? Who will be pleased?
- How will this affect future interactions?

## Tool 3: Status

### What Is Status?

Whenever people meet or interact, there is a hidden negotiation that constantly takes place. We are mostly not conscious of it. It is our negotiation of **status**: who is in charge of the current interaction?

**Critical Insight**: Status is not a matter of who you are; **status is something you do**.

### The Tramp and the Duchess Example

From Keith Johnstone's *Impro*:

> TRAMP: 'Ere! Where are you going?
>
> DUCHESS: I'm sorry, I didn't quite catch…
>
> TRAMP: Are you deaf as well as blind?

**What's Happening**: The tramp (who you might expect to be very low status) is taking on an attitude of very high status. Meanwhile, the duchess is playing lower status by apologizing and deferring.

**Lesson**: Any time two or more people interact—whether friends or enemies, collaborators or competitors, masters or servants—a negotiation of status takes place.

### Status Is Subconscious

We do this almost entirely subconsciously with:
- Posture
- Tone of voice
- Eye contact
- Dozens of other detailed behaviors

**Surprising Fact**: These behaviors are consistent across all cultures.

### Typical Status Behaviors

#### Low-Status Behaviors

- **Fidgeting**: Nervous movements, can't stay still
- **Avoiding eye contact**: Looking away, looking down
- **Touching one's own face**: Self-soothing gestures
- **Being tense**: Rigid posture, tight muscles
- **Making oneself small**: Hunched shoulders, taking up less space
- **Turning head while speaking**: Lack of confidence in words
- **Apologizing frequently**: "Sorry," "excuse me"
- **Seeking permission**: "Is it okay if…?"

#### High-Status Behaviors

- **Being relaxed**: Loose, comfortable posture
- **Being in control**: Deliberate movements
- **Making strong eye contact**: Direct gaze, holding eye contact
- **Not moving head while speaking**: Head stays still, voice projects
- **Taking up space**: Expansive posture
- **Slow, deliberate movement**: Not rushed
- **Commanding tone**: Statements, not questions
- **Silence**: Comfortable with pauses

### The Improv Exercise

**Setup**: Split group of actors into two groups who then intermingle:
- **Group 1 (low status)**: Make brief eye contact, then look away
- **Group 2 (high status)**: Make and hold eye contact with others

**Result**:
- Low-status group quickly feels inferior, starts unconsciously taking on other low-status characteristics
- High-status group starts to feel superior, takes on high-status characteristics

**Insight**: This isn't just playing pretend—the behaviors actually **make** you feel higher or lower status.

### Try It Yourself

**High-Status Exercise**:
- Talk without moving your head at all
- Notice how it makes you feel

**Low-Status Exercise**:
- Talk while turning your head frequently
- Notice the difference in feeling

**You'll quickly get the idea.**

### Status Is Relative, Not Absolute

**Example: Darth Vader**
- **With Princess Leia**: Takes on high-status behavior (controlling, dominant)
- **With the Emperor**: Takes on low-status behavior (deferential, obedient)

**Same character, different status depending on who he's interacting with.**

### Surprising Ways Status Is Conveyed

#### Slow Motion = High Status

Seen in:
- *The Six Million Dollar Man*
- *The Matrix*
- Countless shampoo commercials

**Why**: Slow, deliberate movement indicates you're not rushed, not desperate—in control.

#### Space Occupation = Status

- **Low-status characters**: Go to places where they're less likely to encounter others or be noticed (corners, edges, back of room)
- **High-status characters**: In the most important place in the room (center, head of table, best view)

**Application**: Where your characters stand tells us about their status.

### Status Is a Secret Language

We all know it so well we don't know we're speaking it.

**The Problem**: When we create artificial characters, it doesn't occur to us to give them these behaviors, because we aren't consciously aware that we do these things.

**The Solution**: If you **do** give these behaviors to your characters, they will seem aware of each other in a way that most videogame characters are not.

### Game Example: Munch's Oddysee

**Characters**:
- **Munch**: Bound to wheelchair (low status)
- **Abe**: Former slave (low status)
- **Enemies**: Arrogant (high status)
- **Followers**: Slavish (low status)

**What Makes It Work**:
- Interactions between all these are interesting
- Great deal of comedy from **unexpected status reversals**
  - Followers mouthing off at Munch
  - Followers mouthing off at enemies
- Characters show **awareness of each other's presence**
- While crude, a step above many other games

### Status in Arguments

Arguments are interesting because people are **vying for status**:
- Either by raising their own status
- Or lowering the status of their opponent
- The **seesaw of status** is what makes arguments interesting

**As discussed in Chapter 16**: Things that change dramatically are inherently interesting. Status is one of those important things.

### Status Isn't Just Dialog

Status is also about:
- **Movement**: How characters walk, gesture, occupy space
- **Eye contact**: Who looks at whom, who looks away
- **Territory**: Who controls what space
- **What characters do**: Actions that assert or defer

**It's a way of looking at the world.**

### Brenda Harger's Research

Brenda Harger, excellent improv actress and researcher at Carnegie Mellon's Entertainment Technology Center:

**Her Work**: Creating artificially intelligent characters that:
- Are aware of their status
- Aware of the status of other characters
- Automatically adopt appropriate postures, actions, and personal space

**Current State**: Right now, most videogame characters behave the same way no matter who is around.

**Future**: Next generation of interactive game characters will seem more alive because they are aware of status.

### Design Questions About Status

1. **What are the relative status levels of the characters in my game?**
   - Who is higher status than whom?
   - Does this change based on who they're interacting with?

2. **How can they show appropriate status behaviors?**
   - Posture, eye contact, voice, movement
   - Occupation of space
   - Response to others

3. **Conflicts of status are interesting—how are my characters vying for status?**
   - Arguments
   - Power struggles
   - Competitions

4. **Changes of status are interesting—where do they happen in my game?**
   - Unexpected reversals
   - Character growth
   - Plot twists

5. **How am I giving the player a chance to express status?**
   - Dialog choices (deferential vs commanding)
   - Action choices (help vs hinder)
   - Positioning in space
   - Timing (waiting vs acting immediately)

### Practical Applications

**NPCs Reacting to Player**:
- Low-status NPCs cower, fidget, avoid eye contact
- High-status NPCs stand tall, make demands, hold eye contact
- Equal-status NPCs engage neutrally

**Player Expressing Status**:
- Dialog wheel options: polite (low status) vs demanding (high status)
- How close player can approach before NPC reacts
- Whether NPC initiates conversation or player must

**Environmental Storytelling**:
- Where NPCs stand reveals their status
- Who gets the good chair?
- Who sits vs who stands?
- Who enters first?

**Cutscene Blocking**:
- High-status characters center frame, good lighting
- Low-status characters edges, shadow
- Eye-lines show who defers to whom

### Beyond Game Design

Understanding status helps you:
- Understand real-life situations
- Navigate design meetings
- Handle client negotiations
- Manage team dynamics

**We'll discuss those in later chapters.**

## Using All Three Tools Together

### Step-by-Step Process

**1. Start with the Interpersonal Circumplex**
- Quick visual overview
- Identify patterns and gaps
- Ensure variety in relationship types

**2. Create the Character Web**
- Deep dive into each relationship
- Explore motivations and feelings
- Identify asymmetries and conflicts

**3. Apply Status Dynamics**
- For each relationship, consider status negotiation
- Plan specific status behaviors
- Identify status conflicts and changes

### Example: Designing a New Character Cast

**Scenario**: You're creating a space exploration game with a crew of five characters.

#### Phase 1: Circumplex (Quick Draft)

**Central Character**: Captain (player avatar)

**Plot on Graph**:
- **Engineer** (upper right): Friendly, slightly dominant (challenges captain's decisions but supportively)
- **Scientist** (lower right): Friendly, submissive (eager to please)
- **Security Officer** (upper left): Hostile, dominant (constantly questions authority)
- **Medic** (lower left): Hostile, submissive (resentful but obedient)

**Analysis**: Good variety across quadrants, but might want someone neutral to balance.

#### Phase 2: Character Web (Deep Dive)

**Captain (Player) → Security Officer**:
- Respects their skills but frustrated by constant challenges
- Wants their loyalty but won't compromise principles to get it
- Secretly worried they might be right sometimes

**Security Officer → Captain**:
- Believes captain is too soft, will get crew killed
- Respects captain's tactical skills
- Testing captain to see if they're worthy of command
- Deep down, wants to be proven wrong about captain

*[Continue for all relationship pairs]*

**Discovery**: Security Officer and Captain both want each other's respect but approach it differently. This creates interesting tension!

#### Phase 3: Status Dynamics (Behavioral Details)

**Captain-Security Officer Interactions**:

**Status Conflict**:
- Captain has **positional authority** (high status role)
- Security Officer has **experiential authority** (been in more combat)
- They fight for who's actually in charge of each situation

**Behaviors**:
- **Captain**: Tries to maintain high status (strong eye contact, commands) but sometimes falters (looks away when challenged, defensive tone)
- **Security Officer**: Alternates between low status (follows orders, salutes) and high status (interrupts, makes demands)

**Status Change Arc**:
- Early game: Security Officer wins most status conflicts
- Mid game: Balance shifts as captain proves themselves
- Late game: Security Officer genuinely defers, captain genuinely listens

**Result**: Rich, dynamic relationship that evolves over time.

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

### Mistake 1: All Relationships the Same

**Problem**: Every character relates to every other character in the same way.

**Solution**: Use the circumplex to ensure variety across all four quadrants.

### Mistake 2: Symmetric Relationships

**Problem**: If A feels X toward B, then B feels X toward A (unrealistic).

**Solution**: Use the character web to create asymmetries.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Status

**Problem**: Characters behave the same regardless of who they're with.

**Solution**: Plan status behaviors for each relationship pair.

### Mistake 4: Static Relationships

**Problem**: Relationships never change throughout the game.

**Solution**: Plan relationship arcs with status changes and web updates.

### Mistake 5: Over-Explaining

**Problem**: Characters explicitly state how they feel about each other.

**Solution**: Show through behavior, status, and subtext—not exposition.

## Summary: Three Tools, One Goal

**Interpersonal Circumplex**: Quick visual patterns
**Character Web**: Deep psychological understanding
**Status**: Behavioral specifics

**Together**: Rich, dynamic, believable character relationships that:
- Feel authentic
- Create interesting drama
- Support gameplay
- Evolve over time
- Give players meaningful social experiences
