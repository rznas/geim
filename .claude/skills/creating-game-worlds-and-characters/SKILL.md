# Creating Game Worlds and Characters

## Overview

This skill helps you create two interconnected elements of game design:

1. **Transmedia Worlds**: Fictional universes that can exist across multiple media (games, films, toys, comics) and feel solid, real, and worth visiting
2. **Compelling Characters**: Avatars and NPCs with depth, personality, and meaningful relationships that bring your world to life

**When to use this skill:**
- Designing a new game world or expanding an existing one
- Creating a cast of characters for your game
- Developing player avatars that resonate with your audience
- Building worlds intended to span multiple games or media
- Adding depth to character relationships and interactions
- Designing characters for narrative-driven games

**Dependencies:** This skill builds on `designing-core-gameplay-experience` (Agent 1, Skill 1.2) to ensure your world and characters support your core experience goals.

## Core Concepts

### Transmedia Worlds

A **transmedia world** is a fantasy universe that can be entered through many different media—games, films, books, toys, and more. The world exists apart from any single medium, and each medium serves as a "gateway" into that world.

**Key Properties:**
- **Powerful**: Successful worlds become personal utopias that fans fantasize about visiting
- **Long-lived**: Great worlds (Superman, Star Trek, Pokémon) last for decades
- **Evolving**: Worlds change over time as new gateways are added (e.g., Sherlock Holmes' deerstalker cap came from illustrations, not the original text)

**What Successful Worlds Have in Common:**
- Rooted in a single medium (but expandable to others)
- Intuitive (everyone "knows" they exist)
- Created by a single individual or small tight-knit team
- Facilitate telling many stories (not just one plotline)
- Make sense through any gateway (don't require reading the book to enjoy the game)
- About discovery and wish fulfillment

### Game Characters vs Other Media

Game characters differ from novel and film characters:

| Medium | Character Focus | Complexity | Reality/Fantasy |
|--------|----------------|------------|-----------------|
| Novels | Mental struggles (thoughts) | Complex | Reality-based |
| Films | Emotional + physical (actions) | Moderate | Reality → Fantasy |
| Games | Physical actions (gameplay) | Simple | Fantasy-heavy |

This doesn't mean game characters must be shallow—it means you need to work harder to add depth while supporting gameplay.

## World Design Workflow

### Step 1: Define Your World's Fantasy

**Ask yourself:**
- What fantasy does my world fulfill?
- Who does the player fantasize about being?
- What does the player fantasize about doing there?
- How is my world better than the real world?

Reference: **Lens #83: The Lens of Fantasy** (see [world-and-character-lenses.md](world-and-character-lenses.md))

### Step 2: Establish World Properties

Consider how your world will function across media:

**If planning transmedia expansion:**
- Can there be multiple gateways to my world? (game, toy, comic, etc.)
- How do they differ? How do they support each other?
- Is my world centered on a single story, or could many stories happen here?

**Core medium strength:**
- Which medium is your world rooted in? (Pokémon = handheld game, Star Wars = film)
- Ensure your world is strongest in its original medium

Reference: **Lens #84: The Lens of the World** (see [world-and-character-lenses.md](world-and-character-lenses.md))

For detailed world properties and examples, see: [transmedia-world-properties.md](transmedia-world-properties.md)

### Step 3: Design World Consistency

**Consistency is critical.** If gateways contradict each other or provide inconsistent information, the world crumbles.

**Maintain consistency by:**
- Creating a world bible documenting all canonical facts
- Having a single creative vision holder (individual or small team)
- Testing each new gateway: "Does this feel like it belongs in this world?"
- Using the "binocular effect": one medium can fill in details that another medium only hints at

## Character Design Workflow

### Step 1: Design Your Avatar

The avatar is the player's gateway into your world. Two successful approaches:

**The Ideal Form:**
- Characters players have always wanted to be
- Examples: mighty warriors, powerful wizards, attractive princesses, ultra-suave secret agents
- Appeals to the force inside us that pushes us toward being our best

**The Blank Slate (Iconic):**
- Simple, less detailed characters that players can easily project themselves into
- Less detail = more projection opportunity
- Examples: Mario (simple, hardly speaks, nonthreatening), Spider-Man (mask = blank slate)
- Works because people don't play games to be themselves—they play to be who they wish they could be

**Often mixed together:** Ideal form + iconic qualities = powerful avatar (e.g., Spider-Man is powerful but faceless)

Reference: **Lens #85: The Lens of the Avatar** (see [world-and-character-lenses.md](world-and-character-lenses.md))

For detailed avatar design principles, see: [avatar-design-principles.md](avatar-design-principles.md)

### Step 2: Define Character Functions

List all the functions characters need to fulfill in your game, then map characters to functions.

**Example Functions:**
1. Hero (player character)
2. Mentor (gives advice and useful items)
3. Assistant (gives occasional tips)
4. Tutor (explains how to play)
5. Final boss (last battle)
6. Minions (regular enemies)
7. Mid-bosses (tough battles)
8. Hostage (someone to rescue)

**Creative Mapping:**
- Don't default to obvious assignments (princess = hostage)
- Try unexpected combinations (princess = mentor or final boss)
- Combine functions to save development cost (Wise Old Owl = mentor + final boss twist)
- Use multiple characters for one function or one character for multiple functions

Reference: **Lens #86: The Lens of Character Function** (see [world-and-character-lenses.md](world-and-character-lenses.md))

### Step 3: Define Character Traits

Create a short list of traits (3-5) that encapsulate each character's essence.

**Good traits are:**
- Consistent across situations
- Slightly contradictory (like real people)
- Expressed in dialog, actions, and appearance

**Example:**
- *Sabu: trustworthy, short tempered, valiant, fiery lover*
- *Lester: arrogant, sarcastic, spiritual, impulsive*

**Apply traits everywhere:**
- Dialog (word choice, tone)
- Actions (how they walk, fight, interact)
- Animation (jump style, posture)
- Appearance (clothing, expressions)

Reference: **Lens #87: The Lens of Character Traits** (see [world-and-character-lenses.md](world-and-character-lenses.md))

### Step 4: Map Character Relationships

Use multiple tools to understand how your characters relate:

**A. Interpersonal Circumplex:**
- Graph with axes: Friendly/Hostile and Dominant/Submissive
- Plot characters relative to a central character
- Look for gaps, extremes, and patterns

**B. Character Web:**
- Write how each character feels about every other character
- Explore the "why" behind relationships
- Identify unaccounted connections

**C. Status Dynamics:**
- Consider who is "in charge" during each interaction
- Status is something you DO, not who you ARE
- High status: relaxed, strong eye contact, head still while speaking
- Low status: fidgeting, avoiding eye contact, touching own face, tense

References:
- **Lens #88: The Lens of the Interpersonal Circumplex**
- **Lens #89: The Lens of the Character Web**
- **Lens #90: The Lens of Status**

For detailed relationship tools and examples, see: [character-relationships-guide.md](character-relationships-guide.md)

### Step 5: Plan Character Transformation

Great stories transform characters. Create a character transformation chart with characters on the left and story sections across the top.

**Questions to ask:**
- How does each character change throughout the game?
- How am I communicating those changes to the player?
- Is there enough change?
- Are the changes surprising and interesting?
- Are the changes believable?

**Types of transformation:**
- Temporary/small changes
- Great/permanent changes
- Gradual evolution
- Sudden reversals

Reference: **Lens #91: The Lens of Character Transformation** (see [world-and-character-lenses.md](world-and-character-lenses.md))

## Ten Character Tips (Quick Reference)

Complete details in [character-design-guide.md](character-design-guide.md)

1. **List Character Functions**: Separate functions from characters, then map creatively
2. **Define and Use Character Traits**: Create 3-5 core traits and express them in everything the character does
3. **Use the Interpersonal Circumplex**: Graph character relationships on friendly/hostile and dominant/submissive axes
4. **Make a Character Web**: Write how each character feels about every other character
5. **Use Status**: Characters negotiate status in every interaction through posture, eye contact, and behavior
6. **Use the Power of the Voice**: Voice acting should be central to character creation, not peripheral
7. **Use the Power of the Face**: Facial animation deserves attention—especially the eyes ("windows to the soul")
8. **Powerful Stories Transform Characters**: Show how events change characters over time
9. **Let Your Characters Surprise Us**: Do the unexpected thing to keep players engaged
10. **Avoid the Uncanny Valley**: Too-realistic characters can become creepy; stay cartoonish or go fully realistic

## Common Pitfalls

### World Design Pitfalls

**Inconsistency across gateways:**
- Problem: Different media contradict each other
- Solution: Maintain a world bible; have a single creative vision holder

**World centered on one story:**
- Problem: Can't expand to other stories or media
- Solution: Design for multiple stories from the start; leave room for guests to imagine their own stories

**Requiring multiple gateways:**
- Problem: "It makes more sense if you read the book"
- Solution: Each gateway must be understandable and enjoyable on its own

### Character Design Pitfalls

**Flat, unchanging characters:**
- Problem: Characters pass through the story untouched
- Solution: Use Character Transformation Chart to plan changes

**Weak voice acting:**
- Problem: Voice added last, actors just imitating existing animations
- Solution: Involve voice actors early; design characters around their voices (like animated films do)

**Ignoring facial animation:**
- Problem: Dead eyes, no emotional expression
- Solution: Focus on eyes and facial animation; even simple expressions are powerful

**Falling into the Uncanny Valley:**
- Problem: Near-realistic characters that move wrong feel creepy
- Solution: Stay cartoonish OR go fully realistic; avoid the middle ground

**All characters behave the same:**
- Problem: Characters ignore status and relationships
- Solution: Give characters awareness of each other using status and relationship dynamics
