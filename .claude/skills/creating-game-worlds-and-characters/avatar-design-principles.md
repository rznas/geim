# Avatar Design Principles

## What Is an Avatar?

**Definition**: The character that a player controls in a game. The word is derived from Sanskrit, referring to a god magically taking physical form on earth.

**Why the Name Fits**: A similarly magical transformation takes place when players use their avatar to enter the world of the game.

**The Strange Relationship**: There are times when the player is distinctly apart from the avatar, but other times when the player's mental state is completely projected into the avatar, to the point that the player gasps if the avatar is injured or threatened.

---

## The Psychology of Projection

### We Project Into What We Control

**Car Analogy**: When we drive a car, we project our identity into it as if it's an extension of ourselves.
- Examining a parking space: "I don't think **I** can fit in there" (not "my car")
- When another car collides: "He hit **me**!" (not "He hit my car!")

**Result**: We can project ourselves into a videogame character we have direct control over.

### First-Person vs Third-Person View

**The Debate**: Which is more immersive?

**First-Person Argument**:
- No visible avatar
- Direct perspective
- "Pure" projection

**Third-Person Counter-Argument**:
- The power of empathy is strong
- Players wince in imagined pain seeing their avatar suffer
- Sigh in relief seeing their avatar escape harm
- Avatar becomes a kind of "kinesthetic voodoo doll"

**Bowling Example**: Bowlers exert "body English" on the bowling ball as it rolls—subconscious movements from projecting themselves onto the ball. The bowling ball serves as the bowler's avatar.

### Beyond Tool-Like Projection

It's one thing to project ourselves into our avatar as if it's a tool. But the experience of projection can be much more powerful if we actually **relate** to the character in some way.

**Question**: What kinds of characters are best suited for players to project themselves into?

## Two Successful Avatar Archetypes

### Archetype 1: The Ideal Form

**Definition**: The kind of character the player has always wanted to be.

**Examples**:
- Mighty warriors
- Powerful wizards
- Attractive princesses
- Ultra-suave secret agents

**Why It Works**: The force inside us that pushes us toward being our best finds the idea of projecting ourselves into an idealized form very appealing.

**Key Insight**: These characters are not much like our real selves at all, but they are people we sometimes dream about being.

**Design Consideration**: Tap into aspirational desires of your target audience. What do they dream of being?

---

### Archetype 2: The Blank Slate (Iconic)

**Definition**: A character with less detail, giving more opportunity for the player to project themselves into it.

**Core Principle** (from Scott McCloud's *Understanding Comics*):
> The less detail that goes into a character, the more opportunity the reader has to project themselves into that character.

#### Visual Spectrum of Iconicity

**Detailed/Realistic** (More "Other"):
- High detail makes characters seem alien, foreign, or scary
- Harder to project into
- Good for NPCs, enemies, environments

**Simple/Iconic** (More "You"):
- Low detail makes projection easier
- Easy to see yourself in the character
- Good for player avatars

#### The Powerful Combination

**McCloud's Insight**: Combine an iconic character with a detailed world.
- Simple character = easy projection
- Detailed world = immersive, "other" feeling
- Result: "You" in a vivid, alien place

**Application to Videogames**: Same phenomenon appears beyond comics.

**Example: Mario**
- Not much of an idealized form
- Simple design
- Hardly speaks
- Completely nonthreatening
- **Result**: Easy to project yourself into him

---

### Mixing the Archetypes

Often the most powerful avatars mix ideal form and blank slate.

**Example: Spider-Man**
- **Ideal Form**: Powerful and brave superhero
- **Blank Slate**: Mask covers his face, making him almost completely iconic—could be almost anyone
- **Result**: Best of both worlds—aspirational AND projectable

**Design Strategy**: Look for ways to combine:
- Physical power/abilities (ideal form)
- Hidden face, simple features, or universal qualities (iconic/blank slate)

## Common Avatar Design Mistakes

### Mistake 1: The Photo Avatar Gimmick

**The Pitch**: "Put your own photograph on your avatar—the ultimate dream for any gamer!"

**Why It Fails**: These systems never take hold in the long term.

**The Truth**: People don't play games to be themselves—they play games to be the people they wish they could be.

**Better Approach**: Design avatars that represent aspirational identities, not literal identities.

### Mistake 2: Too Much Detail

**Problem**: Highly detailed, realistic avatar with specific personality
**Result**: Harder for diverse players to project into

**Example of Problem**:
- Specific facial features
- Defined personality traits
- Strong vocal performance with specific accent
- Detailed backstory player can't change

**When It Works**: Single-player narrative games where the avatar IS a character (e.g., Geralt in Witcher, Nathan Drake in Uncharted)

**When It Doesn't**: Games emphasizing player agency and choice

**Solution**: Match detail level to your game's goals
- More player choice = less avatar detail
- More authored narrative = more avatar detail acceptable

### Mistake 3: Not Enough Character

**Problem**: Avatar is so blank they have no personality at all
**Result**: Players don't care about them; no emotional investment

**Example of Problem**:
- Silent protagonist with no reactions
- No facial expressions
- No personality traits whatsoever
- Just a camera with a gun

**When It Works**: Pure action games, puzzle games, abstract games

**When It Doesn't**: Story-driven games, character-focused games

**Solution**: Find the balance
- Enough personality to be interesting
- Not so much that projection is blocked

### Mistake 4: Breaking the Projection

**Common Breaks**:
- Avatar does something player didn't intend (cutscene actions)
- Avatar expresses emotions player doesn't feel
- Avatar makes decisions without player input
- Jarring difference between gameplay avatar and cutscene avatar

**Solution**: Maintain consistency
- Minimize cutscenes that take control away
- Make avatar reactions match player intentions
- Keep personality consistent between gameplay and cutscenes

## Design Strategies for Different Game Types

### Action Games

**Goal**: Make player feel powerful
**Strategy**: Ideal form
**Details**:
- Capable, skilled character
- Cool abilities and moves
- Can be simple/iconic (Mario) or detailed (Kratos)
- Focus on how they move and what they can do

### RPGs (Role-Playing Games)

**Goal**: Let player create their own character
**Strategy**: Blank slate with customization
**Details**:
- Character creation tools
- Minimal predefined personality
- Player-driven dialog choices
- Physical customization options

### Narrative Adventures

**Goal**: Tell a specific story
**Strategy**: Ideal form with defined character
**Details**:
- More detail acceptable
- Strong personality and voice
- Focus on making them aspirational or relatable
- Can have specific backstory

### Puzzle/Strategy Games

**Goal**: Solve problems
**Strategy**: Very iconic or abstract
**Details**:
- Minimal character needed
- Can be purely functional (cursor, abstract shape)
- Focus on player intelligence, not avatar

### Social/Multiplayer Games

**Goal**: Express identity, interact with others
**Strategy**: Blank slate with extensive customization
**Details**:
- Player needs to express themselves
- Extensive cosmetic options
- Emotes and expressions
- Social features (name, profile, etc.)

## The Ideal Form Spectrum

Different players respond to different levels of "ideal":

### Super-Powered Ideal
- **Appeal**: Power fantasy
- **Examples**: Superman, Kratos, Master Chief
- **Audience**: Players who want to feel unstoppable

### Skilled Professional Ideal
- **Appeal**: Competence fantasy
- **Examples**: Special agents, expert thieves, master assassins
- **Audience**: Players who want to feel capable and cool

### Relatable Hero Ideal
- **Appeal**: "Ordinary person doing extraordinary things"
- **Examples**: Link (simple farm boy), Nathan Drake (everyman adventurer)
- **Audience**: Players who want to feel "this could be me"

### Underdog Ideal
- **Appeal**: Overcoming limitations
- **Examples**: Characters who start weak and grow stronger
- **Audience**: Players who want to feel progression and achievement

**Design Choice**: Match the ideal type to your target audience's aspirations.

---

## The Blank Slate Spectrum

Different degrees of iconicity work for different games:

### Pure Icon
- **Appearance**: Simple shapes, minimal features
- **Examples**: Pac-Man, Tetris blocks, mobile game characters
- **Best For**: Casual games, puzzle games, abstract games

### Stylized Character
- **Appearance**: Cartoon-like, exaggerated features, simple but expressive
- **Examples**: Mario, Sonic, many Nintendo characters
- **Best For**: Platform games, family games, adventure games

### Masked/Hidden Identity
- **Appearance**: Full body visible but face hidden
- **Examples**: Spider-Man, Master Chief, helmeted knights
- **Best For**: Action games where you want body presence but face projection

### Create-Your-Own
- **Appearance**: Player controls all details
- **Examples**: RPG character creators, avatar systems
- **Best For**: RPGs, MMOs, social games

**Design Choice**: How much projection do you want vs how much character identity?

## Testing Your Avatar Design

### Projection Test Questions

Ask playtesters:

1. **Identification**: "Did you feel like this character was you, or were you watching someone else?"
2. **Emotional Connection**: "When the character was hurt/threatened, how did you feel?"
3. **Decision Ownership**: "Did the character's choices feel like YOUR choices?"
4. **Aspiration**: "Is this a character you'd like to be?"
5. **Projection Blockers**: "Were there moments when the character did something that didn't feel like you?"

### Design Adjustment Based on Feedback

**If projection is too weak**:
- Reduce detail (make more iconic)
- Remove specific personality traits
- Give player more control in cutscenes
- Simplify visual design
- Reduce or eliminate voice acting

**If character is too bland**:
- Add some personality traits
- Give them distinctive abilities or style
- Add facial expressions and reactions
- Make them more aspirational
- Add voice and character moments

## Advanced Technique: Multiple Avatars

Some games let players control multiple avatars:

### Strategy 1: Different Ideal Forms
- **Approach**: Each avatar represents a different aspiration
- **Example**: Fighting games (fast character, strong character, tricky character)
- **Benefit**: Players can choose the ideal that matches their mood

### Strategy 2: Different Roles
- **Approach**: Each avatar has different capabilities
- **Example**: Team-based games (medic, warrior, scout)
- **Benefit**: Players can experiment with different playstyles

### Strategy 3: Different Perspectives
- **Approach**: Each avatar sees the story differently
- **Example**: *Munch's Oddysee* (slave character, wheelchair-bound character)
- **Benefit**: Multiple viewpoints on the world and story

**Design Consideration**: More avatars = more work, but can increase replayability and appeal to broader audience

## The Gender Question

### Challenge: Different Players, Different Ideals

**Problem**: Male and female players often have different aspirational ideals.

**Solutions**:

**1. Gender Choice**
- Let player choose avatar gender
- Works well for: RPGs, MMOs, create-your-own games

**2. Gender-Neutral Design**
- Make avatar iconic enough to work for everyone
- Works well for: Puzzle games, abstract games, very simple characters

**3. Multiple Playable Characters**
- Offer different avatar options
- Works well for: Fighting games, team games, anthology games

**4. Embrace Specific Character**
- Commit to one gender with strong character
- Works well for: Narrative games where avatar IS a character
- **Risk**: May limit appeal to some players
- **Benefit**: Stronger character possible

**5. Sequential Characters**
- Different avatars in different sections/levels
- Works well for: Story-driven games, episodic content

**No Perfect Solution**: Each approach has trade-offs. Match to your game's goals and audience.

## Facial Visibility Considerations

### First-Person: No Face Visible

**Pros**:
- Direct perspective
- Easy projection (no face to conflict with)
- Focus on world, not character

**Cons**:
- Less empathy from seeing character in danger
- Can't use facial expressions
- Character has less personality

**Best For**: Immersive sims, horror games, pure action games

### Third-Person: Face Sometimes Visible

**Pros**:
- Can see avatar's reactions and expressions
- Empathy from seeing character in peril
- Character can have more personality

**Cons**:
- Less direct projection
- Need to maintain visual consistency
- Facial animation required

**Best For**: Action-adventure, platformers, most character-driven games

### Hidden Face (Masks, Helmets, Hair)

**Pros**:
- Body presence without face conflict
- Visual icon (memorable silhouette)
- Easy projection into face area

**Cons**:
- Can't use facial expressions
- May feel less human/relatable

**Best For**: Action games, sci-fi games, characters where mystery helps

---

## Summary: Key Principles

1. **People project into what they control**: Use this natural tendency
2. **Two main archetypes work**: Ideal form (aspirational) and blank slate (projectable)
3. **Mixing archetypes is powerful**: Combine aspiration with projection
4. **Players want to be someone they wish they were**: Not themselves
5. **Detail level matters**: Less detail = easier projection, more detail = harder projection
6. **Match avatar to game goals**: Different game types need different avatar approaches
7. **Test for projection**: Ask players if they felt like the character was them
8. **Face visibility affects projection**: Choose based on your needs
9. **Don't break projection unnecessarily**: Keep character actions aligned with player intent
10. **When in doubt, simpler is better**: Iconic characters have stood the test of time (Mario, Link, Pac-Man)
