# World and Character Design Lenses

## Overview

This file contains all nine design lenses for world and character creation from Chapters 19-20 of *The Art of Game Design*. Each lens provides specific questions to ask yourself during the design process.

**How to Use These Lenses**:
- Use them iteratively throughout your design process
- Some lenses work best at different stages
- Don't try to use all at once—pick relevant ones for current task
- Return to lenses after playtesting to refine your designs

---

## World Design Lenses

### Lens #83: The Lens of Fantasy

**Purpose**: Ensure your world fulfills secret wishes and desires of your players.

**When to Use**:
- Early in world design (defining core appeal)
- When world feels flat or uninteresting
- When players aren't engaging with your world
- Before expanding to transmedia

#### The Questions

1. **What fantasy does my world fulfill?**
   - What secret wish does it satisfy?
   - What do players dream about when they think of my world?
   - Why would players want to visit this world?

2. **Who does the player fantasize about being?**
   - What kind of person lives in this world?
   - What capabilities do they have?
   - What makes them special or powerful?

3. **What does my player fantasize about doing there?**
   - What actions are possible in this world?
   - What adventures can they have?
   - What experiences are unique to this world?

#### Usage Guidance

**Start Broad**:
- Identify the core fantasy (power, freedom, belonging, discovery, etc.)
- Don't be too specific yet

**Get Specific**:
- What EXACT wish does this fulfill?
- "Power" is vague—"Power to protect loved ones" is specific
- "Freedom" is vague—"Freedom to explore without consequences" is specific

**Validate with Audience**:
- Ask target players what they fantasize about
- Check if your world matches their desires
- Adjust world to better align with fantasies

**Examples**:

| World | Fantasy Fulfilled | Who They Are | What They Do |
|-------|-------------------|--------------|--------------|
| Star Wars | Epic adventure, chosen one | Jedi knight, rebel hero | Save galaxy, use the Force |
| Pokémon | Collection, friendship with creatures | Pokémon trainer | Catch 'em all, battle, explore |
| Middle-earth | Heroic quest, fellowship | Brave adventurer | Destroy evil, go on journey |
| Hogwarts | Hidden magical potential | Young wizard | Learn magic, belong to house |

**Red Flags**:
- Can't clearly articulate the fantasy
- Fantasy is generic ("have fun")
- Fantasy doesn't match target audience desires
- World doesn't support the fantasy

### Lens #84: The Lens of the World

**Purpose**: Ensure your world has power and integrity as a place that exists apart from any single medium.

**When to Use**:
- When planning transmedia expansion
- When world feels thin or inconsistent
- After adding new content (checking coherence)
- Before launching in a new medium

#### The Questions

1. **How is my world better than the real world?**
   - What does it offer that reality doesn't?
   - Why would someone prefer to visit my world?
   - What limitations of reality does it remove?
   - What possibilities does it add?

2. **Can there be multiple gateways to my world? How do they differ? How do they support each other?**
   - What media could my world appear in? (game, film, toy, book, etc.)
   - What unique perspective does each gateway provide?
   - How does one gateway enhance the others?
   - Do the gateways contradict or complement each other?

3. **Is my world centered on a single story, or could many stories happen here?**
   - Is there room for new stories?
   - Can players imagine their own stories?
   - Does the world constrain or enable creativity?
   - What stories haven't been told yet?

#### Usage Guidance

**Question 1: Better Than Real World**

Look for these qualities:

**Empowerment**:
- Real world: Limited abilities
- Your world: Special powers, skills, capabilities

**Freedom**:
- Real world: Consequences, restrictions
- Your world: Explore without fear, try without failure

**Belonging**:
- Real world: May feel isolated
- Your world: Community, acceptance, understanding

**Discovery**:
- Real world: Most things already known
- Your world: Mysteries, unexplored areas, hidden secrets

**Safety**:
- Real world: Real dangers
- Your world: Can take risks without real harm

**Question 2: Multiple Gateways**

**For Each Potential Gateway, Ask**:
- Can the world be understood through this gateway alone?
- What does this gateway reveal that others don't?
- Does this gateway stay true to the world's core?

**Gateway Synergy Examples**:

*Pokémon*:
- **Game**: Deep mechanics, collection
- **TV**: Visual beauty, emotional stories
- **Cards**: Social play, portability
- **Synergy**: Game teaches strategy for cards, TV makes game graphics meaningful

*Star Wars*:
- **Film**: Epic story, visual spectacle
- **Toys**: Interactive play, imagination
- **Books**: Deep lore, character thoughts
- **Synergy**: Each adds depth to the others

**Question 3: Many Stories**

**Test by Asking**:
- Can you tell 10 different stories in this world without repeating?
- Can players imagine stories you never planned?
- Does the world have unexplored areas?
- Are there characters whose stories aren't central to the main plot?

**Signs Your World Supports Many Stories**:
- Rich history that predates the main story
- Multiple locations with distinct cultures
- Large cast of characters with independent motivations
- Unexplained mysteries that could spawn new stories
- Fans creating their own stories (fanfiction as validation)

**Red Flags**:
- World exists only to tell one specific story
- Everything in the world relates to the protagonist
- No unexplored areas or untold stories
- World feels "used up" after main story concludes

#### Practical Exercise

**The Three-Gateway Test**:

1. Imagine your world in three different media
2. For each, describe what makes it special in that medium
3. Ensure each can stand alone
4. Identify how they complement each other

**Example**: Fantasy RPG World

**Gateway 1: Game**
- **Unique**: Player agency, character customization, mechanical depth
- **Stands Alone**: Complete story, satisfying gameplay
- **Complements**: Establishes rules, defines world mechanics

**Gateway 2: Novel**
- **Unique**: Character interiority, descriptive prose, complex plots
- **Stands Alone**: Engaging story, rich characters
- **Complements**: Adds lore, explains backstory, explores side characters

**Gateway 3: Board Game**
- **Unique**: Social play, tactical decisions, physical components
- **Stands Alone**: Fun with friends, strategic depth
- **Complements**: Reinforces world rules, creates new stories through play

## Character Design Lenses

### Lens #85: The Lens of the Avatar

**Purpose**: Ensure the avatar brings out as much of the player's identity as possible.

**When to Use**:
- Designing player character
- When players aren't connecting with avatar
- Choosing between multiple avatar designs
- Deciding detail level for avatar

#### The Questions

1. **Is my avatar an ideal form likely to resonate with my players?**
   - Does it represent something they want to be?
   - Is it aspirational?
   - Does it tap into their dreams?

2. **Does my avatar have iconic qualities that let a player project themselves into the character?**
   - Is it simple enough for projection?
   - Can players see themselves in it?
   - Does it avoid details that block projection?

#### Usage Guidance

**Question 1: Ideal Form**

**Identify Your Audience's Aspirations**:
- What do they wish they could be?
- What abilities do they wish they had?
- What roles do they admire?

**Match Avatar to Aspirations**:
- Power fantasy → Powerful warrior, superhero
- Competence fantasy → Skilled professional, expert
- Belonging fantasy → Accepted hero, team leader
- Freedom fantasy → Explorer, adventurer

**Test Questions**:
- Would your target player want to be this character?
- Does the avatar embody qualities they admire?
- Is it someone they'd be excited to control?

**Question 2: Iconic Qualities**

**Check for Projection Blockers**:
- Too much facial detail?
- Specific personality that conflicts with player choice?
- Strong vocal performance with defined accent?
- Backstory that contradicts player's intentions?

**Iconic Techniques**:
- **Simple visual design**: Mario (simple features, minimal detail)
- **Hidden face**: Master Chief (helmet), Link (stylized)
- **Neutral expression**: Doesn't impose emotion on player
- **Customization**: Let player define details
- **Silence or minimal voice**: Player fills in the voice

**The Spectrum**:

```
More Projection <-------------------------> More Character
(Iconic/Blank Slate)                        (Ideal Form/Detailed)

Pure icon     Stylized    Masked     Custom     Defined
(Pac-Man)     (Mario)     (Samus)    (RPG)      (Nathan Drake)
```

**Find the Right Point**:
- Pure action game → Far left (iconic)
- Authored narrative → Far right (defined)
- Player choice matters → Middle to left
- Specific story → Middle to right

**Both Questions Together**:

**Best Avatars Often Mix**:
- Aspirational capabilities (ideal form)
- Simple/hidden features (iconic)
- Example: Spider-Man (powerful + masked)

### Lens #86: The Lens of Character Function

**Purpose**: Make sure your characters are doing everything your game needs them to do.

**When to Use**:
- Early character design
- When character roster feels incomplete
- When characters seem redundant
- Trying to reduce character count

#### The Questions

1. **What are the roles I need the characters to fill?**
   - What game functions must be fulfilled?
   - What story functions are needed?
   - What emotional functions do you want?

2. **What characters have I already imagined?**
   - Who exists in your imagination?
   - What makes each one interesting?

3. **Which characters map well to which roles?**
   - Traditional mappings?
   - Creative alternatives?

4. **Can any characters fill more than one role?**
   - Which functions could combine?
   - How would that change the character?

5. **Do I need to change the characters to better fit the roles?**
   - What adjustments would help?
   - Would changes make them more interesting?

6. **Do I need any new characters?**
   - What functions are unfilled?
   - What kind of character would fill them?

#### Usage Guidance

**Step 1: List Functions**

**Game Functions**:
- Tutorial teacher
- Quest giver
- Merchant/vendor
- Boss enemy
- Regular enemy
- Helper/sidekick
- Comic relief
- Emotional anchor

**Story Functions**:
- Mentor
- Antagonist
- Love interest
- Rival
- Betrayer
- Sacrifice
- Conscience
- Tempter

**Emotional Functions**:
- Makes player feel powerful
- Makes player feel vulnerable
- Makes player laugh
- Makes player care
- Makes player think

**Step 2: List Characters**

Write down all characters you've imagined, with their core traits.

**Step 3: Creative Mapping**

**Avoid Obvious**:
- Princess = damsel in distress ❌ (boring)
- Princess = mentor ✓ (interesting)
- Princess = final boss ✓ (twist!)

**Look for Multi-Role Opportunities**:
- Mentor who becomes final boss (betrayal arc)
- Comic relief who becomes emotional anchor (growth)
- Regular enemy who becomes helper (redemption)

**Step 4: Evaluate**

**Too Many Characters?**
- Combine roles to reduce count
- More depth per character

**Too Few Characters?**
- Split complex roles
- Add specialists

**Characters Without Functions?**
- Give them a function or remove them
- Every character should earn their place

#### Example Application

**Initial Function List (Puzzle Adventure)**:
1. Hero
2. Mentor (teaches mechanics)
3. Comic relief
4. Emotional bond (why we care)
5. Antagonist
6. Henchmen

**Initial Character List**:
- **Asha**: Curious, brave, young
- **Old Robot**: Glitchy, wise, damaged
- **The Sovereign**: Cold, calculating, powerful
- **Drones**: Mindless, efficient

**Traditional Mapping**:
- Asha = Hero ✓
- Old Robot = Mentor + Comic Relief ✓ (glitches = comedy)
- Sovereign = Antagonist ✓
- Drones = Henchmen ✓

**Missing**: Emotional bond function

**Creative Solution**:
- Old Robot = Mentor + Comic Relief + Emotional Bond
- Robot is Asha's only friend, damaged and dying
- Teaching her is the robot's last purpose
- **Result**: Fewer characters, more depth

### Lens #87: The Lens of Character Traits

**Purpose**: Ensure traits show in what characters say and do.

**When to Use**:
- Defining new characters
- Writing dialog
- Designing character animations
- When characters feel flat

#### The Questions

1. **What traits define my character?**
   - What 3-5 words encapsulate them?
   - What stays consistent across situations?
   - What contradictions make them human?

2. **How do these traits manifest themselves in the words, actions, and appearance of my character?**
   - How do traits affect dialog?
   - How do they show in movement?
   - How does appearance reflect traits?

#### Usage Guidance

**Question 1: Defining Traits**

**Choose 3-5 Core Traits**:
- Not too many (diluted)
- Not too few (one-dimensional)
- Can include one contradiction (more realistic)

**Good Traits Are**:
- **Specific**: "Loyal" not just "good"
- **Active**: "Impulsive" not "interesting"
- **Behavioral**: "Short-tempered" not "red hair"

**Example Sets**:
- *Warrior*: Honorable, proud, protective, hot-headed
- *Thief*: Clever, selfish, charming, cowardly
- *Mage*: Curious, arrogant, isolated, obsessive

**Question 2: Manifestation**

**In Dialog**:

*Short-tempered character*:
- "How dare you!" (not "That's inappropriate")
- Interrupts others
- Uses exclamation points
- Short, sharp sentences

*Arrogant character*:
- "Obviously…" "Clearly…" "As anyone would know…"
- Explains things condescendingly
- Name-drops accomplishments
- Long, elaborate sentences

**In Actions**:

*Impulsive character*:
- Runs ahead
- Interrupts conversations to act
- Doesn't wait for plans
- Charges into danger

*Cautious character*:
- Hangs back
- Suggests waiting
- Checks corners
- Moves slowly through new areas

**In Animation**:

*Sneaky character*:
- Crouched posture
- Quick, darting movements
- Looks around nervously
- Jump animation is a roll

*Depressed character*:
- Slumped posture
- Slow walk (doesn't run)
- Minimal gestures
- Sighs during idle animation

**In Appearance**:

*Proud warrior*:
- Upright posture
- Polished armor
- Displays medals/trophies
- Bold colors

*Isolated mage*:
- Withdrawn body language
- Tattered robes
- Muted colors
- Avoids eye contact

**Elegance: Multiple Traits at Once**

Best moments express 2-3 traits simultaneously:

*"By the Gods! I have news!"*
- Spiritual (invokes gods)
- Impulsive (bursts in)
- Excitable (exclamation)

#### Common Mistakes

**Too Many Traits**:
- Character becomes inconsistent
- Can't remember them all
- Dilutes each trait's impact

**Too Few Traits**:
- Character is one-dimensional
- Predictable, boring

**Traits That Contradict**:
- Good: "Brave but superstitious" (adds depth)
- Bad: "Honest but constantly lies" (incoherent)

**Traits Not Shown**:
- Saying "She's funny" but never showing humor
- Dialog doesn't reflect traits
- Actions contradict stated traits

### Lens #88: The Lens of the Interpersonal Circumplex

**Purpose**: Understanding relationships between characters by visualizing them on friendly/hostile and dominant/submissive axes.

*(For complete details, see [character-relationships-guide.md](character-relationships-guide.md))*

#### The Questions

1. **Are there any gaps in the chart? Why are they there? Would it be better if the gaps were filled?**

2. **Are there "extreme characters" on the graph? If not, would it be better if there were?**

3. **Are the character's friends in the same quadrant, or different quadrants? What if that were different?**

#### Usage Guidance Summary

**Create the Graph**:
- Pick a central character
- Plot others relative to them
- Two axes: Hostile ↔ Friendly, Submissive ↔ Dominant

**Analyze Patterns**:
- **Gaps**: Might indicate missing relationship types
- **Extremes**: Create memorable dynamics
- **Clustering**: Shows who communicates most

**Make Adjustments**:
- Fill important gaps
- Add extreme characters for contrast
- Vary quadrants for friends (different relationship types)

### Lens #89: The Lens of the Character Web

**Purpose**: Flesh out character relationships by exploring how each feels about every other.

*(For complete details with worked example, see [character-relationships-guide.md](character-relationships-guide.md))*

#### The Questions

1. **How, specifically, does each character feel about each of the others?**

2. **Are there any connections unaccounted for? How can I use those?**

3. **Are there too many similar connections? How can they be more different?**

#### Usage Guidance Summary

**Create the Web**:
- List all characters
- For each, write about every other character
- Include feelings AND reasons

**Look for**:
- **Asymmetries**: A loves B, but B tolerates A
- **Unaccounted connections**: Characters who never interact
- **Hidden motivations**: Why they really feel that way
- **Opportunities**: Unused relationship dynamics

**Apply to Design**:
- Inform dialog and interactions
- Create relationship arcs
- Plan character development
- Design meaningful choices

### Lens #90: The Lens of Status

**Purpose**: Make characters more aware of each other through status negotiation.

*(For complete details, see [character-relationships-guide.md](character-relationships-guide.md))*

#### The Questions

1. **What are the relative status levels of the characters in my game?**

2. **How can they show appropriate status behaviors?**

3. **Conflicts of status are interesting—how are my characters vying for status?**

4. **Changes of status are interesting—where do they happen in my game?**

5. **How am I giving the player a chance to express status?**

#### Usage Guidance Summary

**Understand Status Behaviors**:
- **High status**: Relaxed, strong eye contact, head still, occupies space
- **Low status**: Fidgeting, avoids eye contact, tense, makes self small
- **Status is relative**: Same character can be high/low with different people

**Design Applications**:
- NPC behaviors toward player
- Player dialog choices (polite vs demanding)
- Cutscene blocking (who stands where)
- Environmental storytelling (who gets the good chair)

**Create Drama**:
- Status conflicts (two characters both want high status)
- Status changes (character rises or falls)
- Unexpected reversals (low status character wins)

### Lens #91: The Lens of Character Transformation

**Purpose**: Ensure characters transform in interesting, believable ways.

**When to Use**:
- Planning story arcs
- When characters feel static
- After creating plot outline
- Reviewing character development

#### The Questions

1. **How does each of my characters change throughout the game?**
   - What events cause change?
   - What is the arc from beginning to end?

2. **How am I communicating those changes to the player? Can I communicate them more clearly, or more strongly?**
   - Visual indicators?
   - Behavioral changes?
   - Other characters commenting?

3. **Is there enough change?**
   - Do characters grow?
   - Are they different at the end?

4. **Are the changes surprising and interesting?**
   - Predictable or unexpected?
   - Do players care?

5. **Are the changes believable?**
   - Properly motivated?
   - Earned through events?
   - Consistent with character?

#### Usage Guidance

**The Character Transformation Chart**

**Structure**:
- Rows: Characters
- Columns: Story sections (acts, chapters, levels)
- Cells: How character has changed

**Example Entry**:
- **Act 1**: Cynical, doesn't trust anyone
- **Act 2**: Begins to open up after being saved
- **Act 3**: Fully trusts team, makes sacrifice

**Types of Change**:

**Temporary**:
- Mood changes (angry → calm → angry again)
- Short-term states (captured → free)
- Useful for pacing and interest

**Permanent**:
- Character growth (coward → brave)
- Relationship changes (enemies → allies)
- Most impactful for story

**Gradual**:
- Slow evolution over time
- More realistic
- Requires careful tracking

**Sudden**:
- Shocking reversals
- Big reveals
- High impact moments

**Communicating Change**:

**Visual**:
- Costume change (hero gets battle-worn)
- Posture change (defeated shoulders → proud stance)
- Color palette shift (bright → dark)

**Behavioral**:
- How they move (hesitant → confident)
- How they speak (formal → casual)
- How they fight (defensive → aggressive)

**Relational**:
- How others treat them (respect increases)
- Who they associate with (new allies)
- Where they fit in hierarchy

**Environmental**:
- Access to new areas (earned through growth)
- New abilities (represents development)
- Changed reactions from NPCs

**Validating Change**:

**Is it Believable?**
- Events justify the change?
- Proper setup and payoff?
- Consistent with character's core?

**Is it Interesting?**
- Surprising but inevitable?
- Makes you care more?
- Creates new story possibilities?

**Is it Clear?**
- Player notices without being told?
- Multiple indicators reinforce it?
- Can track the arc?

#### Common Transformation Patterns

**The Hero's Growth**:
- Naive → Experienced
- Weak → Strong
- Selfish → Selfless

**The Villain's Fall**:
- Noble → Corrupted
- Idealistic → Cynical
- Ally → Enemy

**The Mentor's Sacrifice**:
- Powerful → Weakened
- Guide → Saved by student
- Present → Absent (death)

**The Rival's Respect**:
- Hostile → Competitive
- Competitive → Respectful
- Respectful → Allied

**The Betrayer's Redemption**:
- Trusted → Traitor
- Traitor → Regretful
- Regretful → Redeemed

## Using the Lenses Together

### Workflow Suggestion

**World Design Phase**:
1. **Lens #83 (Fantasy)**: Define core wish fulfillment
2. **Lens #84 (World)**: Ensure world integrity and expansion potential

**Avatar Design Phase**:
3. **Lens #85 (Avatar)**: Balance ideal form vs iconic projection

**Character Design Phase**:
4. **Lens #86 (Function)**: Map characters to needed roles
5. **Lens #87 (Traits)**: Define 3-5 traits per character

**Relationship Design Phase**:
6. **Lens #88 (Circumplex)**: Quick visual of relationship patterns
7. **Lens #89 (Web)**: Deep dive into each relationship
8. **Lens #90 (Status)**: Add behavioral specifics

**Story Arc Phase**:
9. **Lens #91 (Transformation)**: Plan how characters change

### Iterative Use

**Don't Use All Once**:
- Too overwhelming
- Different lenses for different stages

**Return Regularly**:
- After playtesting
- When stuck
- Before major milestones

**Mix and Match**:
- Some lenses work better together
- Some are alternatives to each other

**Trust the Process**:
- Lenses prompt questions
- Questions reveal problems
- Problems become opportunities
