# Transmedia World Properties

## What Are Transmedia Worlds?

**Definition**: Fantasy worlds that can be entered through many different media—print, video, animation, toys, games, and many others.

**Key Insight**: The world exists apart from the media that support it. You're not selling a story or a toy or a game—you're selling a world. The products are gateways into this world.

**Why They Matter**: We want these worlds to be real. Some part of us wants to believe that these worlds aren't just stories in books, sets of rules, or actors on a screen, but that they actually exist and maybe, somehow, someday, we can find our way to them.

---

## Case Study: Star Wars

### The Power of Toys as Gateways

In May 1977, *Star Wars* premiered. Children went to see it again and again. A year later, Kenner Toys produced action figures that became a tremendous success.

**What children did with the toys:**
- Rarely acted out scenes from the movie (plotline was too complex for children)
- Made up all kinds of stories with only loose relationship to the film
- Often gave characters completely different names and relationships

**What this reveals:**
- It wasn't the plotline that was compelling
- It wasn't the characters specifically
- **It was the WORLD of Star Wars** that was so compelling

**Why toys were powerful:**
- Interactive (not passive like film)
- Participatory (children could act, not just watch)
- Flexible (could make up own stories)
- Portable (could take anywhere)
- Social (could play with friends)

**Result**: The toys made the Star Wars world MORE meaningful for children, not less, because they afforded the ability to visit the world, sculpt it, change it, and make it their own.

## Case Study: Pokémon

### Arguably the Most Successful Transmedia World

**Combined sales**: Over $90 billion, second only to Mario in videogame franchise success.

### Gateway 1: The Game Boy Game (1996)

- Spent five years developing and perfecting
- Traditional RPG where you capture monsters and make them part of your team
- Graphics were primitive (four shades of olive, simple wiggling sprites)
- But interactions were rich and properly balanced

### Gateway 2: The Anime TV Show

**Key Strength**: Unlike other game-based shows, it reflected the game mechanics very closely
- Adventures based directly on the Game Boy game quest path
- Watching the show helped players understand game strategies
- Showed Pokémon in full color with dramatic animation

**The Binocular Effect**: After seeing the TV show, players could map those vivid images onto the crude Game Boy graphics in their imagination. The graphics became irrelevant because imagination filled in the details.

**Synergy**: Wanting to succeed at the game gave reasons to watch the TV show, and watching the TV show made playing the game more vivid and exciting.

### Gateway 3: The Collectible Card Game (1999)

- Worked with Wizards of the Coast (Magic: The Gathering creators)
- Held as closely as possible to core mechanics of Game Boy game
- Portable and very social (unlike mostly solo Game Boy game)
- Low price and accessibility made it popular with children
- Fit naturally with Pokémon slogan: "Gotta catch'em all!"

### Why Pokémon Won

**Three complementary gateways** into a single solid world made it unstoppable. Traditional entertainment companies didn't understand this—they thought in terms of "a big Hollywood movie defines the world, then toys/games mimic that."

**The Pokémon Insight**: A world can be based in the ruleset of a handheld videogame. The world gets STRONGER with each new medium you add to it (if done consistently).

## Properties of Transmedia Worlds

### Property 1: They Are Powerful

Successful transmedia worlds exert a powerful effect over fans. Stronger than love of a story—almost as if the world becomes a personal utopia they fantasize about visiting.

**For Some**: Short-term mental break (keeping a Transformer toy as decoration)
**For Others**: Active daily engagement (Scott Edward Nall legally changing his name to "Optimus Prime" on his 30th birthday)

**Pattern**: If you look at "hardcore fans" of any fiction, the properties with the most devoted fans are the strongest transmedia worlds:
- Star Trek
- Star Wars
- Transformers
- Lord of the Rings
- Marvel Comics
- Harry Potter

**What Drives This**: More than enjoyment of a storyline or appreciation of characters, the desire to enter a fantasy world seems to propel fans to extremes.

### Property 2: They Are Long-Lived

Solid transmedia worlds continue for a surprisingly long time:

- **Superman**: Over 75 years
- **James Bond**: 60 years
- **Star Trek and Doctor Who**: 50+ years still thriving

**Walt Disney's Strategy**: Created comic books and Disneyland to keep the worlds of his animation properties alive. Disneyland gave people another gateway into the world of the films.

**Copyright Extension**: The Copyright Term Extension Act of 1998 extended corporate copyrights from 75 to 95 years, partly because some lucrative properties (early Mickey Mouse cartoons) were in danger of falling into public domain.

**Why They Last**: If you cultivate a strong transmedia world well, it can be profitable for a very long time. Particularly true for worlds that appeal to children—when children grow into adults, they often want to share the worlds with their children, creating a cycle that can go on a very long time.

### Property 3: They Evolve Over Time

Worlds do not remain static—they evolve to accommodate new gateways.

#### Example: Sherlock Holmes (100+ Years Old)

**Iconic Elements NOT in the Original Text:**
- Deerstalker cap: First appeared in illustrations by Sidney Paget (who liked wearing one himself)
- Oversized calabash pipe: Added by actor William Gillette in stage plays (visible from back row)

**Evolution Process**: The plays were so popular that future illustrators used photos of Gillette as a model. The pipe and cap became the icons for Sherlock Holmes—icons his creator, Sir Arthur Conan Doyle, never envisioned.

**Key Insight**: As new media provide new gateways to the world, the world itself (or people's perception of it) changes to accommodate the new gateways.

#### Example: Santa Claus (Even Older)

**A Fantasy Utopia People Want to Be Real**: A benevolent figure who considers your heart's desire and gives it to you if worthy.

**Multiple Paths of Entry:**
- Stories, poems, songs, movies
- Letters to Santa
- Visiting Santa in person
- Santa comes to your house and eats your cookies

**Collaborative Authorship**: No single author. Long-lived transmedia worlds are great collaborative efforts.

**Successful Additions:**
- Santa's reindeer (Clement Moore, 1823)
- Rudolph (Robert L. May, 1939)

**Failed Additions:**
- L. Frank Baum's 1902 *Life and Adventures of Santa Claus* (Santa as mortal selected for immortality by nymphs, gnomes, and demons)

**Who Decides?**: Happens as part of collective consciousness. Through unspoken democratic process, everyone decides whether a feature seems appropriate. If well-liked, it takes root. If not, it fades away. **In the long run, the world is governed by those who visit it.**

## What Successful Transmedia Worlds Have in Common

### 1. Rooted in a Single Medium

For all their many gateways, the most successful worlds started with a huge splash in just one medium:

| World | Original Medium |
|-------|----------------|
| Sherlock Holmes | Serialized fiction |
| Superman | Comic book |
| Star Wars | Movie |
| Star Trek | TV show |
| Pokémon | Handheld game |
| Transformers | Toy |

**Key Point**: Each is at its very strongest when in its original medium. Other forms support and expand, but don't replace the core.

### 2. They Are Intuitive

Very little about transmedia worlds needs explicit description—everyone somehow already knows they exist.

**Example: Toontown**: Studying *Who Framed Roger Rabbit*, very little about Toontown was actually described. Everyone already knew it existed—it was intuitive that all cartoon characters live together in a cartoon universe different than ours.

**Example: DC Comics**: The creators of Superman and Batman never intended their characters to share a world, but it was intuitive to comic readers that superheroes lived in the same world—and so now they do.

### 3. Created by a Creative Individual

The majority of successful transmedia worlds are rooted in the imagination and aesthetic styling of a single individual:

- Walt Disney
- Shigeru Miyamoto
- L. Frank Baum
- Tajiri Satoshi
- Jim Henson
- J.K. Rowling
- George Lucas

**Why**: There is something about the holistic vision of a world that comes to a single individual that gives it the strength, solidity, integrity, and beauty necessary to survive the pressure of many gateways.

**Team Creation**: Occasionally small, tight teams can create successful worlds, but it is very rare for large teams to succeed at this.

### 4. They Facilitate Telling Many Stories

Successful transmedia worlds are never based around a single plotline. They have:
- Solidity and interconnectedness that goes far beyond one story
- Room for future stories
- Room for guests to imagine their own stories

### 5. They Make Sense Through Any Gateway

**Kiss of Death**: "It makes more sense if you read the book."

**You never know which gateway guests might enter first**, so you must make all of them equally inviting and welcoming.

**Success Example: Pokémon**
- TV show, comic, video, and card games each understandable and enjoyable on their own
- Any could be a first encounter leading to other gateways later

**Failure Example: The Matrix**
- *Enter The Matrix* videogame: Parallel story that intersected with the movie—confusing if you didn't see the movie
- *Animatrix* animated shorts: Only made sense if already intimate with Matrix universe
- "It only makes sense if you enter through all the gateways" approach was intriguing for few, alienating for most

### 6. They Are About Discovery

Makes sense because a world about discovery encourages visits from many gateways.

### 7. They Are About Wish Fulfillment

Imagining a fantasy world is a lot of work. Players will not do it unless it is a world they truly would like to visit—a world that fulfills some deep and important wish.

## Design Implications for Game Designers

### If Creating a New World

**Priority 1: Define the Core Medium**
- What is your world rooted in? (Your game)
- Make sure it's STRONGEST in this medium
- Other gateways should enhance, not replace

**Priority 2: Focus on Single Creative Vision**
- Ideally one individual or small tight-knit team
- Maintain holistic vision for strength and integrity
- Document everything in a world bible

**Priority 3: Design for Multiple Stories**
- Don't center on a single plotline
- Leave room for future stories
- Let players imagine their own stories in your world

**Priority 4: Make It Intuitive**
- If you have to explain too much, it's not intuitive enough
- Tap into concepts people already understand
- Build on familiar patterns

**Priority 5: Ensure Discovery and Wish Fulfillment**
- What does your world let players discover?
- What wish does it fulfill?
- Why would players want to visit?

### If Expanding an Existing World

**Test for Consistency:**
- Does this new gateway feel like it belongs?
- Does it contradict any existing gateways?
- Can someone enter through THIS gateway first and still understand the world?

**Test for Enhancement:**
- Does this gateway add a new perspective?
- Does it make the world MORE solid, not less?
- Does it give players something they can't get from other gateways?

**Avoid "The Matrix" Problem:**
- Don't require multiple gateways to understand
- Each gateway must stand alone
- Cross-references should enhance, not confuse

## The Future of Transmedia

**Current Trend**: Transmedia worlds are the future of entertainment. No longer sufficient to focus just on creating a great experience in a single medium.

**Designer Challenge**: Create new gateways to existing worlds—not an easy task. Those who can create gateways that excite players by creatively giving them a new perspective on and enjoyment of a known world are much sought after.

**Ultimate Challenge**: Invent a successful transmedia world starting with nothing but an understanding of the audience's secret wishes. This is the most sought-after skill.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Inconsistency

**Problem**: Different gateways contradict each other
**Example**: Game says character has fire powers, comic says water powers
**Result**: World crumbles into "dust and ashes," products become worthless

**Solution**: Rigorous world bible, single creative vision holder

### Mistake 2: Gateway Dependency

**Problem**: Requiring knowledge from one gateway to understand another
**Example**: "It makes more sense if you read the book"
**Result**: Alienates newcomers, limits growth

**Solution**: Each gateway must be complete and enjoyable on its own

### Mistake 3: Single-Story Focus

**Problem**: World built around one plotline only
**Result**: Can't expand, feels constrained, guests can't imagine their own stories

**Solution**: Build room for many stories from the start

### Mistake 4: Committee Design

**Problem**: Large team without unified vision creates world
**Result**: Lacks coherence, solidity, integrity, and beauty

**Solution**: One creative individual or small tight-knit team leads vision

### Mistake 5: Missing Wish Fulfillment

**Problem**: World doesn't fulfill a deep wish
**Result**: Players won't put in the work to imagine it

**Solution**: Identify what secret wish your world fulfills for your audience
